Part I

The role & the cluster

Before the tooling and the techniques, the framing. This role sits at the intersection of researcher and infrastructure — the person who turns "my training run is slow" into "here's where the cycles went, here's the fix, here's how I'll prevent the next one." Knowing what the job actually is, and what a GPU cluster physically does, makes the rest of the work coherent.

§01What this role actually does

The job title says "AI Performance and Efficiency Engineer." In practice it has three modes, and a senior engineer fluidly shifts between them:

Mode 1 — The on-call performance partner

A researcher reports their training is slow, their inference doesn't scale, or their cluster job won't start. You profile the workload, diagnose the issue, write up findings, and recommend a fix. The fix might be a one-line PyTorch change, a kernel rewrite, a topology adjustment, or "your model is correctly tuned and the cluster is the bottleneck."

Mode 2 — The tools and observability builder

Profilers exist; turning their output into something that scales across thousands of jobs does not. You build dashboards, automated trace analyzers, regression detectors, telemetry pipelines. The goal: catch inefficiency before a researcher has to notice. nsys on one rank is a tool; nsys + parser + dashboard + alert across the fleet is engineering.

Mode 3 — The fleet-wide pattern hunter

Mine cluster-wide utilization data, find patterns no individual user could see — "jobs with this submission template all waste 30% of GPU time on dataloader stalls" — and deliver scalable interventions. Better defaults, opinionated frameworks, education, sometimes scheduler changes.

The senior version of this role is not "I optimize kernels" or "I fix slow jobs." It's "I make the cluster systematically more productive for everyone using it, with leverage that compounds across teams." Individual debugging is still part of the work — it's how you learn the patterns — but the deliverable is the system.

What you do NOT do

  • Design ML models or pick research directions. That's the researcher's job; you make their existing work faster.
  • Operate the cluster as an SRE. That's the cluster ops team; you collaborate with them on changes that affect performance.
  • Write library code in PyTorch / CUDA / NCCL. You may contribute upstream, but the primary work is application and integration.

§02The efficiency engineer's mindset

The role demands a specific set of dispositions. None of them are unique to this job, but the combination is.

Measure before you optimize, every single time

The most expensive mistake in performance engineering is fixing the wrong thing. A kernel you "know" must be slow may already be at 95% of peak. The dataloader you "know" must be fine may be the actual bottleneck. The discipline: always profile, never reason from first principles when measurement is available.

Identify the binding constraint

At any moment, exactly one thing is the bottleneck. Speeding up anything else changes nothing. The diagnostic loop:

Q: Is this workload compute, memory, communication, or I/O bound?
   |
   +--> Compute-bound: GPU SM utilization > 70%, low memory stall
   |     fix: better algorithm, Tensor Cores, fused kernels, lower precision
   |
   +--> Memory-bound: high HBM bandwidth use, low SM compute
   |     fix: data layout, coalescing, fusion to keep tensors in cache
   |
   +--> Communication-bound: ranks spend >25% in NCCL collectives
   |     fix: overlap, fewer or smaller collectives, topology, fabric
   |
   +--> I/O bound: GPU idles waiting on dataloader
         fix: prefetch, more workers, faster storage, format change

Speak the researcher's language

You will explain why a model trains slowly to people who don't care about CUDA streams. The skill is translating "your gradient bucket boundaries don't align with your microbatch schedule, causing serialization" into "let's change this flag and you'll see ~15% faster steps." Concrete, action-oriented, calibrated to what they can actually change.

Don't accept "it's just slow"

Every workload has a theoretical performance ceiling: hardware peak FLOPS, memory bandwidth, network bisection, storage throughput. Compute the ceiling, compare to observed, name the gap. The conversation changes when "training is slow" becomes "we're at 31% of the H100 BF16 peak, and the gap is mostly in attention; here are the three options."

Generalize from incidents

One job's bug is a bug; ten jobs' bug is a pattern; a hundred jobs' bug is a tool. The progression from "I helped this team" to "I built the analysis that catches this issue across the fleet" is what makes the role senior.

Know when not to help

Some optimizations don't matter. A 5% speedup on a job that runs for two hours, once, is not worth two weeks of engineering time. Quantify the value before accepting the work; sometimes the right answer is "no, this isn't the highest-leverage thing right now."

The senior efficiency engineer is the person whose involvement makes a team's research move faster, and whose absence is silently expensive across the cluster.

§03GPU cluster architecture, top to bottom

You cannot diagnose performance issues without a model of what's actually running. A modern AI training cluster has six tiers, and a senior engineer can sketch each of them on a whiteboard cold.

The three bandwidth tiers, in order of magnitude

  • HBM — thousands of GB/s. The local working set.
  • NVLink — hundreds of GB/s. Intra-node, GPU-to-GPU.
  • InfiniBand — tens of GB/s. Inter-node.

The gap between NVLink and IB is roughly 10x. This is why model and tensor parallelism — chatty by design — live within a node, and data parallelism — less chatty — spans across nodes. Topology is destiny.

A typical training job's physical path

"Train Llama-class model on 1024 H100s" expands to:

  1. 1024 GPUs spread across 128 nodes (8 per node).
  2. Each node has 8 IB NICs, one per GPU, with GPUDirect RDMA enabled.
  3. Nodes attached to a rail-optimized fabric: GPU 0 of every node connects to leaf-rail-0, GPU 1 to leaf-rail-1, etc.
  4. Within a node: tensor parallelism (4 or 8-way) uses NVLink; gradient sync touches every GPU.
  5. Across nodes: data parallelism reduce-scatters/all-gathers over IB.
  6. Pipeline parallelism (if used) sends activations between adjacent stages, also over IB.
  7. Checkpoints written to a parallel filesystem (Lustre or GPFS).

Every layer above is a place where performance can leak. The job of this role is knowing where to look first.

Why this map matters

When a job's allreduce time doubles, the cause might be: a degraded IB link (Tier 5), a degraded NIC (Tier 3), bad rank-to-node placement (Tier 4), a noisy neighbor on the same NVLink domain (Tier 2), or a software regression in NCCL (across all). Without the architectural map in your head, you debug at the wrong tier.

The profiling toolchain

The JD calls out Nsight Systems and Nsight Compute specifically. Both are essential and complementary; neither is sufficient. Add the framework-level profilers and your own custom telemetry, and you have the four tools that diagnose ~95% of real workloads.

§04The ML workload performance model

Before any tool, the mental model. Every training step is a sequence of phases, each with its own bottleneck profile. Knowing what each phase should look like sets your expectations for what the tools will show.

one training step (one micro-batch on one rank) t = 0 |--data load + augment + transfer to GPU----| \________________________________________/ if > 0, dataloader-bound |---forward pass kernels---| \________________________/ activations memory pressure fused vs unfused ops |---loss + backward---| \____________________/ ~2x forward in FLOPs needs activations or recompute |--allreduce / reduce-scatter--| \____________________________/ can overlap with backward |--optimizer step--| \________________/ reads + writes all parameters t = step_time

The performance equations

Three back-of-envelope formulas a senior engineer applies before opening any tool:

Compute ceiling for forward + backward

Total FLOPs ≈ 6 · N_params · tokens for transformer training (3 for forward, +2 for backward gradients, +1 for activation gradients; the factor of 6 is the conventional estimate). For a 70B parameter model and 4M tokens per step, that's 6 · 7e10 · 4e6 = 1.68e18 FLOPs — about 1.68 EFLOPS-seconds. At H100 BF16 peak (989 TFLOP/s), that's ~1700 GPU-seconds, distributed across however many ranks you have. Reality is usually 30-55% of peak; below 30% says something is wrong.

Memory ceiling

Per parameter, naive training stores: 2 bytes weight (BF16) + 2 bytes gradient (BF16) + 4 bytes FP32 optimizer master + 4+4 bytes Adam moments = 16 bytes per param. A 70B model = 1.12 TB of optimizer state alone, before activations. That's why ZeRO / FSDP / parameter sharding exists.

Communication ceiling

Allreduce of N gradients across W workers, ring algorithm, sends ~2 · N · (W-1)/W bytes per worker. For 70B BF16 params (140 GB) and 1024 workers, each worker sends ~280 GB on every step. At 50 GB/s IB bandwidth, that's ~5.6 seconds per step on communication alone — unless you overlap.

Compute the ceilings before profiling. The ratio of measured to predicted tells you what to investigate first. If forward time is 80% of expected, kernels are healthy; investigate allreduce. If forward time is 30% of expected, kernels are wrong.

What "GPU utilization" means and doesn't mean

nvidia-smi reports a "GPU utilization" percentage. It's the fraction of time at least one kernel was running. It says nothing about whether the kernel was doing useful work. A kernel that loads memory and stalls 99% of the time still shows 100% utilization. Treat nvidia-smi utilization as a coarse signal, not a quality measure.

The honest metrics:

  • SM activity — fraction of cycles SMs were busy issuing instructions. From dcgm or nsys.
  • Tensor Core utilization — specifically how often TC pipes were engaged. Often 5-20% on workloads that "look busy."
  • HBM bandwidth utilization — fraction of peak HBM throughput achieved.
  • MFU / HFU — Model FLOPS Utilization / Hardware FLOPS Utilization. The community-standard metric: achieved FLOPs / peak FLOPs, time-averaged over training.

§05Nsight Systems for ML workloads

nsys is the whole-system timeline. It shows what every CPU thread and every GPU is doing across time — kernels, memcpys, sync points, framework calls, NCCL collectives. It's the right starting point for "where is the time going?"

The basic invocation

# Profile a 30-second window of training, capture CUDA + NVTX + OS runtime
nsys profile \
  -o trace_$(date +%s) \
  --trace=cuda,nvtx,osrt,cudnn,cublas \
  --cuda-memory-usage=true \
  --duration=30 \
  --delay=60 \
  python train.py

Key flags:

  • --delay=60 — skip the warmup (compile/init); profile steady state.
  • --duration=30 — bound the report size; 30 seconds = a few hundred MB.
  • --trace=cuda,nvtx,osrt,cudnn,cublas,nccl — the categories that matter for ML.
  • --cuda-memory-usage=true — tracks allocations; useful for memory debugging.
  • --gpu-metrics-devices=all — periodic GPU counters; SM busy, HBM busy, etc.

NVTX ranges — making the trace readable

Without annotations, an nsys trace of a training loop is a sea of kernels. Add NVTX ranges so the trace's structure mirrors your code:

import torch
import torch.cuda.nvtx as nvtx

def train_step(batch):
    nvtx.range_push("step")

    nvtx.range_push("forward")
    logits = model(batch["input"])
    loss = loss_fn(logits, batch["target"])
    nvtx.range_pop()

    nvtx.range_push("backward")
    loss.backward()
    nvtx.range_pop()

    nvtx.range_push("optimizer")
    optimizer.step()
    optimizer.zero_grad()
    nvtx.range_pop()

    nvtx.range_pop()  # step

PyTorch profiler can emit NVTX ranges automatically via torch.profiler.emit_nvtx(). For training code you don't own, decorate the framework's hot path.

Multi-rank profiling

For distributed training, profile multiple ranks simultaneously and compare. The pattern:

# In your launch script, wrap the worker command with nsys when on selected ranks
if [[ "$RANK" == "0" || "$RANK" == "7" || "$RANK" == "511" ]]; then
  nsys profile -o trace_rank${RANK} --duration=30 --delay=60 python train.py
else
  python train.py
fi

Comparing rank 0 (often the "first" rank, sometimes special) to a typical rank, and to the last rank (often the straggler), is how you find ranks that are slow because of something specific to their position.

Tracing overhead

Profiling adds 5-15% overhead and can change behavior — especially memory profiling and very fine NVTX nesting. For "is this thing real?" investigations, that's fine. For benchmarking, profile separately from measuring.

§06Nsight Compute for kernel deep-dives

Once nsys identifies the kernel(s) that dominate, ncu tells you why. ncu replays a single kernel many times with different instrumentation passes to gather hardware counters: SM utilization, memory throughput, occupancy, stall reasons, bank conflicts.

The basic workflow

# Profile every kernel comprehensively (slow)
ncu --set full -o report ./train

# Profile only specific kernels by name (much faster)
ncu -k "regex:.*attention.*" --set full -o report ./train

# Limit kernel replay (most kernels show steady-state in <10 invocations)
ncu -k "flash_attn" --launch-count 5 --launch-skip 100 ./train

# Compare two versions of a kernel
ncu --import-source on --diff base.ncu-rep candidate.ncu-rep

ncu's view of NCCL

ncu profiles a single kernel's on-GPU work. NCCL collectives have on-GPU and off-GPU phases; ncu shows only the on-GPU portion. To analyze collective performance, use nsys + NCCL's own debug output. ncu can confirm the on-GPU reduce/copy is healthy; it cannot tell you whether the network is the bottleneck.

§07PyTorch Profiler & Holistic Trace Analysis

nsys and ncu speak hardware. PyTorch Profiler speaks framework — which Python function called which op, with framework-aware annotations. For most ML workloads, this is the first profiler researchers reach for, and the one they understand best.

Basic capture

from torch.profiler import profile, ProfilerActivity, schedule

def trace_handler(p):
    p.export_chrome_trace(f"trace_{p.step_num}.json")

with profile(
    activities=[ProfilerActivity.CPU, ProfilerActivity.CUDA],
    schedule=schedule(wait=5, warmup=5, active=3, repeat=2),
    on_trace_ready=trace_handler,
    record_shapes=True,
    profile_memory=True,
    with_stack=True,
    with_flops=True,
) as prof:
    for step in range(100):
        train_step()
        prof.step()

The Chrome trace files open in chrome://tracing or Perfetto (perfetto.dev). They show a per-thread timeline with framework calls (forward, backward, op names) annotated on the GPU's kernel timeline. Much faster than nsys to read for "what op is slow?"

Holistic Trace Analysis (HTA)

HTA (open-source, from Meta) does automated analysis on Kineto traces — the PyTorch Profiler's underlying format. It produces structured reports without manually staring at timelines:

from hta.trace_analysis import TraceAnalysis

analyzer = TraceAnalysis(trace_files=glob("traces/*.json"))

# Per-rank time breakdown: idle, compute, communication, kernel, ...
time_breakdown = analyzer.get_temporal_breakdown()

# Identify CUDA kernels that dominate time
top_kernels = analyzer.get_gpu_kernel_breakdown()

# Communication-compute overlap percentage
overlap = analyzer.get_comm_comp_overlap()

# Straggler detection across ranks
stragglers = analyzer.get_idle_time_breakdown(rank=0)

HTA is what scales: a researcher can profile 100 ranks, run HTA, and get a summary table without manually opening 100 traces. At fleet scale, you'd run it as part of CI for every job.

The framework-level questions PyTorch Profiler answers

  • Which Python function (and line) launched this slow kernel?
  • What were the tensor shapes when the kernel was called?
  • How many times was this op called? (some "small" ops are slow only at high call count)
  • How much memory did each op allocate?
  • What's the FLOPs of each op? (best-effort)

Framework-side instrumentation

import time
import torch

class TrainingMetrics:
    def __init__(self, model, world_size):
        self.world_size = world_size
        self.params = sum(p.numel() for p in model.parameters())
        self.step_start = None
        self.tokens_processed = 0

    def step_begin(self):
        torch.cuda.synchronize()
        self.step_start = time.perf_counter()

    def step_end(self, tokens_in_batch):
        torch.cuda.synchronize()
        dt = time.perf_counter() - self.step_start
        self.tokens_processed += tokens_in_batch * self.world_size

        # MFU calculation
        flops_per_token = 6 * self.params  # transformer rule of thumb
        achieved_flops_per_sec = flops_per_token * tokens_in_batch * self.world_size / dt
        peak_flops_per_sec = 989e12 * self.world_size  # H100 BF16 peak per GPU
        mfu = achieved_flops_per_sec / peak_flops_per_sec

        # Emit to telemetry sink
        emit({
            "step_time_s": dt,
            "tokens_per_sec": tokens_in_batch * self.world_size / dt,
            "mfu": mfu,
        })

Trace sampling at scale

You cannot capture full nsys traces for every job — storage and overhead blow up. The pattern: sample. Capture full traces for 1% of jobs (random), every job for "important" job classes (e.g., a new model architecture), and on every job's first hour to catch startup regressions.

import hashlib, os

def should_profile(job_id: str) -> bool:
    # Random sample 1% of jobs, deterministic per job_id
    h = int(hashlib.md5(job_id.encode()).hexdigest(), 16)
    if h % 100 == 0:
        return True
    # Always profile rank 0 of jobs in the "experimental" queue
    if os.environ.get("SLURM_QUEUE") == "experimental" and \
       os.environ.get("RANK") == "0":
        return True
    return False

The dashboards that matter

  1. Per-job summary — one row per running job: MFU, tokens/sec, comm time%, dataloader stall%, last loss, restarts.
  2. Fleet-wide MFU distribution — histogram of MFU across all running jobs. Tail is where the work is.
  3. Idle GPU detector — GPUs allocated to a job but reporting <10% SM activity for >10 min. Notify owners.
  4. Topology utilization — per-rack, per-rail bandwidth use; spots hot links and underused fabric.
  5. XID error rate — hardware-fault events; correlates with degradation before it becomes failure.

Telemetry done right is force multiplication. One engineer with the right dashboard outperforms ten engineers with shell access.

Part III

Training performance, step by step

Most performance problems in modern ML training reduce to one of five phases of the training step. Knowing each phase's normal behavior, characteristic failure modes, and the right intervention is the bulk of the day-to-day diagnostic work.

§09The training step, broken down

Every iteration of training is structurally the same regardless of model:

  1. Data load & transfer — CPU produces batch, copies to GPU.
  2. Forward pass — compute activations layer by layer, save what's needed for backward.
  3. Loss — usually a small kernel; rarely a bottleneck.
  4. Backward pass — gradients flow back; activations consumed; for distributed training, gradient communication overlaps here.
  5. Optimizer step — apply gradients to weights; update momentum/variance state.

Time profile of a healthy step (LLM training, BF16 + FSDP)

PhaseTypical share of step time
Dataload + transfer (if overlapped)0% — hidden behind compute
Forward pass30-35%
Backward pass (gradient compute)50-60%
Communication (after overlap)0-10% exposed
Optimizer step5-10%

Any phase noticeably larger than its expected share is the bottleneck.

Quick triage: where does time go?

Q: Open nsys trace. What dominates the timeline?
   |
   +-- Long gaps between steps → dataloader stall (§10) or sync waits
   |

   |                                non-Tensor-Core path
   |

Cross-rank variance — the straggler signal

Profile multiple ranks. If rank 0's step is 800 ms and rank 47's is 1200 ms, all ranks wait for rank 47 at every collective. The 47 is a straggler — investigation centers on what's different about that rank's hardware, placement, or input batch.

Common straggler causes:

  • Degraded GPU on that node (temperature throttle, ECC errors, lower boost clock).
  • Bad NIC or PCIe link causing collective slowdown that backs up.
  • Slow storage path for that rank's data shard.
  • NUMA misconfiguration causing GPU-CPU latency on that node.
  • Imbalanced data shards (some ranks see longer sequences).

§10Data loading & the input pipeline

Dataloader stalls are the single most common "easy fix" performance bug. The GPU sits idle, the trace shows long gaps, and the framework is waiting on next-batch fetch. Often discovered when nsys shows a saw-tooth pattern at step boundaries.

The healthy dataloader pipeline

CPU workers [batch N+2 prep] [batch N+3 prep] [batch N+4 prep] Pinned buffer [batch N+1 ] [batch N+2 ] [batch N+3 ] H2D copy stream [N H2D] [N+1 H2D] [N+2 H2D] GPU compute [step N] [step N+1] [step N+2] \________________/\______________/ each step's data arrived before step started

The pipeline needs three stages, all running concurrently: CPU prep ahead, pinned-memory staging buffer, async H2D, compute. If any stage stalls, the GPU waits.

The PyTorch dataloader knobs that matter

loader = DataLoader(
    dataset,
    batch_size=32,
    num_workers=8,           # enough CPU workers to keep up
    pin_memory=True,        # enables fast async H2D
    persistent_workers=True, # don't recreate workers each epoch
    prefetch_factor=2,      # batches each worker pre-builds
    drop_last=True,
)

for batch in loader:
    batch = {k: v.cuda(non_blocking=True) for k, v in batch.items()}
    # non_blocking=True with pin_memory=True => async H2D, no implicit sync
    output = model(batch)
    ...

Sizing num_workers correctly

Too few: GPU waits. Too many: oversubscribe CPUs, workers steal from each other, paradoxically slower.

  • Rule of thumb: num_workers = num_cpus_per_gpu - 1, capped at ~8.
  • Measure: monitor CPU usage during training. Workers saturating cores means scale up; cores idle means workers are the problem (slow disk or codec).
  • For distributed training: num_workers is per rank, so total CPU usage is num_workers × world_size.

§11Forward, backward, optimizer

The three compute phases of a step, each with its own profile.

Forward pass

For a transformer-like architecture, forward time is dominated by:

  • Attention — O(seq_len² · hidden); the dominant cost for long sequences.
  • MLP / FFN — O(seq_len · hidden²); usually the largest in token-count terms.
  • Normalization / activation — memory-bound; fused with neighbors where possible.
  • Embedding lookup — gather kernel; rarely a bottleneck.

The interventions that move forward time

InterventionEffect
Use FlashAttention2-4× speedup on attention, memory-linear in seq_len
BF16 / FP16 / FP8 / TF32Engages Tensor Cores; 2-8× throughput vs FP32
Fused QKV projectionOne GEMM instead of three; ~5-10% wins
Fused activation + linearEliminates memory round-trip
torch.compile10-30% on many workloads; brittle on some
CUDA GraphsRemoves kernel-launch overhead; ~5-15% on small-step workloads

Backward pass

Backward is roughly 2x forward in FLOPs, plus reads of stored activations, plus gradient writes. Three things commonly cause backward to be slower than 2× forward:

  1. Activation recomputation (gradient checkpointing) — reruns forward for memory savings; trades time for memory.
  2. Communication exposed — gradient allreduce/reduce-scatter not overlapped with compute.
  3. Slow custom autograd functions — rare, but a non-fused custom backward can dominate.

Optimizer step

The optimizer step reads gradients, reads optimizer state (Adam moments, etc.), and writes back updated weights. It's purely memory-bound and should be tiny relative to compute. When it isn't:

issue slow · optimizer step is >15% of step
Diagnosis

Profile shows AdamW kernel taking disproportionate time. Each parameter triggers several kernels (load grad, load m, load v, compute, store).

Fixes
  • Fused optimizer — torch.optim.AdamW(fused=True) uses a single CUDA kernel for the update; usually 2-5x faster.
  • foreach=True — less aggressive than fused, still better than the per-parameter default.
  • Optimizer state offload (ZeRO stage 3, FSDP) — trades GPU memory for some CPU traffic; usually wins on large models.
  • Larger micro-batch — amortizes optimizer across more compute.

The launch-overhead floor

Every kernel launch costs ~5 microseconds. A step with 5000 kernels has ~25 ms of pure launch overhead. For small models where steps are <100 ms, this is significant. Three remediations:

  • CUDA Graphs — record the step once, replay many times. Removes per-launch overhead. PyTorch's torch.cuda.graph or via torch.compile mode reduce-overhead.
  • Larger kernels — fuse small ops together; torch.compile Inductor does this automatically.
  • Larger batches — spread the launches over more work.

§12Mixed precision & activation recomputation

Two of the highest-leverage levers for ML training performance, both with subtle correctness implications. Both should be defaults in modern training; both can break.

The precision ladder

FormatBitsRange / precisionWhen to use

TF3219 (in 32 storage)FP32 range, FP16 precisionDefault on Ampere+ matmul

Why BF16 became the standard

BF16 has the same exponent range as FP32 (8-bit exponent), so it doesn't underflow or overflow where FP32 wouldn't. Its 7-bit mantissa is much less precise than FP16's 10, but for gradient accumulation and weight updates the range matters more than the precision. The result: no loss-scaling needed (unlike FP16), Tensor Cores engaged, ~2x throughput. Default for new training code.

The autocast pattern

from torch.amp import autocast, GradScaler

# BF16 training — no scaler needed
for batch in loader:
    with autocast(device_type="cuda", dtype=torch.bfloat16):
        output = model(batch)
        loss = loss_fn(output, batch.target)
    loss.backward()
    optimizer.step()
    optimizer.zero_grad()

# FP16 training — scaler needed to prevent underflow in gradients
scaler = GradScaler()
for batch in loader:
    with autocast(device_type="cuda", dtype=torch.float16):
        output = model(batch)
        loss = loss_fn(output, batch.target)
    scaler.scale(loss).backward()
    scaler.step(optimizer)
    scaler.update()
The autocast surprise

Autocast wraps the forward pass — not the loss computation in many recipes. Make sure cross-entropy or other reductions run in FP32 if they'd lose precision in BF16. Most PyTorch losses cast inputs to FP32 internally; user-written losses may not.

FP8 training

FP8 (Hopper+) gives another 2x throughput over BF16. The mechanism: per-tensor scaling factors (delayed scaling or current scaling) chosen to keep values in the FP8 representable range. Transformer Engine (transformer_engine.pytorch) and PyTorch's native FP8 path handle the bookkeeping.

Caveats: not all ops are FP8-safe (LayerNorm typically stays BF16); convergence sometimes differs subtly; per-tensor scaling adds overhead that only pays off above certain matmul sizes.

Activation recomputation (gradient checkpointing)

Backward needs forward activations to compute gradients. Storing them all is expensive: activation memory grows linearly with depth and quadratically (in some cases) with sequence length. Recomputation: drop activations at some boundaries, recompute them during backward. Trades compute for memory.

from torch.utils.checkpoint import checkpoint

class TransformerBlock(nn.Module):
    def forward(self, x):
        if self.use_checkpointing:
            return checkpoint(self._forward_impl, x, use_reentrant=False)
        return self._forward_impl(x)

    def _forward_impl(self, x):
        x = x + self.attn(self.ln1(x))
        x = x + self.mlp(self.ln2(x))
        return x

The recomputation decision

Q: Does training fit in memory without recomputation?
  yes → Don't use it. Recompute is overhead that costs FLOPs.
  no  → Can you use larger world_size or FSDP to shard further?
          yes → Prefer that first. Adding workers is usually cheaper than recompute.
          no  → Selective or full recomputation?
                  Selective: checkpoint only attention blocks (the memory hogs).
                  Full: every transformer block; 25-35% slowdown vs no recompute.

Selective activation checkpointing

Modern best practice: checkpoint only the most memory-expensive layers (typically attention), not entire blocks. PyTorch's selective_checkpoint_context_fn and Megatron-LM's --recompute-granularity selective implement this. Typical recovery: 70-80% of the memory savings of full recomputation, at 5-10% slowdown instead of 25-35%.

§13Memory pressure & OOM patterns

OOM is the single most common failure mode in modern LLM training. Several classes of OOM, each with a different diagnostic path.

The memory budget

For each rank, GPU memory must hold:

  • Model parameters (sharded under FSDP/ZeRO).
  • Optimizer state (master weights + momentum + variance for Adam).
  • Gradients (one per parameter).
  • Activations (forward outputs needed for backward).
  • NCCL communication buffers.
  • The current batch's input tensors.
  • Allocator overhead (fragmentation, reserved blocks).

The first four are what dominate. The allocator overhead bites in unexpected ways.

The OOM diagnostic flow

The allocator config knob

Distributed training & NCCL

§14NCCL fundamentals & algorithms

NCCL (NVIDIA Collective Communications Library) is the library every distributed PyTorch training run uses for gradient and tensor communication. Behind torch.distributed.all_reduce(...) is NCCL choosing an algorithm, a topology, and a set of channels.

The collectives

The algorithms behind AllReduce

NCCL picks one of several algorithms based on topology, size, and configuration:

AlgorithmBest forProperties
RingLarger messages, regular topologyBandwidth-optimal; latency = 2(W-1) steps

The Ring AllReduce mechanics

The classic algorithm, worth understanding because everything else is a variation. With W workers and N-byte tensor:

Step 1: reduce-scatter phase — W-1 steps Each worker splits the tensor into W chunks. On each step, each worker sends one chunk to neighbor, receives one chunk and adds it. After W-1 steps: each worker has the full sum of one chunk. Step 2: all-gather phase — W-1 steps Each worker sends its complete-chunk to neighbor, receives a complete-chunk from the other neighbor. After W-1 steps: every worker has the full reduced tensor. Total bytes per worker: 2 * N * (W-1) / W Total steps: 2 * (W-1)

The takeaway: with large messages, ring is bandwidth-optimal and serial steps make it latency-heavy. With small messages, the latency dominates and tree wins.

NCCL's algorithm selection

NCCL chooses automatically using tuning tables baked into the library, weighted by message size and detected topology. You can override:

# Force algorithm selection
export NCCL_ALGO=Tree         # or Ring, CollNet, NVLS

# Force protocol
export NCCL_PROTO=Simple       # or LL, LL128

# See what NCCL is doing
export NCCL_DEBUG=INFO
export NCCL_DEBUG_SUBSYS=INIT,COLL,TUNING

Protocols: Simple, LL, LL128

  • Simple — full bandwidth, higher latency. For large messages.
  • LL (Low Latency) — halves bandwidth; data + flag bytes interleaved so consumers don't need separate sync. For small messages.
  • LL128 — LL variant that uses 128-byte transactions; the modern default for many cases.

You rarely set protocol manually, but knowing the trade-off explains the debug-log lines.

The NCCL communication path

Within a node: NVLink-direct (peer-to-peer over NVSwitch). Across nodes: GPUDirect RDMA over InfiniBand (or Ethernet RoCE), bypassing the CPU and host memory entirely. The fast path needs:

  • nv_peer_mem driver loaded (for direct GPU-to-NIC).
  • NIC and GPU on the same PCIe root complex (or NVLink-connected).
  • InfiniBand HCAs in healthy state, link active, no IB-resource exhaustion.
  • NCCL detecting the right topology (verify with NCCL_DEBUG=INFO).

FSDP / ZeRO — the sharded data parallelism

Pure data parallelism replicates the entire model on every rank. For large models, that doesn't fit. ZeRO and its PyTorch instantiation (FSDP) shard parameters, gradients, and optimizer state across DP ranks, gathering them just-in-time for each layer.

StageWhat's shardedMemory savings vs DPExtra comm

FSDP configuration that matters

from torch.distributed.fsdp import FullyShardedDataParallel as FSDP
from torch.distributed.fsdp import MixedPrecision, BackwardPrefetch, ShardingStrategy

mp_policy = MixedPrecision(
    param_dtype=torch.bfloat16,
    reduce_dtype=torch.bfloat16,
    buffer_dtype=torch.bfloat16,
)

model = FSDP(
    model,
    sharding_strategy=ShardingStrategy.FULL_SHARD,    # ZeRO-3
    mixed_precision=mp_policy,
    backward_prefetch=BackwardPrefetch.BACKWARD_PRE,   # prefetch next layer's weights
    use_orig_params=True,                            # preserves param identity for optimizer
    device_id=torch.cuda.current_device(),
    limit_all_gathers=True,                           # memory safety
)

The single highest-impact knob: BackwardPrefetch.BACKWARD_PRE. Without it, each layer's backward stalls waiting for the next layer's all-gather. With it, the gather overlaps with the previous layer's compute.

The sharding-strategy ladder

  • FULL_SHARD — ZeRO-3 equivalent; maximum memory savings, full comm overhead.
  • SHARD_GRAD_OP — ZeRO-2; params replicated, grads + optimizer sharded.
  • HYBRID_SHARD — full shard within a node, replicate across nodes. Cuts inter-node bandwidth massively.
  • NO_SHARD — equivalent to DDP. Sometimes useful for small models that fit.

HYBRID_SHARD is often the right answer for large clusters: NVLink eats the within-node allgather cheaply, IB only carries the per-node reduce. The trade is more memory per rank.

§16Communication-compute overlap

The defining performance technique in distributed training: never let the GPU sit idle while the network does work. Done right, communication time disappears behind compute.

Why it works

NCCL kernels run on dedicated SMs (NCCL takes ~2-4 SMs per channel). Compute kernels run on the rest. As long as you launch them on different streams and they don't have data dependencies, they run concurrently.

The DDP gradient bucket pattern

The original overlap: PyTorch's DistributedDataParallel registers backward hooks that launch allreduce as soon as each bucket of gradients is ready. By the time backward finishes for the first layer, allreduce for the last layer (computed first in backward) is already done:

Backward (per layer) L20 grad: [compute|allreduce ] L19 grad: [ compute |allreduce ] L18 grad: [ compute |allreduce] ... L0 grad: [ compute|allreduce] | No serial allreduce after backward! Last allreduce overlaps with first layer's backward.

The "bucket size" determines the trade-off: smaller buckets mean more allreduces (more launch overhead, more overlap opportunity); larger buckets mean fewer allreduces (less overlap window). PyTorch's default 25 MB is good for most workloads; for very large models, bucket_cap_mb=200 or larger often helps.

FSDP's overlap requires prefetching

FSDP needs to all-gather a layer's weights before computing that layer. Without prefetching, every layer's forward stalls waiting for its weights. With BACKWARD_PRE prefetch:

FSDP backward, with prefetching: Layer N backward: compute [-----------| ] allgather N-1: [---| ] <- prefetched in parallel reducescatter N: [-----| ] <- triggered after compute Layer N-1 backward: compute [--------| ] ... Net: communication mostly hidden.

How to confirm overlap is happening

In nsys, look at the timeline:

  • NCCL kernels (named ncclAllReduce, ncclReduceScatter, etc.) should be on a different stream from compute kernels.
  • The bars should visually overlap — both lit during the same time window.
  • If NCCL kernels appear in serial after compute, overlap is broken.

Gradient accumulation done right

for micro_step in range(grad_accum_steps):
    batch = next(loader_iter)
    is_last = (micro_step == grad_accum_steps - 1)

    # Skip gradient sync on intermediate micro-steps; sync only at end
    sync_context = model.no_sync() if not is_last else nullcontext()
    with sync_context:
        loss = model(batch).loss / grad_accum_steps
        loss.backward()

optimizer.step()
optimizer.zero_grad()

Without no_sync, grad_accum_steps allreduces happen instead of one — usually a 30-50% step slowdown that's invisible in framework code.

The overlap measurement

Use HTA's get_comm_comp_overlap() to compute the percentage of communication time hidden by compute. Healthy is >80%; under 50% means significant work to do.

  • Network type — "IB" or "Socket"; if you see "Socket" when you expected IB, GDR isn't engaging.
  • Channel count — how many parallel data flows NCCL set up.
  • Rings/Trees — the actual ring/tree it constructed; topology issues show here.

The hang — the most-feared NCCL bug

A subset of ranks reach a collective; others don't; everyone waits forever. The watchdog should detect this with TORCH_NCCL_BLOCKING_WAIT=1 + NCCL_TIMEOUT, but root-causing requires more work.

issue hang · subset of ranks blocked at collective
Diagnosis flow
  1. Confirm hang with py-spy dump --pid <pid> --native on stuck ranks. All show NCCL stack.
  2. Identify which ranks are behind. Add per-step logging with step number; compare across ranks.
  3. If exactly one rank is behind: that rank crashed silently, or its kernel diverged (different control flow).
  4. If many ranks behind: a collective was launched only on some ranks — usually a code bug (conditional on rank, missing barrier).
  5. Check for hardware: nvidia-smi -q -d ECC,XID on the suspected hosts; XID errors mean hardware fault.
  6. Check IB: ibstat on the hosts; look for degraded links, port reset events.
Common root causes
  • Rank-conditional code — e.g., only rank 0 reads checkpoint; other ranks hit a collective first.
  • Variable-length sequences with no padding — ranks process different work, finish at different times, collectives mismatched.
  • NCCL bug or version mismatch — rare, but real; verify all ranks have the same NCCL version.
  • Silent hardware fault — ECC retirements, NVLink failure; one rank dropped off the network without crashing.

  4. Check ranks: any one rank with degraded NIC, low NVLink count?

Part V

Network, fabric & storage

Below the framework, below NCCL, sits the physical fabric and the storage that feeds it. The JD calls out InfiniBand/RDMA and Lustre/GPFS specifically — not because the engineer operates them, but because performance problems in this layer manifest as application-layer issues that can only be diagnosed if you understand the substrate.

§18InfiniBand, RDMA, and the network fabric

InfiniBand is the dominant interconnect for AI clusters at scale. It's not Ethernet with extra speed — it's a different protocol, with a different verbs API and a different operational model. Understanding it is essential.

The fundamental concepts

  • HCA — Host Channel Adapter; the IB equivalent of a NIC. Modern clusters have one HCA per GPU (8 per node).
  • QP — Queue Pair; a connection-like construct. NCCL creates many per pair of ranks.
  • RDMA — Remote Direct Memory Access; one host writes directly into another host's memory without involving CPUs.
  • GPUDirect RDMA — RDMA where the source/destination is GPU memory; bypasses host RAM entirely.
  • Subnet Manager — the entity that discovers topology and computes routes; one per IB subnet.
  • SHARP — Scalable Hierarchical Aggregation and Reduction Protocol; switches participate in collectives.

Why RDMA matters for AI

Without RDMA, sending a gradient bucket from GPU A to GPU B (on another host) is: GPU→Host RAM→TCP/IP stack→NIC→wire→NIC→TCP/IP stack→Host RAM→GPU. Five copies, lots of CPU. With GPUDirect RDMA: GPU→NIC→wire→NIC→GPU. One copy. The bandwidth difference is 3-5x at scale.

IBoP — IP over IB / IB over Protocols

The JD mentions "InfiniBand with IBOP." This typically means IPoIB (IP over InfiniBand) — running TCP/IP atop the IB fabric for management traffic, alongside the native verbs for high-performance data plane. Useful for tooling that expects a socket interface (orchestration, monitoring, control), without needing a separate Ethernet network.

SHARP — in-network reduction

NVIDIA Quantum switches support SHARP: the switch itself sums incoming gradient fragments and forwards the result. The net effect: instead of 2(W-1)/W data sent per worker (ring), it's closer to a single send/receive. Halves AllReduce traffic on supported fabrics.

SHARP comes up in trace logs as the "CollNet" algorithm. When it works, AllReduce time drops dramatically; when it doesn't (e.g., topology not supported, license missing), NCCL silently falls back to Ring/Tree.

Topology must match parallelism

SHARP and rail-optimized topologies assume specific GPU-to-NIC patterns. If the scheduler places ranks in a topology that doesn't match (e.g., TP group spread across NVLink domains), SHARP can't engage and IB traffic explodes. The efficiency engineer's job: ensure schedulers honor rank-to-host placement intents.

§19Topology, placement, and rail alignment

Performance is partly a question of which GPU talks to which GPU. Cluster topology + rank-to-GPU mapping = the effective bandwidth between any pair of ranks. Misalignment can cost 30-50% in distributed training.

The rail-optimized network

The dominant AI fabric design. Each node has 8 GPUs and 8 NICs. NIC i on every node connects to "rail i" of the leaf switch. So GPU 0 on every node has a direct (one-hop) IB path to GPU 0 on every other node. Same for GPU 1, GPU 2, etc.

Rail-optimized fabric for 8-GPU nodes: Node A Node B Node C +----+ +----+ +----+ +----+ +----+ +----+ +----+ +----+ |GPU0||GPU1||GPU2|...|GPU0||GPU1|...|GPU0||GPU1|... |NIC0||NIC1||NIC2|...|NIC0||NIC1|...|NIC0||NIC1|... +----+ +----+ +----+ +----+ +----+ +----+ +----+ | | | | | | | | | | | | | | leaf leaf leaf leaf leaf leaf leaf rail0 rail1 rail2 ...rail0 rail1...rail0 rail1 \____|____/ \____|____/ | | | | spine0 spine1 ... GPU0-to-GPU0 across nodes: 1 hop (leaf-rail0 -> spine -> leaf-rail0) GPU0-to-GPU1 across nodes: 2-3 hops (more switches, more contention)

The placement principle

For DP across nodes, the gradient allreduce sees N GPUs across N nodes, where each rank pairs with the same-numbered rank on other nodes. As long as rank i on every node is mapped to GPU i, all DP traffic stays within rail i. This is the "rail-aligned" allocation.

Misalignment looks like:

  • Rank 0 → Node A's GPU 3, Rank 1 → Node B's GPU 5, etc. Now DP traffic crosses rails, going up to spine.
  • Or: rank 0 on Node A's GPU 3 talks to rank 8 on Node B's GPU 1. The pair takes a 3-hop path instead of 1.

The result: spine congestion, longer paths, lower effective bandwidth.

The three storage performance dimensions

  • Aggregate read throughput — how much data can the FS deliver across all clients at once. Usually generous.
  • Per-client throughput — what one node can pull from the FS. Limited by NIC, FS architecture, and striping.
  • Metadata ops/sec — opens, stats, lookups. Often the surprising bottleneck.

The metadata trap

Many ML workloads have millions of small files: one image per file, one audio clip per file. Each open + read is two metadata operations. A dataloader doing 100K opens/sec across the cluster easily saturates the FS metadata server (MDS).

issue slow · dataloader sporadic stalls on metadata MDS
Symptom

Dataloader is fast on warm cache, slow on cold. Per-rank throughput acceptable but cluster-wide aggregate drops when more jobs are added (not scales).

Diagnosis

Lustre: lctl get_param mdt.*.md_stats shows MDT operation rates. If metadata ops/sec is near the published MDT limit, you're saturating.

Fixes
  • File format change — switch to sharded formats: WebDataset, FFRecord, Mosaic streaming, Parquet. Tens of large files instead of millions of small.
  • Per-node local caching — first epoch reads from FS, subsequent epochs from local NVMe.
  • Read-ahead and batch sizing — tune the FS client (e.g., Lustre OST count).

Checkpointing is the other half

Saving a 1 TB checkpoint to a parallel FS often takes longer than expected because the write path is serialized at multiple points: gather to one rank, write through one client, hit one OSS/MDT bottleneck. Modern best practice: distributed checkpointing.

import torch.distributed.checkpoint as dcp

# Each rank writes its shard in parallel to a directory of files
state = {"model": model.state_dict(), "optim": optimizer.state_dict()}
dcp.save(state, storage_writer=dcp.FileSystemWriter("/checkpoints/step-1000"))

# Restore: each rank reads its shard back in
dcp.load(state, storage_reader=dcp.FileSystemReader("/checkpoints/step-1000"))

Distributed checkpointing parallelizes the write across all ranks and many filesystem clients. A 1 TB save can drop from 20 minutes to 60 seconds.

Async checkpointing

Even with parallel writes, you don't want training to pause for checkpointing. Async checkpointing snapshots GPU state to CPU memory in a non-blocking way, then writes to the FS in the background:

from torch.distributed.checkpoint.state_dict_saver import async_save

# Returns a future; training continues immediately
future = async_save(state, checkpoint_id="step-1000", storage_writer=writer)

# Optionally wait for completion at job end or epoch boundary
future.result()

Storage telemetry to watch

  • Per-FS aggregate read/write bytes per second; alert at >70% of nominal capacity.
  • Metadata ops/sec; alert at >80% of MDS limit.
  • Per-job data read rate; outliers may indicate dataloader misconfiguration.
  • Checkpoint write time, per job; trend over time catches regressions.
  • FS client errors / retries from the per-node FS client logs.
The "training is slow" → "filesystem is full" surprise

Lustre and GPFS performance degrades sharply above ~85% capacity (more fragmentation, slower allocations). Senior engineers monitor capacity as a performance metric, not just a quota. Alerts at 70% and 85% lead to capacity expansion before users feel it.

§21Inference performance & serving

Inference has two regimes — offline (throughput-oriented batch jobs) and online (latency-oriented serving) — with conflicting optimization pressures.

Throughput and latency are antagonists. Big batches improve throughput (better GEMM efficiency, amortized launch overhead) but inflate latency (queueing, longer kernels). The job is finding the right tradeoff for the workload.

The compute-vs-memory dichotomy

Inference kernels fall on either side of the roofline:

  • Compute-bound (large batches, prefill) — Tensor Cores busy; lower-precision and fusion help.
  • Memory-bound (decode, batch=1) — HBM bandwidth is the ceiling; batching is the lever.

An LLM decoding one token reads the entire model from HBM for every token. With 70B params in BF16 = 140 GB, on H100 with 3.35 TB/s HBM, the theoretical maximum is ~24 tokens/sec for a single sequence. To go faster: batch more sequences (amortize the weight read), quantize (less data to read), or split the model across GPUs (parallel HBM bandwidth).

Batching strategies

Three patterns, increasingly sophisticated:

  • Static batching — collect N requests, run as a batch, return. Simple; bad latency under variable load.
  • Dynamic batching — wait up to k ms or until batch fills. Better latency-throughput trade.
  • Continuous (in-flight) batching — new requests join mid-decode; finished sequences exit. The LLM serving default; massively higher throughput.

The Triton vs custom-server question

Triton Inference Server is the default for production: model versioning, dynamic batching, gRPC/HTTP, metrics, multi-model. For LLM workloads, vLLM-server (or SGLang's server) is often better — purpose-built for continuous batching, KV-cache management, and the LLM-specific protocols.

§22LLM-specific inference patterns

LLMs broke many of the assumptions that older inference systems were built around. Worth its own chapter because the patterns are now dominant.

The two phases: prefill and decode

PhaseWhat happensBottleneck
PrefillProcess all input tokens in parallel; produce first output tokenCompute-bound (large GEMM)
DecodeGenerate output tokens one at a timeMemory-bound (load weights per token)

This split is why naive serving is awful for LLMs: a request with 1000 input tokens does one giant compute-bound step, then thousands of tiny memory-bound steps. Different work, mixed in queue, poorly utilized.

The KV cache

Each decode step would need to reprocess all previous tokens for attention. The KV cache stores the key/value tensors from previous positions so attention only computes against new tokens. The catch: KV cache for one 8K-context Llama-70B sequence is ~10 GB. With many concurrent sequences, KV cache dominates memory.

PagedAttention — the breakthrough

vLLM's contribution: treat KV cache like virtual memory. Allocate it in fixed-size blocks (pages), let each sequence's KV cache occupy non-contiguous blocks, and free blocks as sequences finish. Result: near-zero fragmentation, 2-4x more concurrent sequences in the same GPU memory, much higher throughput.

If you're serving LLMs and not using PagedAttention (vLLM, TensorRT-LLM, or equivalent), you're leaving 2-3x throughput on the table.

Continuous batching, concretely

Time t=0: batch = [seq_A (prompt 100), seq_B (prompt 50)] prefill seq_A (large), prefill seq_B (small) Time t=1: decode batch [seq_A pos=101, seq_B pos=51] ... seq_C arrives ... Time t=2: decode batch [seq_A pos=102, seq_B pos=52] schedule seq_C for prefill in interleaved step Time t=3: prefill batch includes seq_C; decode others Time t=4: seq_B finishes; KV-cache freed new request seq_D fills the slot Result: GPU stays busy across heterogeneous workloads.

Quantization for inference

The most common inference optimization. Move weights from BF16 to INT8/INT4/FP8/FP4 to shrink the per-token HBM read.

Speculative decoding

Decode-time amplifier: a small "draft" model proposes K tokens speculatively; the big model verifies them in one parallel pass. If accepted, you get K tokens for the cost of one big-model pass. Real-world acceptance rates 60-85%; throughput multipliers 1.5-3x on the right workload.

Tensor parallelism for inference

For models that don't fit on one GPU, or where one GPU's HBM bandwidth caps tokens/sec too low, split the model across GPUs. TP across 4-8 GPUs is standard; communication overhead is fine within a node (NVLink). Across nodes, the comm cost usually kills the gain — prefer pipeline parallelism or expert parallelism for that scale.

The serving sizing heuristic

For a 70B BF16 model targeting low-latency streaming, expect to need TP=4 to TP=8 on a single H100 node. For maximum throughput, set the largest batch the KV cache budget allows, use continuous batching, and quantize to FP8 or INT8. Above ~30 concurrent users per replica, throughput plateaus; beyond that, add replicas.

§23Computer vision, video, and robotics

The JD lists "Robotics, Autonomous vehicles, LLMs, Videos and more." Each workload class has its own performance signature. A senior engineer recognizes the shape of each.

Computer vision training

Image classification, segmentation, detection. The defining characteristic: small models by modern standards, but enormous data pipelines. Common patterns:

  • Dataloader is almost always the bottleneck. JPEG decode is CPU-heavy; even with DALI, the input pipeline often pacing the GPU.
  • Mixed precision is straightforward. BF16 works without surprises.
  • Convolutions favor specific Tensor Core kernel shapes — cuDNN heuristics usually right, but ncu can reveal misfires.
  • BatchNorm is a synchronization point. SyncBatchNorm across DP ranks costs an allreduce per BN layer; can be 10-15% of step time.

Video training

Video models multiply the input data by frame count. A 256-frame clip at 224×224 is 200x the data of one image. Decoding requires actual video decoders (NVDEC on the GPU, or DALI's video reader). Common patterns:

  • 3D convs / temporal attention have unusual access patterns that ncu often shows as memory-bound.
  • Activation memory explodes with frame count; recomputation is mandatory at long sequences.
  • NVDEC throughput is a hard ceiling; one GPU has limited decoders. Larger clusters need offline pre-decoding to NVDEC-friendly formats or to raw tensors.

Robotics & autonomous vehicle workloads

Sensor-fusion models, perception, planning, end-to-end driving policies. Distinguishing features:

  • Multi-modal inputs (camera, LiDAR, radar) — the input pipeline must align across sensors.
  • 3D operations (sparse convolution, voxel grids) — non-standard kernels, often custom CUDA.
  • Inference latency budgets are very tight (10-50 ms total).
  • Simulation-driven training: data is generated by simulators on CPUs/GPUs in parallel with training; coordination matters.

Diffusion / generative video

An emerging workload class with unique patterns. Long sequences (denoising steps + spatial-temporal tokens), heavy attention, sometimes needing 5-30 minutes per sample. The performance profile mixes LLM-style serving with video-style data layouts.

§25Scheduling, packing, and capacity

The performance engineer doesn't run the scheduler, but a job's performance depends on how it's scheduled. Influence over scheduling is one of the highest-leverage interventions.

The scheduler's view vs the performance view

A scheduler optimizes for: fair share, throughput of jobs, preemption fairness, resource utilization. A performance engineer cares about: rank-to-host placement matching parallelism intent, topology alignment, anti-affinity for noisy neighbors.

These goals frequently conflict. The scheduler wants to pack jobs tightly; the performance engineer wants each job's ranks to land on rail-aligned hosts. The remediation: scheduler hints, gang scheduling, topology-aware placement plugins.

Gang scheduling

Distributed training jobs must launch all-or-none: 1023 of 1024 ranks running and one missing is useless. Gang scheduling (in Slurm: standard; in k8s: via Volcano, Kueue, etc.) holds the allocation until all pods can start. Without it, partial allocations deadlock and waste capacity.

The packing-vs-fragmentation trade

Pack jobs tightly: more capacity, but a job needing a contiguous 256-GPU block can't get it. Spread jobs out: better fragmentation, but lower utilization. Defragmentation tools (preemption, migration, draining) live here.

The capacity model

Senior engineers think about capacity at three horizons:

  • Right now — how full is the cluster, what's queued, what's at risk?
  • This quarter — trend lines on utilization, projected demand, when do we add nodes?
  • This year — topology decisions, generational transitions (Hopper → Blackwell), storage scale.

The capacity conversation links efficiency directly to cost. "We need 30% more capacity" and "we have 30% efficiency headroom" are the same conversation; the choice is whether to spend money or engineering time.

§26MLPerf & benchmarking discipline

MLPerf is called out in the JD's stand-out section. It matters partly for absolute results, but mostly because the discipline of MLPerf-style benchmarking — rigorous, reproducible, comparable — is the discipline a performance engineer needs internally.

What MLPerf is, briefly

An industry consortium benchmark for ML training and inference. Defines reference implementations of standard models (ResNet, BERT, GPT-3-class, Llama, Stable Diffusion, recommender systems), strict accuracy targets, allowed optimization categories ("closed" with limited deviations, "open" with broad freedom), and standardized result formats. Vendors submit; results are published. Comparison is real.

What you actually learn from running MLPerf-like benchmarks internally

  • Reproducibility patterns: how to make a benchmark that gives the same number on every run.
  • Reference points: how does our cluster's actual achieved tokens/sec compare to peers?
  • Regression detection: when a software stack update changes performance, MLPerf-style benchmarks catch it.
  • Capability proofs: "we can train Llama-3 with X tokens/sec on Y GPUs" is a meaningful claim only with discipline behind it.

The internal benchmarking practice

A senior engineer maintains a small set of canonical "performance gates":

  1. Single-GPU GEMM perf — cuBLAS run on standard shapes; catches driver / library regressions.
  2. nccl-tests over the fabric — AllReduce bandwidth on N nodes; catches network regressions.
  3. End-to-end model perf — a small fixed model trained for a few hundred steps; catches stack regressions.
  4. Inference TTFT and ITL — a fixed model + fixed inputs; catches serving regressions.

These run on every cluster change: driver update, NCCL update, PyTorch update, kernel update, switch firmware upgrade. The output: a tracked time series showing whether anything got slower today.

The benchmarking pitfalls

Things that make benchmarks lie
  • Warmup not done. First steps are always slower (allocator warm-up, JIT compilation); skip them.
  • Different ranks differently warmed. Some ranks hit cold storage; results don't match.
  • Including initialization in step time. Cold caches, async work pending.
  • Thermal effects. Long runs throttle; short runs don't.
  • Non-deterministic kernels. Some attention kernels have non-deterministic reductions; output varies.
  • Background load. Co-tenants on storage or network affect "isolated" benchmarks.

What "good" looks like

A benchmark report should answer: what was the configuration, what was the measured performance, what's the variance across runs, what's the confidence interval, how does it compare to a baseline, and what changed since the last measurement? Without that structure, you're guessing.

Part VIII

Production cases

Seven worked debugging scenarios drawn from the kinds of problems this role encounters. Each follows the same structure: symptom, initial hypothesis, what the data actually showed, the resolution, and the takeaway. Reading these is how you internalize the diagnostic patterns.

§27Production debugging cases

case 1 "training is slow on the new H100 cluster"
Symptom

Researcher migrated a 70B Llama-class training job from A100 to H100 cluster. Expected ~3x speedup (BF16 peak ratio). Observed ~1.4x.

Initial hypothesis

Kernels not engaging H100 Tensor Cores; expected fix to be precision config.

What the data showed

nsys: forward and backward kernels were ~3x faster than A100 (expected). Allreduce time was unchanged. Communication had been 15% of step time on A100; on H100 it was 35%, because compute got fast and comm didn't shrink.

Resolution

The cluster used Ethernet RoCE, not InfiniBand. Switching to RDMA-optimized configuration (NCCL_IB_HCA tuning, SHARP enable, channel count tuning) recovered most of the gap. The remaining gap was inherent: comm-bound on a too-thin network for this model size. Long-term answer: fabric upgrade.

Takeaway

"3x compute speedup" doesn't mean "3x training speedup." Always profile the whole step on the new hardware. Comm grows in relative cost as compute gets faster.

case 2 "MFU was 45% yesterday and 28% today, no code changes"
Symptom

Daily training run. Same job script, same model, same data. Step time jumped from 800 ms to 1300 ms overnight. No commits in the team's repo. Researcher confused.

Initial hypothesis

Hardware regression or cluster congestion.

What the data showed

nsys traces from both days side by side. NCCL kernels were 80% longer today. nccl-tests outside the job confirmed: AllReduce bandwidth on the same nodes was 40% slower than baseline.

IB error counters on two nodes showed elevated PortRcvErrors overnight. One IB cable had degraded but not failed.

Resolution

Drained the two affected nodes, ops team replaced the cable, returned to service. Performance restored.

Takeaway

Networks degrade silently. Without baseline measurements and continuous monitoring of fabric counters, you'd be debugging the model. The fix took 20 minutes once the cause was clear; finding the cause took the right telemetry.

case 3 "job hangs after exactly 30 minutes, every time"
Symptom

Distributed training (256 GPUs) hangs after ~30 minutes consistently. No error message. PyTorch eventually times out.

Initial hypothesis

NCCL deadlock or rank-specific bug.

What the data showed

py-spy dump on all ranks: 255 of 256 in NCCL allreduce, 1 in user code reading a checkpoint metadata file from a network-mounted FS.

The job had a "checkpoint health check" running every 30 min, only on rank 0, that called stat() on a heavily-loaded NFS mount. The NFS server was failing; stat() would block for ~30 seconds while other ranks proceeded to the next allreduce. Eventually the rank fell so far behind that all collectives mismatched.

Resolution

Moved the health check off the NFS. The deeper fix: never put rank-conditional blocking I/O in the training loop without timeouts.

Takeaway

"Hang at exactly 30 minutes" was the diagnostic clue — periodic, not random. Periodic hangs usually point to a periodic task. Look for cron-like patterns.

case 4 "OOM on step 4823, but only on Tuesdays"
Symptom

Training run OOMs at varying step counts (thousands), only on some days. No reliable repro. Code reviewers found nothing.

Initial hypothesis

Memory leak.

What the data showed

Memory snapshot at OOM: the model and optimizer were fine; activation memory had spiked. The spike correlated with a particular set of training samples — very long documents at the maximum context length. The dataloader had no length capping; rare long sequences spent more memory than the budget assumed.

"Tuesdays" was the day a particular subset of data was scheduled for ingestion.

Resolution

Cap sequence length, bucket by length to make activation memory predictable, set expandable_segments:True for headroom.

Takeaway

Data-dependent OOM is the hardest class. Always ask: do the worst-case inputs fit in memory? If not, you're not OOM-free, you're lucky-so-far.

case 5 "FSDP training is slower than DDP" (the prefetch trap)
Symptom

Researcher migrated 7B model from DDP to FSDP HYBRID_SHARD expecting memory savings without slowdown. Got memory savings and a 25% slowdown.

Initial hypothesis

FSDP's extra all-gathers were inherently slow on this fabric.

What the data showed

nsys trace: FSDP all-gathers were running serially with compute, not overlapped. Forward stalled before every transformer block.

The user had configured FSDP without setting BackwardPrefetch.BACKWARD_PRE. Default behavior at the time didn't prefetch aggressively. Forward had to wait synchronously for each block's weight gather.

Resolution

Set the prefetch flag. 25% slowdown became a 3% slowdown (acceptable for the memory win).

Takeaway

Distributed wrappers (FSDP, DDP, ZeRO) have many knobs and powerful defaults that are silently wrong for some workloads. Always check overlap in the trace; never trust framework documentation that says "overlap happens automatically."

case 6 "inference p99 spiked 10x at 3am"
Symptom

vLLM-based LLM serving cluster. p99 TTFT spiked from 200 ms to 2.5 s for ~20 minutes overnight. No deploy, no traffic spike.

Initial hypothesis

Long-tail request (huge prompt) caused queue head-of-line blocking.

What the data showed

Request logs: TTFT spiked across the board, not just for one request. Server-side metrics: KV cache utilization near 100% for the spike window. Continuous batching was preempting in-flight requests because new requests demanded KV blocks the system had to evict.

Root cause: a research team had launched a "test" batch of 10K requests with 32K-token prompts at 3am, expecting "off-peak." Each prompt's KV cache filled the available memory.

Resolution

Short-term: rate-limit the offending source. Long-term: implement priority tiers + max-KV-per-request quotas + a separate replica pool for batch-style requests with their own SLA.

Takeaway

Continuous batching has a hidden failure mode: KV cache contention. Memory is shared across requests; a few large prompts starve smaller ones. Quota the cache by request, not just rate-limit by RPS.

case 7 "the cluster's MFU dropped 8% over six months — with no obvious culprit"
Symptom

Cluster-wide MFU trend, smoothed over jobs, declined steadily from ~42% to ~34% over six months. Individual jobs looked fine to their owners. Capacity team asking why.

Initial hypothesis

Cluster degradation: drives, fabric, thermals.

What the data showed

Per-job-class breakdown showed the drop concentrated in one job class: a fine-tuning pipeline that proliferated over six months from 5 to 80 jobs per day. The pipeline had a default of num_workers=1 (a copy from someone's notebook). For small models that fit easily, GPU wasn't memory-bound and steps were short, so the dataloader was the bottleneck on every job.

Each individual researcher considered their job's perf acceptable. Aggregated, they shifted the cluster average.

Resolution

Updated the pipeline template default to num_workers=8 + pin_memory=True. Pushed the change via PR; jobs picking up the new template recovered their MFU. Within two weeks the fleet MFU recovered to ~41%.

Takeaway

The largest efficiency wins often come from changing defaults, not individual debugging. A pipeline template used by 80 teams is more valuable than 80 individual investigations.

Every case here is a real pattern. The senior engineer's skill is recognizing them from a few signals, not solving each from scratch.

§29The senior-engineer signals

What distinguishes a senior performance engineer from a strong one. Not the technical knowledge alone (necessary but not sufficient), but the behaviors that compound into leverage.

You can be wrong, productively

Your first hypothesis is wrong often. The senior signal is comfort with that: form a hypothesis, gather data fast, update or discard, repeat. The engineer who clings to their first theory loses days; the one who treats it as cheap finds the answer in hours.

You go to the right tool first

nsys for "where is the time," ncu for "why is this kernel slow," PyTorch Profiler for "what op is this," DCGM for "what is the hardware doing." Knowing which tool answers which question is most of the job.

You write things down

Every investigation produces a one-page writeup: symptom, hypotheses considered, evidence gathered, resolution, takeaway. These compound. After a year, you have a debugging library; after three, you've internalized patterns no one else has seen.

You build the path you wish existed

If three researchers in a quarter hit the same issue, the fourth investigation is a tool, not another investigation. Senior engineers spot the pattern and write the dashboard, the linter, the better default. The leverage is in not having to debug it again.

You speak the researcher's language

"Your gradient bucket boundaries don't align with your microbatch schedule" is true; "set bucket_cap_mb=200 and you'll see ~15% faster steps" is useful. The senior version of any explanation ends with the concrete action.

You quantify before recommending

"Switching to FSDP HYBRID_SHARD" is a proposal; "switching to FSDP HYBRID_SHARD will recover ~8% throughput based on similar workloads, with these risks" is a decision. Trained intuition gives you the number before the experiment.

You hold the ceiling in mind

Every workload has a theoretical ceiling. Knowing it — from hardware peak, from algorithmic FLOPs, from network bandwidth — converts "is this slow?" into "how close to optimal is this?" The conversation changes.

You know when to stop

Some optimizations don't matter. A 5% win on a job that runs for two hours, once, is not worth two weeks of engineering. Saying no to low-leverage work, gracefully, is the most underrated senior skill.

You make others faster

The job's deliverable, in the end, isn't lines of code. It's measured throughput improvements, attributable to your work, that compound across researchers and quarters. The senior engineer is the one whose involvement makes a team's research move faster, and whose absence is silently expensive across the cluster.

A great efficiency engineer leaves the cluster more productive than they found it — not just for the jobs they touched, but for the jobs that will run after them.
