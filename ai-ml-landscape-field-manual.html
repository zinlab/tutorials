BOTH Chapter I

The map · where the field is in 2026.

The frame Five shifts in the last 24 months changed what's mainstream. Knowing them tells you where to spend learning time.

Read everything else through this lens The chapters below trace each shift in depth.

The AI/ML stack of early 2026 looks recognizably like the stack of 2023 — transformers, GPUs, PyTorch — but five quiet shifts have changed what "standard practice" means underneath. Knowing these tells you what to focus on.

Five shifts that redefined 2024–2026

1. Post-training displaced pre-training as the main lever. The biggest capability jumps now come from reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards (RLVR), supervised fine-tuning on synthetic reasoning traces, and preference optimization — not from more pre-training tokens. DeepSeek-R1 demonstrated pure RL could produce emergent reasoning. Every frontier model now uses a multi-stage post-training stack.

2. Test-time compute became a first-class budget. "Think longer, get smarter" is now a deployable feature. Reasoning models spend tokens to plan, verify, and self-correct before answering. This adds a third dimension to model scaling — alongside parameters and pre-training data.

3. Hybrid architectures. Pure transformers are no longer the only viable design. Mamba (SSM), MoE, and hybrids like Jamba, Hunyuan-TurboS, and IBM's Bamba ship in production. The bet: linear-time mixing for long context, attention only where needed.

4. Inference matured into a real engineering discipline. PagedAttention, RadixAttention, continuous batching, chunked prefill, speculative decoding, and prefill-decode disaggregation went from research papers to standard features in vLLM, SGLang, and TensorRT-LLM. Inference is now where most of the engineering happens.

5. The hardware mid-cycle: Hopper → Blackwell. NVFP4 with hardware-native FP4 tensor cores, 8 TB/s HBM, NVLink-C2C between CPU and GPU. Each new generation roughly doubles practical throughput per dollar; the inference cost curve falls about 4× per year through hardware + software together.

Two layers, two cadences

Roughly: algorithmic innovation moves in months (a new RL trick, a new attention variant, a new fine-tuning recipe), while infrastructure innovation moves in years (a new chip, a new fabric, a new framework). The two layers loop into each other — Blackwell's FP4 enables FP8/FP4 quantized training that wasn't practical before; the rise of long-context reasoning forces inference engines to optimize KV cache management harder. The practitioner's task is keeping both layers in view simultaneously.

A meta-observation

2026 is the first year where you can credibly claim "everything important about training and inference performance has settled into a known stack." That stack has many choices, but the choices are well-defined. The frontier has moved up the abstraction ladder — from "how do we make matmul fast?" to "how do we structure post-training, reasoning, and agents?" The hard problems are now about data and process, not kernels.

Architectures · transformer & beyond.

The dense transformer is the workhorse and remains so. But four siblings now compete for specific niches: Mixture of Experts (MoE), State Space Models (Mamba), hybrid architectures, and diffusion-based generative models.

The standard transformer in 2026

What ships in production-grade language models today is a refined transformer with a specific set of components, in this order: rotary positional embeddings (RoPE), grouped-query attention or multi-head latent attention, RMSNorm instead of LayerNorm, SwiGLU instead of ReLU in the MLP, and FlashAttention-3 for the attention computation itself. None of these were in the 2017 paper. Each is a tightly-scoped fix for a specific issue — RoPE for length extrapolation, GQA/MLA for KV cache size, RMSNorm for training stability and speed, SwiGLU for capacity-per-parameter.

Mixture of Experts (MoE)

MoE replaces each MLP block with a router that picks 2–8 "experts" out of many (often 8–256). Only the picked experts run for any given token. Result: a "1T-parameter" model where only ~37B parameters fire per token (e.g., DeepSeek-V3). You get the capacity of a giant model with the FLOPs of a much smaller one.

Trade-offs: training stability is harder (router collapse), memory footprint is full (all experts live in HBM), and expert parallelism adds an all-to-all collective per layer. The first two are well-understood and solvable; the third drove the hardware industry to NVLink-72 and made expert parallelism (EP) a first-class infra primitive.

State Space Models (Mamba)

Mamba replaces attention with a selective state-space model. The computational story: attention is O(n²) in sequence length; SSMs are O(n). For million-token contexts, the gap is unbridgeable. The quality story: pure Mamba models hold their own on short tasks but lose on long-range associative recall (the "needle in a haystack" tasks attention naturally aces).

This is why the production winner is the hybrid — interleave a small number of attention layers (10–20% of total) into an otherwise-Mamba stack. You get linear scaling on most layers and exact recall where it matters. Jamba (AI21), Bamba (IBM), Hunyuan-TurboS (Tencent), and Codestral-Mamba (Mistral) all use this pattern.

Diffusion Transformer (DiT)

Replace the U-Net of original diffusion models with a transformer over noisy patches. At the technical heart of these 2026 models is the Diffusion Transformer (DiT) architecture. Unlike earlier U-Net based models, DiTs scale more effectively with increased data and compute. This is now the architecture behind every state-of-the-art image and video generation model — Sora, Veo 3, Kling, Wan, Stable Diffusion 3+.

The empirical reality

For 99% of practical LLM applications in 2026, you're working with a dense or MoE transformer with the components above. SSM hybrids are emerging in production but still rare. Diffusion is the entire game for image/video. The bestiary matters because the next 3–5 years will see more architectural diversity, not less — pure attention is too expensive at million-token contexts to stay the only choice.

ALGORITHM Chapter III

Attention · from MHA to MLA.

Why this matters Attention dominates both compute (in training) and memory (in inference). Every variant is a different point on the quality/memory/speed Pareto frontier.

The KV cache is everything Every attention variant is judged primarily by how it shrinks the KV cache.

Attention has evolved more than any other component since 2017. Each variant is a specific KV-cache-reduction trick, each with its own quality penalty.

2017 · baseline
MHA
Vaswani et al.
Multi-Head Attention. Each of N heads has its own Q, K, V projection. KV cache: 2 × n_layers × n_heads × head_dim × seq_len. Largest cache; highest quality at small scale.
100% KV
2019
MQA
Shazeer · Multi-Query
Multi-Query Attention. All query heads share one K and V. Massive cache reduction (~Nx smaller). Quality regression for some tasks.
~1/N KV
2023 · the sweet spot
GQA
Ainslie et al. · Grouped-Query
Group query heads in groups of G, each group shares one KV head. Standard in Llama-2/3/4, Mistral, Gemma. Cache size ÷ G with negligible quality loss.
~1/G KV
2024 · DeepSeek
MLA
DeepSeek-V2 · Multi-head Latent
Compress KV into a low-rank latent representation before caching. Expand back during attention. Cache as small as MQA, quality near MHA. Used in DeepSeek-V2/V3/R1.
~1/N KV, MHA quality
2022 · kernel-level
FlashAttention
Dao et al.
Fused, tiled kernel that keeps softmax(QK^T)V in fast SRAM. No quality change; 2–4× faster + lower memory. FA-3 adds FP8 path on H100, FA-4 on Blackwell.
kernel optimization
2024 · long context
Sparse Attention
Sliding window, Longformer, Native Sparse
Each token attends to only a subset of others — sliding window, top-k by score, dilated patterns. Linear or sub-quadratic. Quality cliffs on tasks requiring long-range recall.
sub-quadratic

How to choose

For new training: GQA is the default. It's a free lunch — minor quality cost, large memory savings, and every inference framework supports it natively.

For inference-heavy workloads where you can train from scratch: MLA is increasingly attractive. DeepSeek-V3 demonstrated near-MHA quality at MQA-sized cache, enabling massive batch sizes during serving. The catch is training infrastructure must support it (vLLM, SGLang both do; older frameworks may not).

For million-token contexts: combine GQA/MLA with sparse attention. Pure dense attention on 1M tokens needs ~10TB of KV cache for a frontier model — physically impossible. Sliding window for local context plus a few full-attention layers for global retrieval is the pragmatic path.

The kernel layer matters as much as the algorithm

FlashAttention isn't a different attention; it's the same math implemented to actually use the GPU. FA-3 on H100 runs attention at ~75% of FP16 peak. FA-4 on Blackwell extends this to FP8 and FP4. Without flash kernels, attention is memory-bound; with them, it becomes compute-bound. Every inference framework now defaults to a flash variant; the question is just which variant.

ALGORITHM Chapter IV

Pre-training · recipes & data.

The state Pre-training is now a relatively settled engineering problem at the recipe level. Innovation has moved to data curation and post-training.

The numbers Frontier pre-training runs use 10–20T tokens. Cost: $50–$500M+ for the largest. Multi-month duration on 10–32k GPUs.

Pre-training has converged on a recipe that's now well-understood: a decoder-only transformer with the components from Chapter II, trained with AdamW + cosine schedule on 10–20 trillion tokens of carefully-curated web + code + math + multilingual data. The art is in the data and the scale; the architecture is mostly settled.

The recipe, end-to-end

Data. 60–75% web text (filtered Common Crawl variants like FineWeb-Edu, DCLM, Nemotron-CC), 10–20% code (The Stack v2, GitHub), 5–10% math (proof-pile, OpenMath), 5–15% multilingual + domain-specific (papers, books, dialogues). Quality filtering matters more than raw scale — a well-filtered 5T tokens beats unfiltered 20T.

Tokenization. Byte-pair encoding (BPE) with 100k–256k vocabulary. Larger vocabularies trade embedding cost for sequence efficiency; Llama 3 went to 128k for this reason.

Optimizer. AdamW remains dominant. β1=0.9, β2=0.95 (lowered from default 0.999 for stability at scale). Cosine learning rate with warmup. Recent contenders: Sophia (second-order info), Lion (sign-based, fewer optimizer states). Production rarely moves off AdamW for fear of unknown failure modes.

Precision. BF16 weights with FP32 master copies in the optimizer state. FP8 training now production-ready on H100/H200 and Blackwell, delivering 2x throughput over FP16 with equivalent accuracy. NVIDIA's TransformerEngine and PyTorch's native FP8 paths make this practical.

Schedule. Roughly: 5–10% warmup, then cosine decay over the run. Recent work shows warmup-stable-decay (WSD) schedules give better continuation properties than pure cosine.

The compute math

# Chinchilla optimal: ~20 tokens per parameter
# Modern over-training: 100-300 tokens per parameter (cheaper inference)
# Llama-3 70B: 70B params × 15T tokens ≈ 215 tokens/param
# 
# Cost ≈ 6 × N × D / (HW_throughput × MFU × cluster_size × $/GPU-hour)
# Concrete: 70B params, 15T tokens, H100, 50% MFU, 1024 GPUs at $3/hr:
#   6 × 70e9 × 15e12 = 6.3e21 FLOPs
#   /  989e12 FLOPs/s × 0.5 × 1024 × 3600 = 12.3M GPU-hours
#   ≈ $37M and ~17 days wall-clock

What changed: data is now the primary lever

For most of 2020–2023, getting more parameters and more tokens was the lever. From 2024 onward, the bottleneck moved to data quality. Synthetic data — generated by larger models or by self-distillation — is now a meaningful fraction of frontier training data. Tools like Nemotron-CC (NVIDIA), FineWeb-Edu (HuggingFace), and DCLM (Apple/MIT) curate web text with classifier scores; rejected data is dropped.

The empirical lesson, learned the hard way: filtering 20T tokens down to 5T high-quality tokens produces a better model than training on all 20T. Web data has an enormous noise tail; signal-to-noise ratio matters more than raw count past a threshold.

The state of pre-training research

In 2026, frontier pre-training is mostly an engineering problem with a known shape. The hard research problems have moved elsewhere: how to construct synthetic reasoning data, how to design RL environments, how to make post-training stable, how to align long-context behaviors. The pre-training recipe is the foundation — necessary, mostly settled, and increasingly upstream of where the field's attention lives.

ALGORITHM Chapter V

Post-training · from RLHF to RLVR.

The biggest shift In 24 months, the field moved from "PPO with human preference labels" to "GRPO with programmatic verification." The new stack is cheaper, more scalable, and produces stronger reasoning.

The structure Post-training is now three stages: SFT, preference optimization, RL with verifiable rewards.

Twelve months ago the standard recipe was "pretrain, then RLHF with human preferences." That recipe is dead. Every major model in 2025–2026 uses some variant of a three-stage post-training stack — SFT, preference optimization, RL with verifiable rewards — and the gains over the old recipe are large.

The three stages

Stage 1 — SFT (Supervised Fine-Tuning). Teaches the model the format: how to follow instructions, produce structured outputs, respond conversationally. Trained on (instruction, response) pairs. Data is curated, often synthetic from a strong teacher model. Output: a model that can respond appropriately but isn't yet aligned to preferences.

Stage 2 — Preference optimization. Aligns the model to a preference signal (helpfulness, harmlessness, style). Three current methods:

offline · reference
DPO
Direct Preference Optimization
Train directly on (chosen, rejected) pairs without a separate reward model. Closed-form objective, no rollouts. Simpler than PPO/RLHF; produces good base alignment.
2023
offline · simpler
SimPO / KTO
no reference model
DPO variants that drop the reference model (SimPO) or use binary labels rather than pairs (KTO). Cheaper to run; similar quality on many tasks.
2024
online · RL
PPO/RLHF
Schulman et al. (original)
Train a reward model on preferences, then PPO with KL constraint to optimize. Most expensive; still used at frontier labs for fine-grained behavior tuning. Operational pain is high.
2017/2022

Stage 3 — RL with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR). The 2025 breakthrough. Instead of human preference labels, the reward is automated: did the math answer match the ground truth, did the code pass the tests, did the proof check. DeepSeek-R1 demonstrated that pure RLVR can produce emergent reasoning capabilities. The dominant algorithm: GRPO.

GRPO, explained

Group Relative Policy Optimization. For each prompt, generate G completions (typically 8–32). Score each with the verifier. Compute the advantage of each completion as its reward minus the group mean. Update the policy to upweight above-average completions, downweight below-average. DPO learns from static preference pairs without any RL. GRPO is an online RL method that generates new responses during training and computes advantages by comparing them within groups.

Why this works where PPO struggles: PPO needs a learned reward model (noisy, expensive, drifts) and a learned value function (the "critic," also noisy). GRPO replaces the critic with the within-group mean — a free, unbiased baseline. The reward model is replaced by a programmatic verifier — a free, perfect signal where it applies (math, code, logical proofs).

# Per prompt:
# 1. Generate G completions from current policy
completions = [policy.generate(prompt) for _ in range(G)]

# 2. Score each with verifier (e.g., does math answer match?)
rewards = [verifier(prompt, c) for c in completions]

# 3. Group-relative advantage
mean_r = mean(rewards)
std_r = std(rewards) + 1e-8
advantages = [(r - mean_r) / std_r for r in rewards]

# 4. PPO-style clipped objective, no critic needed
for c, a in zip(completions, advantages):
    ratio = pi_new(c) / pi_old(c)
    loss = -min(ratio * a, clip(ratio, 1-ε, 1+ε) * a)
    loss += β * KL(pi_new || pi_ref)             # anchor to ref

Variants you'll hear about

DAPO — adds dynamic clip ranges and length normalization. Used in Qwen 2.5-Math; better numerical stability than vanilla GRPO.

Dr-GRPO — "double-relative." Normalizes both within group and across groups, helping when reward distributions vary by difficulty.

RLOO — Reinforce with Leave-One-Out baseline; algorithmically simpler than GRPO with similar gains.

VeRL / Open-RLHF / TRL — frameworks. A flexible, efficient, and production-ready RL training library for large language models that serves as the open-source implementation of the HybridFlow framework and supports various RL algorithms (PPO, GRPO), advanced resource utilization, and scalability up to 70B models on hundreds of GPUs.

When to use what

SFT only: domain adaptation where you just need format/style. SFT + DPO: general assistant where preferences are subjective. SFT + DPO + GRPO: reasoning models, math, code — anywhere a verifier exists. The combination is the modern frontier-model recipe; the order is fixed (you can't skip SFT and start with GRPO from a base model).

ALGORITHM Chapter VI

Reasoning & test-time compute.

The new dimension Models can now trade more inference compute for higher accuracy. This adds a knob to your latency/cost/quality tradeoff at deployment time.

The pattern Chain of thought → self-consistency → tree search → RL-shaped CoT.

Test-time compute scaling is the third axis alongside parameters and data. A reasoning model spends extra tokens — thousands of "thinking" tokens before its answer — and gets dramatically higher accuracy on hard problems. This is now a first-class deployment feature.

The progression

Chain of Thought (CoT) prompting. Ask the model to "think step by step." Discovered in 2022, mostly a prompting trick on top of standard models. Gains were real but modest.

Self-consistency. Sample N CoT traces, take the majority answer. More compute → more accuracy. Still just prompting.

Process Reward Models (PRMs). Train a reward model on steps of reasoning, not just final answers. Use it to guide search through the reasoning tree. Powerful but operationally complex.

RL-trained reasoning. The 2025 shift. Train the model to produce long CoT via GRPO with verifiable rewards. Output: a model that naturally produces 1k–10k thinking tokens before answering hard problems. OpenAI's o1/o3, DeepSeek-R1, Anthropic's Claude with extended thinking, Gemini 2.5's "thinking" mode. The model decides how much to think; you can budget it at inference.

What you actually deploy

A reasoning model at serving time looks like a normal model with two new features:

Internal reasoning tokens. The model emits thousands of tokens "in scratchpad" before producing a user-visible answer. Some serving stacks expose these; some hide them. Cost: real tokens, real GPU time. Speed: noticeably slower, but answers are dramatically better on math, code, and analysis.

Reasoning budget. You can cap thinking tokens. Lower = faster + cheaper + dumber. Higher = slower + costlier + smarter. Each model has a sweet spot per task class. For consumer chat, low budgets dominate; for analysis pipelines, high budgets are worth the cost.

Search-based methods

Beyond single-trajectory CoT, the field has explored tree-search-during-inference:

Beam search over reasoning steps with a process reward model scoring each.

Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) — DeepMind's AlphaGo-style search applied to reasoning. AlphaProof and AlphaGeometry use this for theorem proving; current frontier consumer models do not, because the latency is prohibitive.

Self-critique loops — generate, critique own answer, revise. Multiple frameworks (Reflexion, Self-Refine); useful but often beaten by simply giving the base model more thinking tokens.

The implication for infra

Reasoning models break inference cost models. A "1k input, 100 output" assumption becomes "1k input, 10k thinking, 200 output." KV cache blows up 10×. Decode time blows up 10×. Inference engines now optimize specifically for reasoning workloads — long decodes, deep KV cache, often single-request rather than high-batch (because the user is waiting). vLLM, SGLang, and TensorRT-LLM all have reasoning-specific paths now.

BOTH Chapter VII

Agents · the other decoder.

The framing An agent is a model + a tool harness + a loop. The model's job is to decide what to do next; the harness's job is to execute it.

Infra implication Agents change inference workloads completely — bursty, long-tail latencies, KV cache reuse across tool calls.

An agent is a language model wrapped in a loop where each turn includes "decide which tool to call," "call it," "incorporate the result." The model's output is no longer just text — it's text plus structured tool calls. The infrastructure implications are different from chat.

The agentic stack

Tool calling format. Models emit structured calls (typically JSON) for tool invocations. OpenAI's function calling, Anthropic's tool use, MCP (Model Context Protocol) — competing protocols, all with the same structure: function name + typed arguments.

Constrained decoding. The serving framework forces the model's output to conform to a JSON schema for tool calls. vLLM and SGLang both support this via guided decoding. Without it, the model occasionally produces invalid JSON and the whole agent loop breaks.

Tool execution. Out-of-process — Python interpreter, web search, RAG retrieval, code execution, browser automation. Each tool has latency on the order of seconds and is the dominant slowdown.

State management. Conversation history grows with each turn. KV cache reuse across turns is the highest-leverage optimization — every turn's prefix is the previous turn's prefix plus a small delta.

Frameworks

LangChain / LangGraph — most popular; graph-based agent orchestration, large ecosystem, sometimes accused of overengineering.

LlamaIndex — originated as a RAG framework; now full agent support. Stronger on retrieval-heavy patterns.

CrewAI / AutoGen — multi-agent frameworks. Multiple specialized agents (planner, executor, reviewer) collaborating.

DSPy — different philosophy. Treats prompts as programs to be optimized by the framework, not handcrafted. Compelling for evaluation-driven pipelines.

Pydantic AI — emerging; type-safe, integrates with Pydantic models for I/O validation.

MCP (Model Context Protocol) — Anthropic-led standard, now widely adopted. Defines how tools, prompts, and resources are exposed to models. The "USB-C for AI tools" — a single tool can be used by any compatible model.

Why agents stress inference infra

A traditional chat workload has predictable shape: one prompt in, one response out, batch many requests for throughput. Agents break this in several ways:

1. Bursty traffic. An agent loop fires many small LLM calls in rapid succession. The serving stack sees high QPS for a few seconds, then idle while tools execute.

2. Long-tail latency. An agent may make 1 or 50 LLM calls per user request. P50 might be fast; P99 includes everything.

3. Massive prefix reuse. Every turn's input is mostly previous turns' output. Prefix caching becomes critical. SGLang's RadixAttention gives it a 29% throughput edge over vLLM on H100 GPUs and up to 6.4x gains on prefix-heavy workloads like RAG and multi-turn chat — agents are the canonical prefix-heavy workload.

4. Structured output overhead. Constrained decoding for tool calls adds 10–30% latency compared to free-form generation. Worth it; do it once correctly rather than handle malformed JSON downstream.

Where agents are headed

Three live frontiers: computer-use agents (Claude Computer Use, OpenAI Operator) that drive a real browser/OS; code agents (Cursor, GitHub Copilot Workspace, Devin, SWE-agent) that operate on whole codebases with iterative refinement; multi-agent systems where multiple specialized agents collaborate on plans. All three stress the same primitives: long-context, tool calling, prefix caching, and reliable structured output.

ALGORITHM Chapter VIII

Multimodal · diffusion & DiT.

Two threads Understanding (vision-language models) and generation (diffusion). The former is mostly transformer; the latter is mostly diffusion.

The new winner Unified models that do both — Chameleon, Show-o, BAGEL — are emerging but most production systems still specialize.

Multimodal in 2026 is two distinct threads. Understanding (a model that takes images, video, audio and outputs text) uses transformer architectures with vision/audio encoders. Generation (text-to-image, text-to-video, text-to-audio) uses diffusion, increasingly with transformer backbones (DiT). The two are converging but not yet unified.

Vision-Language Models (VLMs)

The standard architecture, dominant since 2023: encode images with a vision tower (typically a SigLIP/CLIP/ViT), project the visual tokens into the LLM's embedding space, prepend or interleave them with text tokens, and let a regular LLM decode. GPT-4o, Gemini 2.5, Claude 3.5+, Llama 4, Qwen2.5-VL — all variants of this pattern.

What's improved in 2025–26:

Higher-resolution image handling. Tiling strategies for large images. Video understanding. Sampling frames as image tokens; temporal positional encoding; some models add a temporal cross-attention. Native multimodal training — train on image+text from the start, not just a vision adapter bolted onto a text LLM. Audio. Encode speech via a Whisper-like encoder, decode either text or speech tokens.

Diffusion models — the generative side

Diffusion models generate by starting with pure noise and iteratively denoising. A neural network (the denoiser) predicts how to remove noise at each step. Train by adding noise to real images and learning to undo it. The classic architecture was U-Net; the modern winner is the Diffusion Transformer (DiT).

DiT: replace the U-Net with a transformer that operates on noisy latent patches. Scales much better than U-Net with compute. Behind Stable Diffusion 3, FLUX, Sora, Veo, Kling, Wan.

Why this matters for infra

Diffusion serving is unlike LLM serving in three ways:

1. Iterative. Each image/video is N steps (typically 4–50) of denoising. The compute pattern is "same forward pass N times."

2. Fixed-shape per request. A single image has predictable compute. Easier to batch, simpler scheduling.

3. Memory grows with resolution. A 1024×1024 generation needs ~1GB of activation memory; a 5-second 1080p video needs much more.

Specialized serving stacks (TensorRT-LLM, NVIDIA Diffusers Library, ComfyUI for prototyping) handle the diffusion workload distinctly from LLM stacks. With SGLang, serving latency improves by ~2.3× on a single H200 for 20 denoising steps — even the LLM serving frameworks are starting to specialize for diffusion.

INFRA Chapter IX

Training infra · frameworks & parallelism.

The taxonomy Four dimensions of parallelism: data, tensor, pipeline, expert. Frontier training mixes all four.

The framework war Mostly settled. PyTorch + FSDP/DCP for most teams; MegaScale / Megatron-LM for hyperscale.

Training infrastructure is now stratified by scale. Solo researchers use plain PyTorch + a single GPU. Small teams use PyTorch FSDP on 8–64 GPUs. Industrial labs use 3D or 4D parallelism (DeepSpeed, Megatron, NeMo) on thousands of GPUs. Hyperscalers run custom stacks (Anthropic, OpenAI, Google have in-house tools).

The four parallelism dimensions

Data parallelism (DP). Each rank holds the full model and processes different data batches. Synchronize gradients via all-reduce. The simplest scaling axis; works perfectly until model doesn't fit on one GPU. PyTorch DDP.

Sharded data parallelism (FSDP / ZeRO). Each rank holds a shard of the parameters, gradients, and optimizer state. All-gather to assemble the full layer before forward; all-gather for backward; reduce-scatter to shard gradients. Memory scales 1/N with rank count. The default modern technique for fitting larger models without explicit tensor parallelism. PyTorch FSDP, DeepSpeed ZeRO-3.

Tensor parallelism (TP). Split each weight matrix across N GPUs. Each GPU computes a slice of each layer's output. Requires an all-reduce per layer. Works best within a single node (NVLink). Megatron-LM's original contribution.

Pipeline parallelism (PP). Split the model by layer across stages, each on different GPUs. Micro-batch pipelining keeps stages busy. Reduces communication volume vs. TP but introduces "bubble" idle time. Used past 8 nodes when TP's all-reduce becomes too expensive.

Expert parallelism (EP). For MoE models, place different experts on different GPUs. Token-to-expert routing implies an all-to-all collective. Needed at scale; benefits from NVLink-72 GB200.

The composition

For frontier training (e.g., a 405B model on 16k GPUs):

# Within a node (8 H100): TP=8 — utilizes NVLink for layer-level parallelism
# Across 16 nodes: PP=16 — splits model into 16 sequential stages
# Across remaining: DP=128 — 128-way data parallelism over the rest
# For MoE: EP=64 — experts distributed across an EP group
# Total ranks: 8 (TP) × 16 (PP) × 128 (DP) = 16,384 GPUs

The math of 3D parallelism is non-trivial — designing the right split for a given model and cluster is its own engineering discipline. Tools like NVIDIA's NeMo Megatron and DeepSpeed Autotuning try to automate the search.

The framework landscape

PyTorch (FSDP, DDP, DCP). The default. PyTorch 2.x with FSDP2 and async checkpointing covers most needs up to 64–256 GPUs.

DeepSpeed. Microsoft's framework. ZeRO-1/2/3 sharding, MoE support, pipeline parallelism. Used for very large dense models. Slower-moving than PyTorch native FSDP.

Megatron-LM / Megatron-Core. NVIDIA's framework. The reference for 3D parallelism. Used in NeMo and powering Llama-3 training. Most mature for the hyperscale regime.

NeMo Framework 2.0. NVIDIA NeMo Framework 2.0 providing end-to-end optimized training pipelines. Wraps Megatron-Core with end-to-end pipelines, data curation, and recipes.

JAX / XLA. Google's stack. Used by Anthropic and Google DeepMind. SPMD-style programming model. Excellent for TPUs; works on GPUs via XLA. Higher learning curve than PyTorch.

HuggingFace Accelerate / Transformers. Wraps PyTorch and DeepSpeed for the medium-scale fine-tuning regime. Most fine-tunes outside frontier labs use this.

TRL / Open-RLHF / VeRL. RL post-training frameworks. TRL is HuggingFace's; Open-RLHF and VeRL are alternatives optimized for scale and integration with vLLM rollouts.

The pragmatic recommendation

Building a 7B–70B model on 8–256 GPUs: PyTorch + FSDP + HuggingFace Accelerate + TRL. Scaling past 256 GPUs: NeMo Framework with Megatron-Core. Doing RL post-training: VeRL or Open-RLHF, both of which integrate vLLM for fast rollouts. Doing very large MoE training: you're in custom-stack territory.

INFRA Chapter X

Inference infra · the engine war.

The current state Three serious open-source engines (vLLM, SGLang, TensorRT-LLM), each winning on a different axis. Picking one matters less than knowing where each excels.

Settled features Continuous batching, PagedAttention, chunked prefill — table stakes everywhere.

Inference moved from "write some PyTorch code and call it production" to a real engineering discipline with mature frameworks. The current landscape is a three-way race between vLLM, SGLang, and TensorRT-LLM, with several niche players around them.

The standard optimizations, present in all of them

These are no longer differentiators — they're table stakes:

Continuous batching. Dynamically admit and evict requests from in-flight batches.

PagedAttention / virtual-page KV cache. No fragmentation, much higher batch sizes.

Prefix caching. Reuse KV across requests sharing prefixes. RadixAttention (SGLang) is the most general version.

Chunked prefill. Smooth TTFT under long-prompt traffic by mixing prefill chunks with decodes.

FlashAttention. FA-3 on Hopper, FA-4 on Blackwell. Tiled, fused, fast.

Speculative decoding. Draft model proposes tokens; target verifies in one step. 1.5–3× speedup on decode-bound workloads.

Quantization paths. FP8 and FP4 weights, FP8 KV cache, AWQ/GPTQ for legacy hardware.

The newer features that still differentiate

Disaggregated prefill/decode. Separate pools for the two phases. KV cache shipped over NVLink/IB. vLLM has demonstrated 26.2K prefill TPGS and 10.1K decode TPGS on GB200 using a deployment with 4 prefill instances (each with 2 GB200) and 1 decode instance (with 8 GB200). Adds operational complexity; pays back at high QPS with mixed prompt lengths.

Wide expert parallelism (Wide-EP). For MoE serving. Distribute experts across many GPUs (e.g., DeepSeek-V3's 256 experts across an EP group). Reduces per-GPU memory for very large MoE; requires high-bandwidth NVLink-72 fabrics to make the all-to-all affordable.

NVFP4 GEMM kernels. 4-bit weights with hardware-native tensor cores on Blackwell. Roughly 2× the throughput of FP8 with careful scaling. Production-ready in vLLM v0.16+, SGLang, TensorRT-LLM.

Weight offloading v2. vLLM implements weight offloading v2 with asynchronous prefetching where weights are explicitly copied to GPU in advance with the next layer onloaded asynchronously on a separate CUDA stream, allowing the onloading delay to be completely hidden. Lets larger models fit on smaller GPU memory budgets.

When to use what

vLLM: default for almost everything. Broad model support, broad hardware, fast iteration. SGLang: when prefix sharing is a big deal (RAG, multi-turn chat, structured outputs) or when DeepSeek-class MoE models are central. TensorRT-LLM: when you're NVIDIA-only, high-volume, and willing to take operational complexity for the last 30–50% of throughput. LMDeploy: quantization-first deployments. Triton: when you need to serve many models of many kinds behind one endpoint.

INFRA Chapter XI

Hardware · from Hopper to Blackwell.

The cadence A new NVIDIA architecture every 18–24 months. Each roughly doubles practical throughput per dollar.

The competitive landscape AMD MI300/MI355 catching up but composability lags. Custom silicon (TPU, Trainium, Groq, Cerebras) winning specific niches.

The hardware mid-cycle of 2024–2026 was Hopper → Blackwell. Each NVIDIA architecture brings new tensor-core data types, more HBM, faster NVLink. The software has to be rebuilt for each generation to fully exploit it; the work moves quickly because the cost incentives are enormous.

What changes with each hardware generation

New data types. Hopper introduced FP8 tensor cores; Blackwell adds FP4 and MXFP6/MXFP4. The software has to support each generation's preferred quantization to extract full throughput.

Memory bandwidth and capacity. Inference is HBM-bandwidth-bound; each generation's HBM upgrade directly improves decode throughput.

NVLink topology. H100 SXM has 8 GPUs per NVLink domain. GB200/GB300 NVL72 has 72. This changes which parallelism strategies are practical — wide-EP MoE serving wasn't viable before NVL72.

Power and cooling. Blackwell racks consume 100–120 kW, requiring liquid cooling. Data center retrofits and power constraints are now first-class concerns at the deployment level.

The economics

NVIDIA's pitch: a $5M investment in GB200 NVL72 generates $75M in token revenue. The hardware genuinely lowers cost-per-token roughly 4× over Hopper for matched workloads. The math holds; the constraint is supply and software maturity, not whether to upgrade.

INFRA Chapter XII

Quantization · NVFP4 & MXFP.

The trajectory FP32 → FP16 → BF16 → FP8 → FP4 in roughly four years. Each step halves memory and roughly doubles throughput.

The constraint Quality. Each step lower has more risk of accuracy regression on hard tasks.

Quantization is the single most important production technique for inference cost reduction. The story of 2024–2026 is the migration from FP16/BF16 to FP8 to FP4, with hardware support arriving roughly one generation behind the algorithm.

Three quantization regimes

Post-Training Quantization (PTQ). Take a BF16 model, calibrate quantization scales on a small data sample, quantize. Cheap; quality varies. AWQ and GPTQ are the dominant methods; both work via SmoothQuant-style scale adjustment.

Quantization-Aware Training (QAT). Train the model with simulated quantization noise. Higher quality; expensive — needs another fine-tune pass.

Native low-precision training. Train in FP8 from the start. This is now production-grade on Hopper-class hardware; FP4 training is research-stage but advancing fast.

What to quantize

Not all parts of a model are equally tolerant of quantization. The common pattern:

MoE expert weights → FP4. Most numerous; quality cost low because experts are sparsely activated.

Dense MLP weights → FP4 or FP8. Most of the FLOPs; FP8 is the safest bet, FP4 with careful calibration.

Attention projections (Q, K, V, O) → FP8. More sensitive to precision; FP8 preserves quality, FP4 sometimes regresses.

KV cache → FP8. Memory bandwidth, not compute. FP8 KV doubles practical batch sizes.

Embeddings, layernorm, residual paths → keep BF16. Small in volume; high impact on accuracy if quantized.

The empirical rule

For inference on Blackwell-class hardware in 2026: NVFP4 expert weights, FP8 attention, FP8 KV cache, BF16 norms. This combination preserves quality within 1–2% on most benchmarks while delivering 4× the throughput of BF16-everywhere. The tooling — TensorRT-LLM's quantization passes, vLLM's integration with FlashInfer kernels — has matured to the point where you don't hand-build this; you flip a flag.

FRONTIER Chapter XIII

The frontier · what's new.

The premise Everything in chapters II–XII is settled-or-settling. Below is what's not yet — research, early production, or things that may or may not work out.

Watch list Five threads that look most likely to redefine the standard stack over 2026–28.

Five research and early-production threads currently look most likely to redefine the standard stack. Each is real (papers, prototypes, sometimes shipped products), but none has yet become "what everyone does."

World Models Long-Horizon RL Native Multimodal Self-Improving Training Hardware-Algorithm Co-Design Liquid Networks Test-Time Training Compositional Reasoning Neural ODE Acceleration Sparse Attention

1. World models & action-conditioned video

The premise: a single model that learns to simulate the world. Given current state + action, predict next state. The training signal can come from huge volumes of unlabeled video. Once trained, the model can be used for planning (imagine future scenarios), RL (simulate environments to train agents in), or robotics (anticipate consequences of actions).

Live work: Genie 3 (Google DeepMind) produces playable interactive environments from prompts. NVIDIA Cosmos provides world foundation models for physical AI. The 2026 landscape is defined by three converging forces: practical momentum in video understanding and generation, the emergence of world foundation models as simulation-first workflows, and the rise of action-conditioned video generation as a bridge from "content" to "prediction".

Why it matters: the unblocked path from video-pretraining to embodied AI. If world models can be trained at scale, robotics gets a foundation-model moment.

2. Long-horizon RL & environments

GRPO with verifiable rewards works beautifully for short, terminal-reward tasks (math problems, code that compiles, proofs). It struggles when rewards are sparse and trajectories are long — multi-step coding tasks, agentic workflows, scientific discovery.

Active research: process reward models (score the trajectory, not just the answer), environment harnessing (build verifiable proxies — SWE-Bench, OSWorld, MLE-Bench — that the RL loop can train against), self-play for tasks with adversarial structure. SWE-RL, Agent-R1, and similar projects are early. The win will be a model that can do "real software engineering" — implement features that pass tests across a real repo — and that requires reward shaping no current method provides cleanly.

3. Native multimodal & unified models

The current state in Chapter VIII has separate models for understanding and generation, with VLMs gluing modalities into a text-output backbone. The frontier is unified models — train image, video, audio, and text tokens together in a single transformer that can output any modality.

Examples: Chameleon and Show-o (Meta), BAGEL (ByteDance), Gemini Native (Google), and rumored unified GPT models. Today these are weaker than specialized models on each modality. The bet is they'll catch up and the unified path will dominate because of training-data efficiency and shared representations.

4. Self-improving training

The pattern: model generates data, judges its own data, fine-tunes on the best. Various names — RLAIF (RL from AI feedback), self-distillation, recursive bootstrapping. DeepSeek-R1 trained a "zero" variant with pure RL and used its outputs to bootstrap a more capable model. Anthropic's Constitutional AI uses an AI rubric for preference data.

The open question: does this converge or diverge? Synthetic-data training without careful design can cause "model collapse" — distributional drift toward the model's biases. Done right, it's a path to capabilities beyond the human-data ceiling. Done wrong, it's noise amplification. The line is thin and actively researched.

5. Hardware-algorithm co-design

Increasingly, new algorithms are designed knowing exactly which hardware feature they exploit. MLA was designed around HBM bandwidth constraints. Mamba was designed around the failure of attention to scale to million-token contexts. NVFP4-aware quantization-aware training designed for Blackwell tensor cores.

The next wave: Liquid networks & structured sparsity (LFM, Tri Dao's work) that exploit specific hardware patterns. Mixture of Depths (Google) that dynamically allocates compute per layer per token. Test-Time Training that adapts model weights during inference — currently too slow on standard hardware, but specialized accelerators may change that. Photonic and analog accelerators for very low-precision matmul.

The meta-trend

The frontier is moving up the abstraction ladder. In 2020, the open questions were about architectures. In 2023, about scaling laws. In 2025, about post-training and reasoning. In 2026, the open questions are about how to construct training environments and reward signals for capabilities we don't yet have. The hard problems are no longer "how to compute things fast" — that's largely solved. They're "what do we want the model to learn, and how do we measure it." Data and process, not kernels.
