Part I — The training step, anatomized

Where the time goes.

Performance work begins with knowing exactly what a training step does and what bounds it. This part dissects a single forward+backward+optimizer step into its compute, memory, and communication, then arms you with the roofline model — the one framework that tells you whether a piece of work is limited by math or by memory traffic. Everything later in this volume hangs off these fundamentals.

01Anatomy of a training step

A training iteration is the same four phases every time. Knowing them lets you place any performance problem, and lets you talk about "where the step's time goes" — the language Greg's performance-modeling interview runs on.

ONE TRAINING STEP (per micro-batch, per GPU): ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐ │ 1. FORWARD x → layer1 → layer2 → ... → loss │ │ compute: GEMMs + attention + activations │ │ stores: activations (kept for backward) → eats HBM │ │ │ │ 2. BACKWARD dLoss → ... → dlayer2 → dlayer1 │ │ compute: ~2× forward FLOPs (grad wrt weights + wrt inputs) │ │ reads: saved activations; produces: gradients │ │ │ │ 3. COMMUNICATION sync gradients/activations across GPUs │ │ data-parallel: all-reduce gradients (overlappable w/ #2) │ │ tensor/expert-parallel: collectives INSIDE fwd/bwd │ │ │ │ 4. OPTIMIZER w ← w − lr · m̂/(√v̂+ε) (Adam) │ │ reads/writes: weights + optimizer state (m, v) │ └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘ step time = max(compute, communication) IF perfectly overlapped = compute + exposed_communication in reality

The single most important relationship in this whole volume is in that last line: step time is compute plus whatever communication you failed to hide behind compute. A well-tuned job overlaps the gradient all-reduce with the backward pass so comm is nearly free; a poorly-tuned one exposes it and every step waits on the network. Most distributed-training performance work is about shrinking the exposed communication.

PhaseBounded by · the efficiency lever
ForwardCompute (big GEMMs) for large models; memory bandwidth for small ones. Lever: precision, kernel fusion, batch size.
Backward~2× forward FLOPs. The biggest compute chunk. Lever: activation recomputation trades compute for memory.
CommunicationNetwork bandwidth/latency. Lever: overlap with compute, collective algorithm, topology (Manual II §03), SHARP.
OptimizerMemory bandwidth (streaming weights + state). Usually small, but distributed-optimizer comm can add up. Lever: ZeRO sharding, fused optimizers.
The forward:backward:optimizer ratio to carry

Rule of thumb: forward ≈ 1 unit of compute, backward ≈ 2 units, so a step is ~3× the forward FLOPs (the basis of the "6 × params × tokens" FLOP estimate in §29 — 2 for the multiply-accumulate, ×3 for fwd+bwd). The optimizer is cheap in FLOPs but can be memory-heavy. When someone asks "where does a training step spend its time," leading with "roughly a third forward, two-thirds backward, plus communication you hopefully overlapped, plus a thin optimizer" shows you think structurally about the step.

02The compute: GEMMs & tensor cores

Almost all the FLOPs in a transformer are matrix multiplies (GEMMs), and they run on tensor cores. Understanding this layer is what lets you reason about MFU and why precision matters — core to both interviews.

It's GEMMs all the way down

A transformer layer is: QKV projections (GEMM), attention scores (GEMM), attention output (GEMM), the output projection (GEMM), and the MLP's two big GEMMs. The non-GEMM parts (softmax, layernorm, activation functions, the attention itself if not fused) are memory-bound glue. So the compute efficiency of training is essentially the efficiency of its GEMMs on the tensor cores.

ConceptWhat it is
Tensor coreA hardware unit that does a small matrix multiply-accumulate (e.g., a tile of D = A×B + C) in one operation. Where the FLOPs come from — orders of magnitude more throughput than the general CUDA cores for matmul.
PrecisionTensor cores run at different rates by precision: FP8 > BF16/FP16 > TF32 > FP32. Lower precision = more FLOP/s, but numerical care needed. Blackwell adds FP4 for inference.
Mixed precisionCompute in BF16/FP8, keep an FP32 master copy of weights + FP32 accumulation for stability. The standard training recipe.
GEMM shapeTensor cores want dimensions that are multiples of the tile size (often 8/16/64). A GEMM with awkward dimensions wastes the cores ("wave quantization"). Why model dims are chosen as nice multiples.
Tensor-core throughput scales as you drop precision (illustrative, Hopper→Blackwell): FP32 ▏▏ baseline TF32 ▏▏▏▏ ~4-8× BF16 ▏▏▏▏▏▏▏▏ ~2× TF32 FP8 ▏▏▏▏▏▏▏▏▏▏▏▏▏▏▏▏ ~2× BF16 ← Hopper/Blackwell training FP4 ▏▏▏▏▏▏...×2 again ← Blackwell, inference (Nemotron NVFP4) Each step down ~doubles peak FLOP/s — IF the numerics hold and the GEMM fills the cores.
Why "100% GPU utilization" can still be slow compute

This is the §15-of-Manual-I point made concrete: DCGM "utilization" only says a kernel was running. A GEMM running in FP32 with poorly-shaped dimensions can show 100% util while delivering a fraction of the tensor cores' BF16/FP8 peak. The truth metric is MFU (§28) — achieved vs peak FLOP/s — and the levers are precision (use BF16/FP8), GEMM shape (nice multiples), and fusion (don't bounce to HBM between ops). When the compute interviewer asks "this job is at 100% util but slow, what's wrong," the answer lives here: utilization ≠ FLOP efficiency.

03The memory: what lives in HBM

GPU memory (HBM) is the hard constraint that forces parallelism in the first place. Knowing the four things that consume it — and their relative sizes — is what lets you reason about OOM, choose a parallelism strategy, and explain why a model "doesn't fit."

WHAT'S IN A GPU's HBM during training (for N parameters): ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐ │ 1. PARAMETERS .............. N × 2 bytes (BF16) │ │ 2. GRADIENTS ............... N × 2 bytes (BF16) [or FP32] │ │ 3. OPTIMIZER STATE (Adam) .. N × (4+4+4) = 12 bytes │ │ └ FP32 master weights + FP32 momentum + FP32 variance │ │ 4. ACTIVATIONS ............. depends on batch × seq × layers │ │ └ the wildcard — grows with batch and sequence length │ └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘ Params+grads+optimizer for Adam ≈ 2+2+12 = 16 bytes/param (model states) → a 7B model needs ~112 GB just for model states, before activations! → this is WHY you shard (ZeRO/FSDP §07) and parallelize (§08-§12)

This 16-bytes-per-parameter figure is the same math behind the checkpoint sizing in Manual I §13 (the 56B → 737 GiB checkpoint stores exactly these FP32 states). Two consequences shape everything:

ConsumerBehavior & how parallelism attacks it
Model states (params+grad+opt)Fixed per parameter (~16 B/param for Adam BF16). ZeRO/FSDP shards these across data-parallel ranks; tensor/pipeline parallel splits the parameters themselves across GPUs.
ActivationsScales with batch × sequence × hidden × layers. The knob you can actually turn at runtime. Activation recomputation (checkpointing) drops most of them and recomputes in backward — trading compute (HFU rises) for memory.
The memory-pressure decision the role faces constantly

"This model OOMs" has a decision tree: (1) reduce activations — smaller micro-batch, activation recomputation, sequence/context parallel (§11); (2) shard model states — ZeRO stages / FSDP (§07); (3) split the model — tensor (§08) or pipeline (§09) parallel. Each has a different cost (recompute = more FLOPs; sharding/TP = more communication; pipeline = bubble). The senior skill, and exactly what Greg probes with "the model doesn't fit, what do you do," is choosing the cheapest option for the situation — which requires knowing both the memory math here and the communication costs in Part II.

04The roofline model

The roofline is the single most useful mental model in performance engineering, and a likely topic in Greg's performance-modeling interview. It answers one question for any kernel or workload: are you limited by compute (FLOP/s) or by memory bandwidth (bytes/s)? — because the fix is completely different for each.

ROOFLINE: achievable FLOP/s vs arithmetic intensity (FLOPs per byte moved) FLOP/s peak ┤ ___________________ ← compute-bound roof (peak FLOP/s) │ / │ / ← the "ridge point": AI where you transition │ / │ / ← memory-bound roof (slope = peak memory bandwidth) │ / │ / └─────┴──────────────────────────► arithmetic intensity (FLOP/byte) ↑ridge LEFT of ridge = MEMORY-BOUND → fix: move fewer bytes (fusion, precision, caching) RIGHT of ridge = COMPUTE-BOUND → fix: do math faster (tensor cores, better GEMM) ridge point = peak_FLOP/s ÷ peak_bandwidth (the AI you must exceed to be compute-bound)

How to use it in practice: estimate a kernel's arithmetic intensity (FLOPs done ÷ bytes moved to/from HBM), find which side of the ridge it's on, and that tells you the ceiling and the fix.

If the work is…SymptomThe fix
Memory-boundLow arithmetic intensity; HBM bandwidth saturated; tensor cores idle-ishMove fewer bytes: fuse ops (avoid HBM round-trips), lower precision, increase data reuse, larger tiles
Compute-boundHigh arithmetic intensity; tensor cores saturated; near peak FLOP/sDo math faster: tensor cores, lower precision, better GEMM shapes — or you're already near the roof
Latency-boundNeither roof saturated; small kernels, launch overhead dominatesFuse kernels, use CUDA graphs, bigger batches — reduce the number of launches (§26)

Roofline turns "it's slow" into a falsifiable hypothesis. Instead of guessing, you measure the kernel's FLOPs and bytes (Nsight Compute gives both, §24), place it on the roofline, and now you know whether to attack compute or memory — and whether you're even close to the achievable ceiling. This is the model behind the whole "measure before you optimize" discipline. Being able to sketch the roofline and say "this attention kernel is memory-bound at low intensity, so fusing it (FlashAttention-style) to keep the scores in SRAM is the win, not faster math" is a standout performance-modeling answer.

05Arithmetic intensity

Where transformers actually sit on the roofline depends on arithmetic intensity, and it's worth knowing the canonical cases because they explain why FlashAttention exists, why batch size matters, and why inference decode is a different regime.

OperationIntensity & roofline position
Big GEMM (MLP, large batch)High AI — reuses each loaded value across many multiply-accumulates. Compute-bound. This is where MFU comes from; tensor cores fill up.
Attention (naive)Low-ish AI — the N×N scores matrix gets written to and read from HBM. Memory-bound. FlashAttention fixes this by keeping scores in on-chip SRAM and never materializing them in HBM, raising AI and making it compute-bound.
LayerNorm / softmax / activationsVery low AI — elementwise, one or two FLOPs per byte. Pure memory-bound glue. Fuse them into neighbors to avoid the HBM round-trip.
Small-batch / single-token decodeLow AI — you load the whole weight matrix to multiply by a tiny activation. Memory-bandwidth-bound. This is why inference decode is memory-bound (MBU, not MFU, is the metric) and why batching helps inference so much.
Same model, two regimes: TRAINING (big batches) → GEMMs have high AI → COMPUTE-bound → optimize FLOP/s (MFU) INFERENCE DECODE (1 token) → load weights for tiny matmul → MEMORY-bound → optimize BW (MBU) batch size is the knob that moves you right (more reuse) on the roofline: batch=1 ▏ low AI, memory-bound batch=8 ▏▏▏ rising AI batch=64 ▏▏▏▏▏▏▏ high AI, compute-bound (training regime)
The two-regime insight Aman will want

"Why is inference optimized so differently from training?" — because they sit on opposite sides of the roofline. Training runs big batched GEMMs (compute-bound, maximize FLOP/s via tensor cores and precision); inference decode multiplies huge weight matrices by a single token's activation (memory-bound, maximize bandwidth utilization via batching, KV-cache management, and quantization). Same hardware, same model, opposite bottleneck — and naming arithmetic intensity as the reason is the kind of first-principles answer that lands. It also explains why the team's storage tensor-loading benchmark cares about loading bandwidth: inference is bandwidth-sensitive end to end.

Every performance question starts the same way: which side of the roofline am I on? Math, or memory? The answer dictates the fix.

Part II — Parallelism dimensions

Splitting the model.

This is the heart of Greg's interview. A frontier model can't fit or train fast enough on one GPU, so you split it — and there are several independent ways to split, each trading memory for a different communication pattern. Master the dimensions (data, tensor, pipeline, expert, sequence) and how they compose, and you can answer the canonical question — "this model doesn't fit / is too slow, how do you parallelize it?" — like someone who's done it.

06The three walls

You parallelize because one GPU hits one of three walls. Naming which wall a workload hits tells you which parallelism dimension to reach for first.

WallWhat it is · which parallelism addresses it
Memory wallThe model + states + activations don't fit in one GPU's HBM (§03). Addressed by sharding states (ZeRO/FSDP) and splitting the model (tensor/pipeline). The most common reason to go multi-GPU.
Compute wallIt fits, but one GPU's FLOP/s means training takes months. Addressed by data parallelism — more GPUs each process different data, so total throughput scales.
Time / convergence wallYou need a target token budget in a fixed wall-clock, so you need a large global batch processed fast. Addressed by combining data parallelism (scale-out) with the model-parallel dims (so each replica fits).
THE DECISION at a glance: "doesn't fit on 1 GPU" → memory wall → shard (ZeRO/FSDP) or split (TP/PP) "fits but too slow" → compute wall → data parallel (more replicas) "need it done by " → time wall → DP × (TP/PP/EP) at scale In practice big training uses ALL of them at once (§12): each replica is made to FIT with TP+PP(+EP), then DP replicates for THROUGHPUT.

Parallelism is memory-vs-communication arbitrage. Every dimension buys you something (more memory headroom, or more throughput) and charges you in communication (a different collective, at a different frequency, over a different part of the fabric). The art — and the thing Greg is testing — is matching the dimension to the wall and the hardware topology, so the communication it adds lands on the fastest link available (TP on NVLink, DP all-reduce on IB/EFA, overlapped with compute). Hold this framing and every specific dimension below becomes an instance of it.

07Data parallelism & ZeRO/FSDP

Data parallelism (DP) is the foundation: replicate the model on every GPU, give each a different slice of the batch, and average the gradients so all replicas stay in sync. Its communication is the gradient all-reduce, and its evolution (DDP → ZeRO → FSDP) is the story of fixing DP's memory cost.

Classic DDP

DDP: every GPU has a FULL copy of the model GPU0: model + batch_shard_0 ─┐ GPU1: model + batch_shard_1 ─┤ each does fwd+bwd on its own data GPU2: model + batch_shard_2 ─┤ → produces local gradients GPU3: model + batch_shard_3 ─┘ │ all-reduce(gradients) ← average grads across all GPUs (the DP comm) │ every GPU applies the SAME averaged gradient → replicas stay identical comm per step: all-reduce of the full gradient (≈ 2× model size on the wire, §14) KEY: overlap this all-reduce with the backward pass → comm becomes ~free (§17)

DDP's flaw: every GPU stores the full params + grads + optimizer state (16 B/param), so it wastes memory replicating identical states. ZeRO and FSDP fix exactly this.

ZeRO / FSDP — shard the redundant states

StageWhat it shards across DP ranks
ZeRO-1Optimizer state (the 12 B/param). Biggest single win; little extra comm.
ZeRO-2+ gradients. Each rank only holds the grads for its shard after reduce-scatter.
ZeRO-3 / FSDP+ parameters. No GPU holds the full model — each holds a shard and all-gathers the layers it needs just-in-time for forward/backward, then frees them. Maximum memory saving, most communication.

FSDP (Fully Sharded Data Parallel) is PyTorch's implementation of the ZeRO-3 idea: parameters live sharded; before a layer runs, its full weights are all-gathered, used, and discarded; gradients are reduce-scattered back to shards. It converts DP's "replicate everything" into "shard everything, gather on demand" — trading communication (all-gather per layer) for a massive memory reduction.

The DP communication-vs-memory trade, stated

DDP: minimal comm (one overlapped all-reduce), maximal memory (full replica). ZeRO-3/FSDP: minimal memory (full shard), more comm (all-gather params every layer, reduce-scatter grads). The right point depends on whether you're memory-bound (go FSDP/ZeRO-3) or comm-bound (stay DDP/ZeRO-1). This is a frequent interview probe — "DDP vs FSDP, when would you use each?" — and the answer is the trade: FSDP when the model states don't fit; lighter ZeRO stages when they do and you want to keep communication low. Your 400× distributed-PyTorch experience is exactly this territory — be ready to say which sharding regime you used and why.

08Tensor parallelism

Tensor parallelism (TP) splits each layer's weight matrices across GPUs, so the GPUs cooperatively compute a single layer. It's the most communication-intensive dimension — which is exactly why it's pinned to the fastest link (NVLink within a node on H100; within the rack on GB200, §04 of Manual II).

TP on an MLP (Y = GeLU(X·A)·B), split across 2 GPUs: ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐ │ A is split COLUMN-wise: A = [A1 | A2] │ │ GPU0: Y1 = GeLU(X·A1) GPU1: Y2 = GeLU(X·A2) │ │ (no comm needed yet — each has part of the hidden dim) │ │ │ │ B is split ROW-wise: B = [B1 ; B2] │ │ GPU0: Z0 = Y1·B1 GPU1: Z1 = Y2·B2 │ │ Z = Z0 + Z1 ← ALL-REDUCE to sum partial outputs │ └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘ comm: 1 all-reduce in forward + 1 in backward, PER LAYER → many collectives per step → MUST be on NVLink (intra-node/rack)

The Megatron-style column-then-row split is the canonical pattern: the first GEMM splits columns (no comm), the second splits rows and an all-reduce sums the partials. Attention is split by attention heads similarly. The crucial property: TP communicates inside every layer, twice (fwd+bwd), so its collective frequency is enormous — which dictates the hardware constraint.

PropertyTensor parallelism
What it splitsThe weight matrices (and activations) within each layer.
Memory benefitParameters, grads, optimizer state, and activations all divided by TP size.
CommunicationAll-reduce (or all-gather + reduce-scatter with sequence parallel) per layer, per direction. Frequent and on the critical path — hard to overlap.
Hardware constraintMust stay within the NVLink domain. TP=8 on H100 (one node); on GB200 NVL72 you can go much larger because the whole 72-GPU rack is one NVLink domain. TP across IB/EFA is a performance disaster.
The number to know: why TP rarely exceeds 8 (on H100)

The storage-checkpoint benchmark's own config encodes it: 56B uses TP=4 on Blackwell, TP=8 on H100. The reason is purely topological — TP's per-layer all-reduces only stay fast inside NVLink, which spans 8 GPUs on an H100 node. Push TP across nodes and every layer's collective crosses IB/EFA, exploding step time. The GB200 NVL72's headline advantage is precisely that this limit jumps to the whole rack (72 GPUs), so much larger TP/EP becomes viable on NVLink. Connecting "TP size" to "the NVLink-domain size" is a strong topology-aware answer for Greg.

09Pipeline parallelism & the bubble

Pipeline parallelism (PP) splits the model's layers into stages, each on a different GPU (or node), and streams micro-batches through like an assembly line. Its communication is cheap (just activations between adjacent stages), but it introduces a unique cost — the pipeline bubble — and understanding the bubble is the key PP insight.

PP with 4 stages (S0..S3), naive schedule, 4 micro-batches (mb): time ─────────────────────────────────────────────────► S0: F1 F2 F3 F4 ......................... B4 B3 B2 B1 S1: F1 F2 F3 F4 ................. B4 B3 B2 B1 S2: F1 F2 F3 F4 ......... B4 B3 B2 B1 S3: F1 F2 F3 F4 .. B4 B3 B2 B1 └─bubble─┘ └────fill────┘ └─bubble─┘ ░ = BUBBLE: stages idle while the pipe fills and drains. bubble fraction ≈ (P − 1) / (M + P − 1) for P stages, M micro-batches → more micro-batches (M) per pipeline flush amortizes the bubble away

The bubble is the price of the pipeline: with P stages, the first and last stages sit idle during fill and drain. The fix is more micro-batches per step (larger M), so the steady-state region dominates and the bubble fraction shrinks. Advanced schedules (interleaved / 1F1B — one-forward-one-backward) reduce it further by interleaving forward and backward micro-batches.

PropertyPipeline parallelism
What it splitsThe layers, into sequential stages across GPUs/nodes.
CommunicationCheap — only the activations (and their gradients) passed point-to-point between adjacent stages. Tolerates slower links (IB/EFA), so PP spans nodes.
The costThe bubble — idle time during fill/drain. Amortized by more micro-batches; minimized by interleaved/1F1B schedules.
Memory benefitEach stage holds only its layers' params + a window of activations.
The TP-vs-PP placement rule

The complementary insight that ties §08 and §09 together, and a great thing to state: TP communicates a lot but cheaply-per-message and must stay on NVLink, so put TP inside a node/rack; PP communicates little and tolerates slow links, so put PP across nodes. A typical layout is TP within the 8-GPU node (NVLink) and PP across nodes (IB/EFA), with DP on top. This mapping of "which dimension to which level of the locality tree" (Manual II §01) is exactly the systems-aware parallelism reasoning Greg wants — it shows you place parallelism by communication cost against the fabric.

10Expert parallelism & MoE

Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) models replace the dense MLP with many "expert" MLPs and route each token to a few of them. Expert parallelism (EP) places different experts on different GPUs — and its communication is the all-to-all, the most topology-sensitive collective. NVIDIA's own Nemotron MoE models and the team's single-rack-alltoall and hybrid-ep benchmarks make this directly relevant.

MoE layer with experts spread across GPUs (EP): tokens arrive on each GPU → a ROUTER picks top-k experts per token │ all-to-all (dispatch): send each token to the GPU holding its chosen expert │ each GPU runs ITS experts on the tokens it received │ all-to-all (combine): send results back to each token's original GPU │ weighted-sum the expert outputs comm: TWO all-to-alls per MoE layer (dispatch + combine) → all-to-all stresses the FULL bisection bandwidth of the fabric → on GB200 this is why nvloom (MNNVL) + single-rack-alltoall tests exist

Why MoE is attractive and why EP is hard, in one breath: MoE gives you far more parameters (capacity) for roughly the same FLOPs per token (since each token only hits a few experts), so it's a way to scale model quality cheaply. But routing tokens to scattered experts requires all-to-all communication, which is the collective that most stresses the network's bisection bandwidth — and it's irregular (load can be unbalanced across experts).

AspectExpert parallelism
What it splitsThe experts (distinct MLPs) across GPUs.
CommunicationTwo all-to-alls per MoE layer (dispatch tokens, combine results). Bisection-bandwidth-bound; sensitive to topology.
The hard partLoad imbalance — if routing sends too many tokens to one expert, that GPU straggles (auxiliary load-balancing losses mitigate it). Capacity factors drop overflow tokens.
Why GB200 helpsAll-to-all over the 72-GPU NVLink rack is far faster than over IB/EFA — rack-scale NVLink makes large EP practical, which is part of why the team benchmarks MNNVL all-to-all so heavily.
The collective-per-dimension summary (memorize this table)

Each parallelism dimension has a signature collective, and naming them instantly shows fluency: DP → all-reduce (gradients, overlappable), TP → all-reduce/all-gather (per layer, on NVLink), PP → point-to-point (activations, cheap, cross-node), EP → all-to-all (token routing, bisection-bound), SP → all-gather/reduce-scatter (§11). When Greg asks about any dimension, leading with "its communication pattern is X, which means it wants hardware Y" is the structural answer that distinguishes real understanding.

11Sequence / context parallelism

Long-context training (100k+ tokens) breaks the memory model differently: activations scale with sequence length, so even a model that fits can OOM on a long sequence. Sequence parallelism (SP) and context parallelism (CP) split along the sequence dimension to fix this.

TechniqueWhat it does
Sequence parallelism (Megatron SP)A companion to TP: the parts of a layer that TP doesn't split (layernorm, dropout — the non-GEMM regions) are split along the sequence dimension instead, so their activations are also divided by the parallel size. Converts TP's all-reduce into all-gather + reduce-scatter. Reduces activation memory with no extra net communication volume.
Context parallelismSplits the sequence itself across GPUs for attention over very long contexts. Each GPU holds part of the sequence; attention requires communicating keys/values across GPUs (e.g., ring attention passes KV blocks around a ring). Enables contexts that no single GPU could hold.
Activation memory scales with SEQUENCE LENGTH: short seq (2k): activations fit → no SP/CP needed long seq (128k): activations explode → split the sequence │ Sequence parallel: split layernorm/dropout activations along seq (with TP) Context parallel: split the tokens themselves; attention communicates KV (ring attention: KV blocks rotate around the GPU ring)
Why this matters for the role's workloads

MARS serves AV, robotics, and video teams (Manual I §01) — workloads with long sequences (video frames, sensor streams, long trajectories), not just text. So context/sequence parallelism isn't an exotic corner; it's how those workloads fit at all. Knowing that "activations scale with sequence length, so long-context workloads need sequence/context parallelism, which adds KV communication" rounds out your parallelism story beyond the LLM-default and connects to the actual diversity of workloads the role supports.

12Composing nD parallelism

Real frontier training uses all these dimensions at once — "3D/4D/5D parallelism." The skill that impresses Greg is being able to lay them out against the hardware so each dimension's communication lands on the right link. This chapter assembles the whole picture.

A typical layout for a large dense model on H100 (illustrative): ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐ │ TENSOR PARALLEL (TP=8) ── within a node, over NVLink │ │ ↑ most comm, fastest link │ │ PIPELINE PARALLEL (PP=k) ── across nodes, over IB/EFA │ │ ↑ cheap point-to-point, tolerates slower links │ │ DATA PARALLEL (DP=m) ── across the remaining GPUs │ │ ↑ all-reduce, overlapped with backward │ │ [+ EXPERT PARALLEL if MoE; + SEQUENCE PARALLEL with TP] │ └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘ total GPUs = TP × PP × DP (× EP, etc.) each (TP×PP) group is ONE model replica that FITS; DP replicates for THROUGHPUT THE MAPPING RULE: fastest link (NVLink) ← most frequent/heavy comm (TP, EP) slower link (IB/EFA) ← infrequent/cheap comm (PP point-to-point, DP all-reduce overlapped)
DimensionComm · placement · purpose
Tensor (TP)All-reduce/per-layer · within NVLink domain · fit the layer's compute across GPUs.
Pipeline (PP)P2P activations · across nodes · fit the model's depth; bubble is the cost.
Data (DP)All-reduce/per-step, overlapped · across replicas · throughput.
Expert (EP)All-to-all · within NVLink ideally · capacity (MoE).
Sequence (SP/CP)All-gather/reduce-scatter or ring-KV · with TP / across seq · long context, activation memory.

Composing parallelism is a constrained optimization: fit each replica in memory, then maximize throughput, subject to landing each collective on the fastest adequate link. You pick TP large enough (and EP) to fit the layer within the NVLink domain, PP to fit the depth across nodes, then DP to fill the rest of the cluster for throughput — and you keep the heavy collectives (TP all-reduce, EP all-to-all) on NVLink while the cheap ones (PP P2P, DP all-reduce) ride IB/EFA overlapped with compute. This is the single most senior thing you can demonstrate in Greg's interview: not just naming the dimensions, but laying them out against the locality tree with the communication cost as the organizing principle. And it's exactly the reasoning behind why GB200 NVL72's 72-GPU NVLink domain is transformative — it dramatically widens the "fastest link" tier, so much more of your parallelism can live on NVLink.

Frontier training isn't one parallelism — it's five, stacked so each one's communication lands on exactly the link that can afford it.

Part III — The collective layer

Moving it on the wire.

Every parallelism dimension's cost is a collective communication, and NCCL is what executes them. 13The collectives

A handful of collective operations cover all of distributed training. Know what each does and which parallelism uses it — this is the vocabulary NCCL questions are asked in.

Why "all-reduce = reduce-scatter + all-gather" matters

This decomposition is not trivia — it's why an optimal ring all-reduce moves a specific, irreducible amount of data (§14), and why FSDP can use the two halves separately (reduce-scatter the grads, all-gather the params). When you can say "all-reduce is just a reduce-scatter followed by an all-gather, each moving (n−1)/n of the data, so all-reduce moves ~2× the buffer regardless of GPU count," you've demonstrated you understand the collective at the level the JD's "debug distributed training with NCCL" requires.

14Ring vs tree & the bandwidth math

NCCL implements each collective with an algorithm, and the two that matter are ring (bandwidth-optimal) and tree (latency-optimal). Knowing the bandwidth math lets you predict a collective's time and explain why it scales the way it does — pure performance-modeling gold for Greg.

The ring algorithm

RING all-reduce: GPUs in a logical ring, data flows around it in chunks. GPU0 → GPU1 → GPU2 → GPU3 → (back to GPU0) reduce-scatter phase: (n−1) steps, each GPU sends 1/n of the buffer onward, accumulating → ends with each owning a reduced chunk all-gather phase: (n−1) steps, the reduced chunks circulate to everyone DATA MOVED PER GPU = 2 × (n−1)/n × buffer_size → as n grows, (n−1)/n → 1, so each GPU moves ≈ 2× the buffer, INDEPENDENT of n → time ≈ 2 × (n−1)/n × buffer / link_bandwidth (bandwidth-optimal!)

The headline result: ring all-reduce time is essentially independent of the number of GPUs (the (n−1)/n factor approaches 1), depending instead on buffer size and per-link bandwidth. This is why data-parallel training scales so well — adding GPUs doesn't increase the all-reduce time much, only the latency term (more hops). The cost is latency: a ring has n hops, so for small buffers latency dominates.

The "busbw" metric the team's nccl-tests report

nccl-tests reports bus bandwidth (busbw), not raw algorithm bandwidth — busbw normalizes out the 2(n−1)/n factor so you can compare against the hardware's peak link bandwidth directly. A healthy all-reduce shows busbw near the fabric's peak (e.g., near NVLink or IB line rate); a low busbw means something's wrong (wrong transport, TCP fallback, a bad link — Manual II §24). When you run all_reduce_perf and read busbw, you're measuring exactly this algorithm's efficiency against the wire. Being able to say "busbw of 30 GB/s on a fabric that should do 100+ means NCCL probably fell back off the fast path" is precisely the NCCL-debugging fluency the JD demands.

15Mapping collectives to topology

NCCL's real cleverness is mapping a collective onto the physical topology (Manual II §02–§03) — using NVLink within a node, rails between nodes, and SHARP where available. Understanding this connects the parallelism dimensions to the fabric.

NCCL builds a HIERARCHICAL collective matching the topology: ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐ │ intra-node: reduce across the 8 GPUs over NVLink/NVSwitch │ │ (fast — one node becomes one "super-rank") │ │ inter-node: ring/tree across nodes over IB/EFA, rail-aligned │ │ (GPUn ↔ GPUn on rail n — parallel rail transfers) │ │ + SHARP: the IB switch sums in-network → less data on wire │ └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘ So a 512-GPU all-reduce isn't one flat ring — it's: reduce within each node (NVLink) → reduce across nodes (IB, rails, maybe SHARP) → broadcast back down. Hierarchy keeps the slow links lightly loaded.

The practical implications you should be able to state:

  • Intra-node first. NCCL reduces within each node over NVLink before touching the slow inter-node fabric, so only one reduced copy per node crosses IB/EFA — keeping the expensive links lightly loaded.
  • Rail alignment (Manual II §03) means the inter-node phase is N parallel rail-local transfers, not one funnel. Break it (wrong NIC binding) and bandwidth collapses.
  • SHARP offloads the inter-node reduction into the IB switches, cutting the data that traverses the fabric — an IB-only win that makes the same all-reduce faster on an IB cluster than on EFA (Manual II §22).

This is where Volume II and Volume III meet. The parallelism dimensions (§06–§12) generate collectives; NCCL maps them onto the locality tree (Manual II §01); the fabric (IB/EFA, SHARP, rails) determines how fast they run. So "why is my all-reduce slow" can be answered top to bottom: is the algorithm right (ring vs tree), is NCCL using the fast transport (busbw, NCCL_DEBUG), is it rail-aligned, is SHARP available on this fabric? Being able to trace a collective from the parallelism dimension that issued it, through NCCL's algorithm and topology mapping, to the physical link — that end-to-end view is the senior systems-and-performance answer that wins both Aman's and Greg's interviews.

17Overlapping comm & compute

The single most important performance technique in distributed training: hide communication behind computation so it costs nothing. Recall from §01 that step time is compute + exposed communication — overlap is how you drive the exposed part toward zero.

WITHOUT overlap (exposed comm): compute ████████████ ████████████ comm ▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓ ▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓ step = compute + comm (serial — comm fully exposed, slow) WITH overlap (hidden comm): compute ████████████████████████████████████████████ comm ▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓ ▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓ ▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓ step ≈ compute (comm runs concurrently on copy engines/separate streams)

How overlap actually happens, per dimension:

DimensionHow its comm overlaps
DP all-reduceAs each layer's gradients finish in the backward pass, kick off their all-reduce while earlier layers are still computing. By the time backward ends, most grads are already reduced. (DDP buckets gradients for exactly this.)
FSDP all-gatherPrefetch the next layer's parameters (all-gather) while the current layer computes, so the weights are ready when needed.
PP point-to-pointWhile stage k computes micro-batch m, it's also sending micro-batch m−1's activations downstream — the pipeline naturally overlaps comm and compute.
TP all-reduceHardest to overlap — it's on the critical path within a layer. Mitigated by keeping it on fast NVLink (so it's short) and by techniques that fuse/split the GEMM with the communication.

"Is the communication overlapped?" is the first question to ask of any distributed-training timeline. A job where the all-reduce shows as a gap after the backward pass (exposed comm) is leaving huge performance on the table — and you can see it directly in an Nsight Systems trace (§20): compute and NCCL kernels should be concurrent, not sequential. The fix is usually enabling/​tuning gradient bucketing (DDP), prefetch (FSDP), or confirming the comm is on a separate CUDA stream. This is exactly the kind of finding the profiling part teaches you to spot, and a textbook example of "investigate and resolve training performance end to end" — you see the exposed comm in the trace, name the mechanism, and apply the overlap fix.

Communication you can hide behind compute is free. The whole game is making the exposed sliver as thin as the fabric allows.

21NVTX & CPU/GPU correlation

Raw kernel names are cryptic. NVTX (NVIDIA Tools Extension) lets you annotate your code with named ranges that show up as labeled bands on the timeline — turning "a sea of GEMMs" into "forward / backward / optimizer / all-reduce." This is what makes a distributed trace readable.

import torch.cuda.nvtx as nvtx

for step in range(steps):
    nvtx.range_push("forward")        # labeled band starts
    loss = model(batch)
    nvtx.range_pop()

    nvtx.range_push("backward")
    loss.backward()                       # the all-reduce overlaps HERE (§17)
    nvtx.range_pop()

    nvtx.range_push("optimizer")
    optimizer.step(); optimizer.zero_grad()
    nvtx.range_pop()

# the same phase decomposition you see in goodput (Manual I §11).

The other half is CPU/GPU correlation: Nsight Systems links each CPU-side CUDA API call to the GPU kernel it launched, so you can see why the GPU is idle. If the GPU row has a gap and the correlated CPU thread is busy in Python/dataloading, you've found a host bottleneck; if the CPU launched the kernel long before the GPU ran it, you may be launch-bound or queue-stalled.

Here's the elegant connection: the NVTX ranges (forward/backward/optimizer/comm) and the OneLogger time-budget phases (Manual I §11 — startup, productive training, checkpoint, etc.) are the same decomposition at different granularities. OneLogger gives you the per-job/fleet view of where time went; NVTX-annotated Nsight Systems gives you the microsecond view of a single step's phases. So when you find that "checkpoint overhead is 22% of runtime" in goodput, the NVTX trace lets you see exactly which operations within the save are slow. Being able to move between the fleet-level goodput decomposition and the kernel-level timeline — same phases, different zoom — is the full-stack observability fluency that defines this role.

25The memory hierarchy, kernel view

Memory-bound kernels are the common case for the non-GEMM parts of a model, and fixing them means understanding the GPU memory hierarchy from a kernel's perspective. This is core CUDA knowledge — your territory — and the basis for why fusion and tiling work.

ConceptWhy it matters · the fix it motivates

26Occupancy, warps & launch overhead

Two latency-related issues round out kernel performance: occupancy (can the GPU hide memory latency?) and launch overhead (are tiny kernels spending more time launching than running?). Both show up constantly in real training and have clean fixes.

Occupancy

The GPU hides memory latency by having many warps resident — while one warp waits on HBM, another runs. Occupancy is resident warps ÷ maximum, and it's capped by per-thread registers, per-block shared memory, and block size. Low occupancy means too few warps to hide latency, so the SMs stall waiting on memory. But occupancy isn't a goal in itself — a kernel can be fast at moderate occupancy if it has enough instruction-level parallelism; chasing 100% occupancy by cutting registers can backfire.

27A worked kernel optimization

Putting Part V together: a complete kernel-optimization narrative, the shape of the story to tell when asked "walk me through optimizing a slow kernel."

worked case A custom fused-layernorm kernel running far below roofline
The trigger (from Nsight Systems)

The timeline (§20) shows a custom layernorm kernel taking a surprising fraction of each step — more than the big GEMMs around it. That's the signal to drop into Nsight Compute on this specific kernel.

Read Speed-of-Light first (§24)

SOL shows Compute 14%, Memory (DRAM) 88% → unambiguously memory-bound. So the fix is about moving fewer bytes, not faster math. Place it on the roofline: low arithmetic intensity, hard against the memory roof — expected for an elementwise op.

Memory Workload Analysis (§25)

The kernel reads the input from HBM, writes the normalized output to HBM, and — the smell — it's a separate kernel from the activation that follows, so the intermediate makes another HBM round-trip. Also, the access pattern is uncoalesced for the chosen layout, inflating transactions.

The fixes

(1) Fuse the layernorm with the following activation (and ideally the preceding op) into one kernel, so the intermediate stays in registers/shared and never touches HBM — raising arithmetic intensity and halving HBM traffic. (2) Fix coalescing by adjusting the layout/access so adjacent threads read adjacent memory. (3) Confirm shared-memory use has no bank conflicts.

Re-measure (the discipline)

Re-profile: Memory% drops, the kernel time falls, and the timeline (§20) shows the step shrink. Crucially, you verified the fix moved the number rather than assuming it — the measurement discipline (§18) closing the loop. In the bigger picture, fusing memory-bound glue is exactly the FlashAttention insight (§05) applied to a custom kernel: keep the data on-chip, touch HBM as little as possible.

The transferable lesson

Memory-bound kernel → move fewer bytes → fuse to keep intermediates on-chip + coalesce access → re-measure. This is the same arc as your 124× story at whatever its specific bottleneck was, and it generalizes: the profiler localizes, the roofline classifies, fusion/tiling/coalescing fixes the memory-bound case, tensor-cores/precision/shape fixes the compute-bound case, and you always confirm by re-measuring.

Be careful with the arithmetic live (verify the 6ND convention and the peak-FLOP figure for the precision) — getting the method right matters more than a precise number, and acknowledging the caveats is itself a senior signal.

29The transformer FLOP model

To compute MFU you need the model's FLOPs, and the transformer FLOP estimate is a back-of-envelope every performance engineer should have. The canonical approximation:

DENSE TRANSFORMER, training FLOPs ≈ 6 × N × D ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐ │ N = number of parameters │ │ D = number of tokens processed │ │ 6 = 2 (multiply + accumulate per FLOP-pair) │ │ × 3 (forward = 1, backward ≈ 2) │ └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘ per step: FLOPs ≈ 6 × N × (tokens_per_step) This IGNORES attention's quadratic term, fine when seq << model width, but ADD it for long context: attention ≈ 12 × layers × seq² × hidden (per the standard accounting) — material when sequence length is large.

The two pieces and when each matters:

  • The 6ND term dominates for typical configurations — it's the matmul FLOPs of the linear layers (QKV, attention output, MLP). For most training, FLOPs ≈ 6 × params × tokens is accurate enough to compute MFU and reason about scaling.
  • The attention quadratic term (~scales with seq²) matters for long-context workloads (the AV/video/robotics jobs, §11) — at 128k tokens, attention can rival the linear FLOPs, which is also why FlashAttention and context parallelism matter for those workloads.
  • MoE adjustment: for a sparse MoE model, count only the active parameters per token (the experts each token actually hits), not the total — which is why MoE gets more capacity for similar FLOPs (§10).
The estimate that makes you dangerous in a modeling discussion

"FLOPs ≈ 6 × params × tokens" plus "step is ~1/3 forward, 2/3 backward" plus "MFU = achieved/peak" is a complete, composable back-of-envelope: given a model size, token rate, GPU count, and hardware peak, you can estimate the expected step time and MFU in your head — and therefore tell whether an observed number is reasonable or hiding inefficiency. This is the essence of performance modeling, and being able to do it live (with the right caveats about attention and precision) is precisely the quantitative fluency Greg is testing. It also lets you sanity-check researchers' claims: "you're reporting X tokens/s on Y GPUs, which implies Z% MFU — that's either great or suspiciously high, let's check."

30Scaling efficiency & the comm knee

"You add more GPUs and throughput doesn't scale linearly — model why" is the canonical scaling question. The answer is a model of how the compute and communication terms behave as you scale, and where they cross.

Strong vs weak scaling

Strong scalingFixed total problem, more GPUs → less work each. "Make this job finish faster."Communication grows relative to shrinking per-GPU compute → efficiency drops (Amdahl).

The communication knee

Per-step time as a function of GPU count (strong scaling): step time │ │██ per-GPU COMPUTE shrinks as you add GPUs (good) │ ███ │ ████___ │ ‾‾‾▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓ COMMUNICATION grows / stays fixed (bad) │ ↑ THE KNEE: where comm overtakes compute └──────────────────────────────► GPU count below the knee: adding GPUs helps (compute-dominated) above the knee: adding GPUs barely helps (comm-dominated) → wasted GPUs The fix isn't "fewer GPUs" — it's pushing the knee right: overlap comm (§17), bigger per-GPU batch (weak-scale), SHARP, better collective, keep heavy collectives on NVLink (§12).

The model in words: per-step time ≈ compute(N)/GPUs + communication. As you add GPUs, the compute term shrinks but the communication term doesn't (and the collective's latency term may even grow). At some GPU count — the knee — communication dominates and adding GPUs stops helping. Quantitatively: if a step is 80% compute / 20% comm at N GPUs, doubling to 2N halves the compute but the all-reduce may not shrink, so efficiency drops toward the comm floor.

This is the model behind your 400× scaling story, and the answer to Greg's scaling question. When asked "why doesn't 2× the GPUs give 2× throughput," the structured answer is: decompose per-step time into compute (scales down) and communication (doesn't), find the knee where comm overtakes compute, and name the mitigations that push the knee right — overlap comm with compute, increase per-GPU batch (move toward weak scaling), use SHARP / a more bandwidth-optimal collective, and keep the heaviest collectives on the fastest link. If your 400× came partly from improving scaling efficiency, this is the framework to explain how — you pushed the knee right by reducing the exposed communication. That's the performance-modeling-meets-real-experience answer that wins the interview.

Slow training resolves through: profile (Nsight Systems) → is the GPU busy? If busy but slow → compute (Nsight Compute: roofline, precision, GEMM shape, MFU). If gappy → what's in the gap? 39A worked end-to-end case

One scenario that exercises the entire volume — the kind of end-to-end investigation the role is built around, and a complete story to have ready.

end to end "Our 128-GPU pretraining is at 22% MFU and we don't know why"
1 — Model the expectation (§28-29)

56B dense model, known token rate, 128× H100. From FLOPs ≈ 6ND and the hardware BF16 peak, the expected MFU for this config is ~42%. Observed is 22% → roughly half the compute efficiency is missing. That gap is the investigation.

2 — Locate the time (§19-20)

Nsight Systems on a few steps (rank 0 + a couple others). The GPU-compute row has periodic gaps. In the gaps: NCCL all-reduce kernels, and every ~N steps a longer stall aligned to the checkpoint interval. Two suspects: exposed communication and checkpoint stalls.

3 — Classify each (§34, Manual II §31)

Comm: the all-reduce sits after backward, not overlapping → exposed (§17). Run all_reduce_perf → busbw is actually healthy, so it's an overlap problem, not a slow collective. Checkpoint: the periodic stall coincides with finalize-async-save drifting up in the log → checkpoint writes are blocking (storage backpressure, Manual II §31).

4 — Check for a straggler (§35)

Per-rank iteration variance is low and even — no straggler. Good; rules that out. (Had one rank been slow, that'd be the lead.)

5 — Fix, cheapest first, re-measure (§18)

(a) Enable gradient bucketing/overlap so the DP all-reduce hides behind backward (§17) → the comm gaps close, MFU climbs to ~36%. (b) Turn on --async-save --ckpt-fully-parallel-save and stripe the checkpoint dir across more OSTs (Manual II §27) → the periodic stalls vanish, MFU to ~41%. Each change re-measured to confirm it moved the number.

6 — Close the loop (Manual I)

MFU now ~41%, matching the model — the job went from 22% to 41%, nearly doubling effective throughput on the same 128 GPUs. In goodput terms (Manual I), you recovered runtime efficiency (overlap) and reduced checkpoint overhead. That's the whole role in one investigation: model the target, profile to localize, classify across layers (comm + storage), fix cheapest-first, and verify against the model — touching parallelism, NCCL, profiling, performance modeling, and storage.

This is the shape of the answer to "tell me about a time you debugged a performance problem end to end." Notice it never guesses: it models an expectation, measures against it, classifies the gap into specific layers, fixes the cheapest lever first, and re-measures each time. And it's inherently cross-layer — the 22%→41% came from a communication fix and a storage fix, found by profiling. That combination of quantitative modeling, disciplined measurement, and full-stack range is exactly what both Aman ("ML-workload performance end to end") and Greg ("performance modeling + observability") are listening for.
