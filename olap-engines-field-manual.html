How we learned to query everything, fast

A guided walk through forty years of analytical query engines — from the MPP appliances of the 1980s, through Hadoop and the great Spark/Presto schism, to the columnar lakehouse, the C++ vectorized renaissance, and the GPU-native composable stack now taking shape around Velox and cuDF.

§0

OrientationThe lens — what actually varies between data systems

Before tracing the history, it helps to fix the coordinate system, because "database" is one word covering a dozen genuinely different machines. Almost every engine in this manual can be located by where it sits on a handful of axes. The whole evolutionary story is engineers discovering that you cannot be excellent at all positions on all axes simultaneously — so they kept building new systems that traded one virtue for another.

Keep these axes in mind; the rest of the manual is essentially a tour of where each system chose to stand.

The seven axes

AxisOne endOther endWhat it buys
WorkloadOLTP — many tiny point reads/writesOLAP — few huge scans & aggregationsTransactional integrity vs analytical throughput
Storage layoutRow-orientedColumn-orientedFast single-record access vs fast wide scans & compression
TopologySingle nodeDistributed / shared-nothingSimplicity & low latency vs raw scale
Working setDisk-residentIn-memoryCapacity & durability vs speed
DeploymentEmbedded / in-processClient–serverZero-ops & locality vs shared concurrent access
CouplingStorage + compute fusedStorage / compute decoupledPredictable performance vs elasticity & cost control
Optimization targetLatency (interactive)Throughput (batch)Sub-second answers vs cheap completion of huge jobs
Why this matters for your question

Your instinct — "we already had Hadoop, why Presto?" — is really a question about two different axes at once. Hadoop chose throughput, disk-resident, distributed. Presto chose latency, in-memory, distributed. They are different points in the same space, not better/worse versions of one thing. No amount of GPUs or scheduler tuning moves you from one corner to the other; you have to rebuild the execution model. That is the thread running through every section below.

The other lens: the cost hierarchy

The second thing to internalize is the brutal latency gap between where data can live. Every major leap in this story is, at bottom, someone moving the working set up this hierarchy — off spinning disk, into RAM, into CPU cache, into GPU high-bandwidth memory — or eliminating a trip down it (a disk write, a network shuffle, a serialization step).

Every generation of this technology is the same move at a smaller scale: stop touching the slow tier. First we stopped writing to disk between jobs. Then we stopped re-reading from disk at all. Then we stopped wasting CPU cycles per row. Then we stopped leaving the GPU idle.
§1

Prologue · 1979–2005Before Hadoop — the MPP data warehouse

Large-scale analytics did not begin with Hadoop. For roughly three decades, "do analytics on a lot of data" meant buying a Massively Parallel Processing (MPP) data warehouse — often a physical appliance — from a vendor like Teradata. These systems were genuinely parallel, genuinely fast, and they pioneered nearly every idea we now consider modern. They lost the 2000s not on technical merit but on economics and rigidity.

The shared-nothing idea

The foundational architecture, articulated by Michael Stonebraker in the 1980s, is shared-nothing: partition the data across many independent nodes, each owning its slice of disk and memory, and push the computation to the data. A query is decomposed so each node works on its own partition in parallel, then partial results are merged. Teradata (1979) built a commercial empire on exactly this, and every distributed engine since — Hadoop, Spark, Presto, Snowflake — is a descendant of the shared-nothing principle.

The column-store and vectorization breakthroughs

Two academic results from this era are load-bearing for everything later in this manual, so it is worth naming them precisely:

  • C-Store (Stonebraker et al., 2005) — the canonical argument for column-oriented storage in analytics: store each column contiguously, so a scan reads only the columns a query touches, and compresses brilliantly because adjacent values are similar. This paper became the commercial database Vertica.
  • MonetDB/X100 (Boncz, Zukowski, Nes; 2005) — the canonical argument for vectorized execution: instead of pushing one row at a time through the operator tree (the classic "Volcano" model, with a virtual function call per row), push a batch — a vector of ~1000 values — at a time. This amortizes per-call overhead, enables SIMD, and stays in CPU cache. This idea sat dormant in mainstream big-data tools for fifteen years before re-emerging as the core thesis of DuckDB, Velox, Photon, and ClickHouse (§8).
The seed planted here

Hold onto C-Store and MonetDB/X100. The entire §8 "native renaissance" is the database research community quietly waiting for the Hadoop-era to rediscover what they proved in 2005. CWI in Amsterdam — birthplace of MonetDB — is also the birthplace of DuckDB. The lineage is direct.

Why this paradigm hit a wall

  • Column stores & vectorization (a decade early)
  • Low query latency for modeled data
Why it lost the 2000s

  • Schema-on-write: you had to model & clean data before loading it
  • Couldn't ingest the mess: logs, clickstreams, JSON, images had no place
  • Scaling = buying another box from one vendor

Then the web companies arrived with petabytes of unstructured logs, no budget for Teradata at that scale, and a willingness to trade SQL elegance for raw, cheap, fault-tolerant scale. Enter Hadoop.

§2

2003–2013The Hadoop revolution — and its ecosystem

Hadoop did not start as a database at all. It started as an answer to a different question: how does a company with thousands of cheap, failure-prone commodity servers store and process more data than any single machine — or any affordable appliance — could hold? The answer, copied openly from three Google papers, reorganized the entire industry.

The three Google papers

Google, operating at a scale no vendor sold for, built its own stack and then published how:

  • GFS — the Google File System (2003): store enormous files by chopping them into blocks, spreading the blocks across many machines, and replicating each block (typically 3×) so node death is routine, not catastrophic.
  • MapReduce (2004): a programming model for processing those distributed blocks. Express your computation as a map (transform each record) followed by a reduce (aggregate records sharing a key). The framework handles distribution, shuffling, and failure-retry.
  • Bigtable (2006): a distributed, sparse, sorted key-value store on top of GFS — the ancestor of HBase and the wide-column NoSQL family.

Doug Cutting and Mike Cafarella implemented open-source versions; Yahoo invested heavily; the result, named after Cutting's son's toy elephant, became Apache Hadoop: HDFS (the GFS clone) + MapReduce (the compute clone).

Why it was revolutionary

The three unlocks

1 · Commodity hardware. Scale by adding cheap x86 boxes, not by buying a bigger appliance. 2 · Schema-on-read. Dump raw data in any format now; impose structure at query time. The "data lake" was born. 3 · Fault tolerance as a default. Replication in HDFS and automatic task-retry in MapReduce meant a cluster of unreliable machines behaved like one reliable system.

How a MapReduce job actually moves

The ecosystem that grew on top

Raw MapReduce was painful to program, so an ecosystem of higher-level tools accreted around HDFS. This is the "Hadoop ecosystem" people refer to:

YARN, precisely — because it's central to your question

Before YARN, MapReduce was both the resource manager and the compute engine (the old JobTracker/TaskTracker). YARN (Yet Another Resource Negotiator) factored cluster scheduling into a standalone layer — a ResourceManager, per-application ApplicationMasters, and per-node NodeManagers. This is what let Spark, Tez, Presto-on-YARN, and friends share a Hadoop cluster. But — and this is the crux — YARN allocates containers (CPU, memory, and yes, eventually GPUs). It does not change how a query executes inside those containers. It is a landlord, not an architect. We'll cash this in directly in §3.

The role of Hive in particular

Hive deserves emphasis because it is the protagonist of the next plot turn. Facebook built it so the thousands of people who knew SQL but not distributed Java could query the warehouse. It worked — Hive made Hadoop usable. But each Hive query compiled down to a chain of MapReduce jobs (FIG. 2.1), inheriting all of MapReduce's disk-bound latency. A query that felt like it should take three seconds took three minutes. That gap — between SQL's interactive expectations and MapReduce's batch reality — is precisely the vacuum that Presto and Impala rushed to fill.

§3

The pivotThe cracks in MapReduce — why one paradigm split into two

By around 2010, MapReduce's limitations were not subtle, and they pointed in two different directions. Two distinct communities, irritated by two distinct failures, walked off to build two distinct things. Understanding which failure motivated which successor is the key that unlocks the whole rest of the manual.

The four structural problems

  1. Disk materialization between every stage. MapReduce writes intermediate results to disk (and the final output to 3×-replicated HDFS) at every job boundary. A query with five logical steps writes and re-reads the entire dataset from disk roughly five times.
  2. Batch latency. JVM spin-up, container allocation, and scheduling impose tens of seconds to minutes of fixed overhead — fatal for interactive, human-in-the-loop analytics.
  3. A rigid two-verb vocabulary. Everything had to be bent into map + reduce. Multi-way joins, iteration, and graph algorithms were awkward, multi-job contortions.
  4. No reuse of hot data. An iterative algorithm (almost all of machine learning) re-reads its input from disk on every single iteration, because there is no notion of keeping a dataset resident in memory across steps.

Now — your exact question, answered head-on

"We had Hadoop, and YARN can even schedule GPUs. Why did Presto appear?"

Because the bottleneck Presto attacks lives at problems #1 and #2 — the execution model — and YARN/GPUs touch neither.

YARN decides which machine a task runs on and how much CPU/RAM/GPU it gets. It cannot decide that intermediate results should stream through memory instead of landing on disk, or that there should be no per-query JVM cold-start. Those are properties of the engine running inside the container.

Running MapReduce on a GPU would make each map task's arithmetic faster — but a Hive query isn't slow because the arithmetic is slow. It's slow because it writes the whole dataset to disk between stages and pays minutes of job-startup tax. You cannot GPU your way out of an architecture whose cost is dominated by disk I/O and scheduling latency. Presto is not "Hadoop, faster." It is a different execution model: long-lived worker daemons that pipeline data through memory across the cluster with no inter-stage disk writes. That is an architectural change, and it had to be a new system.

A scheduler decides where work runs. An execution engine decides how work runs. MapReduce's pain was a how problem, so the cure had to be a new how — not a better where.

The fork in the road

Crucially, the four problems above don't all matter for the same use case, so two cures emerged — and they optimize opposite ends of the latency/throughput axis from §0:

Cure A → Spark (§4)
  • Motivated by #1 + #3 + #4
  • "Keep data in memory across many operations; give us a rich API and iteration"
  • Target: large multi-stage ETL & iterative ML where fault tolerance still matters
  • General-purpose compute engine
Cure B → Presto / Impala (§5)
  • Motivated by #1 + #2
  • "Give us sub-second interactive SQL; we'll sacrifice mid-query fault tolerance to get it"
  • Target: ad-hoc analyst queries over the warehouse
  • Specialized MPP SQL engine

Both ran on the same Hadoop foundation (HDFS for storage, often YARN for scheduling). Both replaced MapReduce as the thing doing the actual work. They are siblings, born of the same frustration, who chose opposite corners of the design space. The next two sections take each in turn.

§4

Divergence A · 2009–presentSpark — general-purpose in-memory compute

Apache Spark came out of UC Berkeley's AMPLab, led by Matei Zaharia, and its central abstraction is one of the most influential ideas in the field. Spark's pitch was not "faster SQL." It was "a unified engine for all large-scale data processing — batch, SQL, streaming, ML, and graph — that keeps data in memory and recovers from failure without replicating intermediate data."

The RDD — the load-bearing idea

The 2012 paper introduced the Resilient Distributed Dataset (RDD): an immutable, partitioned collection of records held across the cluster's memory. Two properties make it work:

  • Lineage-based fault tolerance. Rather than replicating intermediate data to survive failures (MapReduce's expensive approach), an RDD remembers how it was computed — the chain of transformations from its inputs. If a partition is lost to a node failure, Spark simply recomputes that one partition from its lineage. You get fault tolerance without the disk-write tax.
  • Lazy DAG execution. Transformations (map, filter, join) build up a directed acyclic graph but don't execute. Only when an action (count, collect, save) is called does Spark plan the whole DAG at once — pipelining operations, fusing stages, and materializing only where a shuffle genuinely requires it.

The unified platform

Spark's commercial and adoption advantage was breadth: one engine, one API, many workloads.

Spark Core — RDDs / batch Spark SQL — DataFrames Structured Streaming MLlib — machine learning GraphX — graphs

Spark SQL, Catalyst, and Tungsten

The DataFrame API (and Spark SQL) moved users up from hand-written RDD code to a declarative, relational interface — which, crucially, gave Spark room to optimize. Two subsystems made it competitive with purpose-built databases:

  • Catalyst — an extensible query optimizer (rule-based rewrites plus cost-based planning) that turns a DataFrame/SQL query into an efficient physical plan.
  • Tungsten — Spark's answer to the JVM's inefficiency. It introduced whole-stage code generation (compile a chain of operators into a single tight Java loop instead of chained iterators), off-heap memory management to dodge garbage-collection pauses, and cache-aware layouts. This was Spark reaching toward the bare-metal efficiency that §8 pushes much further.
The same group-by, three ways
# 1. Raw MapReduce (Java) — dozens of lines of Mapper/Reducer boilerplate, omitted.

# 2. Spark — RDD style (functional, in-memory, lineage-tracked)
(sc.textFile("events")
   .map(lambda line: (line.split(",")[0], 1))
   .reduceByKey(lambda a, b: a + b)   # the one shuffle

# 3. Spark SQL — declarative; Catalyst plans it, Tungsten codegens it

Where Spark sits — and its competitors

Spark fit into the Hadoop world (read HDFS, schedule on YARN) while displacing MapReduce as the engine. Today it runs equally on Kubernetes, Mesos, or standalone, reading from S3 far more often than HDFS. Its sweet spot is large, fault-tolerant, multi-stage ETL and ML pipelines — jobs that run for minutes to hours, where mid-job node failure is a real risk worth paying for.

Foreshadowing §8

Tungsten took Spark a long way, but it still executes on the JVM. The next great efficiency leap — Photon (Databricks) and Gluten+Velox — is about replacing Spark's JVM execution with native, vectorized C++ underneath the same DataFrame API. Spark keeps the optimizer and the API; the engine room gets rebuilt in C++. Hold that thought.

§5

Divergence B · 2012–presentPresto & the interactive MPP-SQL family

Presto was born at Facebook in 2012 (open-sourced 2013) to solve the exact pain from §2: analysts needed interactive SQL over a petabyte-scale warehouse, and Hive-on-MapReduce delivered answers in minutes. Presto's design is the mirror image of Spark's — where Spark generalized, Presto specialized, and where Spark kept fault tolerance, Presto threw it overboard to win latency.

The architecture: pipelined, in-memory MPP

Presto is a pure query engine — it stores nothing. A cluster is one coordinator (parses SQL, plans, schedules) and many long-lived workers (execute). A query is decomposed into stages, stages into parallel tasks, tasks into splits (chunks of input), processed by a pipeline of operators. The defining property: data streams from stage to stage through memory and over the network — it is never written to disk between stages, and there is no per-query JVM cold-start because the workers are always running.

The two defining design choices

1 · No mid-query fault tolerance (originally)

This is the trade that makes Presto fast, and it is a deliberate inversion of Spark's choice. If a worker dies mid-query, the query fails and you re-run it. For a 5-second interactive query, re-running is cheaper than the constant checkpointing and lineage machinery that fault tolerance demands. Spark made the opposite bet because its jobs run for hours. Same axis, opposite end. (Presto later added optional fault-tolerant execution — "Project Tardigrade" — for longer ETL-style queries, narrowing the gap.)

2 · Connectors & federation

Presto separates the query engine from storage entirely and talks to dozens of systems through a pluggable connector SPI. The same ANSI-SQL query can span a data lake, an operational database, and a message queue. Hive could only see HDFS; Presto sees everything.

Presto / Trino — strengths
  • Sub-second to seconds interactive SQL
  • Full ANSI SQL (not HiveQL)
  • Federation across many sources in one query
  • Decoupled from storage — query the lake in place
Presto / Trino — costs
  • Historically fragile for hours-long jobs (a node death kills the query)
  • Memory-bound — a query whose intermediate state exceeds cluster RAM struggles
  • Not a system of record; no storage, no transactions of its own

The Presto fork: PrestoDB vs Trino

A fact that confuses everyone — worth getting straight

In 2018–19 the original creators (Martin Traverso, Dain Sundstrom, David Phillips, Eric Hwang) left Facebook and forked the project as PrestoSQL, renamed Trino in late 2020 over trademark issues. Facebook kept the name PrestoDB under the Linux Foundation's Presto Foundation. So today "Presto" is two projects. Trino tends to carry the more active community momentum; PrestoDB is the lineage Meta continues to invest in — and notably the one being rebuilt in C++ as Prestissimo on top of Velox (§8–§9). AWS Athena began life as a hosted Presto.

The wider interactive-SQL family (the competitors)

Presto was not alone; several engines attacked the "interactive SQL on Hadoop" gap at once, each with a different bet:

EngineOriginDistinct bet

Apache Drill2012 · MapRSchema-free SQL — query raw JSON/Parquet with no pre-defined schema. Inspired by Google's Dremel.

Spark and Presto are not rivals on the same axis. Asking "Spark or Presto?" is like asking "freight train or sports car?" One moves enormous loads reliably over long distances; the other gets a person somewhere fast. Most mature data platforms run both — Spark for the heavy ETL that builds the tables, Presto/Trino for the analysts who interactively query them.
§6

The substrate · 2013–2016The storage & format layer — the quiet revolution

The engines get the headlines, but a parallel revolution in how bytes are laid out did as much for performance as any execution-engine change. Once you decouple compute from storage (§5, §7), the format that data sits in becomes a shared contract — and getting it right speeds up every engine that reads it. This section is short but disproportionately important.

Row vs columnar — the central choice, revisited

An analytical query like SELECT AVG(price) FROM sales WHERE region='EU' touches two columns out of perhaps fifty. In a row format, the disk must read every row in full to reach those two fields. In a columnar format, each column is stored contiguously, so the engine reads only price and region — and because a column holds values of one type with high similarity, it compresses far better and enables vectorized, SIMD-friendly processing.

The formats that won

FormatKindRole & origin
AvroRow, on-diskSchema-carrying row format; good for streaming/ingest & record-at-a-time writes
ParquetColumnar, on-diskFrom Twitter+Cloudera, inspired by Dremel. The de-facto columnar lake format — compression, predicate & projection pushdown. Read by everything.
ORCColumnar, on-diskThe Hive/Hortonworks columnar format; similar goals to Parquet, strong in the Hive world
Apache ArrowColumnar, in-memoryNot a file format — a standard in-memory representation (2016, Wes McKinney et al.) for zero-copy data interchange between systems and languages

Arrow — the most important entry in this section

Why Arrow is the connective tissue of the modern stack

Before Arrow, every time data crossed a boundary — Spark→Python, pandas→a database, CPU→GPU — it was serialized into one system's internal format and deserialized into another's. That copy-and-convert tax was enormous. Apache Arrow defines one columnar memory layout that everyone agrees on, so a buffer produced by one tool can be read by another with zero copying. pandas, Spark, DuckDB, Polars, cuDF, DataFusion, and Velox all speak Arrow. It is the reason the §9 GPU story and the §10 composable story are even possible: cuDF's GPU buffers are Arrow-compatible, so handing data from a CPU engine to a GPU kernel doesn't require reformatting it. Arrow Flight extends the same idea to network transport.

Execution engines compete; formats federate. The winners of the format wars (Parquet on disk, Arrow in memory) succeeded precisely by being neutral ground that no single engine owns — which is exactly what let the engines above them specialize and interoperate.
§7

2012–presentCloud, decoupling & the lakehouse

The cloud took the §5 idea of "separate compute from storage" and pushed it to its logical extreme. When storage is a cheap, infinite, durable object store (S3, GCS, Azure Blob) that any number of compute clusters can read independently, the economics of analytics change fundamentally — and a new class of system, the lakehouse, emerges to reconcile the data lake's flexibility with the warehouse's rigor.

The decoupling and what it bought

In the appliance era (§1), compute and storage were welded together: to store more, you bought more compute, and vice versa. In the cloud, you store petabytes in S3 for pennies and spin up compute only when a query runs, sizing it independently. This is the architecture behind every modern cloud warehouse.

Data lake (raw files in object storage)
  • Cheap, infinite, any format
  • Schema-on-read flexibility
  • Open — many engines read it
  • But: no transactions, no schema enforcement, easy to turn into a "data swamp"
Data warehouse (managed, structured)
  • ACID, schema enforcement, governance
  • Fast, optimized, reliable
  • Curated & trustworthy
  • But: expensive, proprietary, rigid ingest

The lakehouse: getting both

The lakehouse keeps data as open columnar files (Parquet) in cheap object storage, but layers a table format on top that adds the warehouse virtues — ACID transactions, schema evolution, time-travel (querying a past snapshot), and efficient upserts/deletes — via a transaction log and metadata. Three table formats compete:

Table formatOriginEmphasis
Apache HudiUber · 2016Fast upserts & incremental processing — born from CDC / streaming-update needs
Apache IcebergNetflix · 2017Open table spec with hidden partitioning, robust schema evolution, snapshot isolation. The current momentum leader; engine-neutral by design.
Delta LakeDatabricks · 2019ACID transaction log over Parquet; tightly integrated with Spark/Databricks

The cloud warehouses & platforms

Google BigQuery2011Serverless & Dremel-based — no clusters to manage; query, pay per byte scanned

Databricks SQL + Photon2020+The lakehouse pitch: warehouse performance directly on lake data, via a native C++ engine (§8)
Where Presto/Trino fit in this picture

Trino/Presto are a natural query layer for the open lakehouse: point them at Iceberg or Delta tables in S3 and you get interactive SQL over the lake without loading into a proprietary warehouse. The competition here is philosophical — the open lakehouse (Iceberg + Trino + your choice of engine, no lock-in) versus the integrated platform (Snowflake or Databricks, more convenient, more locked in). Both are thriving; the tension between them drives much of today's roadmap.

§8

2016–presentThe native & vectorized renaissance — escaping the JVM

By the late 2010s, the distributed problem was largely solved — Spark and Presto scaled fine. The new frontier was single-node efficiency: the realization that JVM-based engines were leaving a 5–30× performance factor on the table by ignoring how modern CPUs actually work. The cure was to rediscover the 2005 database research from §1 — vectorization and compilation — and rebuild execution engines in native C++/Rust. This is the direct lineage of Velox.

The problem: the Volcano model meets the modern CPU

The classic query execution model (the "Volcano" or iterator model) processes one tuple at a time: each operator's next() method pulls a single row from its child, via a virtual function call. On a 1980s CPU this was fine. On a modern CPU it is a disaster:

  • Virtual call overhead per row, per operator, dwarfs the actual work for simple operations.
  • No SIMD. Modern CPUs can apply one instruction to 8–16 values at once (AVX-512), but one-tuple-at-a-time can't feed those lanes.
  • Cache & branch-prediction hostility. Jumping between operators per row thrashes the instruction cache and defeats the branch predictor.
  • On the JVM, additionally: garbage-collection pauses, object header overhead, and pointer-chasing through boxed objects.

The two cures (a genuine rivalry of philosophies)

Velox, in focus — the engine as a library

Velox's thesis (the VLDB 2022 paper, "Velox: Meta's Unified Execution Engine") is that every data system was reimplementing the same execution machinery — vectorized operators, expression evaluation, type systems, memory arenas — slightly differently and to slightly different quality. Velox factors that machinery into one reusable, heavily optimized C++ library that many front ends can adopt. It operates on Arrow-compatible columnar vectors ("Velox Vectors"). Consumers include:

Prestissimo — Presto C++ workers on Velox Gluten — Spark offloaded to Velox PyTorch data preprocessing Meta's internal engines

Gluten — the bridge that lets Spark keep its API but swap its engine

This pays off the §4 foreshadowing. Gluten (Intel and collaborators) is a Spark plugin that intercepts Spark's physical plan and, instead of executing it on the JVM, hands it to a native backend — most prominently Velox (also ClickHouse). You keep Spark's DataFrame API, Catalyst optimizer, and ecosystem; the actual row-crunching happens in native vectorized C++. It uses Substrait (§10) as the neutral plan format passed across the JVM→native boundary.

Why DuckDB "thrived" despite being single-node — answering part of your question

DuckDB rode a deflating myth: "big data" is usually not that big. The overwhelming majority of real analytical datasets fit comfortably on one modern machine with hundreds of GB of RAM and fast NVMe — and standing up a Spark cluster to crunch 20 GB is grotesque over-engineering. DuckDB is in-process (it runs inside your Python/R/Node process, like SQLite), needs zero servers, reads Parquet/Arrow at native speed, and integrates seamlessly with pandas. It is the data world's correction to a decade of distributed over-reach: for the single-node case, an embedded vectorized engine beats a cluster on both speed and operational simplicity. That is its entire justification — and it is a very strong one.

The native renaissance is the field finally cashing the cheque written in 2005. C-Store and MonetDB/X100 proved columnar + vectorized execution; the big-data era was a fifteen-year detour through the JVM and distribution; Velox, DuckDB, and Photon are the return to those principles, now armed with Arrow and modern hardware.
§9

2018–presentThe GPU frontier — RAPIDS, cuDF, and GPU-native execution

If §8 was about using the CPU fully, §9 is about leaving it behind for workloads where the GPU's brute-force memory bandwidth and parallelism dominate. Analytical queries — wide columnar scans, filters, hash joins, group-bys — are often bandwidth-bound and embarrassingly parallel, which is exactly what GPUs are built for. This is the present cutting edge, and the place where Velox, cuDF, and high-performance GPU interconnect converge.

Why a GPU at all for SQL?

Two hardware facts (recall FIG. 0.1): a modern data-center GPU offers 1–3 TB/s of memory bandwidth (HBM) versus a CPU's ~100–400 GB/s of DRAM bandwidth, and thousands of arithmetic units versus a CPU's dozens. A columnar scan-and-aggregate is mostly "stream a lot of bytes through simple arithmetic in parallel" — a near-perfect match. The catch is the PCIe bottleneck getting data onto the GPU, and the relatively small GPU memory; both shape the architecture below.

The hard part: getting data between GPUs

Once execution is on the GPU, the bottleneck migrates to the network. A distributed join or aggregation needs a shuffle/exchange — repartitioning data across nodes by key. If every GPU buffer has to travel GPU→CPU→NIC→network→NIC→CPU→GPU, the staging copies dominate and the GPU's speed is wasted. This is precisely the problem your UCX-based exchange/shuffle work addresses.

The GPU paradigm's honest trade-offs

When GPU analytics wins
  • Scan/filter/aggregate/join-heavy queries that fit (or stream through) GPU memory
  • Bandwidth-bound work — the TB/s HBM advantage is decisive
  • Pipelines already on-GPU (ML feature prep feeding training)
When it doesn't
  • Small data — PCIe transfer & kernel-launch overhead swamp the gain
  • Working set ≫ GPU memory — forces host spilling (a problem you work on directly)
  • Highly branchy, irregular control flow — GPUs hate divergence
  • Cost/availability of GPUs vs commodity CPU
§10

2020–presentThe composable-systems movement — stop rebuilding the same database

Step back and look at everything from §4 onward. Spark, Presto, Trino, Impala, ClickHouse, DuckDB, Snowflake, BigQuery — each is a complete, monolithic data system, and each independently re-implemented the same four things: a language frontend, a query optimizer, an execution engine, and a set of connectors to storage. The industry built the same wheel a dozen times. The composable movement is the recognition that this is wasteful, and that those layers should be reusable components with clean interfaces rather than welded-shut internals.

This was crystallized in “The Composable Data Management System Manifesto” (Pedreira et al., VLDB 2023) — a paper out of Meta and collaborators arguing that the next generation of data systems should be assembled from interchangeable open components, the way the compiler world standardized on LLVM IR so that any language frontend could target any hardware backend. The economic argument is simple and brutal: a feature built once in a shared component benefits every system that adopts it, instead of being re-implemented N times at N× the cost and N× the bugs.

The stack, decomposed

The monolith splits into five horizontal layers. The interfaces between the layers are where the standards live — and standards are what make the pieces swappable.

Substrait — the keystone standard

If Arrow standardized how data looks in memory, Substrait standardizes how a query plan looks on the wire. A Spark frontend can emit a Substrait plan that a Velox or DataFusion backend executes, with no shared code between them. This is exactly the decoupling that lets Gluten offload Spark SQL onto Velox: Spark plans the query, serializes it to Substrait, and Velox runs it natively in C++ — the JVM never touches the hot loop. Without a plan IR, every frontend↔backend pairing would need a bespoke translator.

Why this is the through-line of the whole story

Every chapter of this manual has been a step toward separation of concerns. MPP warehouses fused everything (§1). Hadoop split storage from compute scheduling (§2). The lakehouse split storage from the query engine entirely (§7). The native renaissance split the execution engine out as a reusable library (§8). GPUs split where execution physically runs from the engine that orchestrates it (§9). Composability is the name for the destination all of those steps were heading toward: a stack where each layer is best-of-breed, independently improvable, and shared across systems.

Where your work sits. You are squarely in layer ④ and the substrate just beneath it. Velox is the reusable execution engine; cuDF is the GPU kernel library that fills the same slot on the GPU; and UCX-based GPU-to-GPU exchange is the data-movement layer the shuffle operator stands on. When you make Velox’s exchange move data over GPUDirect RDMA instead of staging through host memory, every consumer of Velox — Prestissimo, Gluten-on-Spark, any future frontend — inherits faster distributed joins for free. That compounding leverage is the entire point of building in the composable layer rather than inside one monolith.

§11

the parallel universeThe specialized-database zoo — why a hundred databases all thrived

The engines in §1–§10 all answer one question well: “run a big analytical query over a lot of data.” But that is only one corner of a much larger space. Your intuition — that DuckDB, Redis, Postgres, Spark SQL, and the rest each thrived despite being wildly different — is exactly right, and the reason is that they don’t actually compete. They occupy different coordinates on a surface defined by access pattern, data model, latency target, and consistency. “Best database” is a category error; there is only “best fit for this workload.”

The two forces that shattered the monolith

For decades the relational database was assumed to be the answer to everything. Two pressures broke that assumption:

1 · The access-pattern axis. A system tuned for millions of tiny point reads and writes (OLTP — “charge this card, update this row”) is architecturally opposite to one tuned for sweeping aggregate scans (OLAP — “average revenue by region over three years”). Row stores win the first; column stores win the second. No single physical layout is optimal for both, which is why the field bifurcated and never rejoined.

2 · The CAP / scale axis. Google’s and Amazon’s papers (Bigtable 2006, Dynamo 2007) showed that to scale writes across many machines and survive network partitions, you must relax the strong consistency a classic SQL database guarantees. The CAP theorem made the trade explicit: under a partition you choose consistency or availability, not both. The entire “NoSQL” explosion — key-value, wide-column, document stores — is the design space you reach once you’re willing to give up some consistency for availability and horizontal scale. The industry term for the resulting reality is polyglot persistence: a single product uses Postgres for orders, Redis for sessions, Elasticsearch for search, and ClickHouse for analytics, each chosen on purpose.

The DuckDB lesson, restated

DuckDB is the most instructive entry in this table because it is a quiet rebuttal to the entire distributed-systems arc. After a decade of “scale out to a cluster,” it pointed out that a modern laptop has dozens of cores and tens of gigabytes of RAM, that most analytical datasets fit comfortably in that envelope, and that a cluster you don’t need is pure cost and operational pain. By packaging MonetDB-lineage vectorized execution (§8) into a single-file, in-process library that reads Parquet and Arrow with zero setup, it made “just run it locally” the right answer for a huge fraction of real workloads. The right scale is the smallest one that fits.

Redis vs the warehouses — different axis entirely

It’s tempting to ask “is Redis faster than ClickHouse?” but the question is malformed. Redis answers “give me the value for this one key, right now” in microseconds and keeps everything in RAM; ClickHouse answers “scan a billion rows and aggregate” in milliseconds. One is a point-lookup latency tool, the other a throughput scan engine. A real system uses both — Redis as the speed layer caching results, ClickHouse as the analytical store behind it. That is polyglot persistence in one sentence.

The map, not the ranking. Every system here is a point on a surface with axes of access pattern (point lookup ↔ full scan), data model (relational ↔ document ↔ KV ↔ graph ↔ vector), consistency (strong ↔ eventual), and latency vs throughput. New databases keep appearing not because the old ones are bad, but because new regions of that surface become valuable — the vector-DB explosion happened the instant LLM embeddings made approximate nearest-neighbor search a mainstream need. When you evaluate a store, locate the workload on the surface first; the shortlist falls out almost mechanically.

§12

putting it togetherSynthesis — the decision lens and the grand timeline

Everything in this manual reduces to a single recurring move: each generation found a slow tier and stopped touching it. MapReduce stopped re-reading from a single machine’s disk by spreading work across a cluster — then paid for it by materializing to disk between every stage. Spark stopped touching disk by keeping working sets in RAM. Presto stopped touching disk-based batch latency by streaming through memory pipelines. The lakehouse stopped touching coupled storage so compute could scale alone. Velox stopped touching the JVM. cuDF stopped touching the CPU. UCX stopped touching host memory on the shuffle path. The arc is one long campaign to keep the working data as close to the fastest tier as possible.

The decision lens — six questions that pick your engine

Faced with a real workload, you rarely need to know all of database history. You need six answers:

AskIf one end…If the other end…
1 · Transactions or analytics?OLTP point reads/writes → Postgres, MySQL, SQLiteOLAP scans/aggregates → the column-store world
2 · How big is the data really?Fits one node (≲ a few TB) → DuckDB, ClickHouse, PolarsGenuinely huge → Spark, Trino, Snowflake, BigQuery
3 · Latency target?Interactive (sub-sec) → Presto/Trino, ClickHouse, DruidBatch / ETL (minutes ok) → Spark, MapReduce-era jobs
4 · Embedded or service?In-process, no ops → DuckDB, SQLiteShared cluster/service → everything server-based
5 · Open formats or platform?Open lakehouse → Iceberg/Delta + Trino/SparkIntegrated → Snowflake, Databricks, BigQuery
6 · Bandwidth-bound & fits GPU?Yes → cuDF / RAPIDS, Velox+cuDF, HeavyDBNo / branchy / tiny → stay on CPU engines

Answer those and the shortlist is almost always two or three names. The rest is benchmarking on your data — which, as §11 argued, is the only ranking that means anything.
