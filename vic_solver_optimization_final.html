Part I

The optimization

How a profiler reading of 8% occupancy became a three-kernel pipeline — by classifying the work inside one bloated kernel and launching each piece at the granularity it actually wants.

§00Synopsis & results

The Jupiter CRM advances acoustic & gravity waves with a vertically-implicit correction (vic): every vertical column solves a block-tridiagonal linear system. The original GPU kernel gave one thread an entire column — it assembled the system, ran the serial Thomas sweep, and did the constituent redistribution, all in one launch. That packed three different parallel structures into one thread, starving the GPU (8.3% occupancy) and spilling registers (128/thread).

The fix was kernel fission: split the monolith into three kernels, each launched at its natural granularity — two embarrassingly-parallel maps (assembly, redistribution) over every cell, and one irreducible serial scan (the Thomas recurrence) kept one-thread-per-column.

vic total per call—1.40 ms (≈2.3×)

A kernel should do one kind of parallel work. When a single kernel mixes a map, a reduction, and a serial recurrence, split it: launch each piece at the granularity that lets the GPU fill — and leave the irreducibly serial core alone rather than over-parallelizing it (see §07).

§01The science: a vertically-implicit fluid correction

Atmospheric flows carry fast vertical acoustic and gravity waves. An explicit time step would have to resolve their tiny CFL timescale — crippling. The standard remedy is semi-implicit: treat the fast vertical terms implicitly so the step is governed by the slow (advective) timescale instead. In this code that correction is the VIC solve: along each vertical column it inverts the linearized fast-wave operator.

The block-tridiagonal system & the Thomas algorithm

Discretizing the implicit vertical operator couples each cell only to its two vertical neighbors. Stacking a column's unknowns gives a block-tridiagonal system, where each block is a small dense matrix coupling the conserved quantities at one cell:

⎡ a₀ c₀ ⎤ ⎡Δ₀⎤ ⎡r₀⎤ ⎢ b₁ a₁ c₁ ⎣ ⎢Δ₁⎣ ⎢r₁⎣ ⎢ b₂ a₂ c₂ ⎣ ⎢Δ₂⎣ = ⎢r₂⎣ aᵢ,bᵢ,cᵢ : small dense blocks ⎢ ⋱ ⋱ ⋱ ⎣ ⎢ ⋮⎣ ⎢ ⋮⎣ Δᵢ : the correction at cell i ⎣ bₙ aₙ⎦ ⎣Δₙ⎦ ⎣rₙ⎦
Science note — where the blocks come from

The off-diagonal blocks are Roe-flux Jacobians at the cell interfaces. The Roe solver writes the upwind dissipation as |A| = R·|Λ|·R⁻¹ — eigen-decompose the flux Jacobian, take absolute eigenvalues, recompose. The diagonal block aᵢ bundles the two interface contributions, the timestep inertia I/dt, and a gravity coupling Φ. For the “partial” solve used by Jupiter the block is 3×3 — density, the vertical momentum component, and pressure.

The system is solved by the Thomas algorithm (block-LU for tridiagonals): a forward sweep that eliminates the sub-diagonal, then a backward substitution. Crucially, each step of the sweep depends on the previous one:

forward : aᵢ ← aᵢ − bᵢ·aᵢ₋₁⁻¹·cᵢ₋₁ , Δᵢ ← aᵢ⁻¹(rᵢ − bᵢ·Δᵢ₋₁) (i = 0…N, in order) backward: Δᵢ ← Δᵢ − aᵢ·Δᵢ₊₁ (i = N…0, in order)
The structural fact that drives everything

The Thomas sweep is a scan — an inherently serial recurrence over the ~100 layers of a column. You cannot parallelize within a column for free. But the columns themselves (10,000 of them at 100³) are completely independent. That independence is the parallelism to exploit.

The 2½ jobs hiding in one solve

The original vic_solve_partial did three things in one kernel, with very different parallel structure:

JobWhat it doesParallel structure
1 · AssembleRoe eigendecomposition at every interface → build aᵢ,bᵢ,cᵢmap — each cell independent
2 · Solveforward sweep + backward substitution (Thomas)scan — serial recurrence over layers
½ · RedistributeMS-VIC constituent fix-up (a reduce + a per-cell map)reduce + map
Science note — the “½”: MS-VIC redistribution

A later physics change (MS-VIC) added a step that conserves column-integrated constituent tendencies: after the solve, it computes column reduction sums (the “structure functions”) and then redistributes each species' tendency through a smooth, bounded map capped_exp(x) = 1 + (2/π)·atan(πx/2). This is a reduce (whole-column sums) followed by a per-cell map — two more distinct parallel structures bolted onto the serial solve.

§02Finding the bottleneck

Nsight Systems ranked vic_solve_partial as the heaviest GPU-compute kernel in the run — ~14.5% of all kernel time, ~3.4 ms per call. Nsight Compute then explained why a compute kernel was so slow: it wasn't compute-bound at all.

NCU metric (before)valuereading
Achieved occupancy8.3%almost no warps resident to hide latency
SM throughput2.8%the cores sit idle
Memory throughput4.2%not memory-bound either
Registers / thread128register-limited to ~4 blocks/SM
Grid size (profiling cfg)8 blockson a 170-SM GPU
Diagnosis: latency-bound, doubly starved

Low SM and low memory throughput with low occupancy is the fingerprint of a latency-bound kernel: each thread stalls waiting on its own long dependency chain, and there aren't enough other warps to swap in. Two independent causes: too few threads, and too many registers per thread.

§03Why it was slow: occupancy & latency hiding

A GPU hides memory and pipeline latency by oversubscription — keeping many warps resident so that while one stalls, another runs. Two things must be true: enough threads to launch, and few enough resources per thread that many can be co-resident. The monolithic kernel failed both.

Starvation #1 — too few threads (grid starvation)

One thread per column means the grid is nx2 × nx3 threads. On the profiling configuration that's only ~1,024 threads = 8 blocks of 128 — on a card with 170 SMs that can hold tens of thousands of warps. Most of the machine is dark.

170 SMs — one-thread-per-column lights only a sliver: ████████░░░░░░░░░░░░░░░░░░░░░░░░░░░░░░░░░░░░░░░░░░░░░░ ↑ 8 blocks lit ↑ ~162 idle SMs after fission, the map kernels launch nx1·nx2·nx3 threads → all 170 SMs busy

Starvation #2 — register pressure

Even the few resident threads couldn't multiply, because each carried an enormous live set. The assembly holds, simultaneously, several 5×5 Eigen matrices for the Roe eigendecomposition — Rmat, Rimat, Am, Ap, dfdqf — plus the eigenvalue vector and a clutch of 3×3 blocks. That is ~150 live doubles, which the compiler spends 128 registers/thread trying to hold (and still spills). With a hard cap of 65,536 registers per SM, 128 reg/thread limits residency to ~4 blocks/SM — independent of how many threads you launch.

// vic_assemble_partial_impl.h — the register-heavy live set
Eigen::Matrix<T,5,5> Rmat, Rimat, Am, Ap, dfdqf;   // 5 × 25 doubles
Eigen::Matrix<T,5,1> Lambda;                       // eigenvalues |Λ|
Eigen::Matrix<T,3,3> Am2, Ap2, dfdq_prev, dfdq_curr, dfdq_next;
// … Roe average → |A| = Rmat · Λ.asDiagonal() · Rimat at each interface …
Am.noalias() = Rmat * Lambda.asDiagonal() * Rimat;
A failed-but-instructive first attempt

Phase 1 tried to relieve this by trimming dead temporaries and folding constants — and it was bit-exact and clean. But it bought ~0 speed-up: registers stayed at 128, occupancy at 8.3%. Lesson: shaving a few doubles doesn't help unless you cross an occupancy threshold. The six 5×5 matrices dominate; you can't trim your way under the cliff. The structure had to change, not the bookkeeping.

§04The idea: parallelism-aware kernel fission

The monolith forced the serial structure of the Thomas sweep (one thread per column) onto the map structure of assembly and redistribution (which want one thread per cell). The two are incompatible: you can't have both 10,000 threads and 1,000,000 threads in one launch. Fission resolves the conflict — split into three kernels, each launched at the granularity its work wants:

BEFORE one kernel, one thread per column (10⁴ threads), does everything ┌───────────────────────────────────┐ │ assemble + Thomas scan + redistribute (128 regs, 8% occ) │ └───────────────────────────────────┘ AFTER three kernels, each at its natural granularity ┌─ ① assemble ─┐ ┌─ ② solve ───┐ ┌─ ③ redistribute ┐ │ MAP ─▸ │ SCAN ─▸ │ MAP │ │ 10⁶ threads │ │ 10⁴ threads│ │ 10⁶ threads │ │ 79% SM │ │ serial │ │ 73% SM │ └───────────┘ └─────────┘ └────────────┘ the heavy 5×5 Roe matrices now live only in ① (cell-parallel, tolerates the registers)
Why fission cures both starvations at once

Grid starvation: the map kernels now launch nx1·nx2·nx3 ≈ 10⁶ threads, filling all 170 SMs. Register pressure: the 128-register Roe assembly is isolated in the cell-parallel kernel, where high register use is fine because each thread does one cell's work and exits — there's enough independent work to hide its latency. The serial solve, now stripped of assembly, holds far fewer registers.

§05Implementation, in coding detail

The split is realized as three dispatched kernels, wired through the same DECLARE_DISPATCH pattern as the rest of the implicit module. The CPU path stays fully fused (assembly = no-op, solve = the original combined routine), so the CPU is bit-identical and risk-free; only CUDA splits.

Assemble — the per-cell map

The assembly loop becomes a device function over a single cell i; the CUDA launcher fires ncol × nlayer threads. Each thread recomputes its two interface Jacobians (a small amount of duplicated work — the interface is shared with the neighbor — in exchange for perfect cell independence) and writes a[i], b[i], c[i] into the column's global coefficient arrays.

// vic_assemble_partial_impl.h — the heart of the assembly
// interface i−½ : Roe average of cells (i−1, i)  →  |A−| = R·|Λ|·R⁻¹
RoeAverage(prim, gm1, wl, wr);
cs = SoundSpeed(prim, gm1);
Eigenvalue(Lambda, prim[IVX + dir], cs);
Eigenvector(Rmat, Rimat, prim, cs, gm1, dir);
Am.noalias() = Rmat * Lambda.asDiagonal() * Rimat;   // upwind dissipation
// … same for interface i+½ → Ap …

T half_inv_vol = 0.5 / VOL(i);
a[i] = (Am2*area_i + Ap2*area_ip1 + (area_ip1-area_i)*dfdq_curr) * half_inv_vol - Phi;
a[i].diagonal() += Dt;                              // + I/dt
b[i] = -(Am2 + dfdq_prev) * area_i   * half_inv_vol;     // sub-diagonal block
c[i] = -(Ap2 - dfdq_next) * area_ip1 * half_inv_vol;     // super-diagonal block
if (i == is && first_block) a[i] += b[i] * Bnd.asDiagonal();  // fold BC into a₀
if (i == ie && last_block ) a[i] += c[i] * Bnd.asDiagonal();
// implicit_dispatch.cu — the cell-parallel launch
int64_t total = ncol * (int64_t)nlayer;
at::native::launch_legacy_kernel<128, 1>(total, [=] __device__(int idx) {
  int col = idx / nlayer, i = idx % nlayer;        // one thread = one cell
  auto offsets = offset_calc.get(col);            // per-column base pointers
  /* reinterpret a,b,c,w,gamma,area,vol … */
  vic_assemble_partial_impl(a, b, c, w, gamma, area, vol, i, 0, nlayer-1,
                            dt, grav, dir, ny, stride1, stride2, first_block, last_block);
});

Solve — the irreducible serial scan

With a,b,c pre-assembled, the solve kernel is now only the Thomas sweep, one thread per column (gpu_kernel<9>). For the 3×3 partial block it inverts in place; the recurrence is unavoidably ordered over layers:

// forward_backward_impl.h — ForwardSweep (partial, N=3)
a[il] = a[il].inverse().eval();          // first layer
delta[il] = a[il] * rhs;
a[il] = a[il] * c[il];
for (int i = il + 1; i <= iu; ++i) {        // SERIAL recurrence over layers
  a[i] -= b[i] * a[i - 1];                  // each step needs the previous
  a[i] = a[i].inverse().eval();
  delta[i] = a[i] * (rhs - b[i] * delta[i - 1]);
  a[i] = a[i] * c[i];
}

Redistribute — the second per-cell map

The MS-VIC redistribution is split in two. The column reductions (serial, must see the whole column) stay in the solve kernel as vic_backward_reduce — it does the backward substitution and then the structure-function sums, stashing them in scratch. The per-cell map (capped_exp applied independently at each cell) becomes its own cell-parallel kernel:

// vic_redistribute_impl.h — vic_redistribute_cell (cell-parallel)
T denom = scratch[0], dry_struct = scratch[1];      // column reductions (precomputed)
T a0 = delta[i](0) * VOL(i);
T dryfrac = 1; for (int n = 0; n < ny; ++n) dryfrac -= W(ICY + n, i);

DU(IDN, i) = delta[i](0) * dryfrac *
             capped_exp(a0 * dry_struct / std::max((T)1e-12, denom * dryfrac));
DU(IVX + dir, i) = delta[i](1);
DU(IPR, i)       = delta[i](2);
for (int n = 0; n < ny; ++n) {                    // per-species correction
  T corr = capped_exp(a0 * scratch[2+n] / std::max((T)1e-12, denom * W(ICY+n,i)));
  DU(ICY + n, i) = delta[i](0) * W(ICY + n, i) * corr;
}

Passing data between the phases (zero extra memory)

Splitting a kernel raises a question: where do the intermediates live now that they cross kernel boundaries? The coefficients a,b,c and the solution delta already live in global buffers (the assembly writes them, the solve reads them). The only new data is the column reduction scalars the redistribute kernel needs. The trick: after the forward sweep, the off-diagonal buffer b is dead — so we reuse it as scratch for denom, dry_struct, and the per-species sums. No new allocation, no extra traffic.

// vic_redistribute_impl.h — vic_backward_reduce stashes reductions in the freed b buffer
for (int i = iu - 1; i >= il; --i) delta[i] -= a[i] * delta[i + 1];  // backward scan
T denom = 0, dry_struct = 0;
for (int i = il; i <= iu; ++i) { /* … accumulate structure functions … */ }
scratch[0] = denom;  scratch[1] = dry_struct;       // scratch = b[0].data() — dead after sweep
for (int n = 0; n < ny; ++n) scratch[2 + n] = species_struct;
Why this is safe & bit-identical

The split reproduces the original BackwardSubstitution arithmetic exactly — same sums, same capped_exp, same order — just partitioned across two launches. Reusing a buffer that is provably dead introduces no aliasing. The seeded simulation matched the fused baseline to all digits.

§06The phase-by-phase journey

The optimization landed in stages, each with a lesson:

2Fission the assembly into a per-cell kernelAssembly 2.8%→79% SM, 8→800 blocks; vic total ~2.26× faster.

A meta-lesson on regressions

When the MS-VIC merge bolted a fresh map onto the serial solve, the same anti-pattern reappeared — and the same cure (fission) fixed it. Recognizing the pattern made the second fix mechanical. “One kernel, one kind of parallel work” is a durable rule, not a one-off trick.

§07The PCR detour — a deliberate negative result

After fission, the serial Thomas scan was the last big piece. The tempting next move: parallelize inside the column with Parallel Cyclic Reduction (PCR) — one block per column, one thread per layer, cooperating in shared memory. PCR turns the O(N) serial recurrence into O(log N) parallel steps. It was implemented, validated, and benchmarked.

Configserial Thomasblock-PCRverdict
small (32×32×100)1.18 ms0.43 msPCR 2.7× faster
production (100³)1.40 ms3.29 msPCR 2.35× SLOWER
Why PCR backfired at scale — the key insight

PCR does ~7× more total work (O(N log N) vs O(N)) to win latency. On the small grid the GPU was under-filled (8 columns), so trading work for parallelism paid. But at production there are 10,000 independent columns — the simple one-thread-per-column map already saturates the GPU. Adding intra-column parallelism just piles on the extra work with no occupancy left to buy. Match the parallelism you add to the parallelism you lack. When the GPU is already full, reduce work — don't add it.

PCR was reverted. The serial Thomas scan, one thread per column, is near-optimal for this structure at production scale. The negative result is as valuable as the positive ones — it defines the boundary of the technique.

The question is never “can I parallelize this further?” but “is the GPU already full?”

Part II

Generalizing the technique

Classify the work before you split it. The dependency structure — map, reduce, or scan — tells you how to launch each piece, and the fission–fusion duality ties this story to its companion.

§09Parallelism-aware fission

Classify the work: map / reduce / scan

Before splitting a kernel, label each piece by its dependency structure. The label dictates the launch:

PatternDependencyNatural launchVIC example
Mapnone (per element)one thread/element — saturate the GPUassembly, redistribution
Reducecombine many → onetree / cooperative, or fold into a neighborcolumn structure-function sums
Scanserial recurrenceone thread per independent system; parallelize within only if systems are fewthe Thomas sweep

The recipe

  1. Profile → find a low-occupancy, latency-bound kernel.
  2. Decompose by dependency type — separate the maps, reductions, and scans hiding inside it.
  3. Launch each at its natural granularity; let the embarrassingly parallel parts fill the GPU.
  4. Hand off through existing buffers — reuse dead scratch to avoid new traffic.
  5. Keep a fused reference path (here, the CPU) as a bit-exact oracle.
  6. Leave the irreducible serial core alone unless it is the bottleneck and the GPU is under-filled (§07).

When fission helps — and when it doesn't

SituationVerdict
Kernel mixes map + serial work; map starved by serial granularityfission wins big
High register/shared use from one phase throttles the restisolate it — fission wins
The kernel is already a clean map at full occupancynothing to gain
Phases tiny; launch/handoff overhead exceeds the winkeep fused
Irreducible serial core, GPU already saturatedleave serial (don't PCR)

The duality: fission ⇆ fusion

This study and its companion (evolve_implicit) are mirror images — and reading them together is the real education:

VIC solver (this doc)evolve_implicit (companion)
Movefission — split 1 kernel into 3fusion — merge library calls into 1
Diseaseserial granularity starves the maps; register pressuretiny batched library ops fragment into launches
Cure granularityper-cell maps + per-column scanper-cell fused solve
Numericsbit-identical (same arithmetic, re-partitioned)bit-close (~1e-11, different solver path)
Shared lessonmatch the unit of parallel work to the hardware; don't over-parallelize a saturated GPU (the PCR lesson)

Both moves ask: “what is the right granularity of independent work, and is the GPU full of it?” Fusion increases the work per launch when launches are too small and too many; fission decreases it when one launch crams together incompatible parallel structures. The skill is reading which way the imbalance runs.

Limitations & honest caveats

  • Recomputation cost. Per-cell assembly recomputes each shared interface Jacobian twice (once per adjacent cell). That ~2× redundant flux work is a fair trade for cell independence here, but a denser stencil could tip the balance.
  • Handoff discipline. Reusing the dead b buffer as scratch is safe only because it is provably dead post-sweep; such buffer reuse must be audited, not assumed.
  • The serial core remains. At configurations where columns are few (under-filled GPU), the Thomas scan is the bottleneck and PCR would win — the opposite of production. The right choice is config-dependent; we optimized for prod.
  • More kernels, more launches. Three launches + their syncs replace one. Worth it only because each map is large; for tiny columns the overhead could dominate.

§10Related topics worth your time

Occupancy & the register cliff

Residency is capped by regs/thread × threads ≤ 65,536/SM. Crossing a block-count threshold is discrete — which is why Phase 1's incremental trimming bought nothing. Tools: ncu occupancy section; __launch_bounds__ to cap registers (at the cost of spills).

Roe Riemann solvers

The upwind dissipation |A| = R|Λ|R⁻¹ is the source of those expensive 5×5 eigendecompositions. Approximate/HLLC alternatives trade accuracy for cheaper Jacobians — a different lever on the same cost.

Cyclic reduction family

CR (work-efficient) and PCR (latency-optimal) parallelize tridiagonal solves; hybrids switch between them by size. Essential reading for when intra-system parallelism pays — and §07 for when it doesn't.

Launch overhead & CUDA Graphs

Fission adds launches; if the surrounding loop is launch-bound, a CUDA Graph can replay the whole assemble→solve→redistribute sequence with minimal CPU overhead.

Companion: the implicit chemistry solve

The dual move — fusion — turned a million tiny batched matmuls + LU into one per-cell kernel in evolve_implicit. Same parallelism-matching principle, opposite direction. See evolve_implicit_optimization.html.

Semi-implicit time integration

Why the vertical operator is treated implicitly at all: the acoustic/gravity-wave CFL is far smaller than the advective one. The VIC solve buys a large stable timestep at the price of a per-column linear solve.
