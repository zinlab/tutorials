Part III — Loop strategy

Play the whole board.

Individual hours are won with substance; loops are won with consistency, narrative, and stamina. This part is the loop-level game: being the same person in all five rooms, the story that ties your record to this specific role, managing two days of energy, and how to engage a panel whose work you can literally read — without crossing from prepared into performative.

10One person, five conversations

The five interviewers will compare notes — Monday's three feed directly into Piotr's Tuesday synthesis. That makes consistency a first-order strategy, and it's simpler than it sounds: it's a property of telling the truth.

Consistency dimensionWhat it means in practice
Same motivation everywhereYour "why this role" (§11) should be recognizably identical in Ting's hour, Masaki's hour, and Piotr's — and consistent with what you told Syed. Different depth per audience is fine; different story is not.
Same claims, same numbersYour wins are quantified (400×, 124×, ~239K downloads, 5×) — quote them identically. Numbers that drift across hours read as inflation.
Same honesty calibrationIf you tell Ting you haven't operated PyTorch's CI, don't imply otherwise to Wei. The panel triangulates; calibrated candor everywhere is the only stable strategy.
Retelling stories is fineEach interviewer hears you fresh — reusing your best stories across hours is expected. Just tell each honestly enough that two retellings agree.
Light cross-references help"Wei and I got into the driver-compat side of this" (to Masaki or Piotr) shows engagement and stitches the loop together — one or two, natural, never name-droppy.

Consistency isn't a memory task if you never say anything you'd need to remember. Candidates get tangled when they shade answers per audience — a little more ops experience for the CI interviewer, a little more internals depth for the internals interviewer. Across five hours and a shared notes doc, shading gets caught. The alternative costs nothing: one true account of your record, your motivation, and your gaps, told with emphasis tuned per hour but facts fixed. It's less to hold in your head during an exhausting loop, and it happens to be the exact quality — reliability of word — this team's public, trust-based work depends on.

QWalk me through what it takes to add a new CUDA version to PyTorch's builds and binaries.
The move

Show you see the whole pipeline, in order, with the judgment points — this is her signature work, so structure beats trivia.

Model shape

"Start where the binaries start: the CI docker and manywheel builder images need the new toolkit — install scripts, images for x86 and aarch64. Then the dependency web has to line up: a cuDNN that supports the new CUDA, NCCL, and the pieces PyTorch builds itself, like magma, rebuilt against it. Then the build config: compiler-version handling and the arch list — a new toolkit usually exists for a new architecture, so TORCH_CUDA_ARCH_LIST grows, with the wheel-size-versus-PTX tradeoff decided. Then it enters the binary matrix as a new nightly variant alongside the existing ones, smoke-tested; CI test coverage follows as capacity allows; and eventually it graduates while an old variant gets deprecated. The judgment throughout is sequencing — build enablement first, full test coverage after — and matrix cost, since every variant multiplies build and test load." (Vol. III §04–06 is the substance.)

Why it works

It's the actual anatomy of her PRs — images, magma, sbsa+x86, matrix refactor — told as an engineer who understands the pipeline, with tradeoffs named.

QWhy does PyTorch ship aarch64 CUDA wheels, and what's different about producing them?
The move

Motivation first (Grace changed the answer), then the concrete build-side differences.

Model shape

"Because the host CPU of NVIDIA's flagship systems is now ARM — GH200 and GB200 pair Grace with the GPUs, so 'pip install torch' has to work on aarch64 Linux or the newest hardware has no usable wheels. Producing them means a parallel lane through everything: aarch64 manylinux builder images, the same CUDA/cuDNN/magma stack built for arm, an arch matrix that reflects the datacenter parts, native ARM build-and-test capacity, and a CPU backend story that differs from x86's. Connects platform strategy to build mechanics to user experience — her PR #146378's world, understood rather than recited.

QA nightly docker image build for a new CUDA variant is failing — how do you approach it?
The move

Model shape

15Drills for Wei's hour

QHow should PyTorch decide which CUDA versions to build, test, and ship?
The move

This is a judgment question wearing a factual costume. Name the forces, then propose the pattern that balances them.

Model shape

"Four forces. Users' installed drivers — the oldest shipped CUDA determines who can run stock wheels, and enterprises upgrade drivers slowly. Newest hardware — a new architecture forces the newest toolkit into the matrix immediately, like Blackwell forcing cu128. Cost — every variant multiplies build time, CI capacity, storage, and support surface. And ecosystem coherence — downstream packages pin against what torch ships. The stable pattern those forces produce is a small rolling window: one older variant for driver reach, one mainstream default, one newest for new hardware — introduced as nightly-experimental, promoted, then the tail deprecated with notice. The real skill is the transitions: adding cu-next before its dependency stack is fully mature, and choosing when dropping cu-old strands fewer users than it frees capacity." (Vol. III §04, §09, §13.)

Why it works

He lives this tradeoff; showing you can reason it — rather than recite a version list — is precisely senior signal in his domain.

QA CUDA test is flaky — fails maybe 2% of runs, one GPU type only. What do you do?
The move

Two-track answer: protect the signal now, root-cause properly after — and name the buckets.

Model shape

"Immediate: protect trunk signal — that's what disable-with-tracking-issue machinery is for; a 2% flake across thousands of runs means constant false reds and retry burn, which trains people to ignore CI. Then root-cause with the failure distribution as evidence: one-GPU-type-only points at arch-dependent behavior — numeric tolerance that's too tight for that hardware's math paths, a race that different timing exposes, resource pressure like OOM on a smaller-memory runner, or a genuine arch-specific bug, which is the one you must not miss. Reproduce by hammering the test on that runner type, bisect if it's new, fix the actual cause — loosen tolerance with justification, fix the race, or file the real bug — and re-enable with the issue closed. The discipline is that disabling is a triage step with a paper trail, never a disposal mechanism." (Vol. III §17.)

Why it works

Shows you understand flakiness as both an operational problem (signal integrity) and an engineering one (the bug taxonomy) — his daily war, taken seriously.

QGPU jobs went red across several workflows overnight. Nothing merged looks related. Go.
The move

The infra-vs-product-vs-flake triage, done aloud with the "what changed" instinct leading.

Model shape

"Breadth first: many unrelated workflows red at once means environment, not code — so ask what changed under the code. The usual suspects for GPU fleets: a runner driver or AMI update (does the failure smell like driver/runtime mismatch — initialization errors, 'no kernel image', version asserts?), a docker image rebuild that pulled a drifted dependency, an upstream package release that wasn't pinned, or capacity/hardware trouble on one runner pool. 16Drills for Masaki's hour

QWhy do foreach and fused optimizers exist? How does multi_tensor_apply actually work?
The move

His signature domain — answer from first principles (launch overhead), use the official taxonomy, and reason honestly about the kernel mechanics.

Model shape

"A model has hundreds or thousands of small parameter tensors, and a for-loop optimizer launches several tiny kernels per tensor per step — the GPU spends more time launching than computing. foreach fixes that horizontally: torch._foreach_* ops take whole tensor lists, so one launch covers the same elementwise op across all parameters. fused goes further, vertically: the entire update math — moments, bias correction, the parameter write — becomes one kernel, so fused > foreach > for-loop. The engine underneath is multi_tensor_apply: you can't pass arbitrarily many tensor pointers to a kernel because kernel argument space is small and fixed, so it packs pointers and sizes into chunked metadata structures and launches over chunks, with the functor doing the math. And there's a real memory subtlety — foreach's intermediate tensor-lists can spike peak memory in ways fused avoids, which is part of why fused matters for large models." Then the honest edge: "I know the chunking design conceptually rather than from the source — I'd be glad to be corrected on the details."

Why it works

It's his RFC's framing (he proposed exactly this in #68041 and built fused AdamW), reasoned rather than recited, with the calibrated-honesty close from §07.

QWhat makes an optimizer "capturable" — safe for CUDA graphs?
The move

Start from what graph capture forbids, derive the design constraints — this connects his optimizer work to your Vol. I allocator depth.

Model shape

"A CUDA graph records a fixed sequence of device work and replays it — so anything that reads results back to the host during the step is illegal or breaks correctness: no .item(), no host-side branching on device values, no CPU-resident state that should evolve per step. A capturable optimizer therefore keeps its state — the step counter, and even the learning rate as a device tensor — on the GPU, and expresses everything, including things like the scale-check in AMP flows, as device-side ops. The tell for non-capturable code is the innocuous host sync: a step count kept as a Python int, an lr schedule computed on CPU and pushed in, a debug print of a loss. And underneath, graphs interact with the caching allocator — captured allocations come from a private memory pool so replay addresses stay valid, which is the same MemPool machinery I've gone deep on." (Vol. III §21, §23; Vol. I §10–13.)

Why it works

Derives the rules instead of listing them, and lands on genuine depth you own (the allocator), turning his topic into shared ground.

QExplain how autocast actually works — the mechanism, not the API.
The move

Dispatch-level answer — you've studied PyTorch's dispatcher deeply, so this is your internals credibility on his turf.

Model shape

"autocast is a dispatch-key interception: entering the context activates the autocast key, so op calls route through a layer that applies a per-op casting policy before the real kernel. The policies come from op semantics: matmul- and conv-family ops cast down to fp16/bf16 because tensor cores make them faster and they're numerically tolerant; reductions and normalization-ish ops that are precision-sensitive run in fp32; some ops just promote to the widest input type. It caches casts so the same weight isn't re-cast repeatedly in a step. GradScaler is the fp16 companion for the backward: fp16's narrow exponent range risks gradient underflow, so it scales the loss up, checks for infs/nans device-side, unscales, and skips steps on overflow while adapting the scale — which is why bf16, with fp32's exponent range, usually doesn't need a scaler. The lineage matters here: this design is the in-core descendant of apex.amp." (Vol. III §22.)

Why it works

Mechanism-first, grounded in your genuine dispatcher study, and it acknowledges the apex history he lived without belaboring it.
