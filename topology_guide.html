Section 00

What topology is — and why it sits under everything

Topology is the arrangement of the things that compute and the links between them: which node connects to which, through how many switches, over what kind of wire. It is the map. Everything in the other two guides runs on top of this map — a UCX put or an NCCL all-reduce or a Velox shuffle ultimately becomes "bytes traversing some number of links and switches," and the topology decides how many, how fast, and whether they collide with everyone else's bytes.

The single most important distinction, which the whole guide leans on:

Physical vs logical topology

Physical topology is the literal wiring: GPUs, NICs, switches, cables, and how they're plugged together. It's fixed at deployment time (you can't re-cable a datacenter per job).

Logical topology is the communication pattern a program imposes on top of the physical one: "treat my 8 ranks as a ring," "as a 2D grid," "as a binary tree." It's chosen per algorithm, in software, and can change between collectives in the same job.

The art of high-performance communication is mapping a good logical topology onto the physical one so that logically-adjacent ranks are also physically close. A ring all-reduce is bandwidth-optimal only if consecutive ring ranks sit on fast, direct links. Put the ring's "next hop" across the slowest link in the building and the same algorithm crawls. That mapping problem is what "topology-aware" (§11) means.

The layers of topology you actually deal with

"The network" is really several nested topologies, each with different speed and rules. A byte from one GPU to another may cross all of them:

Within a GPU
on-die / chiplet fabrice.g. 2 dies linked at ~10 TB/s (Blackwell)
Within a node
NVLink / NVSwitchGPU↔GPU, ~1.8 TB/s/GPU
PCIe / NVLink-C2CGPU↔CPU↔NIC
Node ↔ node
InfiniBand / RoCE / Slingshotfat-tree, dragonfly — 100s of GB/s
Rack ↔ rack / pod

Inside the CPU

Each transition down this list is typically 10–100× slower than the one above. The entire game — rail-optimized fabrics, NVLink domains, NUMA binding, topology-aware collectives — is about keeping traffic on the fast layers and minimizing crossings of the slow ones. Hold that thought; it recurs in every section.

Why this matters even if you "just" write query engines or training loops

An all-reduce that should ride NVLink but accidentally goes over PCIe, a shuffle partition that crosses three switch tiers instead of one, a tensor pinned to the wrong NUMA node so every access hops the inter-socket link — these are 2–10× slowdowns that no amount of kernel tuning fixes, because the bottleneck isn't compute, it's the path. Topology literacy is how you recognize "this is a placement problem, not a code problem."

Section 01

The metrics that decide everything

Before comparing shapes you need the vocabulary to compare them. Six numbers characterize any topology; every design is a trade among them.

MetricDefinitionWhy you care
Degree / radixLinks per node (or ports per switch)High radix → fewer hops to reach everyone, but pricier switches & more cables.
DiameterMax hops between the two farthest nodesWorst-case latency. Low diameter = predictable latency at scale.
Bisection bandwidthMin total BW across any cut splitting the network in halfThe master metric for all-to-all / shuffle / all-reduce. It's the bandwidth available when everyone talks to everyone.
Blocking / oversubscriptionRatio of downlink to uplink capacity (1:1 = non-blocking)Oversubscribed links congest under heavy collective traffic. AI training usually demands 1:1.
Cost & scalabilityCables, switches, optics, power vs node countWhat's affordable at 100 nodes is bankrupting at 100,000. Optics > copper in cost & power.
Fault tolerancePath diversity; effect of one link/switch dyingA ring dies on one break; a fat-tree reroutes. Matters more as scale (and failure rate) grows.
Bisection bandwidth is the number to internalize

Imagine slicing the cluster into two equal halves and summing the bandwidth of every link you cut. That's bisection bandwidth — the throughput available when traffic is maximally spread (an all-to-all shuffle, an all-reduce, a distributed join). A topology can have wonderful point-to-point links and still choke a collective if its bisection is low. Full bisection bandwidth means any half can talk to the other half at full node-injection rate with no bottleneck; that's the gold standard for training fabrics and exactly what "non-blocking fat-tree" delivers. When someone says a fabric "scales," ask: at what bisection?

Latency vs bandwidth — two different questions

Diameter governs latency (small messages, barriers, latency-bound collectives); bisection governs bandwidth (big transfers, the bulk of training/analytics). Different workloads weight them differently: a latency-bound HPC solver with tiny halo exchanges cares about diameter and nearest-neighbor locality; a 405B-parameter training all-reduce cares about bisection bandwidth above all. The "best" topology is workload-relative — which is the whole point of §10.

The cost wall is why topologies exist at all

If money and cables were free, every cluster would be fully connected (all-to-all) — diameter 1, perfect bisection. They aren't: full connectivity needs N(N−1)/2 links, which is impossible past a few dozen nodes. Every topology below — ring, tree, torus, hypercube, dragonfly — is a different compromise that buys most of the bandwidth/latency of full connectivity for a fraction of the cables. Understanding each one is understanding which compromise it makes.

Section 02

Physical vs logical — the mapping problem

Section 00 introduced the split; here's why it's the crux of performance. A program never says "send over cable 7." It says "rank 3, send to rank 4" — a logical instruction. Something must translate logical ranks into physical paths. If logically-adjacent ranks are physically far apart, the algorithm is fine but the run is slow. Same code, same physical network, wildly different speed depending purely on the rank-to-hardware mapping.

A concrete example: a ring on a fat-tree

Suppose 8 GPUs across 2 nodes (4 each), and you run a ring all-reduce (logical topology = ring: 0→1→2→…→7→0). Two mappings:

GOOD mapping (ring follows the hardware) node A [g0 g1 g2 g3] node B [g4 g5 g6 g7] ring: 0→1→2→3 ───────────────────→ 4→5→6→7 ──┐ └─────── all intra-node NVLink ─────────┘ only TWO of the eight ring hops cross the slow inter-node link. BAD mapping (ring zig-zags across nodes) node A [g0 g2 g4 g6] node B [g1 g3 g5 g7] ring: 0→1 crosses, 1→2 crosses, 2→3 crosses ... EVERY hop crosses the inter-node link → 8x the slow-link traffic, ~order-of-magnitude slower.

Both are "a ring all-reduce." The physical network is identical. The only difference is which GPU got which rank — and it can be a 5–10× swing. This is why NCCL spends startup time detecting the physical topology and building its rings/trees to match it, and why MPI lets you reorder ranks. The mapping is the performance.

Who does the mapping

You / the scheduler: process placement (which rank lands on which node/GPU) via Slurm/MPI host ordering, CUDA_VISIBLE_DEVICES, rankfiles. The library: NCCL builds its logical rings/trees over the detected NVLink/PCIe/IB graph; MPI can reorder a Cartesian communicator to match the machine. The fabric: routing chooses physical paths between two endpoints (§09). Get all three aligned and the logical pattern rides the fast links; misalign any one and you leak traffic onto slow links.

Keep this lens for the rest of the guide: when we describe a topology (ring, torus, hypercube), we're usually describing a logical communication pattern. Whether it's fast depends on the physical fabric it's mapped onto and the routing underneath.

Section 03

The basics — bus, star, and all-to-all

Start with the three simplest arrangements. You'll rarely build a large system out of these alone, but they're the conceptual atoms, and the star (as a switch) is literally the building block of everything bigger.

Bus — one shared medium

n0 ──┬────┬────┬────┬── n4 n1 n2 n3 (all share ONE link; one talker at a time)

Every node taps a single shared wire. Data travel: a frame is seen by everyone; only the addressee keeps it. Pros: trivially cheap, minimal cabling. Cons: one collision domain — bandwidth is shared by all, and two simultaneous talkers collide. Scenario: legacy 10BASE2 Ethernet, simple on-chip/embedded buses, a PCIe bus segment. Doesn't scale past a handful of busy nodes; obsolete for clusters but the mental model behind "shared bandwidth" warnings.

Star — everyone through one hub/switch

n1 │ n0 ── SW ── n2 every node has its own link to a central switch; │ the switch forwards frames port-to-port. n3

Data travel: source → switch → destination, one hop, no collisions if the switch is non-blocking (it has internal bandwidth ≥ sum of ports). Pros: simple, full per-link bandwidth, one failed link affects only one node. Cons: the switch is a single point of failure and a hard scaling limit — you can only buy switches with so many ports (the radix). Scenario: every modern LAN, and the unit cell of all large topologies — a fat-tree is just stars of switches wired into more stars. When a single switch's radix runs out (say 64 ports), you must combine switches, and how you combine them is the rest of this guide.

All-to-all (fully connected) — a dedicated link between every pair

n0───n1 every node directly wired to every other. │ ╲ ╱ │ diameter = 1 hop. bisection = maximal. │ ╱ ╲ │ links needed = N(N-1)/2 → N=8: 28, N=100: 4,950, N=1000: ~500,000 n3───n2 ...which is why you can't build it at scale.

Data travel: one direct hop, always. The theoretical ideal — lowest latency, highest bisection, total fault tolerance. Cons: the link count explodes quadratically; impossible past tens of endpoints. Scenario: it does exist at small scale where it matters most — NVSwitch makes the 8 GPUs in a node effectively all-to-all over NVLink, and a dragonfly group wires its switches all-to-all (§08). The lesson: full connectivity is the gold standard you approximate locally (within a node, within a group) and give up globally.

The pattern behind every big topology

Large fabrics are hierarchies of stars with all-to-all islands: all-to-all where it's cheap (inside a node via NVSwitch, inside a dragonfly group via copper), trees/stars to aggregate, and careful link budgeting between the islands. Bus is dead; star and all-to-all are the recurring motifs. Everything below is a way to get near-all-to-all behavior without near-all-to-all cabling.

Section 04

Ring — and why all-reduce loves it

A ring connects each node to exactly two neighbors, closing into a loop. Degree 2, dirt-cheap cabling — and, surprisingly, the bandwidth-optimal shape for the most important collective in deep learning. This is the topology to understand deeply because NCCL's default all-reduce is a ring.

n0 ── n1 ── n2 │ │ each node talks only to left & right neighbor. n7 n3 diameter = N/2 hops (bad latency for small msgs). │ │ bisection = just 2 links (bad for random all-to-all). n6 ── n5 ── n4 BUT for structured collectives it's optimal — here's why.

Why a ring is bandwidth-optimal for all-reduce

All-reduce = every node ends up with the sum (or avg) of all nodes' vectors — exactly gradient averaging in data-parallel training. The ring algorithm has two phases, each N−1 steps, moving only chunks:

  1. Scatter-reduce: split each node's vector into N chunks. Each step, every node sends one chunk to its right neighbor and adds the chunk it receives. After N−1 steps, each node owns one fully-reduced chunk (the sum of that chunk across all nodes).
  2. All-gather: circulate those finished chunks around the ring N−1 more steps so everyone ends with all reduced chunks.
scatter-reduce, 4 nodes, vector split in 4 chunks (a,b,c,d): step1: n0 ─a→ n1 ─b→ n2 ─c→ n3 ─d→ n0 (each adds incoming chunk) step2: n0 ─d→ n1 ─a→ n2 ─b→ n3 ─c→ n0 step3: n0 ─c→ n1 ─d→ n2 ─a→ n3 ─b→ n0 now each node holds ONE chunk = sum over all 4 nodes. then all-gather (3 more steps).
The key result: ring all-reduce bandwidth is independent of N

Each node sends 2·(N−1)/N · S bytes total (S = vector size), and every link carries data the whole time. As N grows, per-node bytes →2S — it does not grow with cluster size. That's why ring all-reduce scales to thousands of GPUs at near-constant bandwidth cost. The price is latency: 2(N−1) sequential steps, so big rings have high latency and small messages suffer. This latency/bandwidth split is exactly why NCCL uses rings for large messages and trees (§05) for small/latency-bound ones, picking per call.

Data travel: always to your one neighbor — so if neighbors are NVLink-connected, every byte rides NVLink. This is why the rank-to-GPU mapping (§02) matters so much for rings: NCCL builds the ring order to follow NVLink/NVSwitch links, so "neighbor" is physically one fast hop.

Variants & failure

  • Bidirectional ring: use both directions at once → double the effective bandwidth. NCCL builds multiple rings (and both directions) over the available links.
  • Failure mode: a single broken link or dead node severs a unidirectional ring entirely — no path around. Mitigations: bidirectional/double rings, or rebuild the ring excluding the failed node. Low fault tolerance is the ring's real weakness, which is fine inside a controlled NVLink domain but risky across a flaky network.
Where you've already met this

NCCL's ring all-reduce over NVLink is the workhorse of every multi-GPU training step, and the GPU-analytics exchange (guide 2, §09) shuffles partitions over the same NVLink/NVSwitch fabric. The ring is the canonical "logical topology mapped onto a fast physical island" — and the reason an 8-GPU NVSwitch node (physically all-to-all) can run a ring at full NVLink bandwidth.

Section 05

Tree · Fat-Tree · Clos · Rail-Optimized

This family dominates datacenters and AI clusters. It starts from a plain tree, fixes the tree's fatal flaw, and scales into the spine-leaf and rail-optimized fabrics that connect essentially every large GPU cluster today.

Plain tree — and the root bottleneck

root SW ← ALL cross-subtree traffic squeezes through here / \ sw sw / \ / \ n0 n1 n2 n3

A tree gives low diameter (log N hops) and simple routing (up to common ancestor, back down). But links get narrower toward the root while traffic concentrates there: if n0 and n2 both want to reach the far subtree, they contend for the one root link. Bisection bandwidth is just that top link — terrible for all-to-all. Plain trees are fine for aggregation patterns, useless for shuffle/all-reduce at scale.

Fat-tree — make the links fatter as you climb

Leiserson's insight: widen the links (add parallel paths) toward the root so aggregate bandwidth is constant at every level. Now any half of the leaves can reach the other half at full rate — full bisection bandwidth, the property all-to-all collectives need.

spine spine spine spine ← multiple roots; aggregate BW = leaf BW ╲ ╳ ╳ ╳ ╱ every leaf connects to EVERY spine leaf leaf leaf leaf (a full bipartite mesh between tiers) /│ │ │ ╲ n0 n1 n2 n3 n4 n5 ... ← nodes hang off leaves

Clos / folded-Clos / spine-leaf — building a fat-tree from fixed-radix switches

You can't buy a switch with a million ports, so a fat-tree is built as a Clos network: cheap fixed-radix switches wired so there are many equal-cost paths between any two endpoints. Folded flat, this is the ubiquitous spine-leaf (2-tier) design: leaf (top-of-rack) switches connect to servers below and to all spine switches above; spines connect only to leaves. Two hops leaf→spine→leaf reaches anything.

The scaling math (worth memorizing)

With k-port switches, split each leaf's ports half-down/half-up:

2 tiers (leaf+spine): up to k²/2 endpoints. A 64-port switch → ~2,048 endpoints, full bisection.
3 tiers (leaf+spine+core): up to k³/4 endpoints. 64-port → ~65,000 endpoints. (NVIDIA cites a 64-port 3-tier fat-tree scaling to ~32,768 with 32 down/32 up.)

Higher-radix switches (Quantum-2 = 64×400 Gb/s; Quantum-X800 = 800 Gb/s) push these numbers up without changing the shape — you just need fewer switches/tiers for a given size. This is why "we doubled port speed" scales the fabric without a redesign.

Non-blocking vs oversubscribed

If a leaf has equal down and up bandwidth (1:1), the fabric is non-blocking — no internal link can be the bottleneck. Cheaper deployments oversubscribe (e.g. 4:1 down:up), saving spine switches/optics but creating congestion when many flows go up at once. AI training demands non-blocking because all-reduce/all-to-all saturate uplinks; an oversubscribed fabric quietly tanks collective bandwidth. General cloud/storage traffic tolerates oversubscription. Knowing your fabric's ratio explains a lot of "why is my all-reduce slow."

Rail-optimized (multi-plane) fat-tree — the modern AI fabric

This is the current standard for GPU clusters, and it's a clever twist. A GPU node has 8 GPUs, each with its own NIC ("rail"). Instead of one fat-tree, build 8 parallel fat-trees (planes) — and wire GPU i's NIC on every node to the same leaf switch (leaf set i). So all the "rail-i" NICs share a plane.

rail-optimized: GPUg of every node → leaf-plane g node A: g0 g1 g2 ... g7 node B: g0 g1 g2 ... g7 │ │ │ │ │ │ │ │ L0 L1 L2 ... L7 ← rail planes → L0 L1 L2 ... L7 (A.g3 and B.g3 are both on plane L3 → reach each other in ONE switch hop)
Why rail-optimized is brilliant

In data-parallel training, GPU g on node A mostly talks to GPU g on node B (same tensor shard / same ring position). Rail-optimized puts those exact peers one leaf-switch hop apart. Cross-rail traffic (g3→g5) is rare and, when needed, uses the node-internal NVSwitch to hop to the right local GPU first, then one switch hop on that rail. Result: most inter-node collective traffic crosses a single switch instead of climbing to spine/core — lower latency, less spine load, cheaper (often copper, not optics). NVIDIA's "AI factory" reference fabric and SuperPOD/Spectrum-X designs are rail-optimized non-blocking fat-trees. It's the §02 mapping principle baked into the cabling.

Data travel & routing in a fat-tree

Packet goes up to a common ancestor switch, then down to the destination. Because there are many equal-cost upward paths (every spine is a valid ancestor), the fabric spreads flows across them — by hashing flows (ECMP) or, better, adaptive routing that picks the least-congested uplink per packet (§09). Good spreading is what realizes the theoretical full bisection in practice; poor spreading (hash collisions onto one spine) causes congestion even on a non-blocking fabric.

Tree as a logical collective (NCCL trees)

Separately from the physical fat-tree, "tree" is also a logical collective pattern: reduce up a binary tree to a root, broadcast back down — 2·log(N) latency, far fewer steps than a ring's 2(N−1). So NCCL uses a tree algorithm for small/latency-bound all-reduces and a ring for large/bandwidth-bound ones. Same hardware, two logical topologies, chosen by message size. (And SHARP, §12, does the tree reduction inside the switches.)

Examples DGX SuperPOD: 3-tier fat-tree, Quantum-2 400 Gb/s, full bisection. Hyperscaler AI clusters: rail-optimized spine-leaf, non-blocking, Spectrum-X Ethernet or Quantum InfiniBand. Meta's research notes network mis-config caused ~10.7% of significant GPU job failures — the fabric's correctness, not just its shape, is a first-class concern (§14).

Section 06

Mesh & Torus — neighbors, not switches

Mesh and torus take a radically different approach: no central switches at all. Each node wires directly to its grid neighbors, and routing happens hop-by-hop through the nodes themselves. This trades switch cost and diameter for cheapness and locality — great for some workloads, poor for others.

2D MESH (grid) 2D TORUS (grid + wraparound) n──n──n──n ┌─n──n──n──n─┐ │ │ │ │ │ │ │ │ │ │ n──n──n──n └─n──n──n──n─┘ edges wrap: right of last = first │ │ │ │ │ │ │ │ → halves diameter, uniform links n──n──n──n n──n──n──n (edges have fewer links) (every node has the same degree)

How data travels: dimension-order routing

A packet moves along one dimension until aligned, then the next: "go east 3, then north 2." Simple, deadlock-avoidable (with care), no routing tables. Diameter for an N-node d-dimensional torus is ~d·(N^(1/d))/2 — it grows with size (unlike a fat-tree's fixed log), so a big 2D torus has poor worst-case latency. Adding dimensions (3D, 4D) shrinks diameter at the cost of more links per node.

When torus wins, when it loses

Wins: workloads with nearest-neighbor communication — physics stencils, CFD, lattice QCD, and (crucially) systolic ML where each chip mostly talks to adjacent chips. Traffic stays on short direct links; no switch to buy, build, or congest; bisection is excellent for neighbor patterns; scales cheaply and predictably. Loses: random all-to-all or far-apart communication — diameter is high and a distant message hogs many intermediate nodes' links. A torus is a bet that your communication is local.

Google TPU pods — torus at scale

TPU pods wire chips in a 2D/3D torus precisely because large-model training (with the right parallelism mapping) is dominated by structured neighbor exchanges, and a torus delivers that bandwidth without a giant switch fabric. Modern TPU pods add optical circuit switches to reconfigure the torus topology per job — physically rewiring (logically) so each job gets a torus slice sized and shaped to its needs. AWS Trainium similarly uses workload-optimized direct-connect topologies. It's the counterpoint to "fat-tree everything": if you control the workload's communication pattern, a switchless torus can be cheaper and faster.

Historical Cray XT/Gemini (3D torus), IBM Blue Gene (3D/5D torus) — HPC ran on tori for decades before fat-trees and dragonflies took over general clusters. The idea never left; it just moved to ML accelerators.

Section 07

Hypercube — logarithmic elegance

A hypercube connects nodes whose binary addresses differ in exactly one bit. A d-dimensional hypercube has 2^d nodes, each with degree d, and — the beautiful part — diameter d = log₂(N). Double the nodes, add one link per node and one hop of diameter.

3D hypercube (8 nodes; neighbors differ in one address bit) 110───────111 ╱│ ╱│ 100───────101│ 000-001 differ in bit0 (1 hop) │ │ │ │ 000-011 differ in bits0,1 (2 hops) │010──────│011 000-111 differ in all 3 (3 hops = diameter) │╱ │╱ 000───────001

How data travels: bit-fixing

Route by flipping disagreeing address bits one at a time. From 000 to 101: fix bit0 (→001), fix bit2 (→101). At most log N hops, and there are multiple shortest paths (any order of fixing bits) → natural path diversity and fault tolerance.

Why hypercubes matter even though nobody cables one anymore

The hypercube's structure is the structure of the fastest collective algorithms. Recursive-doubling all-reduce / all-gather: step k, every node exchanges with the partner differing in bit k and combines — done in exactly log₂(N) steps. That's the latency-optimal collective, and it's a hypercube communication pattern regardless of the physical network. MPI's all-reduce/all-gather use recursive doubling for this reason; NCCL's tree algorithm is a close cousin. So you "run a hypercube" logically on a fat-tree every time you do a small all-reduce.

The catch: degree grows with size (log N links per node), so physically wiring a large hypercube means ever-more ports per node — expensive and inflexible. Real machines (older nCUBE, some SGI designs) built modest hypercubes; modern systems keep the algorithm and drop the wiring, using fat-trees/dragonflies physically and hypercube patterns logically. The lesson repeats: logical topology ≠ physical topology, and the cleverest algorithms are logical hypercubes/rings/trees mapped onto whatever fabric you actually have.

Section 08

Dragonfly — minimal cables, minimal hops, maximal scale

Dragonfly is the topology behind the world's top supercomputers (Frontier, El Capitan, Aurora — all on HPE Slingshot). Its goal: connect hundreds of thousands of endpoints with very few hops and far fewer expensive optical cables than a fat-tree, by exploiting high-radix switches and a two-level all-to-all structure.

Dragonfly = groups, each internally all-to-all, groups globally all-to-all ┌── GROUP 0 ──┐ ┌── GROUP 1 ──┐ ┌── GROUP 2 ──┐ │ sw─sw─sw─sw │ │ sw─sw─sw─sw │ │ sw─sw─sw─sw │ ← intra-group: all-to-all (cheap copper) │ ╲ ╳ ╳ ╱ │ │ ╲ ╳ ╳ ╱ │ │ ╲ ╳ ╳ ╱ │ └──────┬──────┘ └──────┬──────┘ └──────┬──────┘ └─────── global optical links ──────┘ ← inter-group: all-to-all (few optics) any node → any node in ≤ 3 hops: local → global → local

How data travels

Minimal route: hop within source group to the switch holding the right global link, cross one global (optical) link to the destination group, hop within that group to the target — at most 3 switch-to-switch hops, even at 250,000 endpoints. Slingshot's 64-port switches build this; each group is all-to-all over copper, groups are all-to-all over optics.

Why dragonfly is cost-effective at exascale

Optical cables (long, fast, expensive, power-hungry) are the dominant cost at scale. Dragonfly minimizes them: most links are short copper inside groups; only the relatively few global links are optical. Net effect — it scales to ~4× the nodes of a 3-tier fat-tree for similar network cost, with diameter 3. That economics is why every recent exascale machine chose it.

The catch: global-link contention demands adaptive routing

Between any two groups there may be only one (or few) global link(s). If many flows want the same group-pair, that minimal path congests instantly — the dragonfly's Achilles heel. The fix is non-minimal (Valiant) routing: deliberately route through a random intermediate group first, spreading load across many global links at the cost of an extra hop (up to ~5 hops). Slingshot's Rosetta ASIC does fine-grained adaptive routing — per-packet, real-time congestion sensing — choosing minimal when clear and non-minimal when congested. Without sophisticated adaptive routing + congestion control, a dragonfly collapses under adversarial traffic. This is why dragonfly and adaptive routing are inseparable (§09).

Job placement matters enormously here: a job that fits in one group (256–512 nodes) stays on fast intra-group copper and avoids global links entirely; a job spanning groups pays global-link bandwidth (low per group-pair) and needs spreading. Schedulers are dragonfly-aware: pack small jobs into single groups, spread large jobs to exploit many global links. Examples Slingshot-11 powers El Capitan, Frontier, Aurora (top 3 of the TOP500); 64-port switches at 12.8 Tb/s, ≤3 hops, 145 km of cabling per machine.

Section 09

How data travels — routing across any topology

Topology defines the possible paths; routing chooses the actual one. Two endpoints in a fat-tree or dragonfly have many equal-cost paths, and which one each packet takes determines whether the fabric's theoretical bandwidth is realized or wasted. This is where a "non-blocking" fabric still congests if routing is dumb.

AxisOptionsTrade-off
Path lengthminimal vs non-minimalMinimal = fewest hops; non-minimal (Valiant) = extra hops to dodge congestion.
Decisiondeterministic vs adaptiveDeterministic = same path always (simple, predictable, can hotspot); adaptive = pick by live congestion (fast, complex).
Granularityper-flow vs per-packetPer-flow keeps ordering (ECMP hashing); per-packet ("spraying") spreads best but can reorder → needs reassembly.
Awarenessoblivious vs congestion-awareOblivious ignores load; congestion-aware reroutes around hotspots.

Deterministic / dimension-order routing

Fixed rule, e.g. "fix dimensions in order" (mesh/torus) or "hash the 5-tuple to pick an uplink" (Ethernet ECMP). Simple and order-preserving, but two flows can hash to the same link and collide while other links idle — hash-collision congestion on an otherwise non-blocking fat-tree. The classic "my all-reduce is slower than the fabric should allow" bug.

Adaptive routing

Switches sense per-port load and steer each packet (or flow) onto the least-congested viable path. Essential for dragonfly (global-link contention) and increasingly standard on InfiniBand and AI Ethernet (Spectrum-X). Slingshot does it per-packet in the Rosetta ASIC; the payoff is high sustained utilization without overprovisioning. The cost is complexity and possible packet reordering.

Deadlock & virtual channels — the subtle correctness issue

Routing can deadlock: a cycle of packets each holding a buffer the next needs, none able to advance — the network freezes. Two defenses: (1) acyclic routing rules (dimension-order routing is deadlock-free by construction — always go X before Y); (2) virtual channels — multiplex several logical buffer lanes on one physical link so the dependency graph stays acyclic even with adaptive/non-minimal routes. Every real fabric (IB, Slingshot, NVLink) uses virtual channels/lanes. When you see mysterious fabric-wide stalls, suspect a routing/credit/deadlock-avoidance issue, not your code.

Congestion control

Adaptive routing handles in-network hotspots but not endpoint congestion — many senders → one receiver (incast), classic in all-to-all shuffles and parameter-server patterns. Fabrics add congestion control: credit-based flow control (InfiniBand), ECN/DCQCN (RoCE), and Slingshot's hardware congestion management that throttles the offending flows to protect everyone else's latency. AI Ethernet (Spectrum-X, Ultra Ethernet) is largely about bringing IB-grade adaptive routing + congestion control to Ethernet.

Routing problems you'll actually hit

Hash-collision hotspots (ECMP): a few flows pile on one uplink → uneven link utilization despite spare capacity. Fix: enable adaptive routing / better hashing / more flows. Incast collapse: all-to-all overwhelms a receiver → throughput craters. Fix: congestion control, message scheduling, smaller in-flight windows. Routing imbalance after a link failure: rerouted traffic overloads survivors. Non-minimal overhead: Valiant routing doubles hops — great under load, wasteful when idle (good adaptive routing only uses it when needed). Diagnosing these means reading per-link counters (§14), not host-side throughput.

Decision tree: which topology / fabric?

What's the dominant communication pattern? ├─ nearest-neighbor / structured (stencils, systolic ML) │ └─ mesh/torus — switchless, cheap, locality-optimal (TPU pods, HPC physics). ├─ data-parallel all-reduce (gradient sync) │ └─ rail-optimized fat-tree + NVLink intra-node; ring/tree collectives + SHARP. ├─ any-to-any / shuffle / mixed (general training + analytics) │ └─ non-blocking fat-tree (spine-leaf). What scale? ├─ one node (≤8–72 GPUs) → NVSwitch all-to-all / NVLink domain — already optimal. ├─ ≤ ~2K endpoints → 2-tier spine-leaf (k²/2) suffices. └─ 10K–100K+ → 3-tier fat-tree or dragonfly; mind optics cost & bisection. Budget vs bisection? ├─ training, money available → non-blocking 1:1 — collectives need it. └─ cloud/storage/mixed, cost-sensitive → oversubscribe (e.g. 4:1) where collectives are light.
The 80% answer

For GPU training/analytics today the default is a non-blocking, rail-optimized fat-tree (InfiniBand or Spectrum-X Ethernet) with an NVLink/NVSwitch all-to-all domain inside each node, and NVLink-scale domains (NVL72) where you can get them. You pick something exotic (torus, dragonfly) only when scale or a known-local communication pattern justifies it. Inside the node, the topology that bites you is NUMA (§13) — don't forget it.

Section 11

Topology-aware — MPI, NCCL, UCX

"Topology-aware" means the software discovers the physical topology and shapes its behavior to match it — instead of treating all ranks as equidistant. Concretely, three levers (from §02): place ranks well, build logical collectives that follow fast links, and pick the fastest transport per peer. Each library pulls these levers differently.

MPI — placement, reordering, and hierarchical collectives

  • Process placement & binding. mpirun --map-by ppr:1:numa --bind-to core, rankfiles, and Slurm placement decide which rank lands on which socket/GPU/NIC. This is the coarsest, highest-impact lever (recall the ring-mapping example, §02). Open MPI uses hwloc to see the machine.
  • Let MPI renumber ranks. MPI_Dims_create + MPI_Cart_create(..., reorder=true) asks MPI to remap your logical grid onto the hardware so Cartesian neighbors are physically close. MPI_Comm_split_type(MPI_COMM_TYPE_SHARED) extracts the on-node ranks so you can do hierarchical collectives: reduce within the node first (shared memory / NVLink), then one rank per node across the network — turning N-way network traffic into (nodes)-way.
// Hierarchical all-reduce: collapse on-node traffic before hitting the network.
MPI_Comm node_comm;
MPI_Comm_split_type(MPI_COMM_WORLD, MPI_COMM_TYPE_SHARED, 0,
                    MPI_INFO_NULL, &node_comm);          // ranks on the same node
MPI_Reduce(send, tmp, n, MPI_FLOAT, MPI_SUM, 0, node_comm);  // 1) intra-node (fast)
if (node_rank == 0)
  MPI_Allreduce(tmp, recv, n, MPI_FLOAT, MPI_SUM, leaders_comm); // 2) one/node across net
MPI_Bcast(recv, n, MPI_FLOAT, 0, node_comm);            // 3) push result back down

NCCL — auto-detected rings/trees over the real graph

NCCL is aggressively topology-aware by default. At init it probes the intra-node fabric (NVLink, NVSwitch, PCIe switches, P2P capability) and the inter-node fabric (which NIC is near which GPU), builds an internal topology graph, then constructs its rings and trees to follow the fastest links — so "ring neighbor" is an NVLink hop and each GPU uses its nearest NIC (rail).

  • Rail awareness / PXN. NCCL routes a GPU's inter-node traffic to the NIC on its own rail; PXN (PCI × NVLink) lets a GPU hop over intra-node NVLink to reach the right rail's NIC, exactly exploiting the rail-optimized fabric (§05) — one switch hop instead of climbing tiers.
  • Algorithm selection. NCCL_ALGO = Ring (bandwidth), Tree (latency/small msgs), NVLS (NVLink SHARP), CollNet (in-network SHARP, §12). NCCL picks per message size + topology.
  • Knobs to know. NCCL_TOPO_DUMP_FILE (see the detected graph), NCCL_P2P_LEVEL/NCCL_NET_GDR_LEVEL (when to use P2P / GPUDirect RDMA), NCCL_IB_HCA (rail/NIC selection), NCCL_DEBUG=INFO + NCCL_DEBUG_SUBSYS=GRAPH (why it chose a given ring/tree).

UCX — transport selection by locality

UCX is the layer that turns "send to peer X" into "use this wire," and it's topology-aware at the transport level. For each peer it selects the fastest available transport (TL): CUDA-IPC/NVLink for same-node GPU peers, shared memory for same-node CPU, IB/RoCE with GPUDirect RDMA for remote GPUs, TCP as fallback — and can stripe across multiple rails (multi-rail) for more bandwidth. It uses hwloc for NUMA/PCIe locality so a transfer uses the NIC nearest the source buffer.

# Make UCX's topology choices explicit / debuggable:
export UCX_TLS=rc,cuda_copy,cuda_ipc,gdr_copy   # allowed transports (IB RC + CUDA paths)
export UCX_NET_DEVICES=mlx5_0:1,mlx5_1:1         # which rails/HCAs to use (multi-rail)
export UCX_RNDV_SCHEME=get_zcopy                 # zero-copy rendezvous for big msgs
export UCX_LOG_LEVEL=info                         # print chosen transports per peer
How the three stack up

They compose: MPI (or a training framework) issues collectives; NCCL implements GPU collectives by building topology-matched rings/trees; UCX (under MPI, and as one of NCCL's net plugins) moves each message over the fastest transport for that peer. All three lean on hwloc to see the machine. "Topology-aware" is the same idea at three altitudes: place ranks near their partners, shape the collective to the fabric, and pick the right wire per hop. Misconfigure any layer (bad binding, NCCL falling back to PCIe, UCX choosing TCP) and you leak onto slow links — the §14 symptoms.

Section 12

In-network computing — SHARP & NVLS

The topologies so far move data; in-network computing makes the topology compute on it in flight. NVIDIA SHARP (Scalable Hierarchical Aggregation and Reduction Protocol) offloads collectives — all-reduce, reduce, broadcast, barrier — into the switch ASICs themselves, so the reduction happens as data climbs the fabric, not on the CPUs/GPUs.

Without SHARP: every GPU sends to every other (ring/tree on endpoints) g0 ─┐ g1 ─┼─→ lots of endpoint↔endpoint traffic, GPUs do the adds g2 ─┘ With SHARP: switches form an aggregation tree; ANs reduce on the way up g0 g1 g2 g3 leaves send once → ╲ │ │ ╱ [ switch AN ] ← Aggregation Node reduces here │ [ root AN ] ← final sum formed in-network, then distributed back down ~half the data on the wire, GPUs freed, less jitter

How it works

SHARP builds aggregation trees across the physical fabric; switches act as aggregation nodes (ANs). Partial results enter at the leaves and are reduced at each AN as they ascend, so the global sum forms inside the network and is distributed back down — the textbook tree all-reduce (§05/§07), executed in switch silicon. Benefits: roughly halves the data crossing the network and ~doubles effective all-reduce bandwidth, cuts latency (fewer endpoint round-trips), and slashes jitter (the switch path is deterministic, unlike contended endpoint reductions). 3rd-gen SHARP builds multiple aggregation trees over one fabric so many jobs share the benefit; it integrates with NCCL automatically.

Two flavors: SHARP (network) and NVLS (NVLink)

SHARP / CollNet runs in the InfiniBand Quantum switches — inter-node collectives. NVLS (NVLink SHARP) runs the same idea in the NVSwitch — intra-node/NVL72 collectives reduced inside the NVLink fabric. NCCL exposes both via NCCL_ALGO=CollNet and NCCL_ALGO=NVLS. On an NVL72 (72-GPU NVLink domain) NVLS lets a 72-way all-reduce reduce in the switch fabric — a major reason that domain behaves like "one big GPU." This is the topology layer reaching up to do the collective's arithmetic.

Caveat: SHARP needs supported switches/HCAs (Quantum IB, ConnectX) and correct fabric configuration (UFM, aggregation-tree setup). When it's misconfigured it silently doesn't engage and you fall back to endpoint collectives — a common "why didn't my all-reduce speed up" mystery (§14). Verify with ibdiagnet SHARP counters and NCCL debug output.

Section 13

NUMA topology — the network inside the node

Everything so far was between nodes. But there's a topology inside each node that quietly decides a huge fraction of performance: NUMA (Non-Uniform Memory Access). A modern server has multiple CPU sockets, each with its own memory controllers and PCIe lanes; reaching your socket's memory is fast, reaching the other socket's memory crosses an inter-socket link (Intel UPI / AMD Infinity Fabric) and is slower. The node is a tiny network, and GPUs/NICs hang off specific sockets.

A 2-socket GPU node is itself a topology: [ DRAM0 ] [ DRAM1 ] │ │ ┌─Socket 0─┐ ──UPI/IF── ┌─Socket 1─┐ ← cross-socket = the slow "link" │ PCIe │ │ PCIe │ │ GPU0 GPU1│ │ GPU2 GPU3│ │ NIC0 │ │ NIC1 │ └──────────┘ └──────────┘ GPU0↔NIC0 = local (fast). GPU0↔NIC1 or GPU0↔DRAM1 = crosses UPI (slow).

Why it matters — three concrete hits

  • Memory affinity. A thread on socket 0 touching memory allocated on socket 1 pays the UPI crossing on every access — easily 1.5–2× slower bandwidth and higher latency. Linux uses first-touch (a page lands on the NUMA node of the thread that first writes it), so initialization order silently sets affinity.
  • GPU–NIC affinity. For GPUDirect RDMA, the GPU should use the NIC on its own PCIe/NUMA domain. Cross-socket GPU↔NIC traffic detours over UPI and can halve effective network bandwidth — this is the node-level version of rail-optimization, and it's why NCCL/UCX try to pair each GPU with its local NIC.
  • GPU–GPU path. Two GPUs may be NVLink-connected (fast), on the same PCIe switch (ok), or split across sockets so P2P must traverse UPI (slow) — or be disallowed, forcing a host bounce.

See the topology, then bind to it

# See GPU↔GPU / GPU↔NIC paths (NV# = NVLink, PIX/PXB = PCIe, SYS = crosses NUMA/UPI):
nvidia-smi topo -m
# Full machine topology — sockets, caches, PCIe, NUMA distances:
lstopo            # (hwloc) graphical/text map of the node
numactl --hardware # NUMA nodes + distance matrix

# Bind a rank to the NUMA node local to its GPU and NIC:
numactl --cpunodebind=0 --membind=0 ./train   # socket-0 cores + socket-0 memory
# MPI equivalent: --map-by numa --bind-to core; set CUDA_VISIBLE_DEVICES to local GPUs.
The classic NUMA bug

A data loader or allocator runs on socket 0, allocates the pinned host staging buffer there (first-touch), but the GPU and its NIC are on socket 1. Every H2D copy and every GPUDirect transfer now crosses UPI — throughput drops ~2× and nobody knows why because the GPU and code look fine. Fix: bind the process to the GPU's NUMA node, allocate pinned buffers after binding, and confirm GPU↔NIC are PIX/PXB (local), not SYS (cross-socket), in nvidia-smi topo -m. On Grace-Hopper/NVL72 the CPU↔GPU link is NVLink-C2C and coherent, which softens (but doesn't erase) NUMA effects — placement still matters for which NIC/rail a GPU uses.

NUMA is the same lesson, one level down

"Keep traffic on the fast layer; minimize crossings of the slow one" applies inside the node exactly as it does across the fabric. Inter-socket UPI is the node's "spine link," GPUs/NICs are leaves on sockets, and binding is the node-level analogue of rail-optimized placement. Topology-aware software (NCCL, UCX, MPI via hwloc) reads this internal map too — but only if you place processes correctly. The fanciest fabric in the world won't save a job that's fighting its own motherboard.

Section 15

Worked examples — the same job, three fabrics

Concrete traces to cement the ideas. We all-reduce a vector of size S across the cluster and watch how topology + algorithm change the cost.

Example 1 — all-reduce, three ways

RING (bandwidth-optimal, N GPUs) steps: 2(N-1) per-GPU bytes moved: 2·(N-1)/N·S ≈ 2S (independent of N!) latency: grows with N (many sequential steps) → great for BIG S, poor for small S TREE / recursive-doubling (latency-optimal) steps: 2·log2(N) per-GPU bytes: ~2S latency: log N → great for SMALL S (this is the hypercube pattern from SS07, used by NCCL Tree & MPI for small msgs) FAT-TREE + SHARP (in-network) reduction happens INSIDE the switches (SS12): ~half the data crosses the wire, ~2x effective all-reduce BW, low jitter. GPUs send once, get the sum back.

Same operation, same hardware bandwidth — but NCCL picks Tree for small S (latency wins), Ring for large S (bandwidth wins), and CollNet/NVLS when SHARP is available (offload wins). That per-call choice is topology-awareness in action.

Example 2 — why rail-optimized cuts hops

data-parallel: GPU3 on node A all-reduces with GPU3 on node B (same ring position) plain fat-tree: A.g3 → leaf → spine → leaf → B.g3 (3+ switch hops, climbs tiers) rail-optimized: A.g3 → leaf-plane-3 → B.g3 (ONE switch hop, same rail) → less latency, no spine load for the common case. cross-rail (g3↔g5) uses NVSwitch inside the node to reach the right local GPU first, THEN one rail hop.

Example 3 — the NUMA tax

8-GPU node, dataloader pinned-buffer on socket 0, GPU6+NIC1 on socket 1 BAD : host buf (sock0) ──UPI──> GPU6 (sock1) every H2D + GDR crosses UPI → ~2x slower GOOD: bind rank to sock1; alloc pinned AFTER bind local PCIe path, full bandwidth verify: nvidia-smi topo -m shows GPU6↔NIC1 = PIX/PXB (local), not SYS (cross-socket)
The thread through all three

Every example is the same principle: match the logical communication to the physical fast path, and minimize crossings of slow links — whether the "slow link" is a spine tier, a dragonfly global link, or an inter-socket UPI hop. Get placement and algorithm aligned with the topology and you ride the fast layers; misalign them and you leak onto the slow ones. That single idea spans this entire guide, from an 8-GPU motherboard to a 250,000-endpoint dragonfly.

The whole guide in four sentences

Topology is the shape of the wires; physical is fixed cabling, logical is the pattern software lays on top, and performance is how well they're matched (§02). Each shape — ring, torus, hypercube, fat-tree, dragonfly — is a different compromise between diameter, bisection bandwidth, and cost (§01–08), and routing decides whether the shape's potential bandwidth is actually realized (§09). "Topology-aware" software (MPI, NCCL, UCX) discovers the physical layout and shapes placement, collectives, and transport to keep traffic on fast links — extended into the switches by SHARP (§11–12) and down into the node by NUMA awareness (§13). Across every scale the rule is one sentence: keep traffic on the fast layer, minimize crossings of the slow one.
