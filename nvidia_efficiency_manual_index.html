What to read, when.

compute · clusters · ML-workload performance
Expect to reason about why a workload is slow and fix it — the roofline (compute vs memory bound), the parallelism dimensions, the profiling stack, and a clean end-to-end debugging method. Be ready to go deep on a kernel, where your 124× CUDA work gives you real authority.

How to use this

A study sequence.

If you have time for nothing else, this order gets the most value per hour.

Read Volume I end to end, first

It orients everything — the role, the team, the goodput concept, and a card for every interviewer. An hour here makes the other three volumes (and every interview) click into place.

Before each interview, read its primary volume(s)

Follow the per-interview reading path above. Front-load the two ★ required interviews (Matt → IV, Greg → III+IV) — those are the ones you must pass, so give them the deepest passes.

Skim the cheatsheets the morning of

The final part of every volume is a condensed reference — decision trees, command cheatsheets, the master diagrams. A quick re-read refreshes the recall-level facts (field names, thresholds, NCCL env vars, FLOP constants).

Revisit your stories & the closing tie-ins

Volume I Part VI has the STAR stories and questions to ask; each volume's final chapter maps its content to the specific interviewers. Reread these right before the loop so the connections between your real work and their questions are fresh.

The one discipline that runs through all four volumes — and that every interviewer is really testing for: measure, don't guess; decompose the problem into its layers; attack the worst layer first; fix the class, not the instance; and keep your findings trustworthy enough that a busy researcher will act on them. The manuals are the map of what to know. Your own work — 400× and 124× from re-architecting rather than patching, pydisort's 1000× from a fundamentally better approach, deep CUDA and distributed systems from the inside — is the evidence that you already think this way. Walk in as someone who does the thing, not someone who studied it.

Goodput is the product. Three layers, three instruments, one discipline. You bring the proof.
