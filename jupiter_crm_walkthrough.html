Modeling Jupiter's Weather Layer

A systematic walkthrough of run_jupiter_crm.py with jup_crm_100.yaml — what each computation does, and the physical mechanism behind it. This is a cloud-resolving model (CRM): a 3-D box of Jupiter's atmosphere in which moist convection, condensation, and precipitation are simulated directly.
snapy · dynamical core kintera · thermo + chemistry paddle · glue layer PyTorch tensors / GPU finite-volume Godunov operator splitting

The model answers a concrete question: if you heat a column of Jupiter's hydrogen atmosphere from below and cool it from the top, how do convective storms organize, and where do water, ammonia, and ammonium-hydrosulfide clouds form and rain out? The 100 in the filename is the imposed heat flux (100 W m⁻², an accelerated stand-in for Jupiter's internal heat).

0The software stack & how data flows

The Python driver is thin orchestration. The physics lives in compiled C++/CUDA libraries exposed to Python through pybind11, all operating on PyTorch tensors so the whole simulation can run on a GPU.

moves mass & momentum around (transport) decides phase, temperature, reactions (local physics) orchestration

pyharp and pydisort (radiative transfer) also live in the repo set, but this particular CRM configuration represents radiation as simple prescribed top-cooling / bottom-heating rather than a full radiative-transfer solve.

1The inputs — decoding jup_crm_100.yaml

Every physical choice the simulation makes is declared here. The blocks map one-to-one onto modules in the libraries.

Geometry & grid

Cartesian box, 100³ cells with 3 ghost layers. x1 is vertical (0→300 km, so ≈3 km cells); x2,x3 are horizontal (0→2400 km, ≈24 km cells).

cells: nx1:100 nx2:100 nx3:100
bounds: x1 0–300 km · x2,x3 0–2400 km

Boundary conditions

Reflecting top & bottom (no flow through the lid/floor, free-slip); periodic on the four sides — the box is a representative patch of a much larger atmosphere.

Forcing — what drives the weather

Gravity g = 22 m s⁻² downward. A radiative-style cooling of −100 W m⁻² spread over the top 5 cells, and heating of +100 W m⁻² over the bottom 5 cells. Cooling aloft + heating below = an unstable column that convects.

Initial thermodynamic state

Base of column at P=100 bar, T=600 K; temperature floored at Tmin=110 K. Deep volatile abundances (mole fractions): H₂O 3×10⁻³, NH₃ 3.2×10⁻⁴, H₂S 1×10⁻⁵ — roughly Jovian deep composition.

The 10 species — a "dry" gas plus three condensible systems

The first species is the background dry H₂/He gas (it is never created or destroyed). Each condensible element appears as a vapor and as two condensed forms: a small cloud particle and a larger, falling precipitation particle (suffix ,p).

The negative internal-energy offsets u₀ are the latent-heat bookkeeping: turning vapor into condensate releases energy that warms the surrounding gas — the engine of moist convection.

The reaction network — cloud microphysics as "chemistry"

Nine reactions, organized as three vapor→cloud→precip conveyor belts. Each belt has the same three steps; the third belt builds Jupiter's signature NH₄SH cloud from two vapors.

condensation when supersaturated small→large particle growth re-evaporation when subsaturated

2Initialization — building a physically consistent starting atmosphere

Before any time-stepping, setup_profile(block, param, method="pseudo-adiabat") (in paddle) constructs the vertical structure. You can't start from arbitrary numbers — the column must be in hydrostatic balance and follow a thermodynamically sensible temperature lapse, or the model would just be correcting nonsense for the first thousand steps.

computation

Integrate upward, cell by cell

Starting from the surface (T,P, composition), it marches up one grid cell at a time, calling thermo_x.extrapolate_dz(...) to find the (T,P,x) of the next cell.

science

A moist/pseudo-adiabat under gravity

Two constraints are enforced simultaneously: hydrostatic balance dP/dz=−ρg and constant entropy (adiabatic) — but corrected for latent heating whenever a species condenses. "pseudo" = condensate rains out instead of being carried along.

hydrostatic:   dP/dz = −ρ g   →   Pnew = P0 − ½(ρ+ρ₀) g Δz discretized, trapezoidal
adiabatic + latent:   solve T so that   smolar(T,P,x) = starget   with effective heat capacity cpeff = cpdry + cplatent extrapolate_ad.cpp

The code iterates (Newton-style) on temperature until the entropy of the new cell matches the target entropy. The effective_cp term adds the latent contribution by differentiating the saturation-vapor-pressure curve — this is what makes a moist adiabat bend away from the dry one inside cloud layers. Finally, the driver seeds convection by adding a tiny random vertical velocity: hydro_w[kIV1] += 0.1*rand.

3The main loop — one timestep, end to end

The integrator advances until tlim = 5×10⁷ s. Each cycle has two big phases applied in sequence (this is operator splitting): first move the fluid (snapy), then react the chemistry in place (kintera).

Why split? Transport (moving air) and reactions (changing phase) act on very different timescales and are numerically easiest to solve with different methods. Splitting lets snapy use an explicit-in-horizontal shock-capturing scheme while kintera solves the stiff condensation chemistry implicitly in each cell independently.

4Phase A — the dynamical core, computation by computation

Variable layout (so the indices in the code make sense)

State is stored as two tensors per block: primitive W and conserved U. The Python constants index into them:

computation 1

WENO5 reconstruction

Cells store volume averages; fluxes need values at the faces between cells. WENO5 builds a 5th-order interpolation of W to each face from a 5-cell stencil.

why

Sharp edges without ringing

Cloud boundaries and convective fronts are near-discontinuities. The weighted essentially-non-oscillatory scheme automatically down-weights stencils that cross a shock, giving high accuracy in smooth regions but no spurious oscillations at edges (the shock flag enables the detector).

computation 2

HLLC Riemann solver

At every face the left/right states form a tiny "shock-tube" problem; HLLC computes the resulting fluxes of mass, momentum, energy, and every tracer.

why

The "C" resolves the contact

HLLC keeps the contact discontinuity wave — the surface separating air parcels of different composition. That's essential here: it stops cloudy and clear air from numerically smearing together as they advect.

computation 3 + 6

RK3 + vertically-implicit update

Time advances with a 3-stage Runge–Kutta scheme (type: rk3). The vertical direction is treated implicitly (implicit-scheme: 1).

why

Don't let sound waves set the clock

Vertical sound & gravity waves are fast (~km/s) over thin 3 km cells; an explicit step would need a tiny dt. Solving the vertical acoustic terms implicitly removes that stiffness so the timestep is set by the convection (CFL 0.9), not by acoustics.

The conservation law being solved

∂U/∂t + ∇·F(U) = Sgrav + Sradiative + Ssed compressible Euler + sources

where U = (ρ, ρv, E, ρYᵢ). Steps 1–3 build the flux divergence ∇·F; step 4 adds the source terms S.

source: gravity

ρg

Momentum source in the vertical and the corresponding work term in the energy equation. Maintains hydrostatic structure and lets buoyant parcels rise.

source: top-cool / bot-heat

Radiative proxy

−100 W m⁻² removed near the top, +100 W m⁻² added near the bottom, each spread over 5 cells. This is the destabilizing forcing that powers the convection — Jupiter loses internal heat to space at the top and supplies it from the hot interior below.

source: sedimentation

Precip falls out

The (l,p)/(s,p) particles are given a constant settling velocity −20 m/s, added as an extra downward advective flux. This is what physically separates condensibles in the vertical and forms discrete cloud decks.

The equation of state type: ideal-moist ties it together: it converts between conserved and primitive variables (W→T, etc.). "Moist" means the molecular weight and heat capacity vary with composition (a virtual-temperature effect) and condensation contributes latent heat. Density/pressure floors and an iterative limiter (max-iter:20, ftol:1e-6) keep the inversion stable in near-vacuum cells aloft.

5Phase B — cloud microphysics, the heart of the science

After transport, evolve_kinetics updates composition in each cell. It is pure local physics: given T, P and how much of each species is present, decide how much vapor condenses, how much cloud grows into precip, and how much precip re-evaporates.

Step 1 — saturation from the Clausius–Clapeyron relation

Everything keys off the saturation vapor pressure (SVP): the maximum vapor a gas can hold at temperature T. kintera uses an integrated Clausius–Clapeyron form (vapor_functions.h):

ln psat(T) = (1 − Tr/T)·β − γ·ln(T/Tr) + ln pr logsvp_ideal

β encodes the latent heat (L ≈ βR Tr), γ the heat-capacity difference between phases. Each species has its own constants and switches at its triple point — e.g. water uses one set above 273.16 K (liquid) and another below (ice):

SpeciesReference (Tr, pr)Note
H₂O273.16 K, 611.7 Paliquid/ice branches
NH₃195.4 K, 6060 Paliquid/solid branches
NH₄SHlog₁₀(pNH₃·pH₂S) = 14.82 − 4705/T  (Lewis 1969 reaction equilibrium of two vapors)

Step 2 — the three rate laws

nucleation

vapor → cloud

When the partial pressure exceeds psat (supersaturation), vapor condenses onto particles. Driven directly by the SVP curve; for NH₄SH it fires when the product pNH₃·pH₂S exceeds equilibrium.

coagulation

cloud → precip

Small suspended particles collide and merge into larger, falling ones ("autoconversion"). Modeled as a simple first-order Arrhenius rate (A = 10⁻⁴ s⁻¹).

evaporation

precip → vapor

Falling particles entering dry air re-evaporate. Diffusion-limited rate set by particle size and a saturation deficit; active only when subsaturated (clamped ≥ 0).

evaporation:   rate = κ·η,   κ = 12 D vm/d²,   η = exp(ln psat − …) − ∏ Cν  (saturation deficit) evaporation.cpp

κ collects the vapor diffusivity D, molar volume vm, and particle diameter d — bigger drops evaporate slower. η is how far below saturation the air is; clamping it to ≥0 means precip never "condenses" in this channel, it only evaporates.

Step 3 — the implicit chemistry solve

Condensation can be extremely fast compared with the fluid timestep (stiff). Solving it explicitly would blow up, so kintera linearizes the reaction system and takes a backward-Euler step (evolve_implicit.hpp):

( I/Δt − S·J ) · ΔC = S·r solve a linear system per cell

Here S is the stoichiometry matrix (which species each reaction consumes/produces), r the reaction rates, and J = ∂r/∂C the Jacobian (kintera even includes the temperature feedback ∂r/∂T from latent heating, via PyTorch autograd). Solving for ΔC gives the concentration change; it is converted to a density change Δρ and added back to the conserved tracers. Element mass is conserved by construction.

6Outputs & what the run produces

NetCDF snapshots (every 10⁵ s)

prim = ρ, mass fractions, velocities, P.   thermo = T, sound speed, γ.   uov = user variable (here qtol, the total condensible loading).   path = column-integrated condensate (kg m⁻²) for each species — the model's "cloud map".

Restart dumps (every 2×10⁶ s)

Full state for resuming. The driver supports --restart to continue a run, reloading block_vars and the simulation clock instead of rebuilding the initial profile.

What you'd expect to see physically

As the run proceeds, bottom heating launches buoyant plumes. Where each plume cools to a species' condensation temperature, that vapor nucleates into cloud, coagulates into precip, and rains down — re-evaporating below. Because the three species saturate at different temperatures, they sort into stacked cloud decks, the deepest/warmest being water, then NH₄SH, then ammonia ice highest and coldest — the classic Jovian cloud structure.
