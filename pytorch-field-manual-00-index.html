Part I — Orientation

How to read this manual.

The series is a ladder. The core track (Volumes I–IV) takes you from zero to a competent, efficient practitioner who can train real models well. The two extended tracks (V and VI) then go deep on the two areas most people specialize in today: large language models and computer vision. You can climb the whole ladder or jump to the rung you need.

01Who this is for, and what it assumes

This manual assumes you can read and write basic Python — functions, loops, classes, lists and dictionaries — and nothing more. It does not assume you know machine learning, calculus beyond the idea of a slope, or any linear algebra past "a matrix is a grid of numbers." Everything else is built up from the ground, in the order you need it.

The style is deliberately code-first. Rather than open with pages of theory, each chapter shows you the PyTorch that does the thing, then explains what every line means and why it works. Concepts are never skipped — they arrive exactly when the code forces you to understand them. By the end you should be able to read almost any PyTorch codebase and know what it is doing and why.

The one habit that matters

Type the code out and run it. Change a number, break something on purpose, watch the error. PyTorch is learned in the fingers, not the eyes — a manual can only point.

03Conventions & the PyTorch version

Code blocks are dark, with the language in the corner. A leading # comment usually states the intent; the prose then unpacks it. Tensor shapes are written as comments like # (B, C, H, W) — batch, channels, height, width — because in practice most PyTorch bugs are shape bugs, and naming the shape is how you catch them.

The manual targets the PyTorch 2.x line — current as of this writing is 2.13 (mid-2026), with releases now landing roughly every two months. Almost everything here — tensors, autograd, nn.Module, the training loop, the major architectures — has been stable for years and will not change. Where a detail is version-sensitive or fast-moving (compiler internals, the newest distributed APIs, LLM tooling), the text says so rather than pretending permanence.

Fast-moving surfaces

Three areas move quickly: the torch.compile stack, large-scale distributed training (FSDP2 and friends), and the LLM ecosystem. Treat exact flags and class names in Volumes IV–V as current-but-perishable; the concepts underneath them are durable.

04The shape of every volume

Each volume is four parts of four chapters. Parts move from mechanics to judgment: the first parts show you how the tools work, the last parts show you how to use them well — how to debug, how to make things fast, and the decisions experienced practitioners make without thinking. Worked cases box up real failures and their fixes. Principle boxes hold the one idea from a chapter worth carrying forever.

Read for the code; stay for the judgment. The syntax is a week's work — knowing what to reach for is the career.
