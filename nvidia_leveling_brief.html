Section 1 — The first three minutes

Open, then stop talking.

01The opening

defaultWarm, then hand him the question

"Thanks for making time for this. Before we get to leveling — I want to say the role itself is what I'm most excited about. Performance and efficiency work on the internal research clusters is close to exactly what I've spent my career on, and our earlier conversation is a big part of why I chose this team.

On leveling: rather than open by making a case, I'd rather understand how you see it. How do you think about the difference between IC3 and IC4 on your team — what does an IC4 own that an IC3 doesn't? And where do you see me against that bar?"

Why it works

Two short paragraphs ending on a question rather than a claim. The enthusiasm is load-bearing: it tells him you're not haggling, which lowers the temperature before the substantive part begins.

altIf he's brisk

"Thanks for making time. I'll be direct — Cyndi mentioned you see me as a strong IC3, and I'd like to understand that better. Not to argue with it, but because I want to know what the bar actually is and where you see the gap.

So before I say anything about my own read: how do you think about IC3 versus IC4 here? What does an IC4 own that an IC3 doesn't, and where do you see me against it?"

Why it works

"Not to argue with it" is the load-bearing phrase — disarming, and true. Note "Cyndi mentioned" rather than "you said": accurate, and it doesn't pin him to a position he may have offered informally.

Then stop talking

The hardest part and the most important. After the question, let the silence sit. If he takes ten seconds to think, that's ten seconds of him constructing the bar you're about to answer to. People lose this conversation by filling that gap. Take visible notes on his answer — it signals you treat his criteria as real rather than as an obstacle, and you'll want his exact wording later if you ask for a written review.

Four ways not to open

Numbers or the comp form — level first, comp is downstream. Apology — "I hope it's okay to ask" concedes that asking is presumptuous; it isn't. Your résumé — he's read it, and reciting it before he's named the bar means arguing at a target you can't see. "I was told I'm a strong IC3, but…" — the but makes it adversarial inside ten seconds.

02Your questions, in order

  • "What does an IC4 own that an IC3 doesn't?"
  • "Where do you see me against that bar? What's the specific gap?" ← the call turns on his answer
  • "How does project assignment work by level — is scope allocated by level, or by fit?"
  • "What's typical time-to-promotion here, and what's the process?"
  • "Is a level review possible before a full annual cycle, if the evidence is there?"
  • "Is leveling your call, or calibrated more broadly? If broader, is there a way to put the case forward?"
  • "If the level is settled, what else has flexibility — particularly the equity grant?"

03What "scope" actually means

Scope isn't a vague word for "important work." In every engineering ladder it decomposes into roughly six dimensions. Use this to interpret his answer to question 1 — whichever dimensions he names are the ones you answer to, and the ones he skips are not your problem.

DimensionIC3IC4
Problem definitionGiven a defined problemIdentifies which problem is worth solving
AmbiguityWorks to a specWrites the spec
Blast radiusYour own workOther people's work depends on your decisions
Time horizonSprint to quarterQuarter to year
AccountabilityThe task completesThe outcome happens
Failure modeYou're behindThe team went the wrong direction

This is why one line does so much work: "I want scope where I'm defining the problem, not just the solution." It names the boundary in the ladder's own vocabulary — row one of that table — and it signals you understand what the level measures rather than just wanting the number attached to it.

It also gives you a factual way to describe your own history. "I was given open-ended research problems rather than defined tasks" is a scope claim with a verifiable shape. "I did important work" is not.

Section 2 — Language and scripts

How to say it, and what to say.

04The line between senior and entitled

One pattern unifies it: entitlement centers you; scope-talk centers the work. Two tests you can run on any sentence before it leaves your mouth.

  • Could someone disagree without disputing a fact? "I owned pydisort end to end and 239,000 people downloaded it" — hard to argue with. "I'm operating at IC4" — pure opinion, and it invites him to hold the opposing one.
  • Does the sentence describe what you did, or what you should get? The first is evidence. The second is a claim you're asking him to grant.
Sounds entitledSounds senior
"I should be IC4.""Here's what I've owned — where does that land for you?"
"I have a PhD, that's worth two years.""I'd flag the PhD, since it's counted as experience-equivalent in some places and not others."
"People less senior than me are at IC4."(never say anything comparative — the fastest way to lose the room)
"I deserve this level.""Help me understand the gap. What would you need to see?"
"The market pays more than this.""Given there's no bonus at this level, is there room on the grant?"
"My last company held me back.""The work I was given was open-ended rather than maintenance."
"It's unfair that I'm leveled here.""If we start at IC3, can we write down what IC4 would look like?"
The right column isn't softer

It's harder to refuse, because none of it asks him to agree with an opinion. Every line on the right is either a fact he can verify or a question he has to answer.

05The scripts

Speakable language for each of his likely moves. Short by design — nobody delivers a paragraph in a live call. The pattern is always: concede something real, answer the specific gap, hand the question back.

he says"You're a strong IC3, but IC4 needs demonstrated scope."

"Fair. Let me test that against how I think about scope — for me it's ownership and blast radius rather than headcount. The clearest case is pydisort: I designed the API, shipped it, and I've maintained it for years. Around 239,000 downloads, roughly a thousand times faster than what it replaced. That's something I owned end to end that other people now depend on.

Which dimension do you see as unproven — the technical ownership, or something more like cross-team influence?"

Delivery

The closing question offers him two options, and either one you can answer. It converts a vague objection into a specific one you can work with.

he says"I'd want to see strong growth inside the team first."

"That makes sense, and I'd rather you be right about that than talk you out of it. The one thing I'd want to think through with you: promotion usually happens from where you sit, and the projects that demonstrate IC4 scope tend to get staffed with IC4s. So how does project assignment work here — would I have a realistic path to that scope in the first year?"

Delivery

"I'd rather you be right about that than talk you out of it" is disarming and genuinely true. It lets you raise the chicken-and-egg problem as a shared concern rather than a complaint. The most delicate exchange in the call — don't rush it.

he says"Leveling is calibrated org-wide — it's not just my call."

"Understood — that's usually how it works. Two things then: is there a mechanism to put the case forward to whoever calibrates? And if the level is genuinely fixed, what does have flexibility? I'd rather we spend the time on the parts that can move."

Delivery

Don't fight this one; it's often true. The redirect turns a wall into a map, and the closing line is pragmatic rather than deflated.

he says"Your years of experience fit IC3."

"On years, I'd say look at density rather than duration. A PhD, national-lab GPU systems work, a maintained open-source library with real users, and landed contributions to Velox and RAPIDS is a lot of surface area for the time. I'd also flag the PhD explicitly, since I know it gets counted as experience-equivalent in some places and not in others."

Delivery

Make this point once and move on — it's your weakest ground and over-pressing reads as entitlement. The PhD line raises it without demanding it be counted.

he says"Your background is research, not product engineering."

"I'd push back gently there. pydisort is versioned, packaged, supported software with real users — I've fielded issues on it for years. That's product engineering, just not at a product company. And Velox and cuDF are production codebases with high review bars; landing GPU-to-GPU RDMA exchange into Velox wasn't a research prototype."

Delivery

"I'd push back gently there" signals appropriate confidence without heat. Your strongest rebuttal — and his team runs internal research clusters, so your background is on-target, not adjacent.

he says"The bands overlap — comp will be similar either way."

"That's a fair point on cash. Which actually makes me more curious about the other side of it — if the compensation is similar either way, what's the practical difference in the work? That's the part I care about more."

Delivery

Either answer helps you. If he says "not much difference," the level should be the higher one. If he names real scope differences, that's exactly what you want — so now you're talking about how to get there.

Section 3 — Your affirmative case

Only after he's named the gap.

06The case, in one breath

"The pattern across my work is taking ill-defined performance problems, defining the approach myself, and shipping something other people build on. pydisort is the clearest case — I designed the API, shipped it, and have maintained it for years; roughly 239,000 downloads and about 1000× faster than what it replaced. Alongside that, a 400× speedup on distributed PyTorch/Ray training, a 124× CUDA optimization, and GPU-to-GPU RDMA exchange landed in Velox. I've also already worked in the RAPIDS ecosystem, so I'd be productive in your stack quickly. That's the ownership and reach I understand IC4 to be about — but I'd rather hear where you see the gap than argue past you."

07The four worth scripting

strongestResearch-cluster fit — both sides of the machine

"One thing I'd offer that isn't obvious from the résumé: I've been on both sides of a research cluster. My PhD work and my research at Ctrip meant I was the person generating the workloads — and at LBNL I was building the GPU systems underneath them. So when a researcher on your team says their job is slow, I've been that person, and I have a sense of what they actually need versus what they're asking for. On a team running internal research clusters, I think that's worth something."

Why it lands

A fit argument, not an entitlement argument, and the one thing here nobody else in the pipeline can claim. Pair it with shipping evidence so it reads "researcher who ships," not "researcher."

use thisVertical range — concrete, never abstract

"The less common thing I'd bring is vertical range. I've optimized CUDA kernels at the instruction level, built the distributed training layer above them, run workloads on Slurm and parallel filesystems, and been the researcher consuming the output. On a research cluster most performance problems live in the seams between those layers — and I can debug across them without handing off."

Why the phrasing matters

"I connect all the layers" as a bare claim gets discounted to zero — every candidate says it. Attach the evidence and it becomes a staff-level attribute, since "operates across layer boundaries" is literally in most senior rubrics.

extract the factThe projects you were given — without the grievance

"At Haus I was consistently the person given the open-ended research problems rather than the maintenance work. What I'd want going forward is for that to be the expectation of the role rather than something allocated case by case."

The fact is excellent; the comment about it is unusable

Being repeatedly assigned ill-defined research problems while others did fixes is the IC3/IC4 distinction — row one of the scope table (§03). Someone with authority decided you were the person for ambiguous work. Say that.

But do not relay that colleagues called it unfair. That asks PY to evaluate a peer complaint with no context, take your side against absent people, and infer the flattering reading rather than the unflattering one. Even a sympathetic listener comes away with a faint impression of team friction — handed to him for free, when the underlying fact was available without it.

The second sentence above is quietly doing the leveling argument — the work was already at that altitude, I'd like the role to match — with no grievance in it and nobody named.

only if asked"What are you looking for?" — the ownership answer

"The through-line in my work has been taking ill-defined problems and owning them end to end — pydisort, the distributed training work. What I'm looking for now is a role where that's the expectation from day one rather than something I have to carve out."

And if you add one more sentence, make it this: "I want scope where I'm defining the problem, not just the solution."

The rules

Use this only as the answer to what you want — never as evidence for IC4. Desire isn't scope, and "I want more ownership" quietly implies I don't have much now. Cut "challenges" (abstract, reads junior). Drop "next stage" (reads as stepping-stone — talk about his team).

Section 4 — Landing it

The asks, and the close.

09The two asks that matter most

criticalThe dated review with named criteria

"Here's what would make me comfortable. If we start at IC3, could we write down now what you'd want to see for IC4, and a point at which we'd look at it together — six months, say? Not a promise of the outcome, just criteria and a date. I'd rather aim at something specific than a general intention."

Why this wording

"Not a promise of the outcome, just criteria and a date" makes it a small, reasonable ask that is genuinely hard to refuse. If he won't commit to either the date or the criteria, that is itself information about how promotion actually works there — and it's information you want before you sign.

the leverThe equity pivot, if level is settled

"If the level is settled, then I'd want to focus on the equity side. Given there's no bonus at this level, the grant is really the whole variable — is there room there? And can you tell me whether the grant is denominated in units or in dollar value?"

Why ask about units

NVIDIA offers get quoted either way, and on a stock this volatile the distinction can dominate everything else in the package. Asking also signals you understand how you're actually being paid.

#Fallback ladder, in priority order
1Larger initial equity grant — most discretionary lever, and it compounds through refreshes.
2Written level review, dated, with criteria — email is enough. The one people forget to ask for.
3Sign-on — easiest component to move when level is frozen.
4Top-of-band base — you're a "strong" IC3 in his own words; the band runs to $241.5K.

10The close

if it movedYou got IC4, or a real commitment

"That's great — thank you for working through it with me rather than just handing me a number. I'll get the comp expectations over to Cyndi today, and I'm genuinely looking forward to this."

if it didn'tRead it back — this is the important one

"I appreciate you being straight with me about it. Let me make sure I've got it right: we're starting at IC3, we'd look at level again around [date] against [criteria], and the flexibility is on [equity / sign-on]. Does that match your understanding?

Either way — the work is what I want to be doing, and I'd rather have a clear picture than a vague one. I'll follow up with Cyndi on the numbers."

Then send the email

A short note to PY the same day summarizing what you agreed: dates, criteria, flexible components. Friendly, not legalistic. That email is what turns a verbal "we'll revisit" into something real — and the read-back in the call is what makes the email uncontroversial.

Four things not to do, all call

Don't bluff competing offers. You have interviews, not offers. "Two other late-stage processes, nothing in hand" is true, still carries weight, and can't detonate.

Don't accept a vague "we'll revisit." Date and criteria, or it isn't a commitment.

Don't go transactional before he's named the criteria. Questions first, always.

Don't let it turn adversarial. He's your future manager, and this is his first data point on how you handle disagreement. How you argue matters as much as what you argue.

Accept a reasoned no gracefully — but ask for the structure in the same breath.

notes

  • Ladder context. NVIDIA runs IC1–IC8 with a parallel manager track. IC3 is commonly "Senior Engineer," IC4 "Senior or Staff," IC6 "Principal," IC7 "Distinguished." NVIDIA publishes no official map, and whether "Senior" means IC3 or IC4 is genuinely disputed between aggregators — which is why this conversation is about scope rather than a rubric.
  • Rough cross-company equivalents, orientation only: Google L5, Meta E5, Amazon L6 (SDE III), Microsoft 63–64, Apple ICT4 are all "Senior," and all are terminal levels where most engineers settle — roughly 85–90% of engineers at large tech companies sit at or below Senior. Amazon's ladder runs about one level off the others.
  • Comp figures mentioned in conversation are third-party aggregate estimates (levels.fyi and similar, 2026), not NVIDIA's actual bands. The IC3→IC4 step is the largest on the ladder. Orientation, not a quotable source.
  • Your bands, per Cyndi: L3 $152–241.5K, L4 $184–287.5K base. The $184–241.5K overlap is the argument that level is about scope and trajectory, not cash. From IC3 up there is no bonus — pay is base plus NSU equity, so the grant is the real variable.

The whole call in one line: make him name the bar and the gap before you make any case — then answer the specific gap with specific evidence, and if the answer is still no, trade the level for a dated review and a bigger grant. A well-reasoned no with structure attached is a good outcome. A vague yes-later with nothing written down is not.
