FRAME Chapter I

The frame · three independent axes.

The mistake Conflating multi-tenant, multi-cluster, and edge as one problem. They are three independent design dimensions; most real systems combine them, but each has its own logic.

The rule Decide each axis first. Then combine. Avoid jumping to "more clusters" as the answer to a tenancy problem, or "more namespaces" as the answer to a geographic problem.

A platform team running into trouble with isolation between teams often answers by creating more clusters. A team running into trouble with regional latency often answers by adding namespaces. Both responses miss the structure. Multi-tenant, multi-cluster, and edge/air-gapped are three independent design axes, each with its own motivation and its own toolkit. This issue walks them separately, then back together — because most production systems combine two or three, and the failure modes come from misunderstanding which axis you're actually solving on.

The three axes, named

Axis 1 — Tenancy. Multiple distinct logical consumers (teams, applications, customers, business units) sharing infrastructure. The question is isolation: how do tenants stay out of each other's way at the level of resources, network, identity, data, and blast radius. The axis runs from soft tenancy (trusted internal teams, namespace-level separation) through hard tenancy (untrusted customers, kernel-level isolation required).

Axis 2 — Geographic / control-plane distribution. Multiple Kubernetes clusters under a single platform team's responsibility. The question is consistency: how do you keep N clusters running the same versions, the same policies, the same applications, while letting them differ where they need to (region, capacity, customer). The axis runs from single-cluster (one control plane, all the eggs in one basket) through regional fleet (multi-region but cloud-managed) to edge fleet (hundreds or thousands of small clusters).

Axis 3 — Connectivity. The continuity of the network path between control plane, registry, and workload clusters. The question is delivery: how do you ship code and config when the cluster cannot reach a public registry, cannot phone home, or can only sync intermittently. The axis runs from always-connected (cloud-native default) through partially-connected (edge with periodic sync) to air-gapped (zero network path; physical media only).

Why these are independent

The instinct to conflate is strong because real systems often combine. But the design decisions on each axis are made for different reasons:

  • You might have many tenants in one cluster (internal platform; cost optimization). That's a tenancy question alone.
  • You might have many clusters with one tenant per cluster (regulated workloads; per-customer dedicated infrastructure). That's a fleet question.
  • You might have one cluster, one tenant, no network (a single edge appliance in a factory). That's a connectivity question.
  • And of course, you can have all three at once — a global SaaS serving many customers across many regions with edge points-of-presence in some locations.

FRAME Chapter II

The axes cube · where each combination lands.

The cube Three binary axes give eight cells. Real systems live in five of them; the others are dead-ends or vanishingly rare.

The use Place your problem in the cube. The cell determines which subsequent chapters are most relevant.

The three axes form a cube. Each cell is a recognizable deployment pattern with its own tooling defaults. Mapping where you actually are is the first decision; failing to recognize where you are is the most common root cause of "we keep adding clusters and it isn't helping."

The three-axis cube
Tenancy × Fleet × Connectivity — eight cells, five common patterns

Reading the cells

A — single cluster, single tenant, connected. The default; everything in Issues 01-11 lives here. No special multi-X discipline needed. If this is what you have, don't add complexity reflexively.

B — single cluster, N tenants, connected. The "internal developer platform" pattern. One cluster hosts many teams; isolation is by namespace + RBAC + quota + NetworkPolicy + (optionally) Capsule or vCluster. Chapters III-VII walk the spectrum. The right answer for most internal cost optimization.

C — N clusters, 1 tenant each, connected. The "per-customer dedicated cluster" or "per-environment cluster" pattern. Common in regulated industries, large enterprise contracts, or stack-by-region designs. Chapters VIII-XII cover fleet management; the tenancy chapters are mostly skippable.

D — N clusters, N tenants, connected. The global SaaS pattern. Multiple regional clusters, each hosting many tenants. Combines everything; the most complex common case. This is where most large SaaS platforms in 2026 live.

E — single offline cluster. A factory floor, a ship, a vehicle, an oil platform. One cluster, one workload, no internet. Connectivity is the entire problem; tenancy is trivially solved (one tenant). Chapters XIII-XVI cover the delivery mechanics.

G — edge fleet offline. Retail stores, IoT gateways, defense installations. Many small clusters at the edge, often air-gapped or intermittent. Combines fleet management with disconnected delivery. The hardest combination in practice; most chapters apply except the multi-tenant ones.

F and H — multi-tenant offline. Technically possible but vanishingly rare. If you have a single offline appliance, you usually own it entirely (no need for multi-tenancy). If you have many tenants on one edge box, you've usually misclassified what you're building.

The wrong-cell symptoms

When the architecture doesn't match the problem, a recognizable set of symptoms shows up:

  • You're in B but acting like C. "Every new team gets a cluster." Result: cluster sprawl, version drift, per-cluster ops toil, observability silos. The 2026 medium piece on this is named directly: "We Stopped Running 30 Clusters and Finally Got It Right" — the company consolidated to six.
  • You're in D but acting like B. "One mega-cluster across regions, with NetworkPolicies for isolation." Result: cross-region latency in the same cluster, blast radius is the entire fleet, regulatory questions about data residency.
  • You're in G but acting like A. "Each edge site has its own ad-hoc deploy script run by a field tech." Result: drift across sites, no audit trail, no rollback story, no fleet-level observability.
  • You're in C but acting like D. "We have multi-tenant tooling deployed even though every cluster has one customer." Result: unnecessary Capsule/vCluster overhead, RBAC complexity that nobody needs.

The good news

The tooling for each axis matured rapidly through 2024-2026. For tenancy: Capsule v0.7+, vCluster Pro, Kyverno v1.13, Karmada for distributed workloads. For fleet: Argo CD ApplicationSets with progressive sync, the Argo CD Agent technology preview (Tech Preview from OpenShift GitOps 1.17, GA planned later in 2025), Cluster API everywhere. For edge: Zarf v0.40+, K3s embedded registry mirror, Flux OCI artifacts. Most of the heavy lifting is in choosing the right combination; the individual tools are well-trodden.

The cube is a starting point, not a destination

Don't aspire to D just because it sounds more sophisticated. The right architecture is the one that matches your business shape. A small SaaS startup in A or B should stay there as long as possible. A heavy regulated workload in C might rightly never move to D. An edge product in G should resist the temptation to also become multi-tenant. The cells are reference points; the goal is to be in the right one, not a more complex one.

TENANT Chapter III

Tenancy · soft, hard, and the real spectrum.

The dichotomy Industry talks "soft" vs "hard" tenancy as if binary. The real spectrum has at least five rungs.

The rule Match the isolation rung to the threat model. Don't over-isolate trusted teams; don't under-isolate adversarial code.

Multi-tenancy is not a feature, it's a spectrum of isolation choices made against a specific threat model. The most consequential failure is using the wrong rung — either over-isolating internal teams and paying for unnecessary complexity, or under-isolating untrusted customer code and discovering a container escape was always a possibility. This chapter pins the rungs and the threat models they answer.

The Surbhi point

A frequent-enough 2026 take: multi-tenancy is not a product you buy or a feature you enable. It is an engineering discipline — a set of deliberate decisions about isolation, access, resource management, and visibility that together create an environment where multiple teams can share infrastructure safely. The companies getting this right in 2026 are not the ones with the most sophisticated tools. They are the ones that implemented the fundamentals correctly — namespace design, RBAC, NetworkPolicies, resource quotas — and built consistently on top of them.

The threat-model triage

Before picking a rung, name the threat. The four common categories:

  • Accidental. A team's misconfigured Deployment consumes all CPU and starves others. ResourceQuota at rung 1 stops this; no rung 4+ needed.
  • Curious. A team's engineer pokes at another team's pod logs out of curiosity. Strong RBAC + audit logging at rung 1-2 stops this.
  • Adversarial-untargeted. Malicious code (compromised dependency, cryptominer) running in one tenant's pod. Rung 4 (sandboxed runtime) is the bar.
  • Adversarial-targeted. Hostile actor specifically trying to break tenant isolation. Rung 5 (cluster-per-tenant) is the answer; anything less assumes a kernel-level CVE-free world.

The economics

Each rung has a different cost shape:

  • Rung 1-2: near-zero per-tenant overhead. One cluster supports hundreds of tenants. Cost is platform-team engineering time to maintain policies.
  • Rung 3 (vCluster): small per-tenant overhead (~200-500 MB RAM + minor CPU for control plane). Hundreds of tenants per host cluster still feasible. Cost is operational complexity of managing many virtual control planes.
  • Rung 4 (sandboxed runtime + node pools): meaningful per-tenant overhead. Each tenant or tenant-group gets its own node pool. Costs scale with N_tenants × node-pool-minimum.
  • Rung 5 (cluster-per-tenant): high per-tenant overhead. Each tenant pays for its own control plane (managed K8s: ~$73/month per control plane; self-managed: a few VMs). Operationally costly without automation.

The wrong-rung symptoms

Recognizable patterns of mismatch:

  • Rung too low for threat: "Internal" tenant turns out to host third-party JavaScript. Cross-tenant privilege escalation is possible. Bump to rung 4.
  • Rung too high for threat: Internal teams given cluster-per-team. Cluster sprawl; tens of clusters running near-identical platform stacks. Consolidate to rung 2 or 3.
  • Mixed rungs with shared resources: Some tenants on rung 1 sharing nodes with rung-4 hardened workloads. The shared node is rung 1, not rung 4. Either upgrade all, or physically separate.
  • Wrong-rung debugging: A noisy-neighbor incident at rung 2 gets misdiagnosed as needing kernel isolation. The real fix is a tighter ResourceQuota or PriorityClass at the same rung.
The default that suits most

For internal-only platforms (typical engineering org): rung 2 — Capsule or namespace conventions + Kyverno + NetworkPolicies. That's enough. Rung 3 (vCluster) for the small fraction of tenants needing CRD-level isolation (often: platform teams running platform tools that need cluster-admin within their tenant scope). Rung 4+ only when actually hosting untrusted external code.

TENANT Chapter IV

Namespace-per-tenant · the default that works.

The shape One namespace per tenant. RBAC scoped. Quota set. NetworkPolicy enforced. PodSecurity admission applied. The dull, reliable baseline.

The gap Native namespaces are flat — no hierarchy, no per-tenant ownership concept. Frameworks like Capsule add that on top.

The starting point for any multi-tenant Kubernetes platform is namespace-per-tenant. The mechanism is decades-mature, well-understood, and sufficient for most internal cases. The cost is engineering discipline — you have to actually configure all the primitives correctly, every time, for every tenant. The frameworks in Chapter V address that part; this chapter pins what each primitive does and how they compose.

The minimum tenant namespace

apiVersion: v1

RBAC scoped to the tenant

# Tenant admin — full namespace-scoped admin (NOT cluster-admin)
apiVersion: rbac.authorization.k8s.io/v1
kind: RoleBinding
metadata:
  name: tenant-admin
  namespace: tenant-acme
subjects:
  - kind: Group
    name: acme:platform-team           # from your IdP
    apiGroup: rbac.authorization.k8s.io
roleRef:
  kind: ClusterRole
  name: admin                          # built-in K8s admin, scoped via RoleBinding
  apiGroup: rbac.authorization.k8s.io
---
# Tenant developer — edit but not RBAC management
apiVersion: rbac.authorization.k8s.io/v1
kind: RoleBinding
metadata:
  name: tenant-dev
  namespace: tenant-acme
subjects:
  - kind: Group
    name: acme:engineers
    apiGroup: rbac.authorization.k8s.io

  kind: ClusterRole
  name: edit

Two patterns matter. First, never bind a tenant principal to cluster-admin or any ClusterRoleBinding — that breaks the tenant boundary instantly. Always use RoleBinding (namespace-scoped) referencing a ClusterRole. Second, the built-in admin, edit, view ClusterRoles are designed exactly for this — don't hand-roll Roles unless you genuinely need a more restricted permission set.

NetworkPolicy default-deny + explicit allows

# 1. Default deny all ingress and egress in the tenant namespace
apiVersion: networking.k8s.io/v1
kind: NetworkPolicy
metadata:
  name: default-deny
  namespace: tenant-acme
spec:
  podSelector: {}
  policyTypes: [Ingress, Egress]
---
# 2. Allow intra-tenant traffic (any pod in tenant-acme can talk to any other)
apiVersion: networking.k8s.io/v1
kind: NetworkPolicy
metadata:
  name: allow-intra-tenant
  namespace: tenant-acme
spec:
  podSelector: {}
  policyTypes: [Ingress, Egress]
  ingress:
    - from:
        - namespaceSelector:
            matchLabels:
              tenant.platform.example.com/name: acme
  egress:
    - to:
        - namespaceSelector:
            matchLabels:
              tenant.platform.example.com/name: acme
---
# 3. Allow egress to platform services (DNS, observability, registry)
apiVersion: networking.k8s.io/v1
kind: NetworkPolicy
metadata:
  name: allow-platform-egress
  namespace: tenant-acme
spec:
  podSelector: {}
  policyTypes: [Egress]
  egress:
    - to:
        - namespaceSelector:
            matchLabels: { kubernetes.io/metadata.name: kube-system }
      ports:
        - { protocol: UDP, port: 53 }   # DNS
    - to:
        - namespaceSelector:
            matchLabels: { kubernetes.io/metadata.name: observability }

TENANT Chapter V

Capsule, HNC, and Tenant CRDs.

The shift From per-namespace YAML files to declarative Tenant resources. One spec generates the namespace + quota + RBAC + policies.

The 2026 state HNC archived in April 2025. Capsule is the surviving native-namespace tenancy framework. Kyverno + Capsule is the common combination.

The frameworks above the native primitives serve one purpose: stop you from having to maintain N copies of the same YAML by hand. A Tenant CRD captures the tenant's identity, quotas, RBAC subjects, network rules, and policy posture in one declarative spec; the operator materializes the underlying primitives. The same pattern applies whether you build it yourself or adopt an existing framework.

The Capsule Tenant CRD in practice

apiVersion: capsule.clastix.io/v1beta2
kind: Tenant
metadata:
  name: acme
spec:
  # Who owns the tenant — these subjects get tenant-admin equivalent
  owners:
    - kind: Group
      name: acme:platform-team
    - kind: User
      name: alice@acme.example.com
  # Cluster-wide cap; tenant can create namespaces freely up to this
  namespaceOptions:
    quota: 8
    additionalMetadata:
      labels:
        tenant.platform.example.com/name: acme
  # Inherited by every namespace the tenant creates
  resourceQuotas:
    scope: Tenant
    items:
      - hard:
          requests.cpu: "100"
          requests.memory: "200Gi"
          limits.cpu: "150"
          limits.memory: "300Gi"
  networkPolicies:
    items:
      - podSelector: {}
        policyTypes: [Ingress, Egress]
        ingress:
          - from:
              - namespaceSelector:
                  matchLabels:
                    tenant.platform.example.com/name: acme
        egress:
          - to:
              - namespaceSelector:
                  matchLabels:
                    tenant.platform.example.com/name: acme
  imagePullPolicies: [Always]
  containerRegistries:
    allowed:
      - registry.example.com
      - registry.acme.example.com
  storageClasses:
    allowed: ["fast-ssd-encrypted", "standard"]
  nodeSelector:
    workload-class: tenant-shared

Capsule aggregates multiple namespaces in a lightweight abstraction called Tenant, basically a grouping of Kubernetes Namespaces. Within each tenant, users are free to create their namespaces and share all the assigned resources. The Capsule Policy Engine keeps the different tenants isolated from each other. Network and Security Policies, Resource Quota, Limit Ranges, RBAC, and other policies defined at the tenant level are automatically inherited by all the namespaces in the tenant. The crucial property: tenant users can self-serve namespace creation within their tenant scope, without cluster-admin involvement.

What Capsule gives you (and what it doesn't)

Gives: declarative tenant identity, policy inheritance to all tenant namespaces, self-service namespace creation within quota, tenant-scoped RBAC that prevents cross-tenant leakage by design.

Doesn't give: CRD isolation (tenants share the cluster's CRDs), API server isolation (all tenants hit the same API server, etcd, scheduler — so one tenant's chatty controller affects others), full security boundary against malicious code. Per the 2026 multi-tenancy guide, Capsule does not provide CRD isolation. Tenants still share the same API server and etcd. Better suited for soft multi-tenancy than hard.

Kyverno paired with Capsule

The pairing is canonical in 2026. Capsule defines tenant boundaries and basic policies; Kyverno enforces cross-cutting rules and generates default resources. The pattern:

apiVersion: kyverno.io/v1
kind: ClusterPolicy
metadata:
  name: generate-default-deny-netpol
spec:
  rules:
    - name: generate
      match:
        any:
          - resources:
              kinds: [Namespace]
              selector:
                matchExpressions:
                  - key: tenant.platform.example.com/name
                    operator: Exists
      generate:
        kind: NetworkPolicy
        apiVersion: networking.k8s.io/v1
        name: default-deny

The HNC retirement

Hierarchical Namespace Controller was the K8s multi-tenancy working group's attempt to add namespace nesting. Per the 2026 multi-tenancy reference: the HNC repository was archived in April 2025 and is no longer maintained. We recommend migrating to Capsule or vCluster if you are currently using HNC. Its core limitation was that it did not provide stronger isolation than standard namespaces and struggled with quota inheritance across hierarchies. If you find HNC in an existing codebase: plan a migration. New designs should not adopt it.

The Tenant-as-API pattern

Cross-reference Issue 10. Capsule's Tenant CRD is exactly the kind of platform API discussed there — a high-level abstraction that hides ten lower-level primitives behind one developer-friendly spec. The right place to expose it is via a Backstage template ("Create new tenant") that materializes the Tenant resource, registers the tenant in the catalog, sets up CI integrations, and creates initial RBAC bindings via a PR against the GitOps repo.

The minimum viable Tenant API

Even without adopting Capsule directly, the Tenant pattern is worth replicating in your own platform: a single CRD or YAML schema that captures tenant identity + quotas + RBAC subjects + network posture + registry allow-list + storage class allow-list, then materializes the K8s primitives via either an operator or a Helm-rendered set. The discipline is the same; the tooling choice is secondary. Don't accept "every tenant is hand-crafted" as a steady state.

TENANT Chapter VI

vCluster · virtual clusters as tenancy unit.

The shift Tenants get a full Kubernetes API surface — their own CRDs, their own cluster-scoped resources — without paying for a full cluster.

The mechanic A lightweight API server (k3s, k0s, or vanilla) runs inside a namespace on the host. A Syncer translates virtual-cluster pods into host-cluster pods.

When tenants need cluster-admin within their boundary — to install CRDs, run operators, deploy admission webhooks, or to interact with tools assuming cluster-scoped permissions — namespace-based tenancy stops being enough. vCluster fills this gap: each tenant gets a real-looking Kubernetes API, isolated from other tenants and from cluster-scoped resources of the host, while still sharing the host's worker nodes and physical infrastructure. It's rung 3 on the spectrum from Chapter III.

How vCluster works, mechanically

Created by Loft Labs, vcluster is an open-source tool that allows you to run a fully functional Kubernetes cluster inside a namespace of another Kubernetes cluster (the host cluster). Instead of spinning up physical AWS infrastructure, vcluster deploys a lightweight API server (typically k3s, k0s, or a zero-etcd native Kubernetes API) and a controller manager inside a standard Kubernetes StatefulSet within the host cluster. Crucially, a virtual cluster does not have its own worker nodes or a local scheduler. Instead, it relies on a component called the Syncer. When a developer deploys a pod to the virtual cluster, the Syncer intercepts the request. It dynamically translates the pod definition and pushes it to the underlying host cluster's scheduler.


      
What the tenant sees

      
From inside the vCluster, the tenant has cluster-admin and a real-feeling API surface:

      
    
        
  • kubectl get nodes shows virtual nodes (synthesized from host node info, with optional renaming).
    • 
        
  • kubectl apply -f my-crd.yaml installs a CRD scoped to this vCluster. Other tenants don't see it; the host doesn't see it.
    • 
        
  • kubectl create namespace prod creates a namespace inside the vCluster. The host sees only one wrapping namespace.
    • 
        
  • kubectl get clusterroles returns the vCluster's roles, not the host's.
    • 
      


      
What's actually shared with the host

      
Worker nodes (the pods physically run on host nodes). Kernel (same kernel across all tenants — this is why rung 3 is not a security boundary against kernel-level adversaries). CSI volumes (the host CSI provisions, the syncer creates synced PVCs in the host namespace). Service CIDR (CoreDNS in the vCluster forwards to the host's DNS). Image registry pulls (host's containerd does the pulling).

      
The shared kernel is the central limitation. vCluster is rung 3, not rung 4. For untrusted code, combine with sandboxed runtime (gVisor, Kata) — Chapter VII.


      
When vCluster is the right answer

      
    
        
  • Tenant needs CRDs scoped to themselves. Platform teams running platform-stacks (Argo CD, cert-manager, Crossplane, custom operators) that need cluster-scoped resources without polluting the host.
    • 
        
  • Test/staging environment per developer. Each engineer gets a vCluster; full API surface for testing; tear down at end of day.
    • 
        
  • Customer-facing PaaS where customers run their own controllers. Heroku-style platforms where customer code includes their own operators and CRDs.
    • 
        
  • Compliance / data residency where you need separate apparent clusters but real infrastructure efficiency. The vCluster is the audit boundary; the host is the cost boundary.
    • 
      


      
When vCluster is the wrong answer

      
    
        
  • Trusted internal teams with no CRD needs. Rung 2 with Capsule is simpler.
    • 
        
  • Untrusted external code. Shared kernel; rung 4-5 required.
    • 
        
  • Heavy CRD-using workloads at high tenant count. Each vCluster runs its own controllers; the aggregate control plane cost can exceed savings vs separate clusters.
    • 
      


      
Cost shape

      
A vCluster control plane costs roughly 200-500 MB RAM and ~0.1-0.3 CPU at idle. With aggressive sleep-mode (vCluster Pro), idle vClusters can scale their control planes to zero. FinOps reports on vCluster adoption commonly cite 30-80% control plane cost savings versus cluster-per-tenant approaches, particularly in environments previously suffering from cluster sprawl. For 50 internal team-level tenants previously each running a small managed K8s cluster, vCluster-on-shared-host can be a 60-80% cost reduction.

Tenant isolation · network, storage, identity.

The four planes Compute (CPU/memory/kernel), network (pod-to-pod, egress, ingress), storage (volumes, snapshots, backups), identity (RBAC + Workload Identity + secrets).

The rule Isolation must hold on every plane. A perfect NetworkPolicy doesn't help if both tenants share a writable PVC mount path.

Tenancy isolation is a property of four planes: compute, network, storage, and identity. A common failure pattern is achieving strong isolation on one plane while leaving another exposed. This chapter walks each plane's primitives and the typical leaks.

Compute isolation

Pod-level: ResourceQuotas and LimitRanges (Chapter IV) prevent resource starvation. PriorityClasses prevent low-priority tenant work from preempting high-priority. Node-level: per-tenant node pools with taints + tolerations + nodeSelector ensure tenant pods land only on their pool. Kernel-level: rung 4 sandboxed runtimes (gVisor, Kata) provide kernel separation when needed.

Network isolation

Three sub-questions: pod-to-pod (within and across tenants), egress (to internet, to internal services), ingress (from outside to tenant services). NetworkPolicy covers pod-to-pod and egress at L3/L4; service mesh (Istio, Linkerd, Cilium) covers L7 with mTLS, authn/z. Cross-reference Issue 09 for the broader story.

Default-deny is the bedrock pattern (Chapter IV). Explicit allows for intra-tenant, DNS, observability collector, platform services. For shared services consumed by all tenants (database connection pooler, secret service, etc.), use NetworkPolicies with namespaceSelectors that match all tenant namespaces.

Storage isolation

The plane teams most often forget. The questions:

  • StorageClass allow-list per tenant. Without this, a tenant can request a different storage class than intended (perhaps a premium one, perhaps one without encryption). Capsule's storageClasses.allowed enforces this.
  • PVC quotas. Per-tenant cap on total storage and PVC count.
  • Volume snapshot scope. By default, a VolumeSnapshot in namespace A can be sourced by a PVC in namespace B if the snapshot was made cluster-scoped. Use VolumeSnapshotContent's retainPolicy and snapshot RBAC carefully.
  • Backup separation. Velero or similar tools — back up tenants to separate destinations or with tenant-scoped credentials. Otherwise a backup compromise leaks all tenants.
  • Filesystem ownership inside the pod. Pods running as different UIDs in different tenants prevent accidentally readable on-disk artifacts.

Identity isolation

Three sub-layers:

  • Human identity (Kubernetes RBAC). Tenant admins and developers authenticated via OIDC from a central IdP (Okta, Entra, Google). Groups map to tenant-scoped RoleBindings. Never bind groups to ClusterRoleBindings; that's instant cross-tenant leakage.
  • Workload identity (Pod → external service). Each tenant's pods need their own credentials to reach external services (cloud APIs, internal databases, S3 buckets). The 2026 pattern is Workload Identity Federation (IRSA on AWS, Workload Identity on GCP/Azure, SPIFFE elsewhere). Cross-reference Chapter XI for fleet-wide identity.
  • Secrets. Tenant secrets must not be readable across tenants. ExternalSecrets Operator with per-tenant scope; or HashiCorp Vault with per-tenant policies; or Sealed Secrets with per-tenant encryption keys.

The "show me the holes" exercise

Once a year: deliberately set up two test tenants. Attempt to:

  • Tenant A: curl tenant B's services by name. Expected: NetworkPolicy blocks. Reality often: works.
  • Tenant A: list namespaces, ClusterRoles, Nodes. Expected: denied. Reality often: list namespaces succeeds.
  • Tenant A: kubectl exec into tenant B's pod. Expected: denied. Usually works correctly.
  • Tenant A: schedule a Pod onto tenant B's node pool. Expected: blocked by taint. Reality often: passes because tenant A's pod has the wrong toleration somehow.
  • Tenant A: use tenant B's service account token. Expected: token not accessible. Usually correctly blocked.
  • Tenant A: snapshot tenant B's PVC. Expected: blocked. Reality varies based on CSI driver and snapshot RBAC.

Document what passes and fails. The failures are your next quarter's priority list.

The compounding-isolation principle

Isolation on each plane is necessary; isolation on each plane is not sufficient. A tenant could have perfect NetworkPolicy isolation and still leak via shared etcd reads, shared image pull secrets, or shared storage class permissions. The "show me the holes" exercise — run actively, not as a one-time setup — is the only honest way to validate. Cross-reference Issue 09 Chapter XIV on operational verification.

Multi-cluster topologies · hub-spoke, mesh, federation.

The three shapes Hub-spoke (one control cluster manages N workload clusters), mesh (clusters peer with each other), federation (a single logical control plane spans clusters).

The choice Hub-spoke is the most common 2026 default. Federation is rare. Mesh is for cross-cluster networking, not for control-plane unification.

Three topology archetypes recur. Hub-spoke is by far the most common in 2026 production fleets; mesh and federation address narrower needs and come with significant operational cost. Picking the right topology determines what kinds of operations are easy versus impossible.

The hub-spoke pattern

In this model, a central hub cluster runs the ArgoCD control plane, and one or more spoke clusters run workloads. Hub Cluster Runs: ArgoCD API server, ArgoCD Repo server, ArgoCD Application Controller, ArgoCD Dex (optional, for SSO). Spoke Clusters Have: Only the ArgoCD-managed workload namespaces, Service accounts, RBAC.


      
Why hub-spoke wins as the default:

      
    
        
  • One pane of glass. Operators see all clusters from one Argo CD UI, one Backstage instance, one Grafana.
    • 
        
  • Centralized RBAC. Tenant access policies, deployment permissions, observability access — managed in one place.
    • 
        
  • Reuse the platform. The hub's platform services (cert-manager, secret store, registry mirror) are deployed once and serve all spokes.
    • 
        
  • Blast radius is the hub. A spoke outage takes one cluster down. A hub outage stops new deploys but spokes continue serving traffic.
    • 
      


      
The hub-spoke pain points

      
    
        
  • Hub scalability. A single Argo CD instance handles hundreds of clusters comfortably; at thousands, you need to shard (multiple Argo CD instances each managing a subset). For very large-scale deployments involving hundreds or thousands of clusters, it is often recommended to employ multiple Argo CD instances, each managing a subset of clusters, to distribute the load and improve performance and resilience. This approach also helps contain the "blast radius" in case of an outage in a single Argo CD instance.
    • 
        
  • Hub-to-spoke authentication. Argo CD on the hub must hold credentials to all spokes. Per-cluster Secret with kubeconfig in the argocd namespace. Rotation and audit matter.
    • 
        
  • Bandwidth. The hub continuously polls spokes for status. With hundreds of clusters, hub egress and inbound API server load on spokes are non-trivial.
    • 
      


      
The Argo CD Agent — pull-style hub-spoke

      
2026 development: the Argo CD Agent flips the connection direction. The hub still presents the single pane of glass, but the connection is initiated from each spoke, not from the hub. Per the Red Hat 2025 announcement: the Argo CD Agent is a small Go binary that runs on your remote clusters, and communicates back to the Argo CD instance on your central management cluster. The Argo CD Agent topology enables central visibility in the control plane, while offloading the workloads themselves to your remote clusters managed by the local agents. Available in technical preview from OpenShift GitOps 1.17; GA targeted later in 2025 (1.19).


      
The agent model gives:

      
    
        
  • Scalability beyond what centralized topology handles.
    • 
        
  • Better fit for spoke clusters behind firewalls or in DMZs (no inbound API access needed from hub).
    • 
        
  • Compute distribution — the heavy reconciliation work happens on each spoke.
    • 
        
  • Shared centralized components (UI, Application CRDs, RBAC) with distributed execution.
    • 
      


      
The mesh / multi-primary pattern

      
Sometimes mistakenly proposed as a control-plane alternative. The mesh pattern is about data plane: services in cluster A directly reach services in cluster B via mTLS-secured service mesh. Each cluster has its own control plane and its own GitOps; they happen to be peered at the networking layer.

The mechanism One ApplicationSet generates many Argo CD Applications from a template + a generator. The generator enumerates clusters, environments, or values; the template renders Application specs.

The leverage Adding a cluster, environment, or service to the fleet becomes a declarative change. No manual per-cluster Application objects.

ApplicationSets are the leverage point for fleet-level GitOps. ApplicationSets transform multi-cluster Kubernetes deployment management by reducing deployment time from 30+ minutes to just 5 minutes, eliminating manual errors, and providing automated scaling.

The drift problem and what fixes it

      
Without ApplicationSets, each cluster's Applications drift independently. A team patches a values file in one cluster's app; the others lag. Six months later, "production" means something different in each region. Symptoms: incident postmortems that say "we thought we'd already shipped that fix to prod-eu, but apparently it only went to prod-us." Fix: every Application owned by an ApplicationSet, never hand-created. The ApplicationSet enforces uniformity by design.


      
The ApplicationSet pitfalls

      
    
        
  • Template typos with no validation. Variables that reference nonexistent fields silently render as empty. Use goTemplate: true and the strict Go template behavior to catch these.
    • 
        
  • Generators that produce too many Applications. A Matrix of 50 clusters × 100 services = 5,000 Applications. Argo CD can handle it but reconciliation pressure goes up. Consider sharding Argo CD instances.
    • 
        
  • Deletion semantics. Removing an item from the generator deletes the Application — and, if prune: true, the workloads. The preserveResourcesOnDeletion: true spec flag prevents accidental tenant data loss.
    • 
        
  • Per-cluster overrides hidden in values files. Override sprawl across N values files becomes hard to audit. Keep overrides minimal and predictable.
    • 
      


      

        
The 80% rule for fleet GitOps

        
80% of fleet operations should be ApplicationSet-driven. New cluster registered → ApplicationSets cover it automatically. New service launched → ApplicationSet for "all services" generates Applications across clusters. The remaining 20% (one-offs, platform bootstraps, canary clusters) are stand-alone Applications. When the ApplicationSet-driven share drops below 80%, you've lost the leverage.

Progressive delivery · waves, canaries, rollouts.

The shape Never deploy to all clusters at once. Wave 1 → soak → Wave 2 → soak → … Roll back at any wave on alarm.

The tools ApplicationSet Progressive Syncs for ordering. Argo Rollouts for in-cluster canary mechanics. Flagger for service-mesh-driven progressive delivery.

A change deployed to all clusters simultaneously is a fleet-wide outage waiting to happen. Progressive delivery is the discipline of staging the rollout across cluster waves, watching for symptoms after each stage, and rolling back automatically when health degrades. Two levels: cross-cluster waves (which clusters get the new version when), and in-cluster canary (within a cluster, how the new version takes traffic). Both matter; both have distinct tooling.

The wave model

Progressive Syncs in ApplicationSet

Per the 2026 ACK One docs on ApplicationSet Progressive Syncs: define a steps list; the ApplicationSet controller monitors each Application's health and moves to the next step only after all Applications in the current step reach Healthy status.

apiVersion: argoproj.io/v1alpha1
kind: ApplicationSet
metadata:
  name: payments-progressive
spec:
  generators:
    - clusters:
        selector:
          matchLabels: { service-allowed: payments }
  strategy:
    type: RollingSync
    rollingSync:
      steps:
        - matchExpressions:                           # wave 0 — canary
            - { key: wave, operator: In, values: ["0"] }
          maxUpdate: 100%
        - matchExpressions:                           # wave 1 — small prod
            - { key: wave, operator: In, values: ["1"] }
          maxUpdate: 100%
        - matchExpressions:                           # wave 2 — medium prod
            - { key: wave, operator: In, values: ["2"] }
          maxUpdate: 50%             # half at a time
        - matchExpressions:                           # wave 3 — rest
            - { key: wave, operator: In, values: ["3", "4"] }
          maxUpdate: 100%
  template:
    # ... usual template ...

Each step waits for all Applications in the step to be Healthy before advancing. If any Application's health doesn't recover, the rollout halts; an operator decides whether to fix-forward or roll back.

The soak time question

How long do you soak between waves? Three factors:

  • Detection latency. If your error budget alerts fire within 5 minutes of degradation, soak ≥ 10 minutes. If detection takes 30 minutes (slow customer reports, long aggregation windows), soak ≥ 1 hour.
  • Cache warming. If the workload has 10-minute cache warmup before new pods serve at full performance, soak ≥ 30 minutes.
  • Time-of-day correlation. Deploying at 3 AM and soaking 10 minutes catches none of the daily peak. Soak through at least one full traffic cycle for high-stakes changes.

Typical numbers in 2026 production fleets: 15-30 minutes between early waves, 1-2 hours between later waves, and "wait for next business day" for the final wave to all production clusters.

The Argo Rollouts in-cluster canary

Within each cluster reached by a wave, the new version still rolls out gradually to pods. Combine with Argo Rollouts for progressive delivery (blue/green, canary). The Rollout CRD replaces Deployment and adds canary/blue-green semantics:

apiVersion: argoproj.io/v1alpha1
kind: Rollout
metadata: { name: payments }
spec:
  replicas: 10
  strategy:
    canary:
      steps:
        - setWeight: 10           # 10% of traffic to new version
        - pause: { duration: 5m }
        - analysis:                # compare canary vs stable metrics
            templates:
              - templateName: error-rate-check
        - setWeight: 25
        - pause: { duration: 10m }
        - analysis:
            templates: [{ templateName: error-rate-check }]
        - setWeight: 50
        - pause: { duration: 30m }
        - setWeight: 100          # done — promote
      trafficRouting:
        istio:
          virtualService: { name: payments-vs }
  selector: { matchLabels: { app: payments } }
  template:
    # ... pod spec ...

The analysis steps query Prometheus for error rate, latency p99, or custom SLO indicators and either advance or roll back based on thresholds. The combination — fleet waves + in-cluster canary — gives two independent safety nets.

Flagger for mesh-driven delivery

An alternative to Argo Rollouts; Flagger uses service mesh (Istio, Linkerd, App Mesh) primitives to shift traffic during the canary. The model is similar but the traffic-splitting mechanism is the mesh, not application-level routing.

Common failure patterns and the fix

  • Wave 1 deployed but Argo CD reported Healthy too early. Healthy doesn't necessarily mean "the new version is serving requests well." Use Argo CD health checks that include real probes; pair with Rollouts analysis.
  • SLO alarms tied to a single cluster don't catch fleet-wide regression. Sum-of-clusters dashboards alongside per-cluster ones. A 1% regression per cluster compounds to a noticeable fleet problem.
  • Different clusters have different SLO baselines. Cluster A normally runs at 99.99%; cluster B at 99.5%. A drop to 99.7% is a regression for A but fine for B. Per-cluster SLO targets in the analysis, not a single fleet number.
  • Rollback procedure not rehearsed. Argo CD's rollback button works in theory; in practice teams discover the rollback rolls back Helm release N-1 but the database migration only goes forward. Rehearse rollbacks of the full system, not just the Helm release.
The deployment-from-Friday rule

The longer-standing rule: never deploy to your last wave on Friday. Most teams add: never deploy to wave 2+ on Friday. The wave model preserves this — wave 0 and 1 can run Friday morning; the larger waves wait for Monday. The progressive delivery isn't just risk reduction during the rollout, it's risk shaping over the calendar.

Cross-cluster identity · Workload Identity, SPIFFE.

The two problems Humans accessing many clusters. Workloads in one cluster authenticating to services in another cluster (or to cloud APIs).

The principle No long-lived shared credentials. Federated identity for humans; workload identity tokens for services.

Identity is where fleet complexity bites hardest. With one cluster, you can hand-craft a kubeconfig and call it done. With fifty clusters across regions and clouds, the right answer is federated identity for both humans (one IdP, RBAC mapped consistently) and workloads (no secrets in repos; trust is established by short-lived tokens). The 2026 toolkit is mature for both.

Human identity across the fleet

The pattern: one central identity provider (Okta, Entra ID, Google Workspace) issues OIDC tokens; every cluster accepts the same OIDC issuer and maps the same groups to the same RBAC. Adding a new engineer to "payments-team" group propagates to all clusters; removing them propagates instantly when their session expires.

Workload identity — pod to cloud API

The historical anti-pattern: bake a cloud credential file into the container image or a Secret. With fleet-scale, that's untenable — credentials per cluster, per service, per cloud, rotating differently. The 2026 answer is Workload Identity Federation: each cloud provider can verify a Kubernetes ServiceAccount token (via the cluster's OIDC issuer) and exchange it for a short-lived cloud credential.

apiVersion: v1

Workload identity across clusters — SPIFFE/SPIRE

FLEET Chapter XII

Cross-cluster networking · mesh, multi-cluster services.

The four scenarios User-to-cluster (ingress), pod-to-pod within cluster (CNI), pod-to-pod across clusters (mesh / MCS), pod-to-external (egress).

The rule Cross-cluster data plane is opt-in. Most services should stay local; only the explicitly-shared ones cross boundaries.

When one cluster grows into a fleet, the networking question gets thornier. Users still need to reach the right cluster; services in one cluster sometimes need to call services in another; failover requires traffic to shift cleanly when a cluster degrades. The good news: 2026 has mature primitives. The bad news: most teams enable them globally when they should be precise about which services need cross-cluster reach.

User-to-cluster: global ingress patterns

  • DNS-based routing. Round-robin or weighted DNS across regional load balancers. Simple; coarse-grained; DNS TTL is the only failover knob (slow).
  • Global load balancer. Single global anycast IP fronts regional clusters. AWS Global Accelerator, GCP Global Load Balancer, Azure Front Door. Health checks per backend region; automatic failover.
  • Multi-cluster Ingress. GKE Multi-Cluster Ingress, AKS Ingress controllers with shared backend pools. Map multiple GKE clusters to a single Google Cloud Load Balancer, with one cluster as the control point for the Ingress controller.
  • Service mesh ingress. Istio multi-cluster, Linkerd multi-cluster. The ingress is the mesh gateway; routing is L7 with per-request balancing.

Active-active vs active-passive vs sharded

  • Active-active. Each region serves user traffic. Cross-region replication keeps state consistent (eventually or otherwise). Best latency; hardest data consistency story.
  • Active-passive. One region serves; others are warm standby. Failover via DNS/LB. Easier data story (one primary); higher steady-state cost.
  • Sharded. Each region serves a different subset of users (geographic or hash-based sharding). No cross-region replication needed; no failover unless tenants migrate. Right for compliance / data residency.

The "every region is the same" trap

Common mistake: deploy identical workloads to all regions and hope DNS routes users correctly. Reality: regions differ in capacity, version, dependencies. Treat each region's deploy as a separate decision (which is what waves + ApplicationSets in Chapter X are for). The illusion of uniform regions hides drift; explicit per-region treatment surfaces it.

Cross-cluster egress and observability

  • Telemetry collection. OTel Collector deployed per cluster forwards to a central backend. Cluster identity is a tag on every span/metric. Cross-cluster trace stitching only works when trace context propagates (W3C Trace Context, B3 headers).
  • Egress policies per cluster. A pod in cluster A reaching an external API should go through that region's egress, not be routed across regions.
  • Cost attribution per cluster. Cloud egress is expensive; cross-region calls show up as a line item. Audit unexpected cross-cluster traffic — often a misconfigured service mesh routing through wrong region.
The "default local, opt-in cross" principle

Adopt the inverse of the obvious: assume services are cluster-local by default; explicitly opt-in to cross-cluster reach via MCS, mesh exports, or named gateway routes. The reason: tracing dependencies across an opaque "everything mesh" topology is brutally hard at 6 PM during an incident. Specific cross-cluster bindings show up as YAML in Git; opaque mesh-wide reachability shows up only at runtime, in error logs.

Edge topology · the connectivity spectrum.

The spectrum Always-connected → high-latency → intermittent → scheduled-sync → fully air-gapped. Each requires different tooling and discipline.

The mistake Treating "edge" as one thing. A retail store with 4G is not a submarine, but both might be called "edge."

"Edge" in 2026 spans an enormous range. Some "edge" clusters are connected over reliable fiber and only "edge" because of physical location. Others have no network path at all and update via physical media. The right delivery toolkit depends entirely on which point on this spectrum you're at.

Scenario walk: the retail-store fleet

1,000 retail stores, each with one cluster running a point-of-sale, inventory, and analytics workload. 4G LTE connectivity that drops 0.5-2% of the time. Stores need to operate during connectivity drops (POS must keep working when the link is down).

The architecture: K3s on a small Intel NUC at each store. Embedded registry mirror caches images. Observability via OTel Collector buffering locally; forwards to central when connected. Secrets via ESO pulling from cloud Vault; cached in-cluster Secrets for offline operation.

The retail-fleet pattern is rung 2-3. Heavy connectivity but not always-on. Image pulls would be untenable without the embedded registry mirror; failover during connectivity drops would be brutal without offline-tolerant operators.

Scenario walk: the offshore platform

One oil rig in the North Sea. Satellite uplink, 200 ms baseline, 5% packet loss, 4-hour sync windows daily. One K8s cluster running drilling control, telemetry aggregation, predictive maintenance.

The architecture: Talos Linux + K3s nodes; full local Vault, Gitea, local registry. Updates packaged with Zarf onshore, transferred during daily window. Argo CD or Flux running locally, syncing from local Gitea. Operators built to tolerate the link being down for hours.

This is rung 3-4. The local stack is mostly self-sufficient; updates flow in batches; observability flows out in batches.

Scenario walk: the classified installation

Fully air-gapped facility. Zero network path. Updates arrive on signed external media weekly. Compliance audits require verifiable supply chain.

The architecture: Talos Linux fleet, fully managed via Zarf bundles. Each bundle is cosign-signed; SBOM included. Local registry, local Gitea, local Vault — all bootstrapped from Zarf init package. Updates: build Zarf package on connected build system, sign, transfer via approved removable media, deploy via zarf package deploy --key public.key.

This is rung 5. No assumptions about network connectivity at any point during runtime; the entire delivery is bundle-and-sneakernet.

Identify the rung honestly

The biggest edge-architecture mistake is treating a rung-3 problem with rung-1 tooling, or vice versa. A "we sometimes have flaky internet" cluster trying to GitOps from GitHub will have brutal sync stalls; a "fully air-gapped" claim that secretly has a maintenance VPN will have unreviewed delivery paths. Identify which rung each site actually sits on, and design that site's stack for that rung. Don't aspire to rung 1 uniformly; don't over-engineer toward rung 5 unnecessarily.

EDGE Chapter XIV

Registry mirroring & image caching at the edge.

The mechanism Every node redirects image pulls through a local mirror. The mirror caches on first pull, serves from cache thereafter. Air-gapped variant: mirror is pre-populated, no upstream.

The leverage Bandwidth saved (often 95%+), pull latency reduced (often 10-50×), survives upstream outages.

At the edge, the container registry shifts from a cloud convenience to a bandwidth-critical local service. The pattern in 2026 has converged on three layers: containerd-level registry mirror configuration, a local registry implementation (Harbor, Zot, Distribution, or distro-embedded), and an image-sync workflow that populates it. Each layer has clear choices; the bottlenecks come from skipping one.

The containerd registry mirror config

Containerd (the CRI runtime for every modern K8s distro) reads /etc/containerd/certs.d/ for per-registry mirror configuration. The pattern:

# Applied when no host-specific config exists
server = "https://registry.internal.example.com"

[host."https://registry.internal.example.com"]
  capabilities = ["pull", "resolve"]
  skip_verify = false
# Pulls of docker.io/* try the local mirror first; fall back to upstream if connected
server = "https://registry-1.docker.io"

[host."https://registry.internal.example.com"]
  capabilities = ["pull", "resolve"]

[host."https://registry-1.docker.io"]
  capabilities = ["pull", "resolve"]
  # This entry is removed entirely for air-gapped (rung 5)

The crucial property: the workload's image reference does not change. A pod that specifies image: nginx:1.27 still says nginx:1.27; containerd rewrites the pull target to the mirror transparently. This feature is completely transparent, which means that container images are "magically" pulled from where they need to be — developers and cluster operators do not need to maintain different versions of K8s manifests or Helm values.yaml files depending on whether they are in the air gap or not, relieving that maintenance burden.

K3s embedded registry mirror

K3s ships a built-in registry mirror that runs on every node, removing the need for a separate registry deployment for smaller edge fleets. Per the 2026 K3s docs, K3s supports embedded registry mirrors that cache images locally, enabling fully disconnected operations. Registry mirrors act as pull-through caches. When a node needs an image, it first checks the mirror. If the image exists locally, it's served immediately. If not found, the mirror attempts to pull from upstream (if connectivity exists) and caches it for future use. For air-gapped deployments, you pre-populate the mirror with all required images before deployment.

mirrors:

Bandwidth math for edge fleets

Concrete: 1,000 retail stores, each pulling an updated 500 MB image after every deploy. Without mirror: 500 GB egress per deploy (1000 × 500 MB). With local mirror per store and sync from one upstream: 500 MB upstream + 1000 × 0 MB store-to-store = 500 MB total. With pull-through at regional aggregation: 500 MB × N_regions. The savings compound with image size, fleet size, and deploy frequency.

The "containerd config drift" problem

Containerd's registry config is per-node — written to /etc/containerd/certs.d/. With 1,000 nodes, you need a mechanism to keep this consistent. Options:

  • Node bootstrap. Bake into the node image (Talos, Bottlerocket, custom AMI). The config is set once at provisioning; immutable.
  • DaemonSet placement. A DaemonSet writes /etc/containerd/certs.d/ via a hostPath mount, then signals containerd to reload. Risk: privileged hostPath.
  • cluster-network-operator or similar. For OpenShift/OKD, the cluster network operator manages this declaratively.
  • Kubernetes Image Pull Secret + per-pod imagePullPolicy. Less flexible but works for some cases.

The auth question

Local registries need credentials. Two production patterns:

  • Anonymous read, authenticated write. The mirror serves any node anonymously; the sync workflow authenticates to push. Simplest; works when the network is sufficiently segmented.
  • Token-based access via short-lived ServiceAccount tokens. ImagePullSecrets per namespace; token refresh via External Secrets Operator. Stronger; more setup.
The bandwidth audit you should run

Once a quarter: tcpdump or VPC flow logs on a representative edge node for 24 hours. Count the bytes pulled from your registry. Multiply by node count. If the number exceeds your egress budget by 2× or more, your mirror isn't actually catching most pulls — likely a containerd config issue, a missing registry rewrite, or a pull policy of Always forcing re-pulls that should be cache hits.

EDGE Chapter XV

Air-gapped delivery · Zarf, Hauler, OCI bundles.

The premise No internet. No public registry. No GitHub. Updates arrive on physical media. The whole delivery system must work from that constraint backward.

The tools Zarf (Defense Unicorns) packages everything into a single signed bundle. Hauler is the lightweight sibling. OCI artifacts via Flux/Argo OCI sources work for less-extreme cases.

Air-gapped Kubernetes inverts the cloud-native assumption that everything is reachable. The cluster cannot pull from Docker Hub, cannot fetch a Helm chart, cannot phone home for telemetry. The 2026 solution is package-everything-locally: a tool builds a bundle containing all images, charts, manifests, and even the local registry itself; a sneakernet transfers the bundle; the bundle deploys atomically inside the air gap. Zarf is the tool that has won this category in defense and regulated industries.

The Zarf model

Running Kubernetes without internet access creates an immediate dilemma. You need a container registry to pull images. But to run that registry, you need to pull the registry image itself. Welcome to the bootstrapping problem of air-gapped clusters. Zarf solves the connectivity problem by packaging everything into a single deployable artifact. Images, manifests, charts, the lot.

Zarf bundles everything required to deploy a Kubernetes application — container images, Helm charts, manifests, init scripts, and even a local container registry and package manager — into a single compressed archive.

The Zarf init package

The bootstrap problem solves cleanly: Zarf ships its own init package that contains the registry image itself. Deploying the init package establishes the in-cluster registry, then any subsequent Zarf packages reference it.

the Zarf design: the zarf-agent is a Kubernetes Mutating Webhook that intercepts resources before they enter the cluster and automatically adapts them for the air-gapped environment. When a pod is created, the webhook does two things: rewrites the image path to the internal Zarf registry (for example, docker.io/nginx:latest becomes 127.0.0.1:31999/nginx:latest-zarf-3892838293) and automatically injects the appropriate ImagePullSecrets. This works not just for simple pod deployments, but also for Flux GitRepository resources, ArgoCD applications, and Helm charts.

Hauler — the lighter alternative

      
Per the SEI report on air-gapped K8s: two main tools: Hauler and Zarf. Both tools help simplify the deployment of a private registry in the air gap. Right for: simpler air-gap delivery where teams already manage their own registry; less right when bootstrapping a new air-gapped cluster.

    • 
      


      
Each layer needs its own progressive-delivery story (Chapter X). Workload updates use wave-based ApplicationSets; OS updates use Talos' built-in rollout strategy with per-node canary; K8s upgrades use control-plane-first then node-by-node.


      
Phase 3 — Drift detection

      
The biggest enemy at edge scale: sites that diverge. A site goes offline for two weeks during a connectivity outage; sync window misses; site comes back with stale config; the operator doesn't notice. Six months later, that site is on a different version of three components than the rest of the fleet.

      
The fixes:

      
    
        
  • Argo CD's diff view across the fleet. The dashboard shows which Applications are Synced vs OutOfSync. ApplicationSets enforce that every cluster should be at the same revision; deviations are visible.
    • 
        
  • Cluster inventory dashboard. A Backstage component or Grafana panel listing every cluster, OS version, K8s version, last-sync time, sync status. Anomalies stand out.
    • 
        
  • Periodic full-resync. ApplicationSet's selfHeal: true ensures drifts in cluster state get corrected. Combined with periodic refresh of the registered cluster set, removes "phantom" clusters that haven't checked in.
    • 
        
  • Heartbeat metrics. Every cluster emits cluster_last_sync_timestamp. Alert when timestamp is older than 24h.
    • 
      


      
Phase 4 — Rollback

      
The phase teams rehearse least, regret most. Edge rollback is harder than cloud rollback because:

      
    
        
  • Operator may not have direct network access during failure.
    • 
        
  • Database / data state changes may not be reversible.
    • 
        
  • If the workload manages physical equipment (factory PLC, retail POS), rollback must happen without disrupting operations.
    • 
      


      
Production rollback patterns:

      
    
        
  • Argo CD rollback to previous revision. Works if the failure is contained to the K8s workload and the GitOps revision is intact.
    • 
        
  • Talos A/B partition rollback. The previous OS+K8s image remains on the secondary partition; one command swaps back. Survives even kernel-level failures.
    • 
        
  • Zarf "redeploy the previous package." For air-gapped sites, the previous package remains on the device; redeploying it reverses the state.
    • 
        
  • Application-level feature flags. The actual "rollback" is a flag flip, not a deploy. Most reliable for rapid response.
    • 
      


      
The progressive-rollback model

      
Rollback should itself be progressive. A bad update reaches wave 1 (3 stores). Detection at wave 1: roll back those 3 stores; the rest never got the update. Detection at wave 3 (200 stores): roll back wave 3 first; if rollback itself causes issues, halt; otherwise continue rollback to wave 2, etc. The reverse-wave is the same as the forward-wave but with the new artifact being "the previous version."

The "we don't know what's running" recovery

      
The worst edge incident: a fleet manager realizes they no longer know what's deployed across N sites. Causes: incident history of manual fixes; tools that didn't write to GitOps; field techs who edited config directly. Recovery:

      
    
        
  1. Inventory. Crawl every cluster for actual deployed versions; record to a central registry. Don't trust the GitOps source until reconciled.
    2. 
        
  2. Reconcile. For each site, compare actual vs declared. Resolve discrepancies one site at a time.
    3. 
        
  3. Enforce. Set ApplicationSet selfHeal: true; remove all manual-access paths to the cluster API.
    4. 
        
  4. Audit. Configure cluster audit log to surface any non-GitOps-originated change.
    5. 
      

      
The fix is real engineering work, often multi-month. The prevention is much cheaper: never permit manual changes at edge sites; never run kubectl commands at edges except via approved runbooks.


      

        
The edge lifecycle test you should run

        
Before a new fleet generation goes live: simulate a complete lifecycle on a test cluster. Bootstrap from nothing → deploy three workloads → progressively update one workload → induce a failure mid-update → rollback. Document the time for each step. If the whole loop takes longer than your operational SLA for handling a real incident, fix the slow phases first. Most teams discover rollback is the slowest phase and the least-tested.

Anti-patterns the picker prevents

  • Cluster sprawl as a tenancy solution. The picker forces you to answer the tenancy question separately from the fleet question. You don't end up with one-cluster-per-team for internal trust reasons.
  • Federation as a default. The picker doesn't suggest federation. Hub-spoke with ApplicationSets covers nearly all cases; if you genuinely need federation, you'll know it.
  • Service mesh as a control-plane. The picker separates fleet (control plane) from networking (data plane). You don't end up using Istio multi-cluster as a substitute for Argo CD.
  • Air-gap pretensions without air-gap discipline. The picker asks if connectivity is truly absent or just rare. Many "air-gapped" claims actually fit rung 3 or 4, which is a different architecture than rung 5.
The picker as a forcing function

Run the picker exercise with a new team member, or with stakeholders proposing a new architecture. The questions are mostly social: "do we trust these tenants?", "do we genuinely have no internet?", "how many clusters do we want to operate?" — they're not really technical. The picker's value is making these social questions explicit before the technical decisions follow.
