Part I — The cluster, physically

What a supercluster is made of.

You can't reason about scheduling, networking, or efficiency without a concrete physical model of the machine. This part builds that model from the bottom up: a single GPU node, the network that fuses thousands of them, the GB200 rack that rewrites the rules, and the node taxonomy you operate within.

01Anatomy of a MARS cluster

A MARS GPU cluster is a few thousand GPUs organized into a strict hierarchy, plus a small control plane that schedules work onto them and a storage system that feeds them. Every performance question eventually resolves to "where in this hierarchy is the bottleneck."

CLUSTER (e.g. aws-iad-cs-002 — one provider, one region) │ ├── CONTROL PLANE │ ├── head node(s) ........ runs slurmctld (the scheduler brain) + slurmdbd │ ├── login nodes ......... where users SSH in and submit jobs │ └── data-mover nodes .... high-bandwidth transfer / data-copier hosts │ ├── COMPUTE (the GPUs — the product) │ └── racks │ └── nodes (H100: 8 GPUs/node ; GB200: a 72-GPU NVLink rack) │ └── GPUs + NICs + CPUs + local NVMe (/raid) │ ├── NETWORK │ ├── compute fabric ...... IB / RoCE / EFA — the GPU-to-GPU data plane │ └── mgmt/ethernet ....... control, storage, and out-of-band │ └── STORAGE ├── parallel FS .......... Lustre / GPFS (/lustre, /home, scratch) ├── scale-out NAS ........ VAST / NFS (some clusters) └── object store ......... S3 / SwiftStack (datasets, images)

The numbers that orient you (from the team's real show_partitions output and Stormbreaker's platform list): a single cluster can hold ~1,800+ nodes; the fleet is ~18 active clusters across AWS, OCI, GCP, CoreWeave, and others. Each cluster is independent — its own Slurm controller, its own storage, its own network stack — which is exactly why "cluster scoping is mandatory" recurs through every tool (job IDs, partitions, and storage all reset per cluster).

The mental model to carry into every interview: a cluster is a tree of locality. GPUs inside a node share NVLink (fastest); nodes inside a rack/leaf share a switch (fast); racks connect through spine switches (slower); clusters connect across the WAN (slowest, rarely used for a single job). Every scheduling and efficiency decision is about keeping a job's communication as low in this tree as possible. When you hear "topology-aware," this tree is what it means.

02Inside a GPU node

The 8-GPU node (HGX H100 / H200) is the fleet's fundamental compute brick. Knowing its internal topology is what lets you reason about why a kernel, a checkpoint copy, or a collective is fast or slow.

The pieces and why each matters for efficiency:

ComponentWhy you care
NVLink + NVSwitchAll 8 GPUs talk at full NVLink bandwidth (hundreds of GB/s per GPU) through on-board NVSwitches. This is why tensor-parallel groups are kept within a node — TP is the most communication-intensive parallelism and wants the fattest link.
NICs / railsMultiple network adapters per node (often one rail per GPU or GPU-pair). "Rail-aligned" topology means GPUn on node A talks to GPUn on node B over a dedicated rail, avoiding cross-rail switch hops. Misalignment silently halves effective bandwidth.
NUMAThe two CPU sockets are separate NUMA domains; each owns half the GPUs and NICs. Pinning a process to the NUMA node local to its GPU and NIC avoids cross-socket QPI/UPI traffic — a real and often-missed dataloader/host-copy penalty.
PCIeThe path between CPU/host memory, NVMe, and the GPUs. The GPU→CPU staging phase of async checkpointing (Manual I §13) rides PCIe; a bad PCIe link shows up as one rank's stall.
local NVMe (/raid)Fast node-local scratch. The team sbcasts container images here so every node reads the image locally instead of hammering the shared filesystem at job start.
The one command that reveals it all

nvidia-smi topo -m prints the connectivity matrix — which GPUs are NVLink-connected (NV#), which share a PCIe switch (PIX/PXB), which cross a NUMA node (SYS), and which NIC is affine to which GPU. When the storage benchmark says "cross-reference a stalled rank with nvidia-smi topo," this is the tool. Being able to read this matrix and say "GPU3's checkpoint stall is because its affine NIC/PCIe path is degraded" is exactly the node-level fluency the compute interviewer is checking.

03The network is the computer

At scale, the interconnect is the supercomputer — a 1,000-GPU training job spends a large fraction of every step moving gradients and activations. The fabric topology determines how fast that movement is, and it's the single biggest performance differentiator between clusters.

The fat-tree (Clos) topology

GPU clusters are almost universally wired as a fat-tree (a folded-Clos network): leaf switches connect to nodes, spine switches connect leaves, and bandwidth is provisioned so the network is non-blocking (or near it) — every node can talk to every other at full rate, in principle.

┌──── SPINE switches ────┐ (core; connects all leaves) │ │ │ │ │ ┌──┴──┐┌──┴──┐ ┌──┴──┐ ┌──┴──┐ │LEAF ││LEAF │ │LEAF │ │LEAF │ (one leaf per rack-ish group) └──┬──┘└──┬──┘ └──┬──┘ └──┬──┘ nodes nodes nodes nodes Locality ladder (latency/​bandwidth, best → worst): 1. intra-node ............ NVLink/NVSwitch (no network at all) 2. intra-leaf ............ one switch hop (same rack group) 3. leaf→spine→leaf ........ three hops (different rack groups) 4. cross-cluster / WAN .... avoid for a single job

Two concepts the network interviewer (Aman) and the ops interviewer (McQueen/O'Kelly) both expect:

  • Oversubscription. A "non-blocking" fabric is expensive; many clusters oversubscribe at the spine (e.g., 2:1), meaning cross-leaf traffic can contend. A job placed across many leaves can hit this; a job packed into one leaf won't. This is why topology-aware scheduling matters (§12).
  • Rails & rail-alignment. Each GPU has a dedicated NIC/rail to the fabric. NCCL is rail-aware: GPUn ↔ GPUn traffic stays on rail n, so a collective is really N parallel rail-local transfers. Break rail alignment (wrong NIC binding, mismatched plugin) and you funnel everything through fewer paths.
SHARP: in-network reduction (an IB superpower)

NVIDIA's InfiniBand switches support SHARP (Scalable Hierarchical Aggregation and Reduction Protocol) — the switch itself performs the sum for an all-reduce, so gradient data is reduced in the network rather than shuttled around a ring among GPUs. This can roughly halve all-reduce traffic and latency for large jobs. It exists on IB fabrics (OCI/CoreWeave-style) but not on EFA — one concrete reason the same job performs differently across providers. Knowing SHARP exists and is fabric-specific is a strong networking signal.

04GB200 NVL72: the rack as a node

The GB200 NVL72 is the most important architectural shift in the fleet, and it deserves its own chapter because it inverts the locality model from §02–§03. Two of the team's most pointed pieces of work (the DCGM-NVLink harness, the topology utilities) exist because of it.

H100 WORLD GB200 NVL72 WORLD ────────── ───────────────── NVLink domain = 8 GPUs (one node) NVLink domain = 72 GPUs (one RACK) inter-node = IB/EFA intra-rack = MNNVL (multi-node NVLink) hosts = x86 hosts = ARM (aarch64) placement unit = node placement unit = TRAY within a BLOCK topology reasoning = leaf switch topology reasoning = which trays are free A GB200 NVL72 rack: ┌───────────────────────────────────────────────┐ │ 18 compute trays × 4 Blackwell GPUs = 72 GPUs │ │ 9 NVSwitch trays → one flat NVLink fabric │ │ all 72 GPUs talk at full NVLink BW, rack-wide │ │ Grace (ARM) CPUs paired with the GPUs │ └───────────────────────────────────────────────┘ node naming: nvl72NNN-TYY (rack NNN, tray YY)

What changes, concretely, for your work:

DimensionThe GB200 difference
Parallelism mappingYou can run tensor-parallel or expert-parallel groups across 72 GPUs at NVLink speed — impossible on H100, where TP was capped at 8. This unlocks larger TP/EP and changes the optimal parallelism layout.
PlacementWhich trays your job lands on matters. The slurm-utils get-avail-topology shows free nodes per block precisely so jobs pack into NVLink-local trays. Scattered tray placement can leave a job spanning blocks.
Architecture (ARM)Containers and binaries must be aarch64. The NCCL-plugin containers are built multi-arch for exactly this; a stray x86 image simply won't run.
The fabric-memory pathMNNVL uses CUDA fabric-memory APIs (cuMemCreate(FABRIC), cuMemSetAccess, cuMemImportFromShareableHandle). These become hot — and the DCGM-NVLink bug (Manual I §24) showed that telemetry polling can contend on the driver lock and slow these cuMem* calls ~10×.
nvloom / nvshmemNew benchmarks exist specifically to measure the MNNVL fabric (nvloom) and the NVSHMEM one-sided model (nvshmem) at rack scale — they didn't matter when NVLink stopped at 8 GPUs.

On H100 the node is the unit of NVLink locality. On GB200 the rack is. Every placement, parallelism, and telemetry decision shifts accordingly.

05Login / head / compute / data nodes

Operating literacy starts with knowing which node does what — and which you're allowed to touch. The team's "best practices" doc is explicit about this, and the Slurm/customers interviewer will absolutely check it.

Node typeRole & rules
Login nodeWhere you SSH in, edit code, and submit jobs from. Shared, modest resources. The correct place for sbatch/srun. Not for heavy compute.
Head / controller nodeRuns slurmctld (the scheduler) and slurmdbd (accounting DB). Never submit jobs from here — it's the control plane; loading it can destabilize the whole cluster. The team's rule #3: "Do NOT submit jobs from the head node."
Compute nodeThe GPU workers where jobs actually run. SSH to a compute node is usually only allowed if you have a job running on it (some clusters block it entirely). You reach them via Slurm, not directly.
Data / data-mover nodeHigh-bandwidth hosts for staging large datasets and pulling logs. On data-intensive clusters there may be an explicit login node for this. sisyphus even uses "data copier nodes" for SCP-ing execution logs.
The classic onboarding mistakes (and what they signal)

Running a build or a python train.py directly on a login node (instead of srun-ing onto a compute node), or worse on the head node, is the canonical new-user error — it degrades a shared resource and, on the head node, the scheduler itself. The team's fix is to make the right thing easy: setup-cluster.sh sets a default account, puts slurm-utils on your PATH, and provides a "dev box" pattern (srun --pty ... /bin/bash) so even interactive work lands on a compute node. If asked "what would you tell a new researcher first," the answer is this node taxonomy plus "submit from login, compute via Slurm, never touch the head node."

Part II — Slurm, the scheduler

How work gets placed.

Slurm is the operating system of the cluster — it decides which job runs where and when, binds GPUs to processes, and accounts for every GPU-second. This is the operations interview's core territory, and the layer where scheduling-goodput (Manual I, Layer 1) is won or lost. We build Slurm from its architecture up to fairshare, topology, and accounting.

06Slurm's architecture

Slurm (Simple Linux Utility for Resource Management) is three daemons and a database. Knowing the pieces lets you reason about where a scheduling problem lives.

┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐ │ HEAD NODE │ │ slurmctld ── the brain: holds the queue, makes │ │ │ scheduling decisions, tracks node state │ │ │ │ │ slurmdbd ──► MySQL ── accounting: every job's usage, │ │ associations, fairshare, limits │ └──────┬─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘ │ RPC (controls + heartbeats) ┌─────┴───────┬───────────────┬───────────────┐ v v v v slurmd slurmd slurmd slurmd ── one per compute node: (node 1) (node 2) (node 3) (node N) launches & supervises the job's tasks locally USER on a login node: sbatch / srun / squeue / sacct ── talk to slurmctld (and slurmdbd for history)
ComponentJob
slurmctldThe controller. Maintains the pending queue, runs the scheduling loop (priority + backfill), tracks every node's state, and dispatches jobs. Single point of coordination — which is why you never load the head node.
slurmdThe per-node agent. Receives "run this step" from the controller, sets up cgroups, binds GPUs, launches the user's tasks, and reports back. One slurmd per compute node.
slurmstepdA per-step shepherd that slurmd spawns to actually manage a running job step's processes.
slurmdbd + MySQLThe accounting database. Stores associations (user↔account↔partition), usage history (for fairshare), and enforces limits. sacct/sacctmgr/sreport read it.
Two scheduling loops, one big lever

slurmctld runs two passes: the main scheduler (strict priority order — start the highest-priority job that fits) and backfill (start lower-priority jobs in the gaps, but only if they finish before they'd delay a higher-priority job — which is why accurate --time limits matter; a job that lies about its runtime breaks backfill for everyone). When a researcher's small job "jumps ahead" of a big pending one, that's backfill working as designed. Understanding backfill is a senior Slurm tell.

07The job lifecycle & state machine

Every job moves through a state machine, and the terminal states are exactly the vocabulary of scheduling-layer goodput loss (Manual I §10). Reading these states is the first move in any "my job died/won't start" investigation.

submit (sbatch) │ v ┌──────────► PENDING ──────────────────────────┐ │ (reason: Priority / Resources / │ │ QOSMaxJobs / ReqNodeNotAvail …) │ │ │ scheduler picks it │ │ v │ │ CONFIGURING ──► RUNNING │ │ │ │ requeue ◄──┬─────────┬──────────┼──────┬────────┤ │ │ │ │ │ │ v v v v v v COMPLETED FAILED TIMEOUT NODE_FAIL PREEMPTED CANCELLED (exit 0) (exit≠0) (hit (hardware (higher- (scancel / --time) died) prio job) OOM kill)
StateWhat it means · efficiency angle
PENDINGWaiting. The REASON field is gold: Priority (others ahead), Resources (nothing free yet), QOSMax* (you hit a limit), ReqNodeNotAvail (a constraint can't be met, e.g. a topology request). Diagnose with squeue --start.
RUNNINGExecuting. This is where runtime-goodput (Layer 2) accrues.
COMPLETEDExit code 0. Clean.
FAILEDNon-zero exit. App crash, OOM, bad config — back to the researcher (or FACT for attribution).
TIMEOUTHit --time. Often means the job under-estimated, or genuinely needs checkpoint-resume to continue. A lost-work risk if checkpointing was sparse.
NODE_FAILHardware died under the job. A pure scheduling-layer loss; triggers FACT bad-node attribution and Heimdall remediation. With --requeue, Slurm re-queues automatically.
PREEMPTEDA higher-priority job took the nodes. Scheduling-layer loss; re-pays startup tax on requeue.
REQUEUEDBack to PENDING for another cycle. jobsinfo-done records one row per cycle — the execution backbone (Manual I §18).
The requeue/startup compounding loop

A job on a flaky cluster cycles RUNNING → NODE_FAIL → REQUEUED → CONFIGURING → RUNNING repeatedly. Each cycle re-pays the full startup tax (container load, model init, checkpoint load — Manual I §12) and risks lost work back to the last checkpoint. So a Layer-1 problem (bad nodes causing requeues) manifests as a Layer-2 cost (repeated startup) and a Layer-1+2 cost (lost work). When you see a high requeue count in jobsinfo-done, you've found a goodput sink — and the fix splits between ops (drain the bad nodes) and the researcher (checkpoint more often so each failure costs less).

08sbatch / srun / salloc

Three ways to ask Slurm for resources, and knowing when to use each is basic operational fluency.

CommandUse
sbatchSubmit a batch script to the queue and return immediately. The script's #SBATCH directives request resources; the body runs when scheduled. The normal way to run training jobs.
srunLaunch a job step — either standalone (blocks until it runs) or, more importantly, inside an sbatch script to spawn the parallel tasks across the allocated nodes. srun is what actually starts your N processes on N nodes.
sallocGrab an allocation for interactive use — you get a shell with nodes reserved, then run srun commands by hand. Good for debugging and the "dev box" pattern.

The sbatch + srun pattern

#!/bin/bash
#SBATCH --job-name=nemotron-pretrain
#SBATCH --account=av_alpamayo_training   # the PPP project → Slurm account
#SBATCH --partition=batch                # which queue (see §09)
#SBATCH --nodes=8                        # 8 nodes...
#SBATCH --gpus-per-node=8                # ...× 8 GPUs = 64 GPUs
#SBATCH --ntasks-per-node=8              # one task PER GPU (the convention)
#SBATCH --time=04:00:00                  # walltime (honest = good backfill)
#SBATCH --requeue                        # auto-requeue on node failure
#SBATCH --output=%x_%j.out               # %x=jobname %j=jobid

# The body runs ONCE on the first node. srun fans out the real work:
srun --container-image=./pytorch.sqsh \
     --container-mounts=/lustre:/lustre \
     python train.py --config nemotron5.yaml

The key mental model: the sbatch body executes once on the first allocated node; srun is what replicates your command across all tasks/nodes. A common bug is putting the training command outside srun — then it runs on one node only, and the other 7 sit idle (a goodput catastrophe that looks like a slow job).

The rule the storage tests encode: ntasks-per-node == gpus-per-node

The convention across the team's scripts is one task (one process) per GPU, so --ntasks-per-node must equal --gpus-per-node. The storage benchmark's parameter table literally notes "--ntasks-per-node must match --gpus-per-node." Each task then binds to its GPU via LOCAL_RANK. Getting this mismatched gives you too few or too many processes per GPU — either idle GPUs or oversubscription.

09Partitions, QoS & limits

Partitions and QoS are how a cluster carves its nodes into queues with different rules. The team's real show_partitions output is the perfect concrete example.

Partitions: queues over node sets

A partition is a named set of nodes plus policy (time limits, priority, access). From the team's actual cluster:

Partition          Nodes   State        Time limit    GPUs/Node
batch*             up      4:00:00       (default)      8
batch_large        up      4:00:00                      8
batch_large_long   up      14-00:00:0    (14 days)      8
batch_short        up      2:00:00                      8
interactive        up      4:00:00                      8
backfill           up      7-00:00:00                   8
high_prio          up      7-00:00:00                   8

cpu_long           up      7-00:00:00                   0

Reading this: batch is the default (the *), capped at 4h. Need longer? batch_large_long (14 days). Just iterating? batch_short (2h, schedules fast because it's easy to backfill). The storage-benchmark troubleshooting advice — "job stuck in Priority? try --partition=batch_short" — is exactly using a faster-scheduling queue. Different partitions can also map to different hardware (an H100 partition vs a GB200 partition).

QoS: policy modifiers

Quality of Service (--qos=) layers limits and priority on top of partitions: max jobs per user, max GPUs per account, a priority boost, preemption rights. QoS is how the cluster enforces "team A gets at most N GPUs" and "these jobs may preempt those." A QOSMaxJobsPerUserLimit reason in squeue means you hit a QoS cap, not a resource shortage — a completely different fix (wait/ask for more quota vs nothing-is-free).

"My job won't start" → read the reason, then the limit

The single most common support question. The decision tree: squeue --start shows the estimated start and the REASON. Resources → genuinely nothing free, check show_idle_nodes. Priority → others ahead, fairshare conversation or a shorter partition. QOSMax*/AssocMax* → a limit, check quota with sacctmgr. ReqNodeNotAvail → an unsatisfiable constraint (often a topology or feature request that no free node matches). Naming this tree is exactly the operational competence the Slurm interviewer wants.

10Fairshare & multifactor priority

How does Slurm order the pending queue? Through a multifactor priority score, of which fairshare is usually the dominant term. This is the mechanism behind every "why is my job behind theirs" question and the lever behind fair access — squarely in the systems/customers interview.

The priority formula

Priority = (PriorityWeightAge × age_factor) # how long it's waited + (PriorityWeightFairshare × fairshare_factor) # are you under/over your share? + (PriorityWeightQOS × qos_factor) # QoS priority boost + (PriorityWeightPartition × partition_factor) # partition priority + (PriorityWeightJobSize × size_factor) # bigger / smaller jobs − (nice / penalties) fairshare_factor: high when you've used LESS than your allocated share recently, low when you've used MORE. Decays over time (half-life).

The crucial term is fairshare. Each account (PPP project) has an allocated share of the cluster. Slurm tracks recent usage with a decaying half-life and computes a fairshare factor: use less than your share and your priority rises; burn through your share and it drops, letting others catch up. It's not a hard quota — an over-share account can still run when the cluster is idle — it's a dynamic priority that balances usage across teams over time.

CommandShows
sshare -A acctAn account's allocated share vs its recent usage and resulting fairshare factor — the "why is my priority low" answer.
sprio -j jobidThe full priority breakdown for a pending job (age/fairshare/QoS/partition components).
scontrol show job jobidEverything Slurm knows about a job, including its current priority and pending reason.

Fairshare reframes capacity fights as a self-correcting system. When a researcher complains their jobs are behind another team's, the honest, mechanism-grounded answer is: "Your account has used more than its share recently, so fairshare lowered your priority to let others catch up — it'll recover as your usage decays." Then the efficiency angle, which is your superpower here: "If your jobs weren't wasting 40% of their GPU-hours to startup and lost work, you'd accomplish the same science with less of your share, and you'd sit higher in the queue." Efficiency work directly buys queue priority. That connection — fairshare × efficiency — is a standout answer for the systems/customers interview.

11Reservations

A reservation sets aside specific nodes for a specific purpose, time window, or set of users — bypassing the normal queue. They're how the team does acceptance testing, customer guarantees, and maintenance.

  • Validation/acceptance: Stormbreaker and the cluster-workload tests "run on nodes that are part of your main Slurm pool or a reservation." A new cluster's bring-up testing runs in a reservation so it doesn't fight production jobs.
  • Customer guarantees: a team with a deadline can get a reservation guaranteeing N GPUs for a window — capacity carved out of fairshare.
  • Maintenance/draining: reservations mark nodes as off-limits while they're being repaired or updated.
# Use a reservation (the team's configs set this via env/config.sh)
$ sbatch --reservation=my_reservation train.sbatch
# Admin view: what reservations exist and which nodes they hold
$ scontrol show reservations
Reservations are the "break glass" for fair scheduling

Because reservations bypass fairshare, they're powerful and slightly dangerous — every node in a reservation is a node the general queue can't use. The judgment question an interviewer might pose: "When do you give a team a reservation vs telling them to wait in the queue?" The senior answer balances genuine business need (a hard external deadline, a coordinated multi-team run, acceptance testing) against the cost to everyone else, and prefers time-boxed reservations with a clear end. It's a fairness-vs-urgency trade, and naming it as such is the point.

12Topology-aware scheduling

This is where scheduling meets the network (§03) and the GB200 rack (§04), and it's a genuine efficiency lever — the same job runs faster when its nodes are network-close. The Slurm interviewer and the compute interviewer both care.

How Slurm knows the topology

Slurm's topology/tree plugin reads a description of the switch hierarchy (which nodes hang off which leaf, which leaves off which spine) and tries to allocate a job's nodes under as few switches as possible — minimizing the spine hops the job's collectives must cross. On a fat-tree with any oversubscription, this directly reduces communication contention.

Job needs 16 nodes. Two placements: ────────────────────────────────────────────── BAD (topology-blind): 4 nodes on leaf A, 4 on B, 4 on C, 4 on D → every all-reduce crosses the spine → contention GOOD (topology-aware): 16 nodes all under leaf A (if they fit) → all-reduce stays leaf-local → full bandwidth Slurm with topology/tree prefers the GOOD packing automatically.

The GB200 block/tray case

On GB200 NVL72 the unit is the tray within a block (a rack's NVLink domain). The team's get-avail-topology exists precisely to surface free capacity per block so jobs pack into NVLink-local trays:

$ slurm-utils/get-avail-topology
Blocks with Available Nodes
Block Name   # Available Nodes   Node List
block001     15                  nvl72011-T01, nvl72011-T02, ... T18
block021     18                  nvl72037-T01 ... T18   ← a whole block free
block017     1                   nvl72032-T13           ← nearly full, avoid

A researcher running a rack-scale job wants their nodes inside one block (one NVLink fabric), so they'd target a block with enough free trays (block021's 18, not block017's 1). This is the GB200 analog of "pack under one leaf," and being able to read this output is concrete GB200 operational fluency.

Topology constraints can also strand a job

Requesting tight topology (e.g., "all my nodes in one block/leaf") makes a job harder to schedule — if no single block has enough free trays, the job sits PENDING with ReqNodeNotAvail even though the cluster has free GPUs scattered elsewhere. So topology is a trade: tighter placement = better runtime performance but worse schedulability. The senior move is to know when a workload genuinely needs the locality (large TP/EP spanning the rack) versus when it can tolerate spread (pure data-parallel with overlappable all-reduce).

Part III — Running real workloads

From sbatch to running.

Slurm allocates nodes, but a training job is a containerized, GPU-bound, multi-process distributed program. This part covers the machinery that turns an allocation into running ranks: rootless containers (Enroot), the Slurm container plugin (Pyxis), the distributed launch, and how GPUs actually get bound to processes.

14Containers on HPC: Enroot

Every job on these clusters runs in a container, but Docker is not available — and knowing why is a litmus test for HPC literacy. The team's best-practices doc states it flatly: "Docker is not available on clusters. We use Enroot."

Why not Docker

  • Docker needs a root daemon (dockerd) and grants effectively-root capabilities — unacceptable on a shared multi-tenant cluster where users must not have root.
  • Docker's networking/namespacing model fights Slurm's resource control (cgroups, GPU binding) and the HPC fabric (it wants the host's IB/EFA devices directly, not a bridge).
  • HPC needs the container to inherit the host's high-performance devices (GPUs, the IB/EFA NICs, the parallel filesystem mounts) with near-zero overhead, not isolate them.

What Enroot does

Enroot is NVIDIA's rootless, unprivileged container runtime built for exactly this. It converts an OCI image into a flat squashfs .sqsh file and runs it as the user, with the GPUs and host fabric passed straight through.

# Import an image from NGC → a .sqsh file (this is the slow, one-time step)
$ enroot import nvcr.io#nvidia/pytorch:25.08-py3
# produces nvidia+pytorch+25.08-py3.sqsh  (squashfs: a single compressed file)

# The team's pull-image.sh wraps this to be docker-like and place it on Lustre
$ ./pull-image.sh nvcr.io#nvidia/nemo:24.12-rc0
# auto-picks a data_path (lustre/projects) and names it nemo_24.12-rc0.sqsh
PropertyEnroot vs Docker
PrivilegeFully unprivileged (runs as you). Docker needs a root daemon.
Image formatA single .sqsh squashfs file on the shared FS. Docker uses a layered local store.
Device accessGPUs + IB/EFA NICs passed through natively, full performance. Docker isolates by default.
IntegrationDesigned to be launched by Slurm via Pyxis (§15). Docker is daemon-centric.
StateRead-only image + writable overlay; artifacts in mounted /home or /lustre persist after exit.
The ARM gotcha and the /raid trick

Two operational facts worth knowing: (1) GB200 clusters are ARM, so you must import an aarch64 image — an x86 .sqsh simply won't run there (the multi-arch NCCL-plugin containers exist for this). (2) At job start, reading a multi-GB .sqsh from the shared filesystem on every node at once hammers storage; the storage benchmark's mitigation is to sbcast the image to node-local /raid first, so each node reads it locally. This is a startup-tax (container-load) optimization in action — connecting Manual I §12 to the storage layer.

16The distributed launch

A 64-GPU training job is 64 processes that must find each other, agree on ranks, and form NCCL communicators. How they bootstrap is a frequent confusion and a real interview topic (it sits between Slurm and the application).

The rank coordinate system

Every process needs three numbers and a rendezvous point:

VariableMeaning
WORLD_SIZETotal processes across all nodes (e.g., 64).
RANKThis process's global index (0…WORLD_SIZE-1).
LOCAL_RANKThis process's index within its node (0…7) — determines which GPU it binds to.
MASTER_ADDR / MASTER_PORTThe rendezvous host:port (rank 0) where everyone meets to bootstrap.

Two launch styles

srun-native (one task per GPU)

Slurm's srun --ntasks-per-node=8 launches 8 processes per node directly, and Slurm sets SLURM_PROCID→RANK, SLURM_LOCALID→LOCAL_RANK. The app reads these. Clean, Slurm-native, no extra launcher. The team's storage scripts derive MASTER_ADDR from the Slurm nodelist.

torchrun (one task per node)

srun --ntasks-per-node=1 launches one torchrun per node, and torchrun spawns the 8 local workers and sets the rank env. Common in PyTorch-land. Either works; mixing them (torchrun under 8 tasks) double-spawns and breaks.

# Deriving the rendezvous from Slurm (the common pattern)
export MASTER_ADDR=$(scontrol show hostnames $SLURM_JOB_NODELIST | head -n1)
export MASTER_PORT=29500
export WORLD_SIZE=$SLURM_NTASKS
# with one-task-per-GPU srun, the app maps SLURM_PROCID→RANK, SLURM_LOCALID→LOCAL_RANK
srun python train.py
The "hang at init" classic — and why it's a NCCL/launch question

A distributed job that hangs at startup with no error is usually a rendezvous or NCCL-bootstrap failure: wrong MASTER_ADDR (not reachable from all nodes), a firewalled port, a NIC-selection mismatch (NCCL trying an interface that can't route — fixed with NCCL_SOCKET_IFNAME/NCCL_IB_HCA), or a rank-count mismatch (WORLD_SIZE ≠ actual processes). The storage scripts expose exactly these knobs (NCCL_IB_HCA, NCCL_SOCKET_IFNAME, UCX_NET_DEVICES) because they're the usual culprits. When the rules engine fires LOG-CRASH-NCCL-TIMEOUT, this launch/bootstrap layer is where you look first.

18SPANK plugins & the task lifecycle

SPANK (Slurm Plug-in Architecture for Node and job Kontrol) lets code hook into the job/task lifecycle at well-defined points. It's how Pyxis works, and it's how Abdalla's write-tracer attaches to jobs — so understanding SPANK connects the operations layer to the efficiency tooling (and gives you something specific to discuss with the culture interviewer).

SPANK hook points (a plugin can run code at each): slurm_spank_init ............ controller/allocator startup slurm_spank_init_post_opt ... after option parsing ── per node ── slurm_spank_user_init ....... before the user's task starts (still privileged) slurm_spank_task_init ....... in the task's context, just before exec slurm_spank_task_exit ....... after the task finishes Pyxis uses these hooks to instantiate the Enroot container per task. write-tracer's SPANK plugin uses task_init / task_exit to: • POST the task's PID to the tracer's REST API on start → begin tracing • DELETE the PID on exit → stop tracing

The write-tracer integration is a clean illustration: its SPANK plugin (built against Slurm dev headers + libcurl, producing slurm_write_tracer.so, registered in plugstack.conf) automatically registers every Slurm task's PID with the eBPF tracer at task_init and unregisters at task_exit. So write tracing turns on and off exactly with the job — no researcher action required. That "instrumentation that attaches itself to the workload transparently" pattern is the team's recurring design value (make the right thing automatic).

Why this matters beyond trivia

SPANK is the mechanism behind "the platform observes jobs without the researcher doing anything." Pyxis (containers), write-tracer (I/O tracing), and in principle any future instrumentation all ride it. When an interviewer asks "how would you instrument all jobs on a cluster transparently," SPANK task_init hooks are a legitimate, fleet-native answer — you attach at task launch, with no change to anyone's training code. That's a strong systems-thinking response.

19GRES, cgroups & GPU binding

The final mechanism: how Slurm actually gives a task its GPUs and stops it from touching others'. This is the enforcement layer under --gpus-per-node, and it's where "my job sees the wrong GPUs" bugs live.

GRES — Generic RESources

GPUs are modeled as GRES (generic resources). The cluster config declares each node's GPUs (Gres=gpu:8), and a job requests them (--gpus-per-node=8 or the older --gres=gpu:8). Slurm tracks GPU allocation cluster-wide just like it tracks CPUs and memory.

cgroups — the enforcement

Slurm uses Linux cgroups to actually confine a task to its allocated GPUs, CPUs, and memory. The cgroup/devices controller exposes only the granted GPUs to the task — so even if two jobs share a node, neither can see or touch the other's GPUs. cgroups also bind CPU cores (for NUMA locality, §02) and cap memory.

Node with 8 GPUs, two 4-GPU jobs sharing it: ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────┐ │ Job A cgroup: devices = GPU0-3, cores 0-47 │ ← A sees only GPU0-3 │ Job B cgroup: devices = GPU4-7, cores 48-95 │ ← B sees only GPU4-7 └──────────────────────────────────────────────┘ Inside Job A, CUDA_VISIBLE_DEVICES / the device list shows 4 GPUs (0-3), and LOCAL_RANK (§16) picks which of those 4 each task uses.
MechanismRole
GRES (gpu)The accounting/allocation model — how many GPUs each node has and a job requests.
cgroups (devices)The enforcement — confines a task to exactly its GPUs; isolates co-located jobs.
cgroups (cpuset)Binds CPU cores, enabling NUMA-local placement relative to the task's GPUs/NICs.
CUDA_VISIBLE_DEVICESWhat the app sees — within the cgroup, the GPUs are renumbered 0…N; LOCAL_RANK selects one.
"My job sees the wrong GPU" — the binding bug

When ranks collide on GPUs or a task uses the wrong device, it's almost always a binding mismatch: the app reading a hard-coded device index instead of LOCAL_RANK, a manually-set CUDA_VISIBLE_DEVICES fighting the cgroup, or a launcher mismatch (§16) so rank↔GPU mapping is off. The fix is to let Slurm's cgroup binding own device assignment and map rank→GPU via LOCAL_RANK, never a hard-coded index. This is the kind of low-level "how does a GPU actually get attached to a process" detail that separates someone who's truly operated these clusters from someone who's only read about them.

GRES counts the GPUs, cgroups fence them, LOCAL_RANK picks one. That chain is how 64 processes land on exactly 64 GPUs without stepping on each other.

Part IV — The network fabric

Moving the gradients.

At scale the interconnect decides performance, and it's the single biggest difference between clusters — which is exactly why the team maintains per-provider NCCL plugins and an EFA-version tool. This part covers the fabrics (InfiniBand, RoCE, EFA), the NCCL transport abstraction that hides them, and how to diagnose the network when a job is comm-bound. (The collective algorithms are Manual III; here it's the wire.)

20InfiniBand: verbs & RDMA

InfiniBand (IB) is the gold-standard HPC fabric and the baseline most clusters (OCI, CoreWeave, on-prem) use. Its defining feature is RDMA — Remote Direct Memory Access — and understanding RDMA is understanding why GPU clusters are fast.

RDMA: the kernel-bypass idea

CLASSIC TCP send: RDMA send: app → copy to kernel buffer app posts a work request to the NIC → TCP/IP stack (CPU) → NIC reads app memory DIRECTLY (DMA) → NIC → wire → wire → remote NIC writes remote → remote: NIC → kernel → copy memory DIRECTLY, no remote CPU → app → done. CPU never touched the data. RDMA wins: zero-copy, kernel-bypass, near-zero CPU, very low latency. With GPUDirect RDMA, the NIC reads/writes GPU memory directly (no GPU→CPU bounce).
ConceptWhat it is
VerbsThe low-level RDMA API (libibverbs). NCCL/UCX/libfabric sit on top; you rarely call it directly, but it's the substrate.
Queue Pair (QP)The connection abstraction — a send queue + receive queue between two endpoints. A collective sets up many QPs.
GPUDirect RDMAThe NIC DMAs straight to/from GPU HBM, bypassing the CPU and host memory entirely. Essential for GPU-to-GPU bandwidth.
SHARPIn-network reduction on the IB switch (§03) — the switch sums all-reduce data. IB-only; a real differentiator vs EFA.
HCAHost Channel Adapter — the IB NIC. ibstat lists them; NCCL_IB_HCA selects which NCCL uses.
Your UCX background is directly relevant here

This is exactly the layer your prior UCX / GPU-to-GPU communications work lives in — UCX is a verbs/RDMA-level transport library, the same stratum as the NCCL net plugins. You already understand queue pairs, registered memory, one-sided transfers, and GPUDirect. For the compute/network interviewer, this is a place to go deep with genuine authority: "I've worked at the verbs/UCX layer, so I understand what NCCL is doing underneath — registering GPU buffers, posting RDMA writes over QPs, and why rail alignment and memory registration cost matter."

21RoCE: Ethernet as fabric

RoCE (RDMA over Converged Ethernet) brings RDMA semantics to Ethernet hardware — you get verbs/RDMA, but the wire is Ethernet, not IB. Some cloud GPU fabrics use it. The catch is that Ethernet wasn't built to be lossless, and RDMA hates packet loss.

DimensionInfiniBandRoCE (v2)

Why RoCE makes for subtle, cluster-specific bugs

RoCE's performance hinges on the switch fabric being correctly configured for losslessness (Priority Flow Control, ECN). When it's slightly off, you don't get a clean failure — you get intermittent NCCL slowdowns or timeouts that only appear under heavy all-reduce load, and only on that cluster. This is precisely the "same job, different cluster, mysteriously slower" pattern (Manual I §07): the fabric type and its tuning are a hidden variable. Knowing RoCE's fragility explains a whole class of provider-specific performance tickets.

22AWS EFA & libfabric

AWS doesn't offer InfiniBand — it has EFA (Elastic Fabric Adapter), its own RDMA-capable interconnect. EFA behaves differently enough that the team maintains a dedicated NCCL plugin and a version-management tool (aws-efa-tools) just for it. Knowing EFA's quirks is concrete multi-cloud fluency.

What EFA is

EFA stack on AWS GPU instances: ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────┐ │ NCCL │ │ └── aws-ofi-nccl (NCCL→libfabric plugin) │ ← the bridge the team builds │ └── libfabric (the OFI API) │ │ └── EFA device (SRD transport) │ ← AWS's RDMA-ish transport └─────────────────────────────────────────────┘ SRD = Scalable Reliable Datagram: reliable delivery WITHOUT strict ordering, with multi-path spraying across the network for high aggregate bandwidth.

aws-ofi-ncclThe plugin mapping NCCL's network ops onto libfabric/EFA. The team builds container images bundling specific aws-ofi-nccl + EFA-installer versions.

Why EFA ≠ InfiniBand (and why it matters)

  • No SHARP — no in-network reduction, so large all-reduces move more data than on an IB+SHARP fabric. The same job can be more comm-bound on AWS.
  • SRD's unordered model means performance depends heavily on the libfabric/plugin version handling multi-path well — hence the obsessive version management (§25).
  • Tuning knobs differ — EFA uses libfabric/OFI environment variables, not the IB ones. A config tuned for IB won't transfer.
The one-line EFA framing for an interview

"EFA gives you RDMA-class GPU networking on AWS via libfabric and the aws-ofi-nccl plugin, but its transport is SRD — reliable, unordered, multi-path-sprayed — and it lacks IB's SHARP in-network reduction. So on AWS, all-reduce-heavy training is more sensitive to plugin/libfabric versions and can be more communication-bound than on an IB cluster, which is exactly why there's a per-provider NCCL plugin and a version tool." That sentence demonstrates you actually understand the multi-cloud fabric reality the team lives in."

"Version management" is efficiency work wearing boring clothes. A subtly wrong EFA/libfabric/NCCL combination doesn't crash — it quietly costs every job on the cluster some fraction of network bandwidth, which shows up as lower MFU and slower steps fleet-wide. Catching and fixing it is exactly the "discover a systemic inefficiency pattern and ship a scalable fix" mandate (Manual I §04). If asked for an example of non-obvious fleet waste, "a stale network-plugin version silently capping all-reduce bandwidth across a whole cluster" is a sophisticated answer.

The network either performs or it doesn't, and on EFA the difference is often a version number, not a cable.

The tier-mismatch waste

Two classic mistakes the team's docs implicitly guard against: (1) running training I/O against /home instead of /lustre — home isn't built for parallel bandwidth, so the dataloader starves the GPUs; (2) reading a dataset directly from the object store every epoch instead of staging it to the parallel FS first — object stores have high per-request latency that murders random-access dataloading. Both show up as low GPU utilization that looks like a compute problem but is really a storage-tier problem. "Which tier is this I/O hitting?" is a legitimate early question for any starved-GPU ticket.

27Lustre: parallel striping

Lustre is the dominant parallel filesystem on the fleet (the /lustre/fsw/portfolios/... paths are everywhere). Its architecture — separating metadata from data and striping files across many servers — is what gives it the aggregate bandwidth a 256-GPU checkpoint write needs.

LUSTRE architecture: ┌──────────────┐ ┌─────────────────────────────────────────┐ │ MDS / MDT │ │ OSS 1 OSS 2 OSS 3 ... OSS N │ │ (metadata: │ │ OST OST OST OST │ │ names, │ │ │ │ │ │ │ │ dirs, layout)│ └───┼───────┼───────┼────────────┼─────────┘ └──────────────┘ └───────┴───────┴────────────┘ one big file is STRIPED across many OSTs: stripe 0 → OST1, stripe 1 → OST2, stripe 2 → OST3 ... 256-GPU checkpoint: 512 files written in PARALLEL across all OSSs/OSTs → aggregate bandwidth = sum of all the OSTs. THIS is why Lustre suits AI.

Why Lustre suits the distributed-checkpoint pattern perfectly

Recall (Manual I §13) that Megatron's async checkpoint has every rank write its own shard simultaneously — 256 GPUs → 512 files at once. Lustre's striping is built for exactly this: many writers hitting many OSTs in parallel saturates aggregate bandwidth. This is why the tensor-loading benchmark shows Lustre clusters loading a 642GB model in ~40s while the same on a per-stream NAS is far slower. When checkpoint writes are the bottleneck, the question "how is this striped, and how many OSTs are we hitting?" is the Lustre-native diagnosis.

GPFS (IBM Spectrum Scale) is the other major parallel filesystem in the AI/HPC world, and the JD names it alongside Lustre. It solves the same problem — high aggregate bandwidth via parallel access — with a different architecture, and knowing the contrast shows breadth.

The interview-ready contrast

"Both Lustre and GPFS give you parallel aggregate bandwidth for AI workloads; the practical difference is the metadata model. That's enough to show genuine familiarity without overclaiming — and the metadata-bottleneck insight is the kind of thing only someone who's thought about real workloads says.

29VAST / NFS: scale-out NAS

Not every cluster uses a parallel filesystem. Some use scale-out NAS — VAST or NFS-based storage — and the tensor-loading benchmark makes the performance difference a first-class comparison ("cw-dfw (Lustre)" vs "nscale (VAST)"). Understanding why they differ is the key to a whole class of cross-cluster storage tickets.

The fundamental difference: parallel striping vs per-stream

LUSTRE (parallel FS): VAST / NFS (scale-out NAS): one file striped across N OSTs one file served over a network stream → N disks in parallel per file → throughput tied to stream/thread count → a single big read saturates → a single-threaded reader gets ONE stream aggregate bandwidth → need MANY threads to fill bandwidth Consequence (from the storage benchmark): SGLang single-threaded load of a 642GB model: Lustre ~40s (striping fills BW even single-threaded-ish) VAST slower (one network stream per thread → single-thread is limited) Multi-threaded loading helps VAST a lot more than it helps Lustre.

The storage benchmark's own "common causes of slow loading" list names the NAS-specific culprits directly: read-ahead misconfiguration (expanding_readahead_mmap_enable on VAST/NFS), and "NFS uses 1 network stream per thread vs Lustre's parallel striping." So the same model-loading code can be healthy on Lustre and degraded on VAST purely because of how the filesystem delivers bandwidth.

PropertyScale-out NAS (VAST/NFS) implication
Bandwidth modelPer-stream/per-thread — single-threaded readers are limited; concurrency is required to fill the pipe.
Read-aheadKernel/client read-ahead settings matter a lot; misconfiguration is a top cause of slow loads.
Tuning surfaceMount options, thread counts, NFS client params — different from Lustre's striping knobs.
Where it showsModel/checkpoint loading and large sequential reads — exactly what the tensor-loading test measures.

"Same job, slower on cluster X" is very often a storage-architecture difference, not a regression. When a researcher reports model loading or checkpointing is slow on one cluster, the storage type is a prime suspect: a single-threaded loader that flies on Lustre's striping can crawl on a per-stream NAS. The fix might be "use the multi-threaded loader," "fix the read-ahead setting," or "stage to faster storage" — not "the cluster is broken." Knowing this turns a confusing cross-cluster ticket into a quick diagnosis, and it's a direct application of the JD's "fast distributed storage" stand-out skill.

Part VI — Operating the fleet

Keeping it healthy.

Hardware at this scale breaks constantly — a GPU throws an XID, a NIC degrades, a node falls over mid-job. A huge fraction of "wasted GPU-hours" traces to bad hardware that should have been caught and drained. This part is the fleet-health craft: validating nodes, reading the failure vocabulary, draining and remediating, and bringing a new cluster online.

32Health & validation: stormbreaker

The premise of stormbreaker is simple and powerful: find underperforming hardware by running real ML workloads on every node or every rack, and flag the ones that come back slow or broken. Synthetic tests miss things; real workloads surface them.

stormbreaker sweep: for each healthy node (or rack) in the Slurm pool / reservation: run a representative workload (NCCL AllReduce / AlltoAll / Loopback, Nemotron5 proxy 8B/56B, AV training, Cosmos) measure performance → node performs to baseline → OK → node underperforms / fails → FLAG as suspect hardware Only nodes in a healthy state (not DRAINED/DOWN) are selected. Platforms: A100 / H100 / L40s / B200 / B300 / GB200 / GB300 (L40s runs a reduced set — NCCL AllReduce/AlltoAll + one Nemotron proxy)

Why "run real workloads" beats pure micro-benchmarks: a node can pass a basic GPU diagnostic but still be 10% slow under a real training step because of a marginal NVLink lane, a thermal issue, or a degraded NIC. Stormbreaker catches the performance outliers, not just the dead nodes — and performance outliers are exactly the silent fleet waste (a job that lands on a 10%-slow node drags its whole collective down to that node's pace, because every all-reduce waits for the slowest rank).

The straggler-amplification insight

This is the single most important reason node-level health matters for efficiency, and a great point to make: in synchronous distributed training, one slow GPU slows the entire job. Every step has an all-reduce barrier — all ranks wait for the slowest. So a single marginal node in a 512-GPU job doesn't waste 1/512 of the throughput; it caps all 512 GPUs at its degraded speed. Finding and draining that one node (stormbreaker's job, confirmed by the WB-ITER-VARIANCE straggler rule from Manual I) can lift the whole job's goodput. That asymmetry — one bad node, fleet-wide cost — is why so much ops effort goes into health validation.

33XID codes & bad-node detection

When a GPU misbehaves, the driver logs an XID — a numeric error code that is the canonical vocabulary of GPU failure. FACT correlates XIDs to job disruptions (CS-XID-CORRELATED), and reading them is core ops fluency.

XID (class)Meaning · response
XID 13 / 31GPU memory / illegal-address errors — often app bugs (out-of-bounds), but can be failing memory. Correlate: one app vs one GPU across apps.
XID 48 / 63 / 64Double-bit ECC errors / row-remapping — genuine hardware memory faults. Drain the node; the GPU needs attention.
XID 79GPU "fallen off the bus" — the GPU stopped responding (PCIe/power/thermal). Hard hardware failure; drain immediately.
XID 94 / 95Contained/uncontained ECC errors — newer-arch memory error signaling.
XID 119 / 120 (GSP)GPU System Processor errors — firmware/driver-level; often need a reset or driver action.
NVLink errors (e.g. 145)NVLink/fabric faults — especially relevant on GB200's rack-scale NVLink. A degraded link starves the whole NVLink domain.

The diagnostic flow: an XID appears in the node's dmesg / syslog, FACT captures it and (where it can) links it to the job disruption it caused, attaching it to that execution's failure attribution (hw_cause, s_epilog_bad_nodes). The key skill is distinguishing app-caused XIDs (an illegal memory access from a buggy kernel — same XID follows the app across nodes) from hardware XIDs (ECC/bus/NVLink faults — the XID follows the node across apps). One is a researcher conversation; the other is a drain.

How the team's tools surface this

The sdl test in mlops-tests ("detect random hardware and systematic defects") and the dcgm diagnostic catch many of these proactively; FACT's XID parser correlates them after the fact; nvidia-smi -q shows ECC error counts and remapped rows per GPU. When investigating a job that keeps dying with NODE_FAIL, pulling the XID from FACT (or the node's logs) tells you in one number whether it's hardware (drain it) or the app (fix it) — turning a vague "the cluster is flaky" into a precise, actionable attribution.

The hardest part of efficiency work isn't finding the waste — it's getting a busy researcher to act on it. A perfectly correct finding that lands as criticism gets ignored; the same finding framed as "here's an easy change that makes your training finish 20% sooner" gets adopted. This is why the team invests so heavily in the delivery of efficiency — the nudges, the self-serve tools, the defaults. For the customers interview, the line that captures it: "My job is to make the right thing the default, and when I do need to ask a researcher to change something, to bring them data about their own job and a fix that's clearly in their interest — never to lecture them about shared resources." That's the team's culture, stated.

A worked support interaction

worked case "My pretraining is too slow and I'm out of GPU quota"
The ask

A researcher pings #sw-mars-mlops-support: their Nemotron pretraining is slow, they've burned their fairshare, and they want more GPUs. The naive response is "you're over your share, wait." The better response treats it as an efficiency opportunity.

Diagnose first (don't moralize)

Pull their jobs (idle-waste-toolkit / sisyphus): goodput is 48%, with 22% lost to startup and 18% to lost work across frequent requeues. Cross-check: the requeues trace to two nodes throwing NVLink XIDs (drain candidates, §33), and the lost work is because checkpoints are every 6h on a cluster that's failing them every ~2h.

Bring data + an in-their-interest fix

Reply with the evidence and three concrete, easy wins: (1) "Two nodes are bad — I'm getting them drained, which stops the requeue-storm." (2) "Your checkpoint interval (6h) is longer than your mean-time-to-failure (~2h), so every failure throws away ~4h of training — drop SAVE_INTERVAL so each failure costs less." (3) "Startup's at 22%; staging the image to /raid and fixing the dataloader init will cut it." Net framing: "These get your effective throughput up ~40% — you'll finish this run within your current share, faster, without needing more quota."

The outcome and the lesson

The researcher gets more science per GPU-hour, the cluster reclaims wasted capacity, and the capacity fight dissolves — without anyone being told they did something wrong. That's the whole role: the systems view (bad nodes, checkpoint economics, startup tax) delivered as the researcher's own win. The quota request was a symptom; efficiency was the cure.

Finding the waste is the easy half. Making someone want to fix it — by handing them their own faster run — is the craft.
