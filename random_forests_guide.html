Two algorithms that look almost identical from a distance, but answer fundamentally different questions: what will happen? versus what would have happened?

§ 01The Random Forest, in mechanism

The random forest, introduced by Leo Breiman in 2001, is an ensemble of decision trees. Each tree is grown on a noisy view of the data and on a noisy view of the features, so that the trees disagree in their errors. When you average the disagreements, the noise mostly cancels and the signal mostly survives. That is the entire idea, but the details are where it earns its reputation.

There are exactly two randomization devices, and they are doing different jobs:

1 · Bagging (bootstrap aggregating)

For each of the $B$ trees, draw a sample of size $n$ with replacement from the training data. Roughly $1 - 1/e \approx 63.2\%$ of the original points end up in any given bootstrap sample; the rest become the tree's out-of-bag set, useful later for free validation. Bagging gives each tree a slightly different empirical distribution to fit.

2 · Random feature subsetting (the "random" in random forest)

At every candidate split inside every tree, only a random subset of $m$ features is considered. Typical defaults are $m = \sqrt{p}$ for classification and $m = p/3$ for regression. This is the crucial second source of randomness — without it, bagged trees would all latch onto the same dominant feature and correlate too heavily, defeating the averaging benefit.

Why both sources matter
The variance of an average of $B$ identically distributed but correlated variables with pairwise correlation $\rho$ and variance $\sigma^2$ is $\rho\sigma^2 + (1-\rho)\sigma^2/B.$ As $B \to \infty$, only the $\rho\sigma^2$ term survives. So reducing $\rho$ — the correlation between trees — directly lowers the floor of the ensemble's variance. Random feature subsetting is the lever that reduces $\rho$.

Growing a single tree

Each tree is built greedily and top-down. Starting from the root (the whole bootstrap sample), at each node we:

  1. Sample $m$ features at random from the $p$ available.
  2. For each of those features, scan all candidate split points $s$ (typically midpoints between consecutive observed values) and compute an impurity reduction score.
  3. Choose the (feature, split) pair that maximizes that reduction.
  4. Partition the node into left ($X_j \leq s$) and right ($X_j > s$) children, and recurse.

Trees are grown deep — typically until each leaf has very few observations (default min_samples_leaf = 1 or 5) — without pruning. Deep trees are low-bias and high-variance, which is exactly what the averaging step needs.

Aggregation

For a new $x$:

  • Regression. Predict $\hat{f}(x) = \frac{1}{B}\sum_{b=1}^{B} \hat{f}_b(x)$, the mean of the per-tree predictions.
  • Classification. Each tree casts a vote (or returns class probabilities); the forest returns the modal class or the average probability vector.

One way to view this aggregation, useful later, is as a kernel. For regression we can rewrite

$\hat{f}(x) \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^{n} \alpha_i(x)\, y_i, \qquad \alpha_i(x) \;=\; \frac{1}{B}\sum_{b=1}^{B} \frac{\mathbf{1}\{x_i \in L_b(x)\}}{|L_b(x)|}$ forest as an adaptive kernel — $L_b(x)$ is the leaf of tree $b$ that contains $x$

The forest is implicitly learning which training points are similar to $x$, with similarity defined as "lands in the same leaf, often." This kernel view is the bridge to the causal forest.

§ 02Splitting criteria — for prediction

The splitting rule is the heart of the tree. It encodes what counts as a useful question to ask. For ordinary random forests, "useful" means "reduces the noise in our prediction of $Y$."

Regression: variance reduction

At a node $N$ with response values $\{y_i : i \in N\}$ and mean $\bar y_N$, define the within-node sum of squares

$\mathrm{SS}(N) \;=\; \sum_{i \in N}(y_i - \bar y_N)^2$ total squared deviation in node $N$

A split $(j, s)$ partitions $N$ into $N_L$ and $N_R$. The criterion chooses the split that maximizes the drop in SS:

$\Delta(j,s) \;=\; \mathrm{SS}(N) - \bigl[\mathrm{SS}(N_L) + \mathrm{SS}(N_R)\bigr]$ equivalent to maximizing between-group variance of $\bar y$

Equivalently — and this reformulation matters later — maximize $\frac{|N_L|\,|N_R|}{|N|}\,(\bar y_L - \bar y_R)^2$: separate observations into groups whose means differ as much as possible.

Classification: Gini and entropy

Let $p_{N,c}$ be the fraction of observations of class $c$ in node $N$. Two standard impurities:

$\text{Gini}(N) = \sum_c p_{N,c}(1 - p_{N,c}), \qquad \text{Entropy}(N) = -\sum_c p_{N,c}\log p_{N,c}$

The split is again chosen to maximize $\text{impurity}(N) - \frac{|N_L|}{|N|}\text{impurity}(N_L) - \frac{|N_R|}{|N|}\text{impurity}(N_R).$ Gini and entropy almost always pick the same splits in practice; Gini is slightly faster and the scikit-learn default.

Key point to carry forward
In all of these criteria, the target of splitting is the outcome itself. A node splits because doing so makes the children's predictions of $Y$ more accurate. That assumption — that prediction accuracy on $Y$ is what we want — is exactly what changes when we turn to causal forests.

§ 04The causal random forest

Suppose we have observations $(X_i, W_i, Y_i)$ where $W_i \in \{0,1\}$ is a treatment indicator (e.g. received a drug, was shown an ad, enrolled in a program). The quantity we care about is no longer the mean outcome but the conditional average treatment effect:

$\tau(x) \;=\; \mathbb{E}\bigl[\,Y_i(1) - Y_i(0) \,\big|\, X_i = x\,\bigr]$ CATE — how much does treatment help someone with covariates $x$?

The notation $Y_i(1), Y_i(0)$ is from the Rubin potential-outcomes framework: $Y_i(w)$ is the outcome we would observe if unit $i$ received treatment $w$. The fundamental problem is that for each unit we only ever see one of these — the other is counterfactual. You cannot directly compute $Y_i(1) - Y_i(0)$ for anyone. So a causal forest cannot simply minimize residual error on $\tau$; the target is unobservable.

The causal random forest, developed by Wager and Athey (2018) and extended to the Generalized Random Forest (Athey, Tibshirani, Wager, 2019), solves this with three structural modifications to the standard recipe:

  1. Honesty. Within each tree, the data used to choose splits is disjoint from the data used to estimate leaf values.
  2. A different splitting criterion. Instead of maximizing variance of $Y$ between children, maximize the difference in estimated treatment effects between children.
  3. Local moment estimation. Inside each leaf — or rather, inside the neighborhood implicitly defined by the forest — solve the moment condition for $\tau$ locally, rather than just averaging $Y$.

Where the random forest asks "which subgroup has different $Y$?", the causal forest asks "which subgroup responds differently to treatment?"

§ 05Honesty and the leaf problem

Why honesty? Because if the same data is used to find a split and to estimate the treatment effect inside the leaf created by that split, you get a textbook winner's-curse problem. The tree picks the split that makes children's treatment-effect estimates look maximally different. By construction, those estimates will be biased away from each other — they were chosen on that criterion.

Honesty fixes this by partitioning, for each tree, the subsample $\mathcal{S}_b$ into two halves:

  • $\mathcal{J}_b$ — the splitting set, used only to decide the tree's structure (which feature, which threshold, at each node).
  • $\mathcal{I}_b$ — the estimation set, used only to compute the leaf-level statistic ($\hat\tau$ in each leaf) once the tree is built.

An $\mathcal{I}_b$ observation never influenced which leaf it falls into; the leaf was defined without seeing it. The estimate in each leaf is therefore an honest empirical estimate from an i.i.d. sample drawn from the population restricted to that leaf's region. This is precisely what is needed for the leaf-level estimator to be asymptotically Gaussian — and hence for the whole forest to support pointwise confidence intervals.

Costs of honesty
Honesty halves the data used for each job. Empirically, honest forests have worse point-prediction accuracy than non-honest forests on the same data — but their uncertainty quantification is valid, which is the whole point. If you only want to predict, use an ordinary forest. If you want to draw inferences about $\tau(x)$, the trade is worth it.

A second technical change: causal forests use subsampling without replacement rather than bootstrap. Theoretical guarantees (consistency, asymptotic normality) rest on subsample size $s_n$ that grows more slowly than $n$, and the analysis is cleaner without replacement.

§ 06Splitting on heterogeneity in $\tau$

The simplest causal-forest splitting criterion is a direct analog of variance reduction, but applied to $\tau$ instead of $Y$. At a candidate split $(j, s)$ of node $N$ into $N_L, N_R$, compute

$\hat\tau_L = \frac{\sum_{i \in N_L, W_i=1} Y_i}{|\{i\in N_L: W_i=1\}|} - \frac{\sum_{i \in N_L, W_i=0} Y_i}{|\{i\in N_L: W_i=0\}|}, \qquad \hat\tau_R = \text{(same for $N_R$)}$ leaf-level treatment effect, child by child

And maximize the between-child variance of $\hat\tau$:

$\Delta_\tau(j,s) \;=\; \frac{|N_L|\,|N_R|}{|N|}\,\bigl(\hat\tau_L - \hat\tau_R\bigr)^2$ causal splitting objective — find subgroups whose treatment responses differ

This is structurally the same expression as the regression criterion in §02, but with $\hat\tau$ playing the role that $\bar y$ played. The tree now grows by carving out subpopulations with maximally different sensitivity to treatment, not maximally different outcome means.

The gradient trick (Athey–Tibshirani–Wager)

Computing $\hat\tau_L$ and $\hat\tau_R$ for every candidate split would be expensive — naïvely, $O(n)$ per split. The GRF paper introduces an elegant approximation: linearize the moment condition around the parent-node estimate, derive a pseudo-outcome $\rho_i$ for each observation, and apply ordinary regression-tree splitting (i.e. variance reduction) on the $\rho_i$. Because regression-tree splits can be evaluated with running sums, this brings the cost back to $O(n \log n)$ per tree, identical to the classical algorithm.

Pseudo-outcomes, in spirit
For each observation, $\rho_i$ is the score of the parent-node moment condition with respect to $\tau$, evaluated at observation $i$. Splits that produce children with very different sums of $\rho_i$ correspond, locally, to splits that produce children with very different fitted $\tau$ values. The math is in §2.2 of Athey–Tibshirani–Wager (2019); the operational consequence is that a causal-forest split looks computationally just like a regression-forest split, but on a transformed label.

Local centering (the R-learner connection)

Before fitting the causal forest, it's standard to residualize both $Y$ and $W$ with respect to $X$:

$\tilde Y_i = Y_i - \hat m(X_i), \qquad \tilde W_i = W_i - \hat e(X_i)$ $\hat m(x) = \widehat{\mathbb E}[Y|X=x]$, $\hat e(x) = \widehat{\mathbb E}[W|X=x]$ (the propensity score)

The nuisance functions $\hat m$ and $\hat e$ are typically estimated by separate (non-causal) random forests, often via cross-fitting. Building the causal forest on $(\tilde Y, \tilde W)$ instead of $(Y, W)$ makes the procedure orthogonal in the Neyman–Robinson sense: small errors in $\hat m$ and $\hat e$ have only second-order effect on the estimate of $\tau$. This is the same logic that underlies double machine learning and the R-learner.

Without this centering, a causal forest can still be consistent, but its variance is sensitive to misspecification of the conditional mean of $Y$. With it, you get rate-double-robustness essentially for free.

§ 08The GRF generalization

Causal forests are one instance of a wider framework. The Generalized Random Forest views the forest as a way to learn adaptive weights $\alpha_i(x)$ — exactly the kernel from §01 — and then plug those weights into a local estimating equation:

$\hat\theta(x) \;=\; \mathrm{argmin}_\theta \;\Bigl\| \sum_{i=1}^n \alpha_i(x)\, \psi_\theta(O_i) \Bigr\|$ local moment problem at $x$; $\psi_\theta$ is the score function of the parameter of interest, $O_i$ is the data tuple

Different choices of $\psi_\theta$ give different forests:

EstimandScore function $\psi_\theta(O_i)$Forest name
Conditional mean$Y_i - \theta$Regression forest
Treatment effect (unconfounded)$(Y_i - \theta\,W_i)(W_i - \hat e(X_i))$Causal forest
Treatment effect (with IV $Z_i$)$(Y_i - \theta\,W_i)(Z_i - \hat z(X_i))$Instrumental forest
Conditional quantile$\mathbf 1\{Y_i \leq \theta\} - q$Quantile regression forest
Survival / hazardpartial-likelihood scoreSurvival forest

The forest's role, in this view, is not to predict but to discover similarity. The estimator at the end is whatever classical estimator you would use if you actually had an i.i.d. sample of observations resembling $x$ — except now the "resembling $x$" part is learned by the trees, and weights are adaptive to the local structure of the problem.

§ 10Assumptions

What random forests assume

Strikingly little. The procedure is consistent for $\mu(x) = \mathbb E[Y \mid X = x]$ under modest regularity conditions:

  • i.i.d. or exchangeable training data.
  • The conditional mean $\mu$ is Lipschitz (or at least continuous) on the support of $X$.
  • Subsample size grows but $s_n / n \to 0$ — under classical settings this is automatic.

That's why random forests are so robust as a prediction tool. They make almost no distributional or functional-form claim, and they handle mixed types, missing data, and high-dimensional inputs without ceremony.

What causal random forests additionally assume

Unconfoundedness (conditional ignorability)

$\{Y_i(0), Y_i(1)\} \;\perp\!\!\!\perp\; W_i \mid X_i$ treatment assignment is as good as random within strata of $X$

All variables that simultaneously influence treatment assignment and the outcome must be in $X$. If some confounder is missing, the causal forest's $\hat\tau(x)$ recovers something — but it is not the causal effect. This is the assumption that does the most theoretical heavy lifting and cannot be tested with the data alone.

Overlap (positivity)

$0 \;<\; \eta \;\leq\; e(x) := \mathbb P(W=1 \mid X=x) \;\leq\; 1 - \eta \;<\; 1 \quad\text{for all } x$ every subpopulation has a nonzero chance of being either treated or untreated

If some region of covariate space has $e(x) \approx 0$ or $e(x) \approx 1$, the causal effect there is unidentified — you have essentially no control units, or no treated units, locally. Practical advice: estimate $\hat e(x)$ first, inspect its distribution, and trim or warn on regions with extreme propensities.

SUTVA — Stable Unit Treatment Value Assumption

Two parts. No interference: unit $i$'s outcome depends only on $i$'s treatment, not anyone else's. Consistency: there is a single, well-defined version of "the treatment" — no hidden variation in what $W=1$ means. Violations of SUTVA are common in social settings (network effects, market equilibria) and in clinical trials with crossover, and they bias the estimator in subtle ways.

None of these assumptions are testable from $(X, W, Y)$ alone.
They are identifying assumptions — they let you read causal quantities off the joint distribution. The data can sometimes flag overlap problems and obvious interference, but unconfoundedness must be argued from substantive knowledge of how treatment was assigned. A causal random forest doesn't relieve you of that argument; it just makes the estimator very flexible given the argument.

§ 11Use scenarios

When a random forest is the right tool

  • Prediction in tabular settings — fraud risk, churn, default, claim severity. A strong default whenever interpretability isn't paramount and the data isn't too high-dimensional in pathological ways.
  • Feature screening — quick ranking of which variables matter for predicting $Y$, via permutation importance or mean decrease in impurity. Treat these as suggestive, not definitive.
  • Robust nonparametric regression as a building block — e.g. estimating nuisance functions $\hat m(x)$ and $\hat e(x)$ in double machine learning.
  • Anomaly detection via isolation forests (a cousin algorithm).

When a causal random forest is the right tool

  • Heterogeneous treatment effects from an experiment. A/B test or RCT data where you want to ask "for whom did this work?" rather than just "did it work on average?"
  • Personalized medicine. Estimating $\tau(x)$ from clinical trial data to inform whether a particular patient profile is likely to benefit from a therapy.
  • Policy targeting. A government program with a fixed budget — which subpopulations gain the most from being enrolled? The forest's $\hat\tau(x)$ feeds directly into an optimal-policy learner.
  • Observational data with rich controls and plausible unconfoundedness — uplift modeling in marketing, where you ran a randomized holdout and have detailed user covariates.
  • Instrumental variables with heterogeneous LATEs — encouragement designs, fuzzy regression discontinuity. Use the instrumental version of the forest (GRF with IV moment).

When neither is the right tool

  • Image, text, audio data. Deep models dominate. Forests rarely beat a fine-tuned transformer on raw unstructured input.
  • Causal questions without overlap or with severe unmeasured confounding. No flexible estimator rescues an unidentified parameter; you need a different design (instrument, regression discontinuity, difference-in-differences).
  • Very small $n$. Tree ensembles need a few hundred observations at minimum for any kind of stability; causal forests want more like a few thousand because of the honesty halving.

§ 12Inference and variance

A standard random forest gives a point prediction. A causal random forest aims for more: a confidence interval for $\tau(x)$ at every $x$ of interest. The machinery is Wager and Athey's infinitesimal jackknife (IJ) variance estimator.

Conceptually: each training observation $i$ enters some subset of trees and not others. The IJ estimator measures how much $\hat\tau(x)$ would change if observation $i$ were perturbed, via the covariance between $\hat\tau_b(x)$ (the per-tree estimate) and $N_{ib}$ (whether $i$ is in the $b$-th subsample). High covariance means high influence; total influence across observations is the variance.

$\widehat{\mathrm{Var}}\bigl[\hat\tau(x)\bigr] \;=\; \frac{n-1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \mathrm{Cov}_b\bigl(\hat\tau_b(x),\, N_{ib}\bigr)^2$ infinitesimal jackknife — $b$ indexes trees, $N_{ib}$ is 1 if $i$ is in tree $b$'s subsample

Under the regularity conditions of Wager–Athey (subsample size $s_n$ growing as roughly $n^\beta$ with $\beta < 1$, honest trees, well-behaved DGP), $\hat\tau(x)$ is asymptotically Gaussian and the IJ estimator is consistent for its variance. You then form CIs as $\hat\tau(x) \pm 1.96 \sqrt{\widehat{\mathrm{Var}}}$. The grf R package returns these automatically.

This is one of the genuinely impressive accomplishments of the causal-forest framework: nonparametric estimation of a heterogeneous causal effect, with calibrated pointwise confidence bands, from a single i.i.d. sample. The closest comparable methods are local linear regression and kernel methods, which struggle in moderate to high dimensions.

§ 13Pitfalls and practitioner notes

For random forests

  • Mean decrease in impurity importance is biased toward high-cardinality and continuous variables. Prefer permutation importance or model-agnostic methods (SHAP).
  • Extrapolation is bad: forests cannot predict values outside the convex hull of the training $Y$. For a regression task with strong trends, gradient boosting or a linear model may extrapolate more gracefully.
  • Probabilities from classification forests are not well calibrated out of the box. Use Platt scaling or isotonic regression if you need them to mean what they say.
  • Hyperparameters that actually matter: min_samples_leaf (or min.node.size) and max_features. Number of trees mostly stops mattering past a few hundred.

For causal random forests

  • Always check overlap first. Fit $\hat e(X)$ as a standalone regression forest. Plot its distribution by treatment group. If propensities pile up near 0 or 1, $\hat\tau(x)$ for those regions is essentially uninformative.
  • Use cross-fit nuisance estimates. Don't estimate $\hat m$ and $\hat e$ on the same data you use to build the causal forest; cross-fitting (or out-of-bag predictions) is required for the orthogonality guarantee to hold.
  • Don't interpret a single $\hat\tau(x)$ without its CI. Local treatment effects are noisy; the point estimate is uninterpretable in isolation. The grf package gives you the standard error — read it.
  • Variable importance for a causal forest ranks features by how often they were split on, weighted by depth. It tells you which variables drive heterogeneity in $\tau$, not which drive $Y$. These can be entirely different sets.
  • If the average treatment effect is your goal, average $\hat\tau(x_i)$ over the sample — but use the doubly-robust ATE estimator that grf::average_treatment_effect provides, not the simple mean. It has better small-sample properties.
  • The forest does not validate your unconfoundedness assumption. No amount of cross-validation can. Argue it from the design.

A causal forest is a flexible estimator wrapped around a rigid identifying assumption. The flexibility is the easy part.
