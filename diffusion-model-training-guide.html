1 Diffusion fundamentals

The forward and reverse processTheory

The forward process (adding noise)

Given a clean image x₀, the forward process gradually adds Gaussian noise over T timesteps, producing a sequence x₁, x₂, ..., x_T where x_T is approximately pure Gaussian noise.

Forward process (single step):
q(xₜ | xₜ₋₁) = N(xₜ; √(1-βₜ) · xₜ₋₁, βₜ · I)

Key insight — jump directly to any timestep t from x₀:
xₜ = √(ᾱₜ) · x₀ + √(1 - ᾱₜ) · ε where ε ~ N(0, I)

Where:
αₜ = 1 - βₜ (single-step signal retention)
ᾱₜ = ∏ᵢ₌₁ᵗ αᵢ (cumulative signal retention — how much of x₀ remains at step t)

At t=0: ᾱ₀ ≈ 1 → xₜ ≈ x₀ (clean image)
At t=T: ᾱ_T ≈ 0 → xₜ ≈ ε (pure noise)

The reverse process (denoising)

The model learns to reverse this process — given noisy xₜ and timestep t, predict either the noise ε, the clean image x₀, or the velocity v that was used to reach xₜ.

Reverse process:
p_θ(xₜ₋₁ | xₜ) = N(xₜ₋₁; μ_θ(xₜ, t), σₜ² · I)

The model predicts one of:
• ε_θ(xₜ, t) — the noise that was added (ε-prediction, DDPM)
• x₀_θ(xₜ, t) — the clean image directly (x₀-prediction)
• v_θ(xₜ, t) — the "velocity" (v-prediction, used by SDXL)

All three are mathematically equivalent (interconvertible):
x₀ = (xₜ - √(1-ᾱₜ) · ε) / √(ᾱₜ)
v = √(ᾱₜ) · ε - √(1-ᾱₜ) · x₀
Intuition: Training a diffusion model is training a denoiser. You take a clean image, add a known amount of noise (sampled uniformly across timesteps), and train the model to predict either the noise, the clean image, or the velocity. At inference time, you start from pure noise and iteratively denoise, each step removing a bit of noise using the model's prediction.

2 Training objectives

ε-prediction, v-prediction, x₀-prediction, and flow matchingObjective
ObjectiveTargetLossUsed byPros / cons
ε-predictionNoise ε added to x₀‖ε - ε_θ(xₜ, t)‖²DDPM, Stable Diffusion 1.x/2.xPro: stable, well-studied. Con: equal weight to all noise levels, which means high-noise (structureless) timesteps get as much gradient as low-noise (detail-preserving) ones.
v-predictionv = √ᾱₜ·ε - √(1-ᾱₜ)·x₀‖v - v_θ(xₜ, t)‖²SDXL, Imagen VideoPro: smoother signal at both endpoints (doesn't predict nearly-zero noise at t≈0 or nearly-random noise at t≈T). Better for high-resolution and video.
x₀-predictionClean image x₀‖x₀ - x₀_θ(xₜ, t)‖²Some early models, DALL-E 2Pro: intuitive. Con: prediction is very noisy at high t (model sees near-random noise and must predict a sharp image). Typically combined with loss weighting.
Flow matching (rectified flow)Velocity field v = x₁ - x₀‖(x₁ - x₀) - v_θ(xₜ, t)‖²Stable Diffusion 3, FluxPro: simpler formulation (linear interpolation, not diffusion noise schedule), straighter sampling paths → fewer steps needed. The current frontier.
# ε-prediction loss (DDPM)
noise = torch.randn_like(x_0)
t = torch.randint(0, T, (batch_size,), device=device)
x_t = sqrt_alpha_bar[t] * x_0 + sqrt_one_minus_alpha_bar[t] * noise
predicted_noise = model(x_t, t)
loss = F.mse_loss(predicted_noise, noise)

# v-prediction loss (SDXL)
v_target = sqrt_alpha_bar[t] * noise - sqrt_one_minus_alpha_bar[t] * x_0
predicted_v = model(x_t, t)
loss = F.mse_loss(predicted_v, v_target)

# Flow matching loss (SD3/Flux)
t = torch.rand(batch_size, device=device)          # continuous t ∈ [0,1]
x_1 = torch.randn_like(x_0)                        # noise sample
x_t = (1 - t.view(-1,1,1,1)) * x_0 + t.view(-1,1,1,1) * x_1  # linear interp
target_velocity = x_1 - x_0                        # straight-line velocity
predicted_v = model(x_t, t)
loss = F.mse_loss(predicted_v, target_velocity)
The industry trajectory: ε-prediction (2020–2023) → v-prediction (2023–2024) → flow matching (2024+). Flow matching is simpler mathematically, trains faster, and produces straighter sampling trajectories (meaning fewer sampling steps for the same quality). If starting a new project, flow matching is the recommended choice.
Loss weighting: Min-SNR and timestep-dependent weightingObjective

Not all timesteps contribute equally to image quality. Without weighting, the loss is dominated by mid-range timesteps, while low-noise timesteps (critical for fine details) and high-noise timesteps (critical for global structure) are under-learned.

WeightingFormulaEffect
Uniform (default)w(t) = 1All timesteps weighted equally. Simple but suboptimal — mid-range dominates.
SNR weightingw(t) = SNR(t) = ᾱₜ / (1-ᾱₜ)Upweights clean timesteps (low noise). Can focus too much on trivial predictions.
Min-SNR-γw(t) = min(SNR(t), γ) / SNR(t)Caps the SNR weight at γ (typically γ=5). Balances low-noise detail learning with high-noise structure learning. Recommended default.
P2 weightingw(t) = 1 / (k + SNR(t))^pPerception-prioritized. Upweights perceptually important timesteps.
# Min-SNR-γ weighting (recommended)
snr = alpha_bar / (1 - alpha_bar)                 # signal-to-noise ratio
gamma = 5.0
min_snr_weight = torch.clamp(snr, max=gamma) / snr
loss = (min_snr_weight[t] * F.mse_loss(pred, target, reduction='none').mean(dim=[1,2,3])).mean()

Continuous (flow matching):
t ~ logit-normal(0, 1) clipped to [0, 1]

The zero-SNR issue: Standard schedules never reach ᾱ_T = 0 exactly, meaning the model never trains on pure noise. At inference, the first denoising step starts from pure noise — a distribution the model has never seen. This causes artifacts (over-saturated colors, bright images). Fix: use zero-terminal-SNR schedule or rescale the schedule so the last timestep has ᾱ_T = 0. This is increasingly standard.

4 Architecture

U-Net vs DiT: architecture comparisonArchitecture

U-Net (Stable Diffusion 1.x/2.x, SDXL)

Encoder-decoder with skip connections. Processes images at multiple resolutions with downsampling and upsampling blocks.

ComponentDetails
Downsampling blocksResNet blocks + self-attention (at lower resolutions) + downsample via strided conv or pooling. Typically 3–4 resolution levels (64→32→16→8 for latent diffusion).
BottleneckResNet + self-attention at the lowest resolution.
Upsampling blocksResNet blocks + self-attention + upsample via transpose conv or interpolate+conv. Skip connections from encoder.
Timestep conditioningSinusoidal embedding → MLP → added to each ResNet block via adaptive normalization (scale and shift).
Text conditioningCross-attention layers between image features and text encoder (CLIP/T5) embeddings.
Attention placementSelf-attention only at lower resolutions (16×16, 32×32) to manage O(s²) cost. No attention at 64×64.

DiT — Diffusion Transformer (DiT, Flux)

Pure Transformer operating on a sequence of latent patches. No U-Net structure, no skip connections between resolutions.

ComponentDetails
PatchificationLatent image (e.g., 32×32×4) → patches (e.g., 2×2 patches → 256 tokens of dimension 4×2×2=16, projected to hidden dim).
Transformer blocksStandard Transformer with self-attention + MLP. Typically 28–38 layers.
Timestep conditioningadaLN-Zero: adaptive layer norm where scale/shift/gate are predicted from the timestep embedding. The gate initializes to zero so the model starts as identity.
Text conditioningCross-attention (DiT-XL) or joint attention (MM-DiT in SD3/Flux).
Position encoding2D sinusoidal or RoPE-2D. Must handle 2D spatial positions, not 1D sequence positions.
OutputLinear projection from hidden dim back to patch dimensions → unpatchify to latent image.
Why DiT is winning: DiTs scale better than U-Nets with compute — they benefit from the same scaling laws as LLMs. They're simpler (no skip connections, no multi-resolution engineering). They work with FlashAttention and torch.compile out of the box. The downside: no multi-resolution inductive bias means DiTs need more training data to learn spatial hierarchies that U-Nets get "for free" from their architecture.

MM-DiT — Multimodal DiT (SD3, Flux)

Joint Transformer that processes image and text tokens together in the same attention operation.

InnovationHow it works
Joint attentionImage patches and text tokens are concatenated into one sequence. Self-attention sees both modalities simultaneously — no separate cross-attention needed.
Separate weights per modalityEach modality has its own QKV projections and MLPs, but they share the attention operation. This gives each modality its own representation space while enabling direct interaction.
Bidirectional text conditioningUnlike cross-attention (text→image only), joint attention allows image→text information flow too. The text representation can adapt to the image being generated.
Timestep and conditioning injection mechanismsArchitecture
MechanismHow it worksUsed in
Adaptive LayerNorm (adaLN)Timestep embedding predicts scale (γ) and shift (β) for each LayerNorm. Modulates the normalization: y = γ·LayerNorm(x) + βDiT, SDXL
adaLN-ZeroadaLN plus a learned gate that multiplies the attention/MLP output. Gate initializes to zero → each layer starts as identity → improves training stability.DiT (recommended)
Cross-attentionImage features are Q, conditioning (text) is K/V. Each attention layer has a cross-attention sublayer.Stable Diffusion U-Net
ConcatenationConditioning tokens concatenated with image tokens → joint self-attention.MM-DiT (SD3, Flux)
Addition (embedding)Conditioning embedding added directly to the input or to hidden states.Class-conditional models, DDPM
# adaLN-Zero implementation
class AdaLNZero(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, d_model, cond_dim):
        super().__init__()
        self.norm = nn.LayerNorm(d_model, elementwise_affine=False)
        # Predict 6 values: scale1, shift1, gate1, scale2, shift2, gate2
        self.proj = nn.Linear(cond_dim, 6 * d_model)
        nn.init.zeros_(self.proj.weight)  # zero-init → starts as identity
        nn.init.zeros_(self.proj.bias)

    def forward(self, x, cond):
        s1, b1, g1, s2, b2, g2 = self.proj(cond).chunk(6, dim=-1)
        # Attention sublayer
        h = self.norm(x) * (1 + s1) + b1
        h = self.attn(h)
        x = x + g1 * h                # gate starts at 0 → no effect initially
        # MLP sublayer
        h = self.norm(x) * (1 + s2) + b2
        h = self.mlp(h)
        x = x + g2 * h
        return x

5 Latent diffusion (VAE)

The VAE: encoding images to latent spaceVAE

Latent diffusion doesn't operate on raw pixels. A pretrained VAE compresses images from pixel space (3×H×W) to a lower-dimensional latent space (C×H/f×W/f), where f is the downsampling factor.

AspectDetail
Downsampling factor (f)Typically f=8 (SD 1.x/2.x/XL, Flux) or f=16 (some video models). A 512×512 image → 64×64 latent at f=8.
Latent channels4 (SD 1.x/2.x, SDXL), 16 (SD3, Flux). More channels = richer representation but larger latents.
KL regularizationThe VAE is trained with a KL divergence term to keep the latent distribution close to N(0,1). This is critical — the diffusion model assumes Gaussian latents.
Training the VAEPretrained separately on image reconstruction (not jointly with the diffusion model). Frozen during diffusion training.
VAE quality mattersThe VAE sets the quality ceiling — any detail lost in VAE encoding can never be recovered by the diffusion model. Modern VAEs (SDXL, Flux) have better reconstruction than SD 1.x.
# Using the VAE during diffusion training
vae = AutoencoderKL.from_pretrained("stabilityai/sdxl-vae").to(device)
vae.eval()
vae.requires_grad_(False)  # frozen — never train the VAE during diffusion

# Encode images to latents (once per batch, no gradients)
with torch.no_grad():
    latents = vae.encode(images).latent_dist.sample()
    latents = latents * vae.config.scaling_factor  # scale to unit variance

# The diffusion model operates on these latents
noise = torch.randn_like(latents)
noisy_latents = scheduler.add_noise(latents, noise, timesteps)
predicted = model(noisy_latents, timesteps, conditioning)
loss = F.mse_loss(predicted, target)

# At inference — decode latents back to pixels
with torch.no_grad():
    images = vae.decode(latents / vae.config.scaling_factor).sample
The scaling factor is critical. The VAE's latent space has a specific variance. The diffusion model expects inputs with unit variance (for the noise schedule to work correctly). The scaling factor normalizes the latents. Using the wrong scaling factor — or forgetting it entirely — is a common training bug that causes the model to learn a shifted noise distribution, producing washed-out or over-saturated images.
Precomputing latents for training efficiencyOptimization

Since the VAE is frozen during diffusion training, you can run it once on the entire dataset and cache the latent representations. This eliminates the VAE encoder from the training loop entirely.

# Precompute latents (one-time cost)
for batch_idx, images in enumerate(full_dataset_loader):
    with torch.no_grad(), torch.autocast('cuda', dtype=torch.float16):
        latents = vae.encode(images.cuda()).latent_dist
        # Save both mean and std for proper sampling
        torch.save({
            'mean': latents.mean.cpu(),
            'std': latents.std.cpu(),
        }, f'latents/batch_{batch_idx}.pt')

# During training — load precomputed latents (no VAE needed!)
class LatentDataset(Dataset):
    def __getitem__(self, idx):
        data = torch.load(f'latents/batch_{idx}.pt')
        # Sample from the distribution (stochastic encoding)
        latent = data['mean'] + data['std'] * torch.randn_like(data['mean'])
        return latent * scaling_factor, caption_embedding

Benefits

Removes ~10–15% of step time (VAE forward) and ~2–5 GB of GPU memory (VAE weights). Also eliminates image decode from the data pipeline — you load pre-encoded tensors instead of JPEGs. The trade-off: requires disk space for the cached latents (~2× the original dataset for f=8, C=4 at FP16).

Classifier-Free Guidance (CFG)

CFG is the key technique that makes conditional generation work well. During training, randomly drop the conditioning (replace with null/empty) with probability p_drop (typically 10%). At inference, run the model twice — once with conditioning, once without — and extrapolate:

CFG formula:
ε_guided = ε_uncond + w · (ε_cond - ε_uncond)

Where w is the guidance scale:
w = 1.0: no guidance (just the conditional prediction)
w = 3–5: typical for photorealistic images
w = 7–9: standard for SDXL, high-quality but sometimes oversaturated
w = 15+: extreme — high contrast, artifacts, loss of diversity 
# Training with CFG (random conditioning dropout)
p_drop = 0.1  # 10% of the time, train unconditionally

# For each sample in the batch:
drop_mask = torch.rand(batch_size) < p_drop
conditioning[drop_mask] = null_conditioning  # empty/zero text embedding

# Inference with CFG (two forward passes)
noise_pred_uncond = model(x_t, t, null_conditioning)
noise_pred_cond = model(x_t, t, text_conditioning)
noise_pred = noise_pred_uncond + guidance_scale * (noise_pred_cond - noise_pred_uncond)
CFG doubles inference cost — every sampling step requires two model forward passes (conditional + unconditional). This is why guidance distillation (training a model to approximate CFG in a single forward pass) is a key research area. Some recent approaches (Flux-dev, LCM) eliminate or reduce the need for CFG through training modifications.

8 Training recipe

Hyperparameters for diffusion trainingRecipe
HyperparameterTypical valueNotes
OptimizerAdamWSometimes Adam (no weight decay) for the denoiser. Weight decay on the denoiser can hurt because it pushes weights toward zero, but the model needs to make fine-grained predictions across all timesteps.
Learning rate1×10⁻⁴ (U-Net), 1×10⁻⁴ (DiT)Lower than LLM pretraining. Diffusion models are more sensitive to LR — too high causes mode collapse or quality degradation.
β₁, β₂0.9, 0.999 (or 0.99)β₂=0.999 is more common for diffusion than the 0.95 used in LLMs. Some use β₂=0.99 for faster adaptation.
Weight decay0.0 or 0.01Often zero. Some use small weight decay (0.01) on attention weights only.
EMA decay0.9999Critical — always use EMA for diffusion. The EMA model produces much better samples than the raw trained model. Typical: 0.9999 (update EMA every step).
Batch size256–2048 imagesLarger batches improve quality. SDXL used effective batch size of 2048.
Training steps500K–1M+ stepsMuch longer than typical supervised training. Quality continues improving slowly even at 1M+ steps.
LR scheduleConstant with warmup, or cosineMany diffusion models use constant LR (no decay) with 10K-step warmup. Simpler than LLM schedules.
Gradient clipping1.0Standard. Prevents explosive gradients at high-noise timesteps.

EMA is non-negotiable for diffusion. The EMA (exponential moving average) model averages weights over training, smoothing out noise in the optimization trajectory. Without EMA, generated samples have noticeable artifacts and lower quality. Always maintain an EMA model and sample from it at inference. The decay rate of 0.9999 means the EMA has an effective window of ~10K steps.

9 Training loop

Complete latent diffusion training loopCode
# Complete latent diffusion training loop (DDPM-style ε-prediction)
import torch, torch.nn.functional as F
from diffusers import AutoencoderKL, DDPMScheduler

# Components
vae = AutoencoderKL.from_pretrained("...").to(device).eval()
vae.requires_grad_(False)
text_encoder = CLIPTextModel.from_pretrained("...").to(device).eval()
text_encoder.requires_grad_(False)
model = UNet2DConditionModel(...)  # or DiT
ema_model = copy.deepcopy(model)

scheduler = DDPMScheduler(
    num_train_timesteps=1000,
    beta_schedule="scaled_linear",
    prediction_type="epsilon",      # or "v_prediction"
)

optimizer = torch.optim.AdamW(model.parameters(), lr=1e-4, betas=(0.9, 0.999), fused=True)

for step in range(max_steps):
    images, captions = next(dataloader)
    images = images.to(device)

    # 1. Encode images to latent space
    with torch.no_grad():
        latents = vae.encode(images).latent_dist.sample() * vae.config.scaling_factor

    # 2. Encode text
    with torch.no_grad():
        text_embeds = text_encoder(captions).last_hidden_state

    # 3. CFG dropout — randomly drop conditioning
    drop_mask = torch.rand(latents.shape[0]) < 0.1
    text_embeds[drop_mask] = torch.zeros_like(text_embeds[drop_mask])

    # 4. Sample noise and timesteps
    noise = torch.randn_like(latents)
    timesteps = torch.randint(0, scheduler.config.num_train_timesteps,
                               (latents.shape[0],), device=device)

    # 5. Add noise to latents
    noisy_latents = scheduler.add_noise(latents, noise, timesteps)

    # 6. Predict noise (forward pass)
    with torch.autocast('cuda', dtype=torch.bfloat16):
        predicted = model(noisy_latents, timesteps, encoder_hidden_states=text_embeds).sample
        loss = F.mse_loss(predicted, noise)

    # 7. Backward + optimize
    loss.backward()
    torch.nn.utils.clip_grad_norm_(model.parameters(), 1.0)
    optimizer.step()
    optimizer.zero_grad()

    # 8. EMA update
    with torch.no_grad():
        ema_decay = 0.9999
        for p_ema, p_model in zip(ema_model.parameters(), model.parameters()):
            p_ema.data.mul_(ema_decay).add_(p_model.data, alpha=1 - ema_decay)

    # 9. Logging + checkpointing
    if step % 100 == 0:
        log({'loss': loss.item(), 'step': step})
    if step % 5000 == 0:
        save_checkpoint(model, ema_model, optimizer, step)
        # Generate samples from EMA model for visual inspection
        generate_samples(ema_model, vae, sample_prompts)

Key monitoring metrics

Loss curve: should decrease smoothly to ~0.03–0.08 (MSE on normalized latents). Loss per timestep bucket: split loss into t∈[0,250], [250,500], [500,750], [750,1000] — all should decrease. If high-noise loss is stuck, the model isn't learning structure. If low-noise loss is stuck, it isn't learning details. Sample quality: generate images every 5K–10K steps from fixed prompts and visually inspect. This is the most important signal — loss improvements don't always translate to visible quality improvements.

# DDIM sampling loop (simplified)
x_t = torch.randn(1, 4, 64, 64, device=device)  # start from pure noise
timesteps = scheduler.set_timesteps(50)            # 50 denoising steps

for t in timesteps:
    # CFG: two forward passes
    noise_uncond = model(x_t, t, null_cond).sample
    noise_cond = model(x_t, t, text_cond).sample
    noise_pred = noise_uncond + cfg_scale * (noise_cond - noise_uncond)

    # DDIM step
    x_t = scheduler.step(noise_pred, t, x_t).prev_sample

# Decode to pixels
images = vae.decode(x_t / scaling_factor).sample

The evaluation reality: FID is the standard metric but correlates imperfectly with human judgment. A model with FID 5 can look worse than one with FID 8 if the FID-5 model has specific failure modes that FID doesn't capture (wrong anatomy, text rendering issues, compositional failures). Always complement FID with human evaluation and visual inspection of generated samples across diverse prompts.

14 Advanced: flow matching, consistency, distillation

The frontier of efficient diffusionAdvanced

Flow matching / Rectified flow

Instead of a diffusion process with a complex noise schedule, flow matching learns a velocity field that transports samples from a noise distribution to the data distribution along straight(er) paths.

AspectDDPM (diffusion)Flow matching
Forward processxₜ = √ᾱₜ·x₀ + √(1-ᾱₜ)·ε (curved path)xₜ = (1-t)·x₀ + t·x₁ (straight line)
Training targetNoise ε or velocity vVelocity v = x₁ - x₀
Noise scheduleComplex (β schedule, ᾱ cumprod)None — just linear interpolation
SamplingReverse SDE/ODE with schedulerSimple ODE integration (Euler)
Steps needed20–50 (with good sampler)20–50 (can be fewer with reflow)
Flow matching training:
1. Sample x₀ ~ data, x₁ ~ N(0,I), t ~ p(t)
2. Interpolate: xₜ = (1-t)·x₀ + t·x₁
3. Target velocity: v = x₁ - x₀
4. Loss: ‖v_θ(xₜ, t) - v‖²

Flow matching is simpler to implement, easier to reason about, and produces straighter sampling paths (meaning fewer sampling steps for the same quality). SD3 and Flux both use flow matching.

Consistency models

Instead of learning to denoise one step at a time, consistency models learn to map any point on the diffusion trajectory directly to the clean image x₀. This enables 1–4 step generation.

VariantHow it's trainedSteps at inference
Consistency distillation (CD)Distill from a pretrained diffusion model. Train the consistency model to match the diffusion model's predictions at adjacent timesteps.1–4
Consistency training (CT)Train from scratch without a teacher model. Use the model's own predictions as the learning signal (self-consistency).1–4
Latent consistency models (LCM)Consistency distillation in latent space (from Stable Diffusion). Most practical for existing image generation pipelines.2–8

Distillation for fewer steps

Distillation trains a student model that approximates many teacher steps in a single student step.

MethodApproachQuality vs teacher
Progressive distillationHalve the steps iteratively: 1024→512→256→...→4. Each round trains the student to match 2 teacher steps in 1.~90–95% at 4 steps
Guidance distillationTrain a model to produce CFG-like outputs in a single forward pass (no separate unconditional pass).~95%+ (eliminates CFG cost, not steps)
SDXL-Turbo / SD-TurboAdversarial + score distillation. Uses a discriminator + the teacher's score to train 1–4 step generation.~85–90% at 1 step, ~95% at 4 steps
SDXL-Lightning / Hyper-SDProgressive + consistency distillation. Combines multiple techniques.~92–98% at 4–8 steps

The diffusion ML engineer's mental model: A diffusion model is a denoiser trained to reverse a noise process. The training loop is simple (add noise to data, predict the noise, MSE loss), but the details matter enormously: noise schedule (use cosine or zero-SNR), prediction type (flow matching for new projects), loss weighting (Min-SNR-γ), conditioning (CFG with 10% dropout), and EMA (always). Data quality is the biggest quality lever — aesthetic filtering and caption re-writing outweigh architectural improvements. The architecture is converging on DiTs (Transformers on latent patches) over U-Nets. After training: evaluate with FID + CLIP score + human inspection, fine-tune with LoRA/DreamBooth for specific domains, and distill for fast inference.
