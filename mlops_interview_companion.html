The strategic read.

Maxime is a 17-year HPC veteran with a PhD in parallel computing and a deep specialty in I/O — his thesis work was on sparse computations and the ASIODS I/O library at Total, with a stint at DDN (the storage company behind Lustre and EXAScaler). He moved through Google HPC Cloud, then AWS guiding GenAI customers to scale training from dozens to thousands of GPU nodes, and joined NVIDIA in July 2025.

Why this matters for your interview

His GitLab work right now — storage-workload-tests with subdirectories for tensor_loading, pretraining_data_loading, and pretraining_checkpoint — is a near-direct continuation of his career-long obsession with the storage/compute seam. The latest commit ("Add ratio sigma avg to data loading") tells you he cares about benchmark methodology rigor: not just averages, but the coefficient of variation.

Practical implication

When you discuss debugging or optimization, lead with methodology first (how do you measure?) before fix (what do you tune?). HPC people respect a rigorous approach more than a flashy result. Always pair numbers with σ/μ.

The four mental models that will carry the interview

1 — Roofline

Every kernel is either compute-bound or memory-bound. The crossover for H100 BF16 is ~295 FLOPs/byte. Below that, only bandwidth or fewer bytes help; more SMs don't. Knowing where your workload sits on the roofline is the first step in any optimization conversation.

2 — Step-time decomposition

Total step time = compute + comm + I/O wait + idle. The question is never "is it slow" — it's "which one dominates, and why." NSight Systems is how you decompose; everything follows from that.

3 — The fleet view

One workload at 80% MFU is great; 1000 workloads at 35% average is the real opportunity. Maxime's team thinks in fleet-wide GPU-hours recovered. Frame your past wins in those terms when possible.

4 — Methodology over heroics

A reproducible 3% improvement that shipped beats a heroic 30% improvement that lived only on one branch. Bench harnesses, CI, σ/μ, version pinning — these are the artifacts of his profession.

Chapter 1 detail

Numbers to memorize cold:

Roofline arithmetic intensity crossover: peak_FLOPS / peak_BW. For H100 BF16: 989 / 3.35 ≈ 295 FLOPs/byte. Kernels below that are memory-bound; above are compute-bound.

Why FlashAttention wins: tiles Q/K/V into SRAM, recomputes softmax stats, eliminates the O(N²) HBM read/write of the attention matrix. Trades compute (recompute) for memory bandwidth.

Chapter 2 detail

nsys decision flow: capture with NVTX-annotated steps → identify GPU idle gaps → trace to source (CPU stall, sync API, allocator, NCCL straggler) → only then drill into individual kernels with ncu.

Standard nsys command:

nsys profile -t cuda,nvtx,osrt,cudnn,cublas,nccl \
  --gpu-metrics-device=all \
  --capture-range=cudaProfilerApi \
  --capture-range-end=stop \
  -o run python train.py

NSight Compute is for one kernel, not the whole run. Always pair with --launch-skip to avoid warmup, and --kernel-name regex for surgical capture.

Stall reasons to know: Long Scoreboard (memory dep) · Wait (barrier) · MIO Throttle (mem ops saturated) · Not Selected (low occupancy) · Drain (warp finishing).

Chapter 3 detail

Collective decomposition:

  • AllReduce = ReduceScatter + AllGather (FSDP exploits this split).
  • Ring AllReduce: 2(P-1)/P × tensor_size data per rank — bandwidth optimal.
  • Tree: latency optimal for small tensors.
  • NVLS: hardware-accelerated small-msg via NVLink SHARP.

Hang debugging: always set TORCH_NCCL_TRACE_BUFFER_SIZE=2000 in production; flight recorder dumps reveal which collective each rank is on when watchdog fires.

Parallelism stacking rule of thumb for big LLM: TP within node (NVLink, fast collectives), PP or DP across nodes (IB, less BW per step), FSDP/ZeRO for parameter/optimizer sharding.

Chapter 4 detail

Rail-aware setup: each GPU should bind to its NUMA-local HCA. Verify with nvidia-smi topo -m; matrix entries should be PIX or NV between GPU and the NIC it'll use, not SYS (crosses sockets).

Bandwidth verification ladder: ib_write_bw rank-to-rank (raw) → nccl-tests all_reduce_perf (collective) → your framework. If raw is fine but nccl-tests slow, suspect topology/algo. If nccl-tests fine but framework slow, suspect overlap or unrelated stall.

Bandwidth target for inter-node NCCL: ~45 GB/s busbw per rail on NDR (400 Gb/s = 50 GB/s line rate, 90% efficiency is realistic).

Chapter 5 detail

This is Maxime's home turf. The biggest opportunity for a memorable interview moment is here.

Lustre tuning levers: stripe count (lfs setstripe -c N), stripe size, OST balance. Wide stripe for big sequential reads; narrow stripe for many small files.

Tensor loading hierarchy (slowest → fastest):

  1. torch.load(pickled.pt) — single-thread, full copy through CPU
  2. safetensors.load_file(device="cpu") — zero-copy mmap
  3. Multi-threaded shard load with pinned staging
  4. cuFile / GDS — NIC/NVMe → GPU bypass CPU

Async checkpoint patterns: stage to CPU pinned (~ms) → background write (~s-min). Training continues. Local NVMe first, replicate later, is the durable production pattern.

Chapter 6 detail

The MFU formula:

FLOPs/token ≈ 6N (dense) + 12·L·S·H (attention)
MFU = (tokens/sec × FLOPs/token) / (n_gpus × peak_TFLOPS)

Target ≥40% on H100 dense; 30%+ on B200 (peaks higher, harder to feed).

FSDP modes: FULL_SHARD (max savings, max comm) vs HYBRID_SHARD (intra-node shard, inter-node replicate — usually faster).

FP8 isn't free: per-tensor scaling cost, amax history, format conversion. Embeddings & LM head often kept in BF16.

Chapter 7 detail

Prefill vs decode: compute-bound vs memory-bound, ideally separated (disaggregated serving) with different parallelism per phase.

Continuous batching: the single biggest throughput multiplier vs static batching. Both vLLM and SGLang do it.

PagedAttention: KV in fixed-size pages, eliminates fragmentation, enables prefix sharing across requests.

For tensor loading cold-start (Maxime's benchmark): weight load dominates. Multi-threaded shard loading critical for 600+ GB models.

Chapter 8 detail

Failure recovery math: with M=10k GPUs and MTTF/GPU = months, fleet MTBF is hours. Cheaper to checkpoint frequently than retrain hours of progress.

Modern stack: Megatron dist-ckpt or PyTorch DCP, async API, sharded format. Each rank writes its piece in parallel.

Resume is often slower than save — cold caches, format conversion. Pre-warm in prolog (Maxime's repo has a prolog.sh).

Chapter 9 detail

Canonical multi-node sbatch pattern: set MASTER_ADDR from scontrol show hostname, srun --cpu-bind=cores, one task per GPU, env vars via Slurm.

Prolog/epilog hooks are how reliability is enforced — drain on health check failure, drop caches for benchmarks, pre-stage data.

Useful diagnostics: sinfo -R (why nodes drained), squeue --start (estimated start), sacct -j JOB --format=... (postmortem).

Chapter 11 detail

PyTorch caching allocator avoids cudaMalloc on hot path. With FSDP / dynamic shapes, PYTORCH_CUDA_ALLOC_CONF=expandable_segments:True helps.

FSDP1 vs FSDP2: FSDP2 uses DTensor, cleaner sharding semantics, better tensor parallel composition. Most new work targets FSDP2.

Megatron-Core is the production reference. parallel_state manages process groups for TP/PP/DP/CP/EP. ModelParallelConfig + TransformerConfig are the entry points.

NeMo 2.0 wraps Megatron with Lightning's MegatronStrategy; nemo.collections.llm has recipes; NeMo-Run handles orchestration.

Debugging in trees.

When Maxime asks "the researcher says training is slow, what do you do?", the expected answer is a structured search, not a guess. These four trees cover the most common scenarios.

T1 — Training step is slow
nsys profile a single iteration with NVTX ranges
  │
  ├─ Decompose: compute% + comm% + idle% + dataloader%
  │
  ├─ Idle > 5%  ──── CPU dataloader bound?
  │                  ├─ tune num_workers, persistent_workers, pin_memory, prefetch_factor
  │                  ├─ host-device sync? search hot path for .item() .cpu() .numpy()
  │                  └─ allocator? check cudaMalloc in hot path
  │
  ├─ Comm > 30% ──── overlap broken? topology bad?
  │                  ├─ verify FSDP forward_prefetch / DDP overlap
  │                  ├─ NCCL_DEBUG=INFO → check rings cross spine?
  │                  └─ one rank slow on every collective? straggler hunt
  │
  └─ Compute & MFU<40% ──── kernel-level investigation
                            ├─ ncu top-3 kernels by time
                            ├─ memory-bound? torch.compile, FlashAttention, fused opt
                            ├─ GEMM bad? alignment, dtype, cuBLAS heuristics
                            └─ recompute too aggressive? switch to selective
T2 — NCCL hang
Set BEFORE the hang occurs:
  TORCH_NCCL_BLOCKING_WAIT=1
  TORCH_NCCL_TRACE_BUFFER_SIZE=2000
  TORCH_NCCL_DUMP_ON_TIMEOUT=1
  NCCL_DEBUG=INFO

When watchdog fires:
  │
  ├─ Inspect flight-recorder dumps per rank
  │   └─ which collective was each rank on?
  │
  ├─ All ranks same collective, same step?
  │   ├─ mismatched tensor shapes/dtypes  ── validate before launch
  │   ├─ mismatched collective ORDER      ── conditional code on one rank
  │   └─ dead rank (silent crash)         ── reduce heartbeat timeout
  │
  ├─ Different collectives across ranks?
  │   └─ classic ordering bug — code path divergence
  │
  └─ Hardware suspicion?
      ├─ ibstatus on every node → any link not at speed?
      ├─ dmesg | grep -i mlx → HCA errors?
      └─ NCCL ring crossing a known-bad node?
T3 — Cold tensor loading is slow (the Maxime question)
Step 1 — establish the FS ceiling:
  fio seq-read 1M direct=1 → record GB/s peak
  This is your floor for what's achievable.
  │
Step 2 — measure naive loader: safetensors.load_file()
  Effective GB/s = model_size / cold_load_time
  Cold = drop_caches first.
  │
Step 3 — close the gap to ceiling:
  │
  ├─ Single-threaded? add shard parallelism
  │   ├─ N threads, each mmaps one shard
  │   └─ tune N — too many → CPU contention reverses gain
  │
  ├─ Pinned memory in use? 
  │   ├─ no → non-pinned does extra copy through driver staging
  │   └─ yes → can issue async H2D, overlap shards
  │
  ├─ GPU Direct Storage possible?
  │   ├─ NVMe + cuFile → bypass CPU bounce
  │   └─ Lustre + LNet/GDS support? check the storage tier
  │
  ├─ Page cache?
  │   ├─ cold start → first run dominates real production
  │   ├─ warm → useful only for repeated loads in same process
  │   └─ ALWAYS drop_caches between cold measurements
  │
  └─ Storage backend chosen well?
      ├─ object store (S3) → range-GET in parallel
      ├─ Lustre → wide striping on the model dir
      └─ local NVMe → straightforward, fastest
T4 — Fleet-wide low utilization investigation
DCGM exporter → Prometheus → "GPUs with SM_active < 30% over 24h"
  │
  ├─ Pattern-of-one: single workload, all its GPUs
  │   ├─ inference cold start? batching off?
  │   ├─ small model, single GPU per node, no batching → educate user
  │   └─ profile their workload → standard step-time decomp
  │
  ├─ Pattern-of-many: same dataloader / framework across teams
  │   ├─ shared FS metadata bottleneck?
  │   ├─ default num_workers too low?
  │   └─ ship fix into common library or doc
  │
  ├─ Pattern-of-node: same physical node always low
  │   ├─ XID errors? hardware degraded?
  │   ├─ thermal throttling?
  │   ├─ network rail down or degraded?
  │   └─ drain and remediate
  │
  └─ Pattern-of-time: certain hours/days
      ├─ shared resource contention (FS, network)?
      ├─ batch jobs colliding?
      └─ scheduling policy work

Coming and going.

Questions he might ask you, with the approach you should reach for. Then: the questions you should ask him — the ones that demonstrate you've already done the work.

Behavioral / experience

"Tell me about a time you found a non-obvious inefficiency at scale."
Structure: (1) the surface symptom someone reported, (2) the wrong first guess you ruled out via measurement, (3) the methodology that pointed you to the real cause, (4) the fix and its measured impact, (5) what you put in place to catch it next time. The last bullet is what separates senior engineers.
"How do you onboard with a new research team without breaking their workflow?"
Read-only first: ride along, watch their actual scripts, understand their cadence. Profile their baseline before suggesting anything. Ship one tiny win (a single nsys trace they didn't have) to build trust. Only then propose larger changes.
"Walk me through a debugging session that took you days."
Honest version is fine. Show the hypothesis tree you built, the experiments that ruled branches out, and the surprise that resolved it. Senior engineers expect days-long bugs; pretending you always nail them in hours signals junior.

Technical scenarios

"A researcher says training is slow. How do you start?"
Answer with Tree T1. Do not start tuning. "First I'd capture an nsys trace of one annotated step, decompose into compute/comm/idle/dataloader, and let that point us to the right chapter." Then walk the branches.
"Cold start of a 600 GB model on Lustre takes 8 minutes. Walk me through optimization."
This is a Maxime-flavored question. Tree T3. Lead with: "First I'd measure the floor — what does fio give us on this Lustre mount with the same access pattern? If the FS ceiling is 30 GB/s, 600 GB shouldn't exceed 20s of pure read; if we're at 8 minutes, that's a 24× gap we need to close."
"We see 30% of step time in AllReduce. What's the investigation?"
Tree T2's cousin. Is overlap broken? Is the ring crossing the spine? Is it a straggler (one rank always last)? Verify with nccl-tests outside the framework first to isolate framework vs network.
"Checkpointing is costing us 4% of training. What's your approach?"
Direct match to Maxime's pretraining_checkpoint work. Async + distributed + local-first. But first measure where the time actually goes — GPU→CPU copy, serialization, disk write, or sync barrier. Each has a different remedy.
"Why might FP8 training be slower than BF16?"
Scaling-factor recomputation overhead on small kernels; format conversion at boundaries; some layers (embeddings, LM head) kept BF16 anyway so you don't get full speedup; amax history tracking; instability requiring more recompute. The point of the question is: do you know that "newer = faster" isn't automatic?

Mini system design

"Design a service that detects underutilized GPUs across the fleet."
DCGM exporter → Prometheus (already common). Define "underutilized" carefully (SM_active + Tensor_active + power — not just SM_util). Aggregate by workload (Slurm job ID), team, node. Surface to teams via dashboard. Crucially: separate "low util on purpose" (small model, deliberate replica) from "low util by accident" (dataloader bottleneck) — the latter is the real signal. Auto-suggest profiling step to flagged workloads.
"Design a checkpoint system for a 10k-GPU training job."
Async writer. Sharded distributed format (each rank writes its piece). Local-first to NVMe scratch (~5s), background replicate to durable Lustre (~min). Retention: every checkpoint + every Nth + every Mth older. Resume is profiled and optimized; warm prolog stages from durable to local NVMe before job starts. Health monitoring: alert if any rank's local write stalls.

The questions you ask Maxime — these matter most

Asking sharp questions signals you've thought about the work. Aim for 3-4 in the time slot.

"I noticed the storage-workload-tests benchmarks — is that point-in-time evaluation, or run continuously as a fleet health signal?"
Shows you've actually looked at his work and understand the difference between a one-shot benchmark and a regression-detection system. Sets up a real conversation.
"What's the team's biggest pain right now — the long pole on one tier-1 workload, or systemic patterns across the fleet?"
Reveals where you'd actually be spending your time, and lets him talk about something he likely thinks about a lot.
"How do you measure team success — GPU-hours recovered, MFU bump on key workloads, fleet utilization, something else?"
Shows you think about impact, not just activity. His answer also tells you how to position your work in the role.
"What's the split between reactive (a researcher escalates) and proactive (you find the issue first) work?"
If reactive is most of it, the role is closer to consulting. If proactive, it's closer to platform engineering. Different shapes.
"How does your team interact with the hardware, network, and storage orgs internally?"
NVIDIA has more deep-internal-knowledge available than any other company — the question shows you understand that's a leverage point.
"For the data-loading benchmarks — when you observe per-node throughput drop as you scale up, what's typically the long pole? OST contention, metadata, network, or something else?"
Bullseye question. Talks directly to his recent work. The answer is gold for you regardless of the interview outcome.
