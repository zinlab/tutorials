Part I — The role, decoded

What they're actually hiring for.

The job title says "Performance and Efficiency Engineer," but the posting hides what the work really is. This part translates the job description into the day-to-day reality of the MARS AI Efficiency team — the problems you'll own, the metrics you'll be judged on, and what "Senior" means on this specific team.

01What this role actually is

Strip the recruiter language from the posting and the role resolves to one sentence: you make NVIDIA's own AI researchers' training and inference workloads run more efficiently on giant GPU clusters, by building the tooling that finds waste and the engineering that removes it.

The posting's verbs are the giveaway. "Collaborate closely with AI/ML researchers to make their models more efficient." "Build tools, frameworks, and apply ML techniques to detect & analyze efficiency bottlenecks." "Proactively monitor fleet-wide utilization patterns... and deliver scalable solutions." That is not a kernel-optimization role and it is not a cluster-admin role. It is a role that sits in the seam between the researchers who run workloads and the infrastructure those workloads run on, and is measured by how much GPU time it converts from wasted to productive.

The three things this team is not

  • Not the cluster operations team. There is a separate "HPC Cluster Engineer" function that keeps Slurm alive, drains bad nodes, and runs the control plane.   • Not a CUDA-kernel performance team. You will read Nsight traces and reason about kernels, but the leverage here is fleet-scale: a tool that lifts goodput 5% across 18 clusters beats a hand-tuned kernel in one job. The role is performance engineering at the system and fleet altitude.
  • Not a pure research team. You ship production services — dashboards, pipelines, bots, eBPF tracers, Kubernetes CronJobs. The MLOps team's repos are real software with CI, releases, and on-call. "Apply ML techniques" appears in the posting, but the spine of the job is systems and data engineering.

The one-line framing to carry into every interview: "This team turns wasted GPU-hours into productive ones, at fleet scale, by instrumenting how researchers' jobs actually behave and then closing the gap — sometimes with a tool, sometimes with a config fix, sometimes by walking a researcher through their own training loop." Everything else is a detail under that sentence.

03The metrics you will own

You will live and die by a small set of numbers. Know them cold — what they mean, how they're computed, and how they mislead. Parts III covers each in depth; here is the orientation.

MetricWhat it measures
Goodput %Fraction of allocated GPU-time that did productive training work. The team's headline efficiency number. Run-time-weighted across jobs. Decomposed into loss causes (lost work, checkpoint, startup, shutdown).
Idle waste (GPU-hours)GPU-time at literally 0% utilization, measured in 5-minute DCGM windows. Application-agnostic, so it works even for un-instrumented jobs. A strict lower bound on real waste.
GPU utilization %Raw DCGM utilization. Coarse and misleading on its own (100% "utilization" can still be doing useless work), but the cheapest fleet-wide signal.
MFU / HFUModel (Hardware) FLOP Utilization — achieved FLOP/s ÷ peak FLOP/s. The gold-standard compute efficiency metric for a training run. Harder to get fleet-wide; needs per-model FLOP accounting.
ThroughputTokens/s (LLM), samples/s, or TFLOP/s/GPU (as the storage benchmarks report). The thing researchers actually feel.
Requeue / preemption rateHow often jobs die and restart. Each restart burns startup tax and risks lost work. A fleet-health and a goodput signal at once.
The metric hierarchy to articulate

If asked "which metric matters most," the senior answer is that they form a ladder of increasing fidelity and cost. Idle waste is cheapest and works everywhere but only catches the most egregious (0%) waste. Utilization is cheap but lies. Goodput is the right business metric but needs OneLogger instrumentation. MFU is the truest compute-efficiency number but needs per-model FLOP models. You pick the highest-fidelity metric the available instrumentation supports for the scope you're working at — fleet sweeps use idle waste/util; a deep engagement with one team uses goodput and MFU.

04Fleet patterns vs deep dives · the level bar

The posting names two distinct modes of work, and you should be ready to speak to both because different interviewers care about different ones.

Mode A — Fleet-wide pattern hunting

"Proactively monitor fleet-wide utilization patterns, analyze existing inefficiency patterns, or discover new patterns, and deliver scalable solutions." This is the top-down half: scan ~18 clusters, find the systemic waste (a framework default that hurts everyone, a checkpoint anti-pattern epidemic, a class of bad nodes), and ship something that fixes it once for everyone. The rules engine's SESSION-* family, the idle-waste fleet reports, and the bi-weekly nudge emails are this mode.

Mode B — Per-customer deep dives

"Collaborate closely with researchers to make their ML models more efficient." This is the bottom-up half: a specific team's jobs are wasting GPU-hours, and you sit with them, pull every telemetry source for their jobs, find the root cause, and walk them to a fix. The idle-waste-toolkit's per-job/per-execution CSVs and sisyphus's single-job triangulation are built for exactly this.

The strongest candidates show they can move between altitudes: spot a pattern in the fleet, drill into one job to understand the mechanism, then generalize the fix back to the fleet. That oscillation is the senior skill on this team.

What "Senior / L3–L4" means here

The posting lists L3 ($152k–$241.5k base) and L4 ($184k–$287.5k base), 5+ years operating large-scale compute infrastructure. Reading the "what we need" and "ways to stand out" lists, the bar resolves to:

  • End-to-end ownership. "Investigating and resolving training & inference performance end to end." You can own a problem from a fleet metric down to a kernel trace and back up to a shipped fix — without a hand-off at each layer.
  • Distributed-training fluency. "Debugging large-scale distributed training using NCCL," Nsight Systems/Compute. You can reason about what a 512-GPU job is doing and why it's slow.
  • Builder, not just analyst. Python/Go/Bash, cloud platforms, parallel computing. The team's output is software; you ship it.
  • The stand-out signals are differentiators, not requirements: CUDA, NCCL, MLPerf, InfiniBand/RDMA, Lustre/GPFS, PyTorch. You don't need all of them; each one you have genuinely lets you skip a level of explanation with the relevant interviewer.
Where to be honest with yourself

This role rewards breadth across the stack more than depth in any one layer. If your strength is, say, CUDA and distributed PyTorch (it is — see §28), your gap to close is the operations and observability altitude: Slurm at fleet scale, the telemetry data model, multi-cloud cluster realities. This manual front-loads exactly that material so the interviews land on your strengths and your prep covers the gaps.

Why GB200 NVL72 deserves special attention

Two of the team's most pointed pieces of work are GB200-specific, and they reveal what makes rack-scale NVLink different:

  • The DCGM NVLink-polling slowdown (Matt's DCGM-NVLink-Test-Harness): on GB200, dcgm-exporter polling certain NVLink performance counters acquires the NVIDIA driver lock at high frequency, contending with user CUDA workloads and causing a ~10× slowdown on cuMem* driver calls (the fabric-memory APIs that MNNVL leans on). This is a pure efficiency bug born from rack-scale NVLink + telemetry colliding. (See §24 — it's a perfect interview story.)
  • Topology-aware scheduling: because a GB200 rack is one 72-GPU NVLink domain split into trays, which trays your job lands on matters for performance. The slurm-utils get-avail-topology command shows free nodes per block precisely so jobs can be packed into NVLink-local placements. On H100 you reason about leaf switches; on GB200 you reason about blocks and trays.
The interview-ready hardware sound bite

"On H100 the NVLink domain is the 8-GPU node and the fabric between nodes is IB or EFA, so topology reasoning is about switch locality and rail alignment. On GB200 NVL72 the whole 72-GPU rack is one NVLink domain over MNNVL, the hosts are ARM, and the fabric-memory cuMem* path becomes hot — so topology reasoning shifts to tray/block placement, and even telemetry like DCGM NVLink polling can perturb the driver lock. Same efficiency questions, different mechanism per generation." Saying this signals you actually understand the fleet.

The pieces of this loop you must be able to discuss

  • Enroot + Pyxis, not Docker. HPC clusters can't give users Docker (it needs root). Enroot is the rootless runtime that converts OCI images to .sqsh squashfs files; Pyxis is the Slurm plugin (--container-image) that runs them per-task. Images are pulled from NGC (nvcr.io) and cached on the shared filesystem. First-job image import dominates first-time cost; the storage benchmarks even sbcast the image to local /raid on every node to avoid re-reading it.
  • Login node vs head node vs compute node. Submit from login nodes; never the head node (it runs the control plane). SSH to compute nodes is often restricted to nodes you have a job on. These rules are in the team's "best practices" and are exactly the kind of operational literacy the Slurm interviewer (§26) will check.
  • Lustre is home base. On AWS/OCI clusters, project data lives at /lustre/fsw/portfolios/<portfolio>/users/<user>. Storage performance (data loading, checkpoint writes) is therefore an efficiency surface — hence the whole storage-workload-tests repo.
  • GB200 = ARM. Containers and binaries must be aarch64 on GB200 clusters. The NCCL-plugin container builds are multi-arch (amd64 + arm64) precisely for this.

Efficiency fixes have to live where the researcher already is. A goodput improvement that requires the researcher to change their whole workflow won't get adopted; one that ships as a better container default, a Slurm setting, a OneLogger hook they already have, or a one-line sbatch change will. The team's tools (startup utilities, opinionated setup-cluster.sh, default Slurm account, container helpers) are all about reducing the friction of doing the efficient thing. Carry this principle into any "how would you roll out a fix" question.

Part III — The efficiency mandate

Goodput, all the way down.

This is the conceptual heart of the role and the territory both required interviewers live in. "Efficiency" sounds vague until you can decompose it precisely — into a layered model of goodput, a per-phase anatomy of where GPU-hours actually go, and a clear distinction from the cruder idle-waste signal. Master this part and you can hold a real conversation about the team's core metric.

10Goodput: the three-layer model

Goodput is the fraction of a resource's time spent doing useful work. For a training cluster, the ultimate definition is: of all the GPU-hours a job was allocated, what fraction advanced the model. The team's sisyphus tool frames this as a three-layer model, and this framing is the single most useful thing to have crisp for the interviews.

ALLOCATED GPU-TIME (what the scheduler gave the job) │ ├── LAYER 1 — SCHEDULING goodput source: Heimdall / jobsinfo │ Did the allocation actually run, or was it lost to │ queue waits, requeues, preemptions, node failures? │ "Was the GPU even running the user's process?" │ ├── LAYER 2 — RUNTIME goodput source: OneLogger │ Of the time the process ran, how much was productive │ training vs startup / checkpoint / lost-work / shutdown? │ "Was the running process doing training?" │ └── LAYER 3 — PROGRAM goodput source: DCGM / Nsight Of the time it was "training," how efficiently did the GPUs compute? (MFU, stalls, comm/compute overlap, stragglers) "Was the training itself using the silicon well?" TOTAL GOODPUT ≈ Layer1 × Layer2 × Layer3 (each layer multiplies)

The power of this model is that it tells you which team owns a given loss and which telemetry source to consult. A job with terrible end-to-end efficiency could be losing at any layer, and the fix is completely different at each:

LayerLoss looks likeOwner / fix
1 · SchedulingJob requeues 17×; long queue waits; preempted; bad node kills itCluster ops + scheduler policy; Heimdall remediation; fix bad nodes
2 · RuntimeProcess runs but 40% of time is startup + checkpoint + lost workThe researcher's training config; checkpoint cadence; dataloader
3 · ProgramTraining "runs" but MFU is 25%; stragglers; comm not overlappedModel/parallelism tuning; NCCL config; kernel-level work

This layered model is your single best interview asset. When any interviewer hands you a vague "this job is inefficient, what do you do," you decompose: first establish which layer is losing — was the GPU running (scheduling)? was the process training (runtime)? was the training efficient (program)? — because each layer has different telemetry, a different owner, and a different fix. Naming the three layers and the data source for each instantly signals you think like this team.

11Runtime goodput, decomposed

Layer 2 — runtime goodput — is where the team's flagship instrumentation, OneLogger, operates, and it's the layer most of the tooling targets. OneLogger is an application-level library that wraps the training loop (in Megatron-LM / NeMo and friends) and emits a structured summary of where every second went. That summary lands in OpenSearch (df-hwinf-dcm-e2e-app-metrics-summary-*) and is the substrate for goodput dashboards and the rules engine.

The time budget

OneLogger partitions a run's wall-clock into accounted buckets. Runtime goodput is the productive bucket over the total:

STARTUP (model init, dataloader, optimizer, │ container load, checkpoint load) ├── d_train_iterations_time_total_productive ... PRODUCTIVE TRAINING ◄── the numerator ├── d_save_checkpoint_sync_time_total ........... CHECKPOINT (synchronous portion) ├── d_validation_iterations_time_total .......... VALIDATION ├── d_lost_work_time .............................. LOST WORK (trained, then discarded) ├── d_unsaved_work_time ........................... UNSAVED WORK (not yet checkpointed) ├── d_uncategorized_time .......................... UNATTRIBUTED (a data-quality smell) └── shutdown ....................................... The field names look arcane but they tell a story, and the rules engine's thresholds (from rules_config.yaml) tell you what "bad" means in production:

Bucket"Too high" threshold & meaning
Startup> 30% of runtime → GL-STARTUP. Init is eating the run. Worse on short jobs and frequently-requeued jobs (every restart re-pays it).
Lost work> 20% → GL-LOST-WORK. Training done after the last checkpoint that got thrown away when the job ended. Pure waste; the checkpoint cadence was too sparse.

Uncategorized> 10% → GL-UNCATEGORIZED. Time the instrumentation couldn't attribute — itself a signal something is off.
Checkpoint interval> 4h avg → GL-CKPT-INTERVAL. Saving too rarely; raises lost-work risk on every failure.

The zero-goodput failure modes

The most dramatic findings are the G0-* family — jobs reporting 0% goodput, often because the instrumentation itself wasn't wired correctly, not because no training happened. This distinction is crucial and a great thing to understand:

  • G0-HOOK — on_save_checkpoint_end() was never called, so productive time reads 0 even though the job trained. A wiring bug, not a training bug. Epidemic-level when a whole team copies a broken config (SESSION-HOOK-EPIDEMIC fires at ≥50%).
  • G0-CKPT-DISABLED — checkpointing disabled, so all time is counted non-productive by the goodput definition (work that can't be saved isn't "good").
  • G0-NO-TRAIN — genuinely zero training iterations despite >10 min runtime. The job really did nothing useful (crash loop, stuck dataloader, mis-config).
  • DQ-WANDB-WRONG-ENTITY — the run logged to the wrong WandB entity, so the monitor never saw it. A pure observability gap masquerading as zero goodput.
The honesty problem: instrumented ≠ measured

Goodput is "instrumented-only" — a job contributes to the metric only if it carries the e2e_metrics_enabled tag and logged correctly. So a fleet goodput number is really "goodput among jobs we could see." A huge part of the team's actual work is coverage: getting more jobs instrumented (the Coverage / Adoption-Funnel dashboards), and catching jobs that think they're instrumented but aren't (wrong entity, missing hook). When you discuss goodput, acknowledging the coverage caveat is a senior tell — it's the difference between trusting a dashboard and understanding it.

12The startup tax

"Startup" sounds trivial until you realize a large job that requeues 17 times pays it 17 times, and that for short jobs it can dwarf the actual training. The team breaks startup into five sub-phases (the Goodput dashboard's "Startup Breakdown" pie), each with its own threshold and its own fix.

Startup phaseWhat it is · how to attack it
Container LoadPulling/extracting the .sqsh image and starting the container. Fix: pre-cache images on the cluster; sbcast to local /raid; pin image tags. First-job import dominates.
Model InitBuilding the model and allocating parameters/optimizer on GPU. Fix: meta-device init, lazy materialization, avoiding redundant CPU→GPU copies.
Optimizer buildConstructing the (often distributed) optimizer state. Fix: distributed-optimizer init patterns; avoid rebuilding when resuming.
Dataloader InitBuilding data iterators, opening dataset shards, warming the input pipeline. Fix: this is where storage and the dataset blend matter; S3/object-store stalls show up here.
Checkpoint LoadReading the resume checkpoint back from storage. Fix: parallel/distributed load (--ckpt-fully-parallel-load); fast storage; this grows with model size and is paid on every requeue.

The thresholds in production: each sub-phase fires a warning at >30% of total startup (with a 60-second floor so trivially short phases don't alarm). So the tooling doesn't just say "startup is high" — it says "startup is high and it's the checkpoint-load phase," which points straight at storage.

Startup is where scheduling-loss and runtime-loss compound

This is the elegant, interview-worthy insight: a Layer-1 problem (frequent requeues/preemptions) becomes a Layer-2 cost (startup tax paid repeatedly). A job that's preempted every 30 minutes and takes 8 minutes to start up is throwing away ~25% of its life to startup alone — and the root cause is a scheduling decision, not a training one. This is exactly the cross-layer reasoning the three-layer model enables, and naming this compounding effect is a strong signal in any of the technical interviews.

13Lost work & the checkpoint economy

Checkpointing is the single richest efficiency topic on this team because it sits at the intersection of training, storage, and failure — and the storage-workload-tests repo is entirely about getting it right. Understand the checkpoint economy and you can speak fluently to Greg (storage/observability), Aman (workload performance), and the Slurm interviewer (failure/requeue).

The two kinds of wasted training

  • Lost work (d_lost_work_time): training that happened after the last successful checkpoint and was discarded when the job ended/crashed. It's gone forever. The cure is checkpointing more often — but that costs checkpoint overhead.
  • Unsaved work (d_unsaved_work_time): training that's been done but not yet persisted — at risk of becoming lost work if the job dies now.

So there's a genuine optimization: checkpoint too rarely and you risk large lost-work on failure; checkpoint too often and you pay checkpoint overhead and stall training. The right interval depends on the failure rate (Layer 1!) and the checkpoint cost (storage). On a flaky cluster you checkpoint more; on fast storage you can afford to.

How modern distributed checkpointing actually works

The storage benchmark exercises the production pattern — Megatron-LM's asynchronous distributed checkpoint — and the mechanism is worth knowing precisely:

At a checkpoint iteration: 1. PAUSE training; sync gradients + distributed optimizer state 2. COPY tensors GPU → CPU (pinned) ........... "GPU→CPU preload" phase 3. RESUME training immediately ◄── this is why it's "async" 4. BACKGROUND writer threads flush CPU buffers → storage (Megatron default: 2 writer threads/GPU → 256 GPUs writes 512 files) Two timing signals in the logs: • save-checkpoint .......... time to serialize + hand off to background (small if healthy) • finalize-async-save ...... time spent WAITING for the PREVIOUS async save to flush ◄── THIS reflects real storage write bandwidth Key rule: a checkpoint must finish writing before the next one starts. If storage can't keep up, training BLOCKS at finalize-async-save, and step-time spikes every SAVE_INTERVAL iterations.

The diagnostic signatures the team's parser looks for are textbook efficiency reasoning:

SignalHealthyDegraded
save-checkpoint timeSmall, constant across savesGrowing — serialization/staging slow
finalize-async-save~0 — writer drained before next saveRising → storage write BW is the bottleneck
Per-step time near saveFlat through SAVE_INTERVAL boundariesSpikes every SAVE_INTERVAL → writes blocking training
Per-rank GPU→CPU σLow, uniform across ranksOne rank's σ explodes → single bad node/PCIe link

And the sizing math is concrete: a 56B-param checkpoint stores FP32 grad-weights + FP32 Adam variance + FP32 Adam momentum + BF16 weights = 56.5B × (4+4+4+2) ≈ 737 GiB per checkpoint. At 256 GPUs that's 512 files of ~1.44 GiB each, written in parallel — which is exactly why you benchmark parallel filesystems (Lustre) differently from per-thread-stream ones (VAST/NFS).

The silent-corruption trap (a great war story to know)

One subtle failure: a job gets its kill signal mid-checkpoint, the save is interrupted, and on the next requeue the resume code finds a malformed checkpoint and silently starts over — losing not just unsaved work but a whole prior checkpoint. The lesson the field has learned: the signal-to-kill warning window must exceed the actual checkpoint write time, and resume code should fail loudly on a bad checkpoint rather than silently restart. This is the kind of cross-cutting (Slurm signal × storage × training) reasoning that marks a senior efficiency engineer.

14Idle waste vs goodput

The team maintains two different waste signals, and confusing them is a rookie error. Knowing exactly why both exist — and why neither subsumes the other — is a strong signal of efficiency-domain maturity. (Matt Nightingale is literally the "GPU Idle Waste PIC," so this distinction is load-bearing for that interview.)

Idle waste

GPU-time at 0% utilization, measured in 5-minute DCGM windows (gpu_util_zero_usage_summary_5m). Not application-aware — it needs no instrumentation, so it works on every job on every cluster, including jobs that don't use OneLogger. It is a strict lower bound on real waste: a GPU at 5% util still counts as "productive" here. Cheap, universal, conservative.

Goodput loss

Allocated GPU-time not spent on productive training, from OneLogger's time budget. Application-aware and far more precise — it knows the difference between checkpoint, startup, and lost work. But it only exists for instrumented jobs, and it's a fuller accounting (a busy-but-useless GPU shows as goodput loss but not as idle waste).

The team explicitly rejects the naive metric 100 − util% as "waste" because it over-counts: most training legitimately cycles through low-util phases (checkpoint, data loading) that aren't really waste. So idle waste deliberately counts only the unambiguous 0% case, accepting that it misses partial waste, in exchange for being trustworthy and instrumentation-free.

REAL WASTE (unknowable exactly) ├── IDLE WASTE (0% util) ........... captured cheaply, everywhere — LOWER BOUND ├── low-util-but-not-idle ......... missed by idle-waste; caught by goodput if instrumented └── busy-but-useless .............. missed by idle-waste; caught by goodput (lost work, etc.) idle waste ⊂ goodput loss ⊂ real waste (each a tighter-but-costlier estimate)

The two metrics are complementary tools for different jobs, not competitors. Use idle waste for fleet-wide sweeps and un-instrumented workloads — it's the only signal that works everywhere, and the bi-weekly nudge emails run on it. Use goodput for instrumented deep-dives where you need to attribute the loss to a cause. A great answer to "how do you measure cluster waste" walks both: "idle waste gives me a trustworthy floor across the whole fleet with zero instrumentation; goodput gives me the precise breakdown wherever OneLogger is wired up; I reconcile the two and chase coverage to shrink the gap."

15The metric zoo: util, MFU, throughput

Beyond goodput and idle waste, you'll be asked about the classic performance metrics. The senior skill is knowing what each one can't tell you.

MetricDefinition & the lie it tells
GPU utilization (DCGM GPU_UTIL)% of time at least one kernel was running. The lie: a memory-bound kernel doing almost no FLOPs shows ~100% util. "Utilization" ≠ "useful." Good for catching idle GPUs, useless for judging compute efficiency.
SM activity / occupancyHow busy the streaming multiprocessors are. Finer than util but still doesn't tell you if the math is useful work or, e.g., recomputation.
MFU (Model FLOP Utilization)Achieved model FLOP/s ÷ peak GPU FLOP/s. The truth metric for training compute efficiency. 35–50% is good for large LLMs; below ~25% something is wrong (comm not overlapped, small batch, bad parallelism). The catch: needs a per-model analytical FLOP count, so it's hard to get fleet-wide.
HFU (Hardware FLOP Utilization)Like MFU but counts all FLOPs including activation recomputation. HFU ≥ MFU; the gap tells you how much you're paying for recompute.
Throughput (tokens/s, samples/s, TFLOP/s/GPU)What researchers feel. The storage data-loading benchmark reports TFLOP/s/GPU and the rule of thumb is "you want a flat line" — variance means something is stalling.
Tokens-per-GPU-per-second / MBUFor inference: Memory Bandwidth Utilization matters more than FLOP utilization, because decode is memory-bound. Different regime from training.

18The join model: the execution backbone

Fusing those sources correctly is a real data-engineering problem, and the idle-waste-toolkit's join model is the canonical pattern. If the observability interviewer asks "how would you combine these," this is the answer.

jobsinfo-done is the backbone

The insight: jobsinfo-done has exactly one row per Slurm execution — i.e., per requeue cycle — so it is the spine onto which every other source is grafted. A job that requeued 17 times has 17 backbone rows; everything else layers onto those rows.

BACKBONE: jobsinfo-done key = (s_jobid, s_cluster, ts_start_time) one row per execution (requeue cycle) │ ├──◄ FACT joins on ts_slurmdb_start_time (±300s clock-skew tolerance) │ one record per execution → failure attribution │ ├──◄ OneLogger joins on ts_start_time (±300s tolerance) │ one record per execution → goodput breakdown │ ├──◄ GPU Util JOB-level (not per-execution) → waste totals repeat │ across every execution row of the same job │ └──◄ AI Job Insights PER-SUBMISSION → classification repeats unchanged across all executions of the job Two output granularities: • per-JOB CSV ......... multi-execution data folded into agg_* columns (customer hand-off) • per-EXECUTION CSV ... one row per requeue cycle (drill-down / triage)

Two non-obvious rules make this robust, and both are great details to cite:

  • Cluster scoping is mandatory. Slurm reuses job IDs across clusters, so the join key must include s_cluster — otherwise job 3301425 on AWS collides with job 3301425 on OCI. Cross-cluster collisions stay distinct via a multi_terms aggregation keyed on (s_cluster, s_jobid).

Graceful degradation is the design requirement, not a nice-to-have

At fleet scale, sources are routinely partial: FACT lags 24h for new clusters, OneLogger only exists for instrumented jobs, AI Insights coverage is spotty. The toolkits are explicitly built so that missing enrichment just leaves blank columns while the Slurm-side backbone facts always render. sisyphus lists "graceful degradation when data sources are unavailable" as a headline feature. If you propose a fusion design in an interview, stating "the backbone always renders; enrichments are best-effort and annotated when missing" signals you've actually thought about production data, not a clean-room demo.

worked case A 512-GPU job reporting 0% goodput
The alert

The Goodput dashboard shows a 512×H100 job on gcp-iad-cs-001 at 0.0% goodput, 4.2h runtime. The fleet rule G0-HOOK fired CRITICAL. Naively this reads as "512 GPUs did nothing for 4 hours" — ~2,150 GPU-hours of pure waste. Is that real?

Step 1 — which layer is losing? (the three-layer test)

Check Layer 1 (scheduling) via jobsinfo-done: the execution ran 4.2h, exit code 0, no requeue — so the GPU was allocated and the process ran. Not a scheduling loss. Check Layer 3 (program) via DCGM util: GPUs showed high utilization the whole time — so they were busy. That's the tell: busy GPUs + 0% goodput means the measurement is wrong, not the work.

Step 2 — confirm the hook hypothesis (OneLogger fields)

The OneLogger record shows d_train_iterations_time_total_productive = 0 but b_is_save_checkpoint_enabled = True and l_save_checkpoint_count = 0. Translation: checkpointing was enabled but zero checkpoints were recorded, and productive time is zero — the classic signature of on_save_checkpoint_end() never being called. The training happened; the instrumentation hook wasn't wired, so OneLogger couldn't attribute any time as productive.

Step 3 — cross-source confirmation (don't trust one signal)

Pull WandB for the run: the training-loss curve is descending normally and iteration metrics are present → training was genuinely progressing. Pull FACT: no failure, no bad nodes. So three sources agree the work was real and only OneLogger's productive-time accounting is broken. The 0% is an observability defect, not 2,150 wasted GPU-hours.

Step 4 — is it systemic? (escalate the scope)

Run the cluster scope: SESSION-HOOK-EPIDEMIC fires — >50% of this team's checkpoint-enabled jobs show the same hook-miss. So it's not one job; the whole team copied a training config with a broken/old OneLogger version that doesn't populate the hook flags (DQ-NO-WANDB-TAG / version check confirms).

Step 5 — act, at the right altitude

The fix is not per-job. Identify the OneLogger version / config snippet the team shares, push the corrected hook wiring (or a version bump) into their template, and verify next-run goodput rises from 0% to its true value. One template fix recovers visibility for the whole team — and the true goodput then reveals whatever the real (now measurable) inefficiency is, which is the next investigation.

The lesson

The headline number (0% goodput) was a coverage bug masquerading as catastrophic waste. The three-layer test (was it scheduled? was it busy? was it measured?) separated "the work was bad" from "the measurement was bad" in two queries — and the right fix was a fleet-level template change, not a per-job patch. That oscillation between one job and the cohort is the whole role in miniature.

Every good diagnosis starts by asking which layer is lying — the scheduler, the runtime, or the silicon — and ends by fixing it at the broadest altitude that still works.

The three pillars he'll probe

1 · Parallelism

Be fluent in the parallelism dimensions and when each is used. Data parallel (replicate model, all-reduce gradients — communication is the gradient all-reduce). Tensor parallel (split each layer across GPUs — heavy intra-layer comm, kept inside an NVLink domain). Pipeline parallel (split layers into stages across nodes — bubble overhead is the cost). Expert parallel (route tokens to experts — all-to-all comm, exactly the single-rack-alltoall test). Sequence/context parallel (split the sequence dimension for long context). The storage-checkpoint benchmark even shows the practical mapping: 56B needs TP=4 on Blackwell, TP=8 on H100, then data parallelism across nodes.

"You have a model that doesn't fit on one GPU. How do you parallelize it, and what's the cost of each choice?"
Walk the hierarchy by communication locality: TP first (but keep it within the NVLink domain — 8 on H100, within the rack on GB200 — because it's the most comm-intensive), then PP across nodes (cheap comm but pipeline bubbles), then DP on top (gradient all-reduce, overlappable). Tie each to the fabric: TP wants NVLink, DP all-reduce tolerates IB/EFA. Mention that GB200 NVL72's 72-GPU NVLink domain changes the calculus — you can do much larger TP/EP inside one rack than on H100.

2 · Observability for ML workloads

This overlaps Matt but from the workload-measurement angle: how do you instrument and benchmark a training job to know it's healthy. His tools are the answer — standardized, repeatable Slurm benchmarks that isolate one subsystem at a time (nccl-tests for the network, GEMM for compute, nvloom for the MNNVL fabric, storage tests for I/O). The philosophy from mlops-tests: diagnostics answer "is it broken" (binary), micro-benchmarks answer "how fast is this component" (isolate a subsystem), workloads answer "how does it handle my real job" (whole stack).

"How would you tell if a cluster is performing to spec?"
Layered benchmarking, exactly his repo's structure: per-node diagnostics (dcgm, sdl) to find broken hardware; micro-benchmarks (nccl all-reduce/all-to-all, GEMM, nvloom, kernel-launch) to measure each subsystem against a baseline; then a real workload (Nemotron proxy) for the whole-stack number. Compare against established baselines per platform — and stormbreaker runs the real-workload version on every node/rack to find the underperformers.

3 · Performance modeling

This is the quantitative core. Be ready to reason with an analytical model rather than just measuring. Roofline (is this kernel compute-bound or memory-bound — ridge point = peak FLOP/s ÷ peak BW). MFU as achieved-FLOPs ÷ peak-FLOPs with the per-model FLOP count (6 × params × tokens for a dense transformer forward+backward, roughly). Scaling efficiency (how throughput grows with GPU count and where comm overhead bends the curve). Amdahl/communication-bound reasoning for "why doesn't 2× the GPUs give 2× the throughput."

"You add more GPUs and throughput doesn't scale linearly. Model why."
Decompose per-step time into compute (scales down with more GPUs if you shard) + communication (often grows or stays fixed — all-reduce volume per step). At some point comm dominates and adding GPUs stops helping; that's the scaling knee. Quantify: if a step is 80% compute / 20% comm at N GPUs, doubling to 2N halves compute but the all-reduce may not halve, so efficiency drops. Then name the mitigations: overlap comm with compute, increase per-GPU batch, use a more bandwidth-optimal collective, keep TP within NVLink.

Greg's domain is the closest to your demonstrated strengths. Your 400× throughput improvement with distributed PyTorch + Ray on Kubernetes is a performance-modeling-and-scaling story — be ready to explain where the speedup came from (better parallelism? removed a serialization bottleneck? overlapped comm?) and how you measured scaling efficiency.
