Part I — The goodput model, formalized

What we actually measure.

Manual I introduced goodput as a three-layer idea; this volume makes it precise. Goodput is the fraction of allocated GPU-time that turned into useful model training — and the reason it needs three layers is that a GPU-hour can be wasted in three structurally different places: before the job runs, while it runs but isn't productive, and while it runs at low utilization. Get this decomposition exact and the entire platform — every index, rule, and tool — falls into place as an instrument for measuring one of these layers.

01What goodput really measures

Start from the question the whole team exists to answer: of all the GPU-hours we paid for, what fraction produced useful training? Everything else is a refinement of this ratio.

GOODPUT = useful training GPU-time ÷ allocated GPU-time allocated GPU-time ├── time the job wasn't even running productively │ ├── SCHEDULING layer: allocated but not training │ │ (queue→start gaps, node failures, requeues, preemption) │ └── RUNTIME layer: running but not productive │ (startup tax, checkpoint stalls, lost work, validation) │ └── time the job WAS training └── PROGRAM layer: training, but at what GPU utilization? (idle waste — 0%-util windows; low MFU) GOODPUT = scheduling_eff × runtime_eff × program_eff (the three layers multiply)

The three layers are not arbitrary — they correspond to three different owners and three different fixes:

LayerThe waste · the owner · the instrument
SchedulingGPUs allocated to a job that isn't training (queue gaps, NODE_FAIL, requeue, preemption). Owner: the platform/ops + the scheduler. Instrument: Heimdall, jobsinfo-done.
RuntimeThe job is running but the time isn't productive training (startup, checkpoint, lost work, validation). Owner: the framework config + the researcher. Instrument: OneLogger.
ProgramThe job is training, but GPUs sit underutilized (idle-waste windows, low MFU). Owner: the researcher's code + the data pipeline. Instrument: DCGM utilization.

The single most important conceptual move in the whole platform: waste is not one thing, so you cannot measure it with one number. "GPU utilization" alone is famously misleading (Manual I §15) — it sees only the program layer, and even there it confuses "a kernel ran" with "useful work happened." A job at 95% utilization can have terrible goodput if it spends 30% of its life re-paying startup after requeues (a scheduling+runtime loss that utilization can't see). The three-layer decomposition is what lets the team attribute every wasted GPU-hour to a specific, fixable cause — and it's why there are separate data sources and tools for each layer. Internalizing this is the foundation for Matt's "system thinking" interview.

02The scheduling layer (Heimdall)

The first layer: GPUs are allocated to a job but the job isn't training. This is the layer the scheduler and the fleet's health own, and it's measured against the execution record (jobsinfo-done) and Heimdall.

SCHEDULING-LAYER losses (allocated ≠ training): ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐ │ queue→start gap — allocated nodes idle before the job starts │ │ NODE_FAIL — hardware died under the job (Manual II §33)│ │ requeue churn — job cycles RUNNING→FAIL→REQUEUE, re-paying │ │ preemption — a higher-priority job took the nodes │ │ hung/zombie jobs — allocated, GPUs idle, no progress (§37 ML3) │ └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘ These are the most EXPENSIVE wastes: every GPU in the allocation is burning while producing zero training. A hung 512-GPU job is the canonical catastrophe.

Heimdall is the scheduling-layer instrument and actor: it detects who or what disrupted a job (a node failure, a preemption, a crash) and drives remediation (drain the node, requeue, alert). It answers "why did this allocation stop producing useful work, and whose fault was it" — bad hardware, the scheduler, or the application. That attribution is what separates a scheduling-layer loss (drain the node) from a runtime-layer loss (fix the config).

Why this layer is Matt's territory

Matt's body of work (the idle-waste toolkit, the bi-weekly nudges, sisyphus, the GPU-Idle-Waste PIC) lives largely at the intersection of the scheduling and program layers — finding GPUs that are allocated but not doing useful work, at fleet scale. The idle-waste definition itself ("0% utilization for a 5-minute window, not application-aware") is deliberately a blunt, allocation-vs-utilization measure that catches both a hung job (scheduling) and a starved one (program) without needing to know the app's intent. Understanding that this bluntness is a feature — it scales to 18 clusters because it needs no per-app knowledge — is exactly the system-thinking framing Matt will probe.

03The runtime layer (OneLogger)

The second layer: the job is running, but not all of that time is productive training. This is the richest layer because the framework instruments itself — OneLogger emits a detailed time budget — and it's where most actionable, researcher-facing efficiency wins live.

RUNTIME-LAYER time budget (the job's wall-clock, decomposed by OneLogger): l_run_time (total runtime) ├── productive training ── d_train_iterations_time_total_productive ← the GOOD part ├── startup tax ── d_app_startup_time (container, init, ckpt load) ├── lost work ── d_lost_work_time (re-run since last checkpoint) ├── unsaved work ── d_unsaved_work_time (progress not yet checkpointed) ├── checkpoint overhead ── save/finalize time └── validation ── eval passes interrupting training runtime_eff = productive ÷ run_time → OneLogger lets you say EXACTLY where a running job's time went.

The key property: OneLogger is application-aware — it knows the difference between "loading a checkpoint" and "training iteration 4,201," because the framework (Megatron/NeMo) emits the events. So unlike the blunt idle-waste signal (§02), OneLogger can attribute runtime waste to a specific phase: this job lost 22% to startup, 18% to lost work, 5% to validation. That precision is what makes the runtime layer the most fixable — each phase has a known lever (Manual I §11–§13).

The two-instrument complementarity

This is a subtle, important point for the observability interview: DCGM utilization (program layer) is application-blind but always available; OneLogger (runtime layer) is application-aware but only present if the framework is instrumented. The platform uses both because they answer different questions and have different coverage — DCGM catches any job's idle GPUs (even uninstrumented ones), while OneLogger explains why an instrumented job's time was unproductive. When they disagree (DCGM says busy, OneLogger says unproductive), that discrepancy is itself a signal (§28). Knowing why you need both application-aware and application-blind telemetry is a sophisticated observability insight.

04The program layer (DCGM)

The third layer: the job is training, but how hard are the GPUs actually working? This is the layer raw DCGM utilization measures — and the layer most prone to the "100% util but slow" trap (Manual III §02).

SignalWhat it sees · its blind spot
GPU utilization (DCGM)Was a kernel running in this sample window? Catches idle GPUs (0%-util = idle waste). Blind to what the kernel did — a slow FP32 GEMM reads 100%.
SM activity / occupancy (DCGM)Finer-grained: were the SMs actually busy, not just "a kernel exists"? Closer to real work but still not FLOP-aware.
Tensor-core activity (DCGM)Were the tensor cores engaged? A better proxy for useful training FLOPs than gross utilization.
MFU (modeled, not DCGM)The truth metric (Manual III §28) — achieved vs peak FLOP/s. DCGM can't compute it directly; it requires the FLOP model. The program layer's real efficiency.
The PROGRAM-layer ladder of fidelity (Manual I §15, formalized): gross utilization ─ "a kernel ran" ← cheap, blind, always available (idle-waste) SM activity ─ "the SMs were busy" ← better tensor activity ─ "tensor cores engaged"← closer to useful FLOPs MFU ─ "useful FLOP/s / peak"← the truth, but needs the FLOP model each rung is closer to "useful training" and harder to measure.
Why idle-waste uses the bluntest rung deliberately

The idle-waste signal sits at the very bottom rung — 0% utilization for 5 minutes — and that's a design choice, not a limitation. The bluntest metric is the most universal: it requires no model, no instrumentation, no per-app knowledge, so it scales to every job on 18 clusters and catches the most egregious waste (genuinely idle GPUs) with near-zero false positives. The finer rungs (tensor activity, MFU) are reserved for deeper per-job analysis where you can afford the modeling. Matching the metric's fidelity to the analysis scope — blunt for fleet sweeps, precise for single-job deep dives — is exactly the engineering judgment the role rewards.

05Composing the three layers

The three layers combine into the headline goodput number, and understanding the composition is what lets you reason about where the biggest fleet wins are.

GOODPUT = scheduling_eff × runtime_eff × program_eff (multiplicative!) Worked illustration: scheduling_eff = 0.90 (10% lost to requeues/queue gaps/failures) runtime_eff = 0.75 (25% lost to startup + lost work + checkpoint) program_eff = 0.85 (15% idle-waste / sub-peak utilization while training) ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────── GOODPUT ≈ 0.90 × 0.75 × 0.85 ≈ 0.57 → only 57% of GPU-hours were useful! The multiplicative structure means: • no single layer dominates — a 0.9 in each still compounds to 0.73 • the BIGGEST lever is the WORST layer (here: runtime at 0.75) • fixing one layer can't exceed the ceiling set by the others

Two consequences shape the whole strategy:

  • Attack the worst layer first. Because the layers multiply, the marginal return is highest on the lowest-efficiency layer. A fleet at 0.9 × 0.75 × 0.85 should fix runtime (the 0.75), not chase the 0.85 program layer. The tools exist to tell you which layer is worst per job and per fleet.
  • The layers interact. A scheduling problem (requeues) causes a runtime problem (re-paid startup, lost work) — Manual II §07. So fixing bad nodes (scheduling) lifts the runtime layer too. The decomposition isn't just bookkeeping; it reveals causal chains across layers.

This multiplicative three-layer model is the intellectual core of the entire role, and the thing to be able to draw on a whiteboard in either required interview. Goodput = scheduling × runtime × program; each layer has its own instrument (Heimdall/jobsinfo-done, OneLogger, DCGM), its own owner, and its own fixes; the layers multiply so the worst one is the biggest lever; and they interact causally so fixes can cascade. Every tool in this volume — the toolkit, sisyphus, the rules engine, the dashboards — is an instrument for measuring, attributing, or acting on one of these three layers. When Matt asks you to "think about fleet efficiency as a system," this composition is the system.

Three layers, three instruments, one product: the fraction of every GPU-hour that became a trained model. They multiply, so the worst layer is always the next thing to fix.

Part II — OneLogger: the field model

The job's time budget.

OneLogger is the runtime layer's instrument — the framework-emitted telemetry that decomposes a job's wall-clock into productive training versus every flavor of overhead. Reading its fields fluently is what lets you say exactly where a job's time went, and it's the schema the rules engine reasons over. This part is the field model in detail, with a worked decode.

06What OneLogger is

OneLogger is a telemetry library integrated into the training frameworks (Megatron-LM, NeMo) that emits structured events about a job's lifecycle — startup phases, iteration timing, checkpoint saves, validation passes — which are aggregated into a per-job summary record.

PropertyDetail
Where it livesInside the training framework; emits as the job runs. Aggregated to the index df-hwinf-dcm-e2e-app-metrics-summary-*.
What it capturesThe runtime time budget: productive iteration time, startup time, lost work, unsaved work, checkpoint timing, validation — the full decomposition of l_run_time.
Application-awareYes — it knows training phases because the framework tells it. This is its power (precise attribution) and its limit (only instrumented jobs emit it).
GranularityPer-job (and per-execution) summary, plus the underlying event stream. The summary is what the rules engine and dashboards consume.
The instrumentation-coverage caveat that matters operationally

Because OneLogger only emits if the framework is instrumented, a job running a custom or older training loop may have no OneLogger record — and then the runtime layer is invisible for that job, leaving only the application-blind DCGM signal (§04). So one real efficiency lever is simply increasing OneLogger coverage — getting more jobs onto instrumented frameworks so their runtime waste becomes visible and fixable. "We can't fix what we can't see, so improving telemetry coverage is itself efficiency work" is a mature observability point, and it's why the team cares about framework defaults (Manual I §39 — corrected OneLogger templates as a shipped fix).

07The time-budget fields

The heart of OneLogger is a set of duration fields that partition the job's runtime. Knowing the key ones by name — and what each represents — is concrete fluency that shows you've worked with the real telemetry.

FieldMeaning
l_run_timeTotal wall-clock runtime of the job (the denominator for runtime efficiency).
d_train_iterations_time_total_productiveThe productive training time — actual forward/backward/optimizer iterations doing useful work. The numerator. Goodput's runtime component is this ÷ run_time.
d_app_startup_timeTime from process start to the first productive iteration: container load, framework init, model build, checkpoint load, the first NCCL bootstrap (§08).
d_lost_work_timeTraining time thrown away — iterations that ran but whose results were lost (e.g., progress since the last checkpoint when the job failed and resumed) (§09).
d_unsaved_work_timeProductive progress made but not yet checkpointed — the work that would be lost if the job failed right now. A risk measure.
checkpoint save / finalize timeTime spent writing checkpoints (and the async finalize tail) — the checkpoint overhead (Manual II §31).
validation timeTime in eval/validation passes that interrupt training.
The fields partition l_run_time (schematically): l_run_time = d_app_startup_time + d_train_iterations_time_total_productive ← the only "good" bucket + d_lost_work_time + checkpoint_save/finalize_time + validation_time + (other overhead / gaps) runtime_eff = d_train_iterations_time_total_productive / l_run_time and each non-productive bucket is a NAMED, ATTRIBUTABLE loss with a known fix.
Why naming the fields matters in the interview

Being able to say "d_train_iterations_time_total_productive over l_run_time is the runtime efficiency, and the gap decomposes into d_app_startup_time, d_lost_work_time, checkpoint, and validation" demonstrates you've actually engaged with the team's telemetry rather than speaking in generalities. The field names are the team's real vocabulary; using them precisely signals you'll be productive on day one. (Verify exact field names against the live schema — telemetry fields evolve — but the decomposition they encode is the durable, important part.)

08The startup tax decomposed

Startup (d_app_startup_time) is often the largest single runtime loss, and it's worth decomposing because each sub-phase has a different fix — and because it compounds brutally with requeues (every restart re-pays it).

d_app_startup_time, phase by phase: ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐ │ 1. container instantiation — Enroot unpacks the .sqsh │ │ fix: sbcast image to /raid (Manual II §14) │ │ 2. framework / library init — import torch, CUDA context, etc. │ │ 3. distributed bootstrap — NCCL rendezvous, build comms │ │ fix: correct MASTER_ADDR / NCCL interface (Manual II §16) │ │ 4. model build — allocate params, optimizer state │ │ 5. checkpoint LOAD — read weights to resume │ │ fix: faster storage / striping (Manual II §27); the │ │ tensor-loading benchmark targets exactly this │ │ 6. dataloader warmup — fill prefetch buffers │ └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘ each phase is independently measurable and fixable.

The compounding insight (Manual II §07) is the operationally critical one: startup is paid once for a clean run, but once per requeue for a flaky one. A job that requeues 5 times pays startup 6 times. So a high d_app_startup_time fraction can be a runtime problem (slow checkpoint load — fix storage) or a symptom of a scheduling problem (frequent requeues from bad nodes — fix the hardware). Distinguishing these requires joining OneLogger (startup time) with jobsinfo-done (requeue count) — which is exactly what the data plane (Part III) enables.

The cross-layer diagnosis this enables

"This job's startup is 30% of runtime" → is it one slow startup (a single big run with a slow checkpoint load → fix storage striping) or many startups (frequent requeues → fix the nodes)? The answer changes the owner and the fix entirely, and you get it by correlating d_app_startup_time with the execution count from jobsinfo-done. This is the kind of multi-source reasoning the platform is built for, and a great concrete example to offer when Matt asks how you'd investigate a high-startup-overhead job: "I'd check whether it's startup-per-run or startup-times-requeues, because those are different problems in different layers."

09Lost work & the checkpoint economy

d_lost_work_time and d_unsaved_work_time capture the checkpoint economy — the trade between checkpointing often (cheap insurance, but checkpoint overhead) and rarely (low overhead, but big losses on failure). This is a genuinely quantitative optimization and a favorite efficiency topic.

THE CHECKPOINT ECONOMY: ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────── checkpoint OFTEN checkpoint RARELY + small lost-work on failure − large lost-work on failure − high checkpoint overhead + low checkpoint overhead ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────── optimal interval balances: E[lost work] = ckpt_interval/2 × failure_rate vs ckpt_overhead × (run_time / ckpt_interval) KEY ASYMMETRY (Manual II §39 worked case): if ckpt_interval (e.g. 6h) > mean-time-to-failure (e.g. 2h), EVERY failure throws away ~interval/2 of work → lost-work explodes. → the interval must be SHORTER than the cluster's MTTF.
QuantityWhat it tells you · the lever
d_lost_work_timeWork actually thrown away. High → checkpoint more often, OR fix the failures causing the resumes (scheduling layer). The cost side of too-sparse checkpointing.
d_unsaved_work_timeWork at risk right now (since last checkpoint). A leading indicator — high unsaved work means a failure would be expensive, even if none has happened yet.
checkpoint intervalThe knob. The rules engine flags ckpt_interval > 4h as a threshold (§18) precisely because long intervals risk large lost work on failure-prone clusters.
checkpoint overheadThe other cost — time writing. Mitigated by async/parallel save (Manual II §31), which lets you checkpoint more often without paying more wall-clock.

The checkpoint economy is where runtime efficiency becomes a real optimization problem, and async checkpointing changes the whole trade. Naively, checkpointing more often reduces lost work but increases overhead — a balance. But async/parallel checkpointing (write in the background while training continues) makes saves nearly free in wall-clock, which means you can checkpoint far more frequently and crush lost-work without paying overhead. So the combined fix for a job bleeding lost work is: shorten the interval AND make saves async — get the insurance cheaply. This connects the OneLogger fields (the measurement) to the storage work (the mechanism) to the rules engine (the threshold) — a complete cross-layer efficiency story that plays well in both required interviews.

10Reading a OneLogger record

Putting the fields together: a worked decode of a job's OneLogger summary, the analytical move at the heart of single-job efficiency analysis.

worked decode A 512-GPU job's OneLogger summary
The record (schematic)
l_run_time:                                  100.0 h
d_train_iterations_time_total_productive:    58.0 h
d_app_startup_time:                          22.0 h
d_lost_work_time:                            12.0 h
checkpoint_save_finalize_time:               5.0 h
validation_time:                             3.0 h
The decode

Runtime efficiency = 58 / 100 = 58%. The 42% loss decomposes: startup 22%, lost work 12%, checkpoint 5%, validation 3%. The two big losses are startup and lost work — and together they suggest a single root cause.

The hypothesis (cross-field reasoning)

Startup at 22% is huge. Lost work at 12% is also high. Both point to frequent failures/requeues: each requeue re-pays startup (→ inflated d_app_startup_time) and discards work since the last checkpoint (→ inflated d_lost_work_time). To confirm, join with jobsinfo-done (§12): if the execution count is high (say 8 executions for this job), the story holds — this is a scheduling-layer failure problem manifesting as runtime waste.

The fix (spanning layers)

(1) Find and drain the bad nodes causing the requeues (scheduling, Manual II §34) — stops the churn at the source. (2) Shorten the checkpoint interval and make saves async (runtime, §09) — so each remaining failure costs less lost work. (3) Stage the image to /raid and check the checkpoint-load storage path (startup, §08) — so each restart's startup is cheaper. Net: runtime efficiency should climb from 58% toward the 80s.

The lesson

A OneLogger record isn't just a report card — it's a diagnostic. The pattern across fields (high startup + high lost work) points to a root cause (requeue churn) that no single field reveals, and the fix spans the scheduling, runtime, and storage layers. Reading records this way — pattern, hypothesis, cross-source confirmation, layered fix — is the analytical skill the efficiency role is built on.

A OneLogger record is a confession: it tells you exactly how a job spent its life. The skill is reading the pattern across the fields, not just the productive percentage.

Part III — The telemetry data plane

Fusing the signals.

No single source sees the whole picture, so the platform joins many. This part is the data engineering: the seven sources and what each contributes, the execution-record backbone that everything joins onto, the join keys and the time-skew problem that makes joining hard, the ETL that lands it all in queryable indices, and the data-quality issues that are an unglamorous but real part of the work. This is squarely Greg's observability territory and the substrate every tool stands on.

Why seven sources and not one big table

Each source is produced by a different system (the framework, DCGM, Slurm, the FACT pipeline, Heimdall, WandB) at a different granularity and cadence, and they're owned by different teams. There is no single system that sees scheduling + runtime + program + logs together — so the platform's job is to fuse them after the fact. This is a classic observability data-integration problem, and naming it as such ("the telemetry is federated across the systems that produce it, so the platform's core challenge is correlating it onto a common key") is exactly the framing Greg's observability interview is looking for.

12jobsinfo-done: the backbone

Among the seven, one is special: jobsinfo-done is the spine everything else joins onto, because it's the authoritative record of what actually executed. Understanding why it's the backbone is the key to the whole join model.

PropertyDetail
What it isThe Slurm accounting record (Manual II §13), transformed into an index: one row per execution (each requeue cycle is its own row), with job ID, cluster, start/end time, state, exit code, node list.
Why it's the backboneIt's the ground truth of execution — every other source describes some aspect of a job that appears here. So you anchor on jobsinfo-done and enrich it with OneLogger (runtime), DCGM (utilization), FACT (failures), etc.
Per-execution granularityCritical: a logical "job" may execute many times (requeues). jobsinfo-done captures each, which is exactly what lets you count requeues (§08) and attribute lost work per cycle.
The join key(s_jobid, s_cluster, ts_start) — job ID + cluster + start timestamp. Cluster is mandatory because job IDs are reused across clusters (Manual II §36); start time disambiguates executions.

Choosing the execution record as the join backbone is a deliberate and correct data-modeling decision. You could anchor on the logical job, or on time windows, or on the researcher — but the execution is the unit at which everything else is actually measured: a OneLogger record, a DCGM utilization summary, a FACT attribution all describe a specific run on specific nodes at a specific time. By making jobsinfo-done the spine and keying on (jobid, cluster, ts_start), the platform gets a stable anchor that every source can attach to. When asked how you'd architect a system to correlate heterogeneous job telemetry, "anchor on the authoritative execution record and enrich outward, keyed on job+cluster+start-time" is a strong, specific answer.

13The join model

Joining the sources is harder than it looks, because the keys don't line up cleanly — timestamps differ slightly between systems, job IDs collide across clusters, and some sources lag. The team's join model handles these, and the details are exactly the kind of data-engineering depth Greg probes.

THE JOIN (anchor = jobsinfo-done): jobsinfo-done row (s_jobid, s_cluster, ts_start) │ LEFT JOIN each source on the same logical key ├── OneLogger ............ matched by job+cluster, time-aligned ├── GPU-util (DCGM) ...... matched by job+cluster, time window ├── FACT ................. matched by job+cluster, time window └── Heimdall / WandB / Loki THE TWO HARD PROBLEMS: 1. CLUSTER SCOPING — job IDs REUSE across clusters. join on (jobid, cluster), NEVER jobid alone, or you mismatch jobs. 2. TIME SKEW — different systems stamp start times slightly differently. match within a ±300s (±5 min) window, not on exact equality.
ProblemThe handling
Job-ID reuseAlways include s_cluster in the key. A bare job ID is ambiguous across the ~18 clusters — joining on it alone silently merges unrelated jobs. (Manual II §36's "cluster scoping is mandatory" is fundamentally a join-correctness requirement.)
Time skewMatch start times within a ±300-second window, because the framework, Slurm, and DCGM don't stamp the same instant identically. Exact-match joins would drop valid correlations.
Lag / late dataSome sources land after the job ends (FACT attribution, finalized accounting). The pipeline must tolerate sources arriving at different times — re-join as late data lands.
Missing sourcesA job may lack OneLogger (uninstrumented) or DCGM (gaps). The join is a LEFT join from the backbone — keep the execution row even when enrichment is missing, and degrade gracefully (§29).
The ±300s window is a real engineering judgment

Why 5 minutes? Too tight and you drop valid matches (clock skew between systems exceeds a few seconds); too loose and you risk matching the wrong execution (two runs of the same job ID on the same cluster close in time). ±300s is the empirical sweet spot — wide enough to absorb inter-system skew, narrow enough to disambiguate executions given that the same (jobid, cluster) rarely restarts within 5 minutes. This is exactly the kind of "the messy reality of joining production telemetry" detail that demonstrates you understand observability data engineering beyond the textbook — and it's a great specific to cite when Greg asks about correlating telemetry across systems.

14The ETL & the indices

The raw telemetry has to be extracted, transformed, and landed somewhere queryable. The team's data plane runs through NVPark/OpenSearch, and understanding the shape of the ETL (not every detail) shows you grasp how the data actually flows.

15Data quality

The unglamorous truth of any telemetry platform: a lot of the work is handling bad, missing, or inconsistent data. The platform has an entire rule family (DQ) for it, which tells you how seriously the team takes it — and being honest about this is a mark of real experience.

Data-quality issueWhy it happens · how it's handled

Inconsistent valuesSources disagree (DCGM says busy, OneLogger says unproductive). The discrepancy is meaningful (§28) — but it must be detected, not silently averaged away.

Data quality is where credibility is won or lost. An efficiency finding delivered to a busy researcher must be right — if you tell someone their job wasted 40% of its GPU-hours and the number is wrong because of a bad join or missing telemetry, you lose their trust and they ignore every future finding. So the DQ rule family, the graceful degradation, the discrepancy detection, and the careful join model all serve one goal: trustworthy findings. The mature engineering stance — "most of the effort in a telemetry platform is not the clever analysis, it's making sure the data underneath is clean enough that the analysis can be trusted" — is exactly the kind of grounded, experienced perspective that lands well, and it's the honest reality of the work.

The clever analysis is the easy part. The hard, credibility-defining part is making the federated, skewed, sometimes-missing telemetry clean enough to trust.

Part IV — The rules engine

Signals into findings.

The curated telemetry (Part III) is just data until something turns it into actionable findings. The team's answer is a 110-rule deterministic engine — not an LLM guessing, but explicit, testable rules that each detect one inefficiency pattern and emit evidence. This part is the engine's design: why deterministic, the rule families, a rule's anatomy, the pipeline, a worked firing, and how 576 tests keep it honest. This is the platform's brain, and the clearest demonstration of the team's measurement-rigor culture.

16Why deterministic rules

A tempting design would be "feed all the telemetry to an LLM and ask what's wrong." The team explicitly does not do this as the core — it uses deterministic rules, with the LLM relegated to narrating (§34). Understanding why is a sharp insight into building trustworthy ML-ops tooling.

PropertyDeterministic rules (the core)LLM-only (rejected for the core)
CorrectnessProvably consistent — same input, same finding, every timeNon-deterministic; ~3.4% hallucination rate observed
TestabilityEach rule has unit tests + golden cases (576 tests, §21)Hard to test exhaustively; behavior drifts with the model
ExplainabilityThe rule IS the explanation — threshold + evidence, auditableReasoning is opaque; "why did it say that?" is unanswerable
TrustA researcher can verify the finding against the rule's logicA wrong finding erodes trust in all findings (§15)
Cost / latencyCheap, fast, runs over the whole fleetExpensive per job at fleet scale

The architectural principle: deterministic where correctness matters, LLM where fluency matters. The detection of an inefficiency must be correct, testable, and explainable — so it's a rule. The explanation of that finding in friendly prose is where an LLM's fluency helps and a mistake is low-stakes — so the LLM narrates a finding the rules already established, rather than deciding the finding itself. This division (rules decide, LLM describes) is a genuinely good piece of ML-ops engineering judgment, and articulating it — "I wouldn't trust an LLM to decide whether a job is inefficient, but it's great at explaining a finding the rules already proved" — is exactly the kind of mature, trust-first system thinking Matt's interview rewards. It also directly addresses the elephant in the room (why not just use an LLM for everything) with a principled answer.

This is good software engineering applied to inefficiency detection — and being able to discuss the engine as a well-factored rule system (rather than a black box) shows you'd contribute to it thoughtfully.

20A worked rule firing

The canonical example from Manual I, now in full mechanical detail: a 512-GPU job showing 0% goodput, diagnosed by a single G0-family rule. This is the engine at its best — turning a baffling symptom into a precise, evidenced root cause.

rule firing G0-HOOK: a 512-GPU job at 0% goodput
The symptom

A 512-GPU training job reports essentially 0% goodput — the dashboard shows it consuming GPUs but producing no productive training time. The GPUs look busy (DCGM utilization is non-zero), which makes it baffling: it's running, it's using GPUs, but no goodput.

What the rule checks

The G0-family rule for this pattern inspects OneLogger: d_train_iterations_time_total_productive is ~0 despite a large l_run_time and non-zero GPU activity. Productive iteration time near zero while the job runs and burns GPU cycles is the signature of "training is happening mechanically but the framework's productive-time accounting is never advancing."

The root cause it surfaces

The evidence points to the checkpoint/iteration hook never firing — specifically, on_save_checkpoint_end (the callback that marks productive progress) is never called, because of a misconfiguration in the training loop's callback wiring. So the job is doing forward/backward passes (GPUs busy), but the instrumentation milestone that records productive progress never triggers — and from the goodput system's view, no useful work is being recorded. Depending on the exact bug, this can also mean checkpoints aren't being written, so the "productive" work isn't even being persisted.

Why DCGM alone would mislead

This is the perfect illustration of why you need OneLogger, not just utilization: DCGM says "GPUs busy, all fine"; OneLogger says "productive time is zero." The discrepancy (busy but unproductive) is the finding — and only the application-aware runtime layer can see it. A utilization-only system would rate this catastrophic job as healthy.

The fix and the payoff

Fix the callback wiring so the hook fires; productive time begins accumulating; goodput jumps from 0% to normal. One config fix recovered the full output of a 512-GPU job — among the highest-leverage single fixes possible (512 GPUs is an enormous allocation to be producing nothing). And the engine found it deterministically, with evidence, no guessing.

The lesson

The most expensive wastes are often the most invisible to blunt metrics — a job that looks busy but produces nothing. The deterministic engine, reasoning over the application-aware runtime telemetry, catches exactly these, attributes them to a specific cause (a named hook not firing), and attaches the evidence. That's the value proposition of the whole platform in one case.

Part V — Instrumentation

Producing the signal.

Before telemetry can be joined and ruled over, it has to be produced — and producing it on live GPUs without disturbing them is its own deep problem. This part covers the instrumentation layer: DCGM for GPU telemetry, the eBPF tracing that Abdalla's write-tracer uses, the SPANK mechanism that attaches instrumentation transparently — and the cautionary tale, straight from Matt's work, of telemetry polling that slowed the very GPUs it measured. This is where observability meets the hardware, and the observer effect becomes real.

22The instrumentation landscape

The signals feeding the platform come from several instrumentation layers, each tapping a different part of the stack. Orienting on who produces what completes the picture from Part III's "sources" — here's where those sources are generated.

InstrumentProduces · how

The central tension of instrumentation: you must observe the system without perturbing it. Every measurement costs something — CPU cycles to trace a syscall, driver time to poll a counter, memory bandwidth to emit an event. On a system whose entire purpose is to maximize GPU throughput, instrumentation overhead is directly at odds with the thing being optimized. So the instrumentation layer is a constant balance: enough signal to diagnose, little enough overhead to not become the problem. The two stories in this part — write-tracer's near-zero overhead (a success) and the DCGM-NVLink slowdown (a failure) — are the two sides of that tension, and holding "the observer must not disturb the observed" as a design principle is exactly the kind of systems wisdom that distinguishes a senior engineer.

The cost of polling counters

DCGM reads hardware counters by querying the GPU and its driver — and that query is not free. For most counters at sensible intervals it's negligible. But some counters, on some architectures, are expensive to read because they require taking a lock or touching a contended driver path — and polling them too aggressively can interfere with the GPU's actual work. This is not hypothetical; it's exactly what happened on GB200 (§24). The lesson DCGM teaches is that telemetry is a load on the system, and which counters you poll, how often, matters — a subtlety most people don't appreciate until it bites.

24The DCGM-NVLink observer effect

The DCGM-NVLink-Test-Harness is one of Matt's most instructive pieces of work, because it's a textbook observer effect: the telemetry meant to monitor GB200's NVLink fabric was slowing down the fabric it measured. This story is worth knowing in detail — it's a perfect "deep debugging" narrative and directly Matt's.

observer effect DCGM NVLink polling slows cuMem* ~10× on GB200
The setup

On GB200 NVL72, the fabric-memory path (the cuMem* APIs for multi-node NVLink — Manual II §04) is hot and performance-critical. Separately, dcgm-exporter polls NVLink performance counters (the PPCNT_* family) to monitor fabric health. Both touch the NVLink/driver subsystem.

The symptom

The cuMem* fabric-memory operations were running roughly 10× slower than expected — a severe, baffling slowdown on a brand-new, expensive platform, hurting exactly the rack-scale workloads GB200 exists for.

The investigation

The slowdown correlated with telemetry being active. The mechanism: polling certain NVLink counters required acquiring a driver lock, and the cuMem* operations needed that same lock — so the monitoring and the actual fabric-memory work were contending on it. The telemetry wasn't just adding overhead; it was serializing against the critical path. A flamegraph (with help from Felix Abecassis) localized the culprit to a specific counter family — the COUNT_* counters were the expensive ones triggering the lock contention.

The harness

To pin it down rigorously, the work became a 5-configuration A/B test harness — systematically toggling which counter groups DCGM polled and measuring cuMem* performance under each — isolating exactly which counters caused the contention and proving the causal link (telemetry config → driver-lock contention → fabric-memory slowdown).

The resolution & the lesson

The fix is to not poll the offending counters (or poll them far less aggressively) — accepting slightly less telemetry granularity to recover the fabric performance. The deep lesson: observability is not free, and on a tightly-coupled system the act of measuring can contend with the work being measured. The telemetry that was supposed to help ensure GB200 ran well was, through driver-lock contention, making it run badly. This is the observer effect made concrete — and the disciplined, A/B-harness, flamegraph-driven way it was diagnosed is a model of rigorous systems debugging.

Why this is a perfect story for Matt's interview

It's his work, so engaging with it substantively shows genuine interest, and it exercises everything the role values: deep systems debugging (driver locks, flamegraphs), rigorous methodology (the 5-config A/B harness — measure, don't guess), the GB200/cuMem* fabric-memory specifics (Manual II §04), and the meta-insight about observability's cost. If the conversation reaches it, being able to say "the observer-effect harness is a great example of the principle that instrumentation is a load on the system — you found that NVLink counter polling was contending on a driver lock with the cuMem fabric-memory path and slowing it 10×, and the A/B harness isolated exactly which counters" demonstrates you've thought deeply about both the specific bug and what it teaches about building telemetry that doesn't harm the thing it watches.

26The SPANK integration

How does write-tracer attach to every job without researchers changing anything? Through SPANK (Manual II §18) — the Slurm plugin mechanism that hooks the task lifecycle. This "transparent attachment" is a recurring design value worth understanding as a pattern, not just a mechanism.

write-tracer's SPANK integration: plugin: slurm_write_tracer.so (registered in plugstack.conf) ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐ │ slurm_spank_task_init ── a job task is about to start │ │ → POST the task's PID to the tracer's REST API │ │ → tracing for this job's writes BEGINS automatically │ │ │ │ slurm_spank_task_exit ── the task finished │ │ → DELETE the PID from the tracer │ │ → tracing STOPS automatically │ └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘ net effect: I/O tracing turns on/off EXACTLY with every job, with ZERO researcher action and zero training-code change.

"Transparent, automatic instrumentation" is one of the team's deepest design values, and SPANK is how it's realized. The same mechanism runs Pyxis (containers), write-tracer (I/O tracing), and in principle any future per-job instrumentation — all attaching at task launch with no change to anyone's code. This matters because the alternative (asking researchers to add instrumentation to their training scripts) doesn't scale — most won't do it, and coverage stays patchy. By hooking at the Slurm task lifecycle, the platform gets fleet-wide coverage for free. When an interviewer asks "how would you instrument all jobs on a cluster," the SPANK task_init answer — "attach at task launch via a SPANK plugin, so it's transparent and universal, the way Pyxis and write-tracer do" — is a fleet-native, scalable answer that shows you think about coverage and adoption, not just the measurement itself.

The best instrumentation is the kind nobody has to opt into. Hook the task lifecycle, and the whole fleet is observable for free.

Part VI — Single-job triangulation

One job, three witnesses.

The fleet tools (toolkit, dashboards) scan broadly; sisyphus goes deep on a single job, cross-examining three independent telemetry layers to build a complete, self-checking picture. This part is the triangulation idea, the discrepancy detection that makes it robust, the graceful degradation that makes it practical, and a worked single-job investigation. It's Matt's tool, and a model for deep-dive diagnosis.

27The triangulation idea

The core insight of sisyphus: no single telemetry source is fully trustworthy, so corroborate a job's story across three independent layers. Where they agree, you're confident; where they disagree, you've found something.

SISYPHUS triangulates ONE job across three layers: ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐ │ SCHEDULING ── Heimdall: what disrupted the allocation? │ │ RUNTIME ── OneLogger: where did the runtime go? │ │ PROGRAM ── DCGM/FACT: were the GPUs working? any XIDs? │ └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘ three independent views of the SAME job │ agree → high-confidence diagnosis disagree → a DISCREPANCY = a finding in itself (§28) spans ~18 clusters; gracefully handles missing layers (§29).

Why three layers and why these three: they're the three goodput layers (Part I), and they're independent — Heimdall (scheduling) is produced by the remediation system, OneLogger (runtime) by the framework, DCGM/FACT (program/hardware) by the GPU layer. Independence is what makes corroboration meaningful: if three independent witnesses agree a job was healthy, it almost certainly was; if the framework says "productive" but DCGM says "idle," one of them is wrong or something subtle is happening — and that gap is the lead.

Triangulation turns the weakness of federated telemetry (Part III) into a strength. The fact that the data is scattered across independent systems — usually a headache — becomes an asset for a single-job deep dive: three independent measurements of the same job are far more trustworthy together than any one alone, and their disagreements are diagnostic. This is a sophisticated observability idea (cross-source corroboration as both a confidence measure and an anomaly detector), and naming it — "sisyphus uses the independence of the three telemetry layers to both build confidence when they agree and surface anomalies when they don't" — is a strong observability-interview point for Greg, while the tool itself is Matt's to discuss.

28Three-way discrepancy detection

The most powerful feature of triangulation is detecting when the three layers disagree — because each disagreement pattern points to a specific kind of problem. This is where sisyphus earns its keep.

The discrepancy IS the diagnosis

The elegant thing is that you don't need any single source to be authoritative — you need them to disagree in a characteristic way. "Busy but unproductive" is a different diagnosis from "productive but idle" is different from "disrupted but clean," and each maps to a different root cause and fix. So triangulation isn't just averaging three noisy measurements into one better number; it's using the structure of their disagreement as a classifier. This is a genuinely clever piece of observability design, and being able to explain it — "the value isn't any one source, it's the pattern of agreement and disagreement across the three; busy-but-unproductive means one thing, productive-but-idle means another" — demonstrates the kind of systems insight that makes you credible on the team's own tooling.

29Graceful degradation

Real telemetry is incomplete — a job may lack OneLogger, DCGM may have gaps, Heimdall may not have a record. A deep-dive tool that only works with perfect data is useless in practice, so sisyphus degrades gracefully, and that robustness is a mark of production-grade engineering.

Missing layerHow the tool adapts
No OneLoggerLose the runtime decomposition, but still triangulate scheduling (Heimdall) + program (DCGM) — and flag the OneLogger absence as a coverage finding (§15). A partial diagnosis beats none.
DCGM gapsUse the runtime and scheduling views; note reduced confidence in the program-layer assessment. Don't claim certainty you don't have.
No Heimdall recordRely on jobsinfo-done state + the other layers for the scheduling story.
Only one layer presentReport what that layer says, clearly labeled as un-corroborated — lower confidence, but still useful, and honest about the limitation.

Graceful degradation is the difference between a demo and a tool. It's easy to build something that produces a beautiful triangulated diagnosis when all three sources are present and clean; it's hard — and far more valuable — to build something that still gives a useful, appropriately-hedged answer when one or two sources are missing, which is the common case at fleet scale. The design stance — "always produce the best diagnosis the available data supports, clearly labeled with its confidence, rather than failing or pretending" — is exactly the production-engineering maturity the role needs. It also connects to the data-quality theme (§15): the tool treats missing data as a first-class condition (a finding and a confidence reduction), not an error. This kind of robustness-and-honesty design is worth foregrounding as something you'd build the same way.

30A worked single-job investigation

Triangulation end to end on one job — the deep-dive counterpart to the fleet sweep, and the analytical pattern for "a researcher's specific job is behaving strangely, figure out why."

triangulation "My job's dashboard goodput looks wrong — can you check?"
The request

A researcher's 256-GPU job shows mediocre goodput on the dashboard, but they insist their training is running fine and the numbers don't add up. A single-job deep dive (sisyphus-style) triangulates the three layers.

Layer 1 — scheduling (Heimdall + jobsinfo-done)

jobsinfo-done shows the logical job has 6 executions — it's been requeuing. Heimdall attributes the disruptions to two compute nodes throwing NVLink XIDs. So there's a real scheduling-layer problem (bad nodes → requeue churn).

Layer 2 — runtime (OneLogger)

OneLogger shows productive training at only 60%, with startup at 24% and lost work at 11% — consistent with the 6 requeues (each re-pays startup, each discards work since the last checkpoint). The runtime view corroborates the scheduling story: the requeues are the cause of the runtime waste.

Layer 3 — program (DCGM/FACT)

DCGM shows that while actually training, GPU utilization is healthy (no idle-waste within the productive periods), and FACT confirms the XID attribution to the two nodes. So the program layer is fine — the problem isn't the researcher's code or data pipeline at all.

The triangulated verdict

All three layers agree on a coherent story: the job's code is healthy (program OK), but bad nodes are causing requeues (scheduling), which inflate startup and lost work (runtime), dragging goodput down. The researcher was right that their training is fine — the waste is hardware-induced, not their fault. The fix is ops-side (drain the two nodes, Manual II §34) plus a runtime tweak (shorten checkpoint interval + async save so the remaining failures cost less).

The lesson

Triangulation produced a complete, corroborated diagnosis that correctly assigned the cause to hardware (not the researcher), and the three layers agreeing gave high confidence. Crucially, it also gave the researcher a blameless answer backed by evidence from three independent sources — exactly the "bring data, not blame" delivery (Manual II §39) that gets fixes adopted. That's the deep-dive tool doing its job: turning a confusing goodput number into an evidenced, layered, actionable, and fair diagnosis.

One job, three independent witnesses. When they agree, you have a diagnosis you can stake your credibility on — and a researcher you didn't wrongly blame.

34LLM narratives & hallucination

The LLM appears twice in the platform — narrating rule findings (SP5, §19) and powering the bot (§33) — and both face the hallucination problem. How the team bounds it (~3.4% measured rate, golden evals, determinism-first) is a model of responsible LLM use in production.

ConcernThe mitigation
Hallucinated findingsPrevented structurally: the LLM narrates findings the rules already established (§16) — it can't invent a problem, only describe a real one. Determinism-first is the primary defense.
Hallucinated numbersThe bot queries real telemetry via MCP tools (§33) rather than recalling — grounding in data, not memory.
Garbled explanationsGolden evals: narrations are checked against known-correct explanations; the ~3.4% hallucination rate is measured, not assumed — you can't manage what you don't measure.

The team's LLM stance is a template for responsible production AI: deterministic core, LLM for fluency, grounded in data, and rigorously measured. They measure the hallucination rate (3.4%) rather than hand-waving it, they confine the LLM to low-stakes roles (describing, not deciding), they ground it in real data (tools, not memory), and they eval it against golden cases. This is exactly how a careful engineer deploys LLMs where correctness matters — and it's a pointed contrast to the naive "just ask GPT" approach. Articulating it — "I'd use the LLM for what it's good at, fluent explanation, keep the decisions deterministic and tested, ground every number in queried data, and measure the hallucination rate so it's a managed quantity, not a hope" — demonstrates both LLM sophistication and the engineering maturity to use it responsibly. Given the team is actively building LLM-assisted tooling, this is a genuinely relevant and differentiating perspective.

35The Neo4j rules graph

The rules and their relationships are represented in a Neo4j knowledge graph — capturing how findings, causes, and fixes relate. This structures the engine's knowledge beyond a flat list and enables richer reasoning.

What the graph capturesWhy it helps
Rule → finding → cause → fix relationshipsA finding isn't isolated; it links to likely causes and recommended fixes. The graph encodes this domain knowledge explicitly.
Causal chains across layersBad node → requeue → startup inflation → goodput loss (the recurring chain). The graph can represent that one root cause produces a cascade of findings across layers (Part I §05).
Related/co-occurring findingsFindings that tend to appear together (high startup + high lost work → requeue churn, §10) can be linked, so the system reasons about patterns, not just individual rules.
Grounding for the LLMThe graph gives the narration/agent a structured knowledge base to reason over — relationships it can traverse rather than infer, improving grounding.
Why a graph and not a table

Inefficiency findings are related — causes produce cascades, findings co-occur, fixes address multiple findings — and those relationships are naturally a graph, not rows. Representing them in Neo4j lets the platform traverse from a symptom to its likely root cause to its fix, and to recognize when several findings share one cause (so you fix the cause, not each symptom). It also gives the LLM layer a structured substrate to ground its reasoning in. This is a thoughtful modeling choice — "the knowledge about how inefficiencies relate is inherently a graph (cause→effect→fix, co-occurrence), so a graph database fits the domain better than a flat rules list" — and recognizing why the data model matches the domain is the kind of design sensibility that rounds out a deep understanding of the platform.

Detection is half the platform. The other half is turning every finding into the right action, delivered the right way — and promoting each recurring lesson into a default no one has to think about.

39A worked fleet-scale case

The capstone scenario — the full systemic-discovery loop (§37) end to end, exercising the entire volume and the entire series. This is the shape of the answer to "tell me about discovering and fixing a fleet-wide inefficiency," the role's literal job.

fleet scale A checkpoint-overhead rule firing across a whole cohort
1 — Observe (fleet view, §36)

On the dashboard, the GL-CKPT-OVERHEAD rule (checkpoint >20% of runtime) is firing on a large fraction of jobs on several clusters — and run-time-weighted goodput on those clusters is notably depressed. Finding-frequency analysis confirms it's not a few jobs; it's a pattern across a cohort (say, all the large-model jobs from a particular team's templates).

2 — Characterize (cross-job, §37)

Using the toolkit's --all-clusters mode, you pull the affected jobs and look for the common thread. The pattern: they're all using synchronous checkpointing with a short interval and many checkpoint files per save — they checkpoint often (good for lost work) but synchronously, so every save blocks training, inflating checkpoint overhead past 20%. It's a shared config inherited from a common template.

3 — Root-cause (deep dive, §30 + Manual II §31)

Run sisyphus on a few representatives: OneLogger confirms checkpoint save/finalize is the dominant non-productive bucket; the storage view shows the saves are serialized (not async, not fully-parallel). The root cause is config, not hardware: --async-save and --ckpt-fully-parallel-save are off, and the checkpoint dir isn't striped across enough OSTs (Manual II §27). The blocking synchronous save is the whole problem.

4 — Ship a scalable fix (§31)

Don't email each job's owner (per-job firefighting). Instead, fix the template: enable async + fully-parallel checkpointing and proper Lustre striping in the shared default that the cohort inherits. Now every current and future job from that template gets non-blocking checkpoints — overhead drops below threshold without anyone changing their own script. Ship a short doc explaining the async-checkpoint default and why it's safe, and add/keep the rule so regressions are caught.

5 — Measure the impact (the feedback loop, §36)

Over the next weeks, the GL-CKPT-OVERHEAD firing frequency falls across the cohort, and the affected clusters' run-time-weighted goodput climbs measurably. The fix is validated at fleet scale by the same platform that surfaced the problem — the loop closes.
